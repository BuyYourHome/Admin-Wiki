# Chapter Four

## Going West

When Mona went home to be with the Lord, the Sandon home seemed to come apart with grief. They were almost beside themselves. In time they decided to go west to Montana and take up a homestead, much as my father had done years before. The available land nearer home had already been claimed. Montana was where the open homesteads remained.

Mr. Sandon wanted Merl and me to go with him. My sister would come too, driving the Model T while the rest of us helped move the livestock.

That Model T was a wonder to look at, though it was not always a wonder to operate. Before it, my father had owned a one-cylinder Brush automobile with wooden axles and a chain drive. Fifteen miles an hour was about all it could do. A man could nearly run alongside it once it got into gear.

The Ford seemed modern by comparison. It had a brass radiator standing straight at the front, brass headlights, brass hubcaps, and a carbide tank on the running board. The top of the radiator had a shape all its own, and leather straps ran back from the frame to keep the top from flying behind the car as it moved.

The headlights required a little ceremony. Carbide looked something like stone. Water dripped onto it inside a heavy tank, and the reaction produced gas. A small tube carried the gas forward to the lamps. You opened the valve and waited with a match while the gas worked its way up. Then you opened the headlight doors and held the match there until the flame caught. To put out the lights, you stopped the water from dripping onto the carbide.

The car's tires were narrow clincher tires. Its transmission used bands for low gear, reverse, and braking. When the brake band wore out, a driver sometimes used low or reverse to help stop. Replacing a band beside the road could take half a day. Everything had to be done through a small opening at the top of the transmission. You loosened the nuts carefully and packed rags around the opening, because dropping one into the oil chamber was a calamity. Then you pulled out the bands, relined them with a hammer and riveting tool, and worked them back into place.

Anyone who drove far carried spare bands and the tools to change them. Years later, traveling to see my family in Florida, I had to pull off the road more than once and make that repair before we could continue. Once we stopped somewhere in North Florida, put up our tent for the night, and waited while I worked on the car.

But on the Montana trip, the Model T carried more than tools and machinery. It carried our hope of beginning again.

I cannot say with certainty how old I was. I was still a teenager. I had already left home once after falling out with my folks, had been away working for my board, and had returned. By then Jesus Christ was real in my heart, even if I still had much to learn.

Someone had fitted the Ford with a new kind of ignition. The device had been advertised as an improvement over the original equipment, but it had not been proved on a journey like ours. Out in the Badlands, on or near a reservation east of Baker, Montana, the Model T could go no farther.

We left it and continued with the livestock.

My sister traveled as far as Baker and boarded a train for home. The rest of us drove the stock south, down toward the Powder River country. The distance from Baker to the homestead was said to be about one hundred miles. Ridgeway was our post office, and mail came down from Baker only occasionally.

Once the animals had reached the new place, Merl and I went back for the Model T. What we found hardly looked like the same automobile. Every bright piece that could be removed was gone—the brass hubcaps, buckles, headlights, and anything else that had shone in the sun. We had no choice but to leave the car where it was and make our way back.

The homestead itself soon became a trial. The country was bleak, and the water carried so much alkali that the livestock suffered. Mr. Sandon had brought a valuable stallion, hoping to raise stock on the new land. Within about a month the horse became sick and died. Other animals began to fail too. Grass and feed were scarce, and finding enough food for the people was becoming a problem of its own.

I grew terribly homesick. The Sandons had all they could do to care for themselves and the surviving stock. At last I decided there was only one thing left for me to do: start back toward Baker and make the best of a bad situation.

I set out alone on that long road. Before I had gone far, hunger and thirst began to overtake me.

# Chapter Five

## Shepherd

By the time I saw the first farmhouse, set well back from the road, I was almost beside myself. I had little strength left. Still, I thought I might offer to work in exchange for something to eat and drink.

An older man met me on horseback. He carried a quirt—a short handle with knotted leather strips hanging from it. He demanded to know what I was doing on his place.

I told him I was sorry to intrude. I was hungry and thirsty, I said, and I wondered whether there was any work I could do for a meal and a drink.

He cursed me. If I wanted to eat, he said, I needed to work, because he had to work for everything he got. Then he took me behind his barn to a wet, boggy patch and handed me a grubbing hoe. He told me to remove a stump.

The edge of that hoe was no sharper than its back. I struck at a root, but instead of cutting, the blade merely peeled the bark. The stump moved, and the whole wet ground around it shook. I tried again, but the tool would not bite.

I carried the hoe back and showed it to him. I said it would not cut and that I was too weak with hunger to do the work without a usable tool. He cursed me again and drove me back toward the road.

I left broken in spirit.

Perhaps a mile or two farther on, I came to another farmhouse. I do not remember how much farther I could have walked. A woman met me at the door, looked at me, and said, “You poor thing. Come into the house.”

There was love in the way she received me. She began preparing food and asked where I had been. I told her about the neighboring ranch.

She said she was sorry I had stopped there. The man was feared even by his neighbors. When her family cut hay near the uncertain property line, they had to watch themselves. He had fired at them before, she said, and once put a bullet through her husband's hat.

She fed me and let me rest. When her husband returned from tending his sheep, she told him what had happened. Between them they decided they could offer me work. If I was willing, I could care for their flock.

That was how I became responsible for approximately fifteen hundred sheep.

The flock ranged away from the farmhouse, and I stayed near them with a camp wagon. The family brought me bacon and sourdough flour. When a letter arrived from home, they brought that too. Mail came a hundred miles from Baker, and it did not come often.

I had been with the sheep for some time when I heard noise beyond a hill. I walked to the top and looked south. Below me, people had gathered at the Ridgeway post office for a Fourth of July picnic. I had not known the date until then.

Among the people I recognized Merl and his mother and father. It was my first contact with the outside world in weeks, apart from the family's supply visits. I went down long enough to learn what day it was and tell them where I had been, but I could not stay. The sheep were my responsibility. Coyotes and thieves were a danger, and I was anxious to please the people who had shown me such kindness.

Near sundown the sheep gathered themselves and began moving toward the camp wagon. I made a circle around them, as I did each evening. On one round I noticed a commotion several animals inside the flock.

An old ewe lay on the ground, still moving. When I lifted her, the side of her neck was wet. She was warm but dying. Only a few feet away, a tall, lean coyote rose from the ground. It had come directly into the flock, pulled down the ewe, and crouched nearby when it saw me approach.

I was frightened. The coyote moved away sideways, snarling, and I did not know whether it might turn on me. I put a shell into my rifle and fired. I missed. I worked another shell into the chamber and fired again. The coyote only ran faster.

That night I placed white flags around the flock. Each flag was a square of white cloth fastened to a handle. Set several feet beyond the resting sheep, the movement of the cloth helped keep coyotes, wolves, and wild dogs at a distance. The coyotes remained on the surrounding hills and cried in the darkness. I have heard coyotes since then, but never again a cry as terrible as the one I heard in that country.

I knew predators must have taken other animals as the flock scattered over the range, but that ewe was the only loss I saw happen so close to me.

Another loss came by my own hand.

There was a little lamb, perhaps fifteen or twenty pounds, square and fat and full of play. One day I lay along the side of a bank and noticed a white mark on a rock some distance away. I decided to practice with the rifle and see how close I could come.

I never considered what the bullet might do after striking stone.

When I fired, the bullet hit the rock and ricocheted into the valley below. The flock was off to my left. I saw the little lamb fall.

I ran to it, but there was nothing I could do. My careless shot had killed it.

I will never forget how badly I felt. Nothing could undo what had happened. I had looked at the rock, thought only about the shot, and failed to consider what lay beyond my target.

In later years the Lord brought that lamb back to my mind. People can be careless with words just as I had been careless with the rifle. We speak without considering where our words will travel or whom they may strike. Young believers listen. Discouraged people listen. Someone hears unkind speech and wonders whether Christian faith makes any difference at all.

A bullet cannot be called back after it leaves the barrel, and a word cannot be withdrawn after it leaves the mouth. It travels from one person to another: “Do you know what Brother Evans said? Do you know how he feels?” Before long the harm has gone far beyond the place where it began.

That lamb taught me to pray for greater care. May our words be words of praise and thanksgiving. May the Lord help us become an influence for his saving and keeping grace.

I still have far to go, but he is helping me.
