Rosebrooks, Jenny’s thoughts.

I remember when Wes first told me about the call from Brynda.  She had
received our pre foreclosure letters.  6 actually.  I checked the CRM and
spreadsheet records. She had received letters over a 6 week period and not
responded, and she was facing the foreclosure being granted by the court
within a few weeks.  He told me he had an appointment to go and meet with her
at the Rosebrooks property. Upon a quick review,  I saw that it was an estate
where she had inherited the townhouse from her deceased sister.  Sounded
pretty typical, for an estate at least.  However, unlike many estates  (we’ve
bought a number of properties from heirs), the heir was actually living in the
house.  We had seen both, but when the house is not the residence of the
heir(s), it’s much simpler. They see any money to them as a windfall. It’s a
“win win”, a positive situation. Not the same as them needing to live there or
somewhere else.  After the appointment, Wes and I talked about Brynda’s
situation.  She wanted to stay in the home, but didn’t have the funds to bring
the mortgage current.  She told Wes she had no place to go and no one to help
her.  This wasn’t the first elderly person who had this situation. We had
already worked out arrangements with 2 others, that we would own the home, and
they could lease it back for as long as they wanted to. Those people were
appreciative. Things were going smoothly with them. I can still hear myself
saying to Wes, half joking, half serious: “This isn’t just a business, it’s a
ministry.” It was a “good” feeling, we were helping these people. Wes and I
have always wanted our business to be one of service to others. Service to
sellers, and to buyers, and to contractors and realtor, and tenants. We have
not taken this opportunity lightly, but to heart. Once we had talked about how
many individuals and their families that we had the opportunity to bless
through the business.  Daily, I thank the Lord for trusting this to us. We
never want to jeopardize that.

Wes and Brynda came to a mutual agreement that included his paying the
arrears, taking over the mortgage payments, and her signing over the deed.  In
return, she could lease back the property for as long as she wanted to live
there. It wasn’t an especially valuable property, maybe the lowest value we
had bought. That was OK.  Dollars were not at the forefront of what we were
about.  Unlike any other “deal” we had made, Brynda called on Wes regularly to
assist her, mostly with transportation to doctor’s appointments, since she
didn’t drive.  He may have taken her to a dozen or more, it seemed often to
me. I quickly noticed she had difficulty getting food, so we took her hot food
on several occasions, and then Wes set up some online ordering systems
(Walmart, Amazon, etc) where she could choose what she needed or wanted.  To
be completely honest, she seemed demanding to me. But. She had needs, and it
appeared she had no one to help her with them. So we did. I have to add that
she took care of all her own medications, finances, bills, appointments, etc.
She was more than capable of maintaining her daily life. Wes encouraged her to
text daily, just so he knew she was OK. At times, her texts were fairly
amusing, when she spoke of recent sports events, holidays, etc. I recall that
she wrote in perfect, complete sentences, correct grammar, and punctuation. As
demanding as she was, she did seem a little bit appreciative.  Not in her
nature, I felt like.

Nearly 11 months after Wes and Brynda met, her estranged daughter came to NC
for a visit.  Her first visit in five years. Wes asked to meet for lunch, and
we did. It was awkward, Brynda was silent. I had a bad feeling about her
daughter, and her motives for the visit.  In the following weeks, Brynda’s
texts were completely different.  No complete sentences, punctuation, just
today’s typical text slang. Something wasn’t right. We went over to the house.
No one answered the knocks on the door.  It was locked up tight and the blinds
were closed.  We suspected she was gone, and later learned her daughter had
taken her to MD, without a word to us. It hurt that we didn’t get to say
goodbye.

Within a month, we were served with a lawsuit, from Brynda, with her daughter
as guardian. It came as a shock to me. I was numb.  Was it fear? Anger? Hurt?
I know other real estate investors say lawsuits are to be expected in this
business.  For them, not for ME. I was insulted, how dare her? I wanted Brynda
to look us in the face and speak WHY. But, deep down, I knew the truth, it
wasn’t Brynda’s doing.  She got caught in the crosshairs of a guilt ridden and
greedy estranged daughter, who never visited or called. Until the home (her
inheritance) was gone.  After getting some referrals, we hired one of the most
respected law firms in all of Raleigh, if not the entire state. We began zoom
calls, and due diligence, and depositions, motions, briefs, hearings.  I
learned a legal language and system, I never wanted to learn. I knew I could
“help” our attorneys with our case.  Surprisingly enough, they welcomed it. I
spent countless hours in reading, researching, and gathering everything I
could that should help our case.  I uncovered some key facts that proved to be
valuable when our attorneys were before the judge. They said I would make a
“good paralegal”, but I felt more like a private investigator, a career I
secretly admired in younger years.   Months went by, the legal fees were
enormous.  Who makes over $600 a hour? I was stunned!  I had to stop looking
at the monthly statements. I had to trust that God would provide. I was
reminded of scriptures teaching us He said, “I will repay you for the years
the locusts have eaten.” Joel 2:25  And “What the devil meant for evil, God
meant for good.” Gen. 50:20.   I know God is the Master of the plan. He takes
the painful things in life and weaves them together into something beautiful.
I clung to these teachings.

Eventually, after 18 months, it all came down to one well written and well
presented Brief.  One hearing in front of the Judge. It’s called Summary
Judgment and both sides get to ask the judge to rule in their favor.  I was a
stranger to courtrooms. I didn’t like anything about being there.  That
setting was meant for someone else, not for me. It felt like I wasn’t really
there. Maybe I was watching a show, someone else’s show, not mine. Brynda’s
attorney told so many untruths and twisted what had been a good thing, into
something we didn’t recognize. I must be old fashioned, but I never knew
people lied bold face in court.  After two hours, it was over and we all left
the courthouse. It was wait now for the judge to rule. I thought that might be
in a few days. It was TEN LONG weeks!  Wes and I prayed every day for the
judge to rule in our favor. And we believed it. Finally, the day came, and we
got an email from our attorney. (AN EMAIL! Just another day in the life of an
important attorney) The judge had denied all of their 12 charges, except for
1. “Undue Influence” Why did that one stay on the table? The judge said it had
“a general issue of material fact”. More legal rhetoric for me to understand.
With a charge still remaining, we were now facing mandatory Mediation, and
possible a jury trial. The LAST thing I wanted to happen.

My reaction was, first of all, relief and thankfulness to God. He had moved a
mountain in front of us. We stopped what we were doing and prayed a prayer of
thanks. We set up a zoom meeting with our attorney and I resumed my “work on
our case” by making a list of questions and thoughts for the meeting. Wes and
I discussed what some possible outcome might be. I was ready to make a
settlement, end it as soon as we could, move on. Was that fear? Was it wisdom?
Wes would consider a settlement, IF it met his expectations. I had doubts we
were going to hear anything close to that. He was ready and willing to go to
trial.  In front of a jury. The thought almost paralyzed me.
