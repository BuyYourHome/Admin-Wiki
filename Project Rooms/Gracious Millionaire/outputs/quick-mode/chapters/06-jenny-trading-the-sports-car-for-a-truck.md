# Jenny: Trading The Sports Car For A Truck

By 1980, we wanted to start a family.

That desire changed the way I looked at everything. It changed how I thought about work, money, cars, time, and what kind of life we were trying to build. Neither of us had fond memories of going to the babysitter while our parents worked, and we agreed that when we had children I would be a stay-at-home mother somehow.

That word somehow carried a lot of weight.

We did not have a large cushion. We were not wealthy. We were not stepping into a life where one income would obviously be enough. But we had made a commitment, and I took commitments seriously. If I was going to leave my job, I wanted to know we could survive it. I wanted proof before I moved.

That is my safe nature. I do not like guessing with the stability of a family.

At the same time, Number One Husband was working for his brother and wanted to start his own remodeling business. That meant he needed a truck. We did not have money sitting around for that, so we sold my beautiful 260Z sports car and used the money to buy him one.

I loved that car.

I do not want to pretend I did not. It was not just transportation to me. It was something I enjoyed, something that felt like mine. But priorities have a way of changing when a baby becomes more than an idea. I was looking forward to motherhood. I was looking forward to being home. I was willing to give up something I loved for something I believed mattered more.

That is one of the difficult parts of telling a life story honestly. At the time, sacrifice can feel noble and practical at the same time. Later, a person can look back and see more layers. The truck was connected to his hope for work. The sale of my car was connected to our hope for family. Both were connected to money. Both were connected to responsibility. Both were connected to trust.

I do not remember thinking of it as a spiritual decision. I remember thinking of it as a practical one.

We also did something very much in keeping with my personality. We saved every paycheck I earned for one solid year to see whether we could afford for me to quit working. We did not merely say, "We hope this works." We tested it. We lived as though my income was already gone and watched what happened.

That was my way of trying to remove uncertainty.

Looking back now, I can see how God honored that commitment. At the time, I may have thought it was our good planning. And yes, it was planning. It was careful. It was disciplined. It was the kind of thing I still believe people should do when making a large family decision.

But planning was not the whole story.

In 1981, our first child, a son, was born. I quit my full-time job, which I did not like anyway, and I did not really return to being an employee during the years of raising children.

That decision shaped the rest of my life. It affected our income, our household, our stress level, our marriage, and the way I saw my role. I was glad to be home with my son. I believed that was where I was supposed to be. But joy in the right decision does not mean the decision has no cost.

The house payment still had to be made. The truck still had to be useful. The business still had to produce. The grocery money still had to stretch. A baby brought love into the home, but also responsibility.

I was learning that family decisions and real estate decisions are rarely separate. The home, the mortgage, the income, the vehicle, the business, and the babies were all tied together.

I wanted a safe life. I wanted a responsible life. I wanted a home where my children could be raised with love and stability.

God was kind to me in that season. He let me have the thing I deeply wanted: to be home with my child.

But He did not promise that the rest of it would be easy.
