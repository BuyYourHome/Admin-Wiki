# Favor

Favor is something I have been learning about deeply for about eight years.

By this point in the story, I hope it is clear that favor is not another word for control.

That distinction matters to me. I have sometimes wanted influence because I wanted people to get on board with my plan. But favor, as I am learning it, is not God helping me pressure people more effectively. It is not charm. It is not persuasion polished up with spiritual language. It is not a way to make ambition look holy.

Favor belongs under lordship.

I should credit the person who has been the most influential to us in this regard. I started watching YouTubes of Lance Wallnau about eight years ago when I was in between jobs. Favor is something you do not just have because of your position or experience. Education and expertise are not the key components of favor.

Remember, everything we are saying here pivots upon our relationship to the King of this Kingdom.

Jesus grew in wisdom and in stature and in favor with God and all the people.

That sentence matters to me because it joins things we sometimes separate. Wisdom. Stature. Favor with God. Favor with people. Jesus did not need to manipulate people. He did not need to posture. He did not need to borrow authority from money, position, or fear. He walked in assignment, and favor followed.

It is just that I believe, from a Christian point of view, a Christian who is striving for the Kingdom of God cannot and should not make success the goal.

Let me back up and speak to the person who is not coming from this point of view.

You can follow the principles of success books and have great success. I am not denying that. There are principles of diligence, focus, stewardship, relationship, gratitude, and wise action that work in the world because God made the world with moral structure. But if you are a Christian striving for the Kingdom of God, your success will be very limited if success, prosperity, wealth, fame, or influence is your goal.

Why is that?

Because God is more interested in what is happening to you internally than what is happening to you externally.

But how can you write a book titled *Gracious Millionaire* and not be teaching about success?

Let me say it plainly. I do want to be successful. I even want to be wealthy. I am not pretending otherwise. But when I put any of these goals ahead of God's purposes, I have always found that God has another card to play.

Jenny and I have benefited greatly from favor. It would be easy to think that the effort we have put into educating ourselves and the industrious plan we have implemented are what have resulted in our success. Of course, this is partly true. I think it is unlikely we would have received any of the results we have had if we had not taken these steps.

But these steps are not taken in a vacuum.

Our mentors in real estate have not taught these steps in a vacuum. Their lessons are prefaced with leading from a servant's heart, being grateful in all things, and demonstrating that by personal example when we are around them.

I want to take a closer look at the role favor has had in our success.

Lance Wallnau's definition of favor is:

**The attraction of God to you, that releases an influence through you, so other people are inclined to like, trust and cooperate with you in the assignment God gave you.**

That is really a mouthful.

I believe it is the centerpiece of our success.

Bill Johnson says there is a kind of confidence in the children of God who put a "demand on who he is as father." I do not take that to mean bossing God around. I take it to mean trusting His nature enough to ask, move, knock, risk, and expect Him to be good. Favor is not entitlement. Favor is confidence that grows out of relationship.

Of course there is nothing specific about real estate in that definition, or any other endeavor. It does not speak to a career or an inclination to do any particular thing. It speaks to the assignment God gave you. That assignment can change from day to day.

I want to be listening every day for what that assignment is. I hope that I am. I invite my Father to correct me. He does not really need my invitation, but I like the posture of being correctable.

A scripture that I have been aware of most of my life is:

You will see your teacher with your own eyes. Your own ears will hear him. Right behind you a voice will say, "This is the way you should go," whether to the right or to the left.

That speaks to being led by God.

We all have assignments. Perhaps many get ignored along the way. We should pay attention to the clues He gives us about our assignment. In the vocation Jenny and I have chosen as real estate investors, we have assignments. We also have assignments that have nothing to do with real estate. Sometimes we have assignments in the context of real estate that really have nothing to do with real estate.

One day I was looking to acquire a particular piece of property. Our normal approach, after identifying a property that looks inviting, is to go knock on the door.

"Let's see what happens."

Of course I am wanting to meet the owner and see if they are interested in selling. My approach is usually humble. I knock on the door and then step back to assess how I am going to be received. I have found that this definition of favor usually follows me in these circumstances.

On this particular day, my assignment was apparently not to buy real estate.

It was to meet and talk to the occupant of the house.

He did not own the house, but he was the executor of the will of his fiancee who had owned it. She had died and left him alone and despondent. He invited me to sit in his living room. In the course of our long conversation, I told him what our interest was, but I also listened to him tell his story. He let me encourage him and pray for God's blessing on him.

I never heard from him again.

But I believe the assignment did him good. Without God's favor, I would not have been well received. He would not have let me into his house and opened up. I do not know what happened to him, but I know that I fulfilled my assignment that day.

Sometimes my assignment leads to a purchase of a property.

Sometimes it leads to a conversation.

Sometimes it leads to prayer in a living room.

When you have a divine assignment, you can approach every situation with less fear about the outcome. Sometimes the outcome leads to profitable situations. Sometimes it does not. But if the assignment belongs to God, the outcome is not the only way to measure whether you succeeded.

That is where expectation has to be purified. I am not expecting every door to open just because I want it open. I am expecting the Father to be good, present, and purposeful. I am expecting Him to show me what is in my hand, and then I am responsible to steward it instead of burying it.

That is the cleanest way I know to say the lesson.

Favor is not the power to get my way.

Favor is grace to walk in His way.
