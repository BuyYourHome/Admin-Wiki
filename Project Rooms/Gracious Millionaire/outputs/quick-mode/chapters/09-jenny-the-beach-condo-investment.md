# Jenny: The Beach Condo Investment

I moved to Raleigh, North Carolina, in 2002.

After the sale of the house, I took much of the proceeds and invested them in an oceanfront condo in Garden City Beach, South Carolina, south of Myrtle Beach. I did not buy it because I intended to live there. I bought it as an investment.

On paper, it made sense to me.

I carefully calculated how the condo would rent to vacationers. I looked at the mortgage, HOA fees, insurance, and the other numbers I knew to consider. I hired an onsite management company to take care of the rental side. They charged 40 percent, which sounds like a lot because it is a lot. But I had done my homework, and 40 percent was the going rate up and down the beach.

That mattered to me. I did not want to be careless. I did not want to be talked into something without checking. If I was going to put a significant portion of my proceeds into a property, I wanted to know that the numbers were based on something real.

The first summer seemed to confirm the plan. The rentals paid an entire year of mortgage payments.

I thought I was on my way.

That is the dangerous thing about an early success. It can feel like confirmation. It can make a person believe the plan was complete because the first visible result was good. I had calculated the rental income. I had accounted for the management fee. I had considered insurance. I had looked at the expenses I understood.

But I had missed something important.

In all my careful research and planning, I had failed to investigate how the HOA dues and assessments might increase.

That is not a small oversight in a condominium investment. It was a structural oversight. HOA fees and assessments are not optional. They are part of ownership. They can change the entire investment picture even when the mortgage is being paid.

Within three or four years, those fees had put me in the red. The rental money still easily paid every mortgage payment, which made the situation even more frustrating. The property was not failing in the way I had expected a property might fail. It was not sitting empty. Vacationers were renting it. The mortgage was covered by rental income.

But the other costs had moved against me.

That was when I panicked.

I do not use that word lightly. This was all on me. I had made the investment decision. I had used the proceeds. I had believed the numbers. I did not see a way to stay afloat.

A person can be cautious and still be caught by what she did not know to ask. That is one of the humbling lessons of my life. I had done homework, but not enough of the right homework. I had calculated, but not completely. I had tried to be wise, but I lacked experience.

That lack of experience was costly.

I hired a contractor to completely remodel the condo. My thinking was that if I could make it more attractive, I could sell it and get out from under the pressure. Again, I was trying to solve the problem in a practical way. Improve the property. Put it on the market. Find a buyer. Stop the financial bleeding.

It was a reasonable plan in one sense.

But timing matters in real estate, and I was about to learn that timing can overpower even a reasonable plan.
