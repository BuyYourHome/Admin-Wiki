# Early Properties

Before private money, before investor dinners, before joint ventures, and before the larger projects, there were the first houses.

Those early properties were not just small beginnings. They were classrooms. They taught me what the spreadsheet could not teach me. They taught me what it felt like to stand at a stranger's door, make an offer, wait for an answer, carry responsibility, manage relationships, discover legal process, and learn where my own confidence needed wisdom.

I can look back now and see that God was gracious in those first deals, not because everything was easy, but because every property exposed something I needed to learn before the business could carry more weight.

### Marthonna

Marthonna was the first property.

That matters because the first one is not just a transaction. The first one is the moment when the idea leaves the spreadsheet and has to stand in front of a real person. I could study foreclosures. I could think through systems. I could build lists and run numbers and imagine what might happen if I knocked on the door.

But Marthonna taught me that I could actually do it.

I could walk up to a door, talk to a stranger, be accepted, and make an offer that was much lower than the value of the property. I could do it without treating the person like a target. I could stand there with empathy and understanding and offer some direction that they could choose or refuse.

That was important to me. I was not trying to force people into anything. I was offering a way out of a dead end they could already see in front of them. Very often, that is what the seller was facing. They were not choosing between my offer and a perfect world. They were choosing between my offer and a property problem they did not know how to solve.

Marthonna also taught Jerry and me that buying the house was only the first lesson. We did not yet have a business around the business. We did not have an attorney. We did not have contractors. We did not have a manager.

We needed one because this property was bought by Sell Your Home, the company owned by our retirement funds. That meant we could not do the work ourselves. We could not sign the documents ourselves. We had found the deal, but we could not simply jump in and manage it by force of personality.

That was a strange kind of helplessness, and it was also instruction. We had to learn structure. We had to learn boundaries. We had to learn who was allowed to do what.

We hired a friend as manager, and he also did a lot of the work. At the beginning, that probably felt natural. We trusted him. He was available. We needed help. But the boundaries were not well defined. Tension grew before the property was even sold, and the relationship soured.

That hurt. It also taught us something we would need again and again. We needed qualified people we could depend on. We needed better communication with the people working with us. We needed relationships, but relationships alone were not enough. They had to be supported by clarity.

God was gracious to us. We worked through the details, got the project completed, put it on the market, entertained several offers, and sold it for what felt like a good profit for a first job.

Later, that profit would look small. It turned out to be the least we would ever make on a project, not counting the one where we lost money. For quite a while, every project made more than the one before it.

But at the time, Marthonna was not small. It was proof. It was the first door.

### Roffler

Roffler was a manufactured home on about an acre of land.

Buying a manufactured home has its own details, and we had to learn them. The seller was a young man named Tony. He was the only heir of a man who had died and left the home in very good condition. The property did not need much. We had to make the deal, clean up the yard, and get it sold.

I found Tony and met him at a Bojangles with his uncle. His uncle was a good man with good sense. Tony had had struggles with the law, and he did not know what to do with the trailer, the estate, or the legal process needed to save the property. He had run out of time.

I wanted to help him and also make a deal that worked. Roffler became the first time I offered a seller a partnership in the profit at the end of the deal.

That kind of offer can sound generous, and sometimes it is. It can also become complicated when expectations are not clear or when fear starts pressing on people. Before we were finished, Tony became impatient and began threatening violence. I ended up making him a lower offer than he would have received if he had stayed in the deal. I met with him, paid him, and had him sign the documents giving all of the trust interest back to us.

That was another lesson in compassion and boundaries. A creative structure does not remove the need for clarity. A generous idea can still become dangerous if the relationship is unstable.

Roffler also taught us something about selling. We had used a flat-fee realtor in the earliest projects and managed the listing and buyer conversations ourselves. That had been cheap and effective when the property and the buyer traffic fit that approach.

This time, we needed professional help.

We had close friends who had helped us buy our first house and sell our daughter's house in Clayton. We had tried to sell Roffler ourselves, but we were not getting the traffic we thought we should. We had staged the house, but not well enough. Ellen came in with a different eye. She suggested painting the wallpaper. She brought in her own professional stagers. She listed it, and we began getting offers.

The first offers were not what we wanted, but soon we got one we liked. We sold the property for more profit than the deal before it.

That increased my appreciation for Realtors. There is a time to save the commission, and there is a time to recognize that someone else can bring skill, credibility, presentation, and traffic that you do not have.

Roffler was not just about a property. It was about learning the difference between being resourceful and being isolated.

### Metacomet

Metacomet taught me that enthusiasm can outrun wisdom.

We had a contract. We had permission to be in the house. We had the keys. But we had not closed. We did not have the deed.

That sounds simple now. It should have sounded simple then. But when I had the keys and a signed agreement, I started cleaning out the property. The owner lived in New England, and somewhere in that process she decided we were trying to cheat her.

I came home from a weekend vacation out of state and found out that the sheriff had contacted my neighbor. The owner had filed a complaint. She was trying to back out of the deal. We had paid earnest money and signed a contract, but we had not yet closed.

That was a foolish mistake, and it nearly cost us.

We had to get our new attorney to write a stern letter and require her to comply with the agreement. It worked. We also sent her the money earlier than we had agreed to send it, which helped get that page turned. But the whole thing could have gone differently.

Metacomet is a good reminder that favor does not remove the need for process. God was watching over us, but watching over us did not make the mistake wise. I had to learn that possession is not ownership. Access is not closing. Permission is not a deed.

There is a kind of faith that moves forward when God says move. There is also a kind of impatience that calls itself faith because it does not want to wait for the last document.

Metacomet made me slow down.

### Hazelnut

Hazelnut was our biggest rehab yet.

It was a small house in a rougher neighborhood. The floor plan was poor. It had four bedrooms and one and a half baths, but the rooms were closed in and dark. The master bedroom had only a half bath. The house needed more than paint and cleanup.

We rearranged the floor plan. We converted two bedrooms into one large master suite with a full bath. We moved the laundry and bathroom so they were larger and better placed. We opened the kitchen and living room area, replacing the tiny boxy rooms with a more open concept.

The house had holes in the drywall. There were cracks under the walls where you could see daylight. In the back yard, there was a grove of huge bamboo that had taken over everything. Its roots were wrapping around the foundation.

Hazelnut was also the first time we hired a general contractor. We interviewed several people and settled on someone who seemed like he would work well with us.

Contractor relationships became one of the hallmarks of the way we did business. So did relationships with sellers and attorneys. Beginning with the first job, the relationships with Jonathan and Walter became long-term relationships that are still involved in our story today.

Hazelnut taught me that a bad floor plan in a bad neighborhood is not necessarily the end of a story. You can do good work. You can bring order to something neglected. You can take a place that looks like a mistake and turn it around.

That is not just a real estate lesson. It is one of the reasons real estate has always felt like more than real estate to me.

Sometimes the value is hidden under bad layout, cracked walls, and bamboo roots. Sometimes the work is not finding something already beautiful. Sometimes the work is seeing what could be there and then doing enough of the right things to let it appear.

### Patricia

Patricia was next.

It was another estate property, and it had been mistreated. The house had been broken into. Mirrors were broken. Holes had been punched in walls. The yard was the most overgrown yard I had ever seen. There were six-foot weeds and brush hiding rubbish in the front yard and the back yard.

On the surface, there was not much about it that looked inviting.

But that is one of the lessons Patricia gave me. Sometimes you have to look past a lot of mess to find the jewel. You have to learn how to see what the right buyer might see after the right work has been done. You have to ask whether the problem is permanent or whether it is simply a problem that needs the right solution.

The responsible party was not easy to find. The heir was the daughter of the person who had died, and I never met her in person. I do not remember the exact path without going back to the records, but somehow I found her. She was living in what seemed like a very temporary situation, maybe almost in the woods or in the mountains near Ashfield. I had to send contracts and certified funds to temporary addresses and keep working through the uncertainty.

That alone would have stopped a lot of investors. Many people would have hit one dead end, maybe two, and moved on to the next lead. We kept looking for the heir. We kept trying to find the person who could actually make a decision. God let us find her, and what could have stayed a no-deal became a good deal.

Then we still had to figure out the property.

The yard would not drain. Adjacent yards were draining into it, and we could not even clear the yard properly until we got some of the water out. Mowers got stuck. The crawl space had water problems too. We ended up putting sump pumps on opposite corners of the house perimeter, running drain lines to parts of the yard, and funneling the water to the pumps and then to the drainage ditch.

It worked.

Drew worked on the outside with landscaping, fence building, and land clearing. The general contractor handled the remodel. The market was heating up, and we moved quickly. When we put the house on the market, several offers came in from institutional investors, and the return was good.

Patricia taught me not to stop at the first layer of disorder. There was broken glass, punched walls, weeds, water, rubbish, and a hard-to-find heir. Any one of those things could have become the reason to quit.

But the assignment in front of us was not to find properties that already looked easy. It was to see what could be redeemed, figure out what had to be fixed, and keep going long enough for the hidden value to show itself.

I do not want to make that sound like a slogan. There was real work in it. There were phone calls, documents, pumps, drain lines, mud, stuck mowers, contractors, and risk. But there was also grace.

God was gracious to help us find the person, solve the problems, and turn another neglected house into something valuable.

That was becoming a pattern.
