# Jenny: Coastal Surrender

Several years after I moved to North Carolina, and after I bought the beach condo, I remarried. I believed it would be a forever relationship. I wanted that to be true. But that marriage ended abruptly, almost before it began, and I came away from it with a kind of emptiness I did not know what to do with.

It is hard to explain that season without sounding dramatic. It was amazingly great and amazingly horrible. That is the only way I know to say it honestly. The end left me raw. It left me feeling like I had failed again. It made me question my judgment. I had always thought of myself as cautious, careful, and protective of the people and things entrusted to me. In that season I felt as though I had violated my own instincts, and that was a painful thing to face.

There were also practical fears. I wanted to get away from the pain, the gossip, the reminders, and especially the threats that made me concerned for my safety. My grown children were away at college in three different states. None of them were in North Carolina. Northern Virginia did not feel like an option. It was too expensive, and I had no desire to return to the winters there. I knew the South Carolina coast because of the beach condo and because I had vacationed there in younger years. So I decided I would move near that area, rent something inland, and start over.

It sounded simple in my mind. I would go down during the winter off-season, stay at the condo, look for a place to rent, and find the next step. No one was renting the condo at that time of year, so I had a place to stay while I searched. I thought the door would open quickly. Instead, door after door closed. I was rejected for one reason or another. My seventy-pound poodle was often part of the problem. Three long days passed, and I had nothing.

On the last night before I was supposed to drive back to Raleigh, I was at the end of myself. I sat down on the sofa and opened my laptop to search one more time. The laptop slipped from my hands, hit the tile floor, and the screen turned into what looked like an Etch A Sketch drawing. That small thing became the thing that broke me. I had been holding myself together, trying to keep moving, trying to solve the problem, trying to get out of danger and pain by making a plan. When the screen scrambled, something in me gave way.

I cried out to the Lord to help me. I do not want to dramatize the darkness of that moment, but I also do not want to hide it. I looked toward the balcony door and had a thought that frightened me. I was exhausted, alone, empty, and ashamed that I had come to such a place. And then the phone rang.

It was Linda, the only person I knew who lived in the area. It was ten o'clock at night. I tried to sound fine, but she immediately asked, "Are you OK?" I said I was. She did not accept that answer. She said, almost like an order, "I'm coming over there."

For the next twenty minutes I stared at that scrambled computer screen and prayed. Linda came. She saw the laptop, called her husband, and somehow the two of them restored it. She stayed for a while and reassured me that things would turn around. I did not have an answer yet. I did not have a place to live. But I was no longer alone in the room.

The next morning I packed, loaded the car, and started driving back toward Raleigh. I still did not know what I was going to do. As I approached the outskirts of the coastal area, my phone rang. It was one of the management companies I had contacted and left messages with. They told me they had several condos for me to choose from.

I turned the car around and drove straight to their office in Pawleys Island. I took my poodle inside with me. If he was going to be the reason they rejected me, I wanted that to happen immediately. Instead, the woman handed me two keys, gave me directions to a new condo development, and told me to go choose.

The condos were brand new and beautiful. I still wondered whether I could afford the rent, but I went back, applied, and was accepted to move in immediately. The relief was beyond words. So was the gratitude.

That was a surrender point for me. I had been gripping the outcome, trying to fix everything myself, and trying to create safety by sheer effort. I did have to act. I did have to search. I did have to make calls and drive and apply. But there came a moment when my own strength ran out, and the Lord was still there. He was there through Linda's phone call. He was there in the timing of the management company. He was there in the practical provision of a place to live.

I did not become less cautious after that. Caution is part of who I am. But I learned something about surrender that caution alone could not teach me. Sometimes the safe way is not the way where I control every variable. Sometimes the safe way is the moment I stop gripping the outcome and let God meet me in the place where my own plan has failed.
