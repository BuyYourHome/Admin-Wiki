# Leadership, Patience, And The Unfinished Story

I cannot say I have learned this lesson.

That is the right place to begin.

There are some lessons in this book that I can look back on and say, at least in some measure, that God taught me something and I received it. I may still need to practice it. I may still need to remember it under pressure. But there is a settled quality to the lesson.

This is not one of those chapters.

This is a story about a lesson I can identify while it is still being worked out in me. Maybe that is an important distinction. A person can see a lesson before he has learned it. He can describe it. He can even believe it. But believing a thing and being formed by it are not always the same.

That is especially true in leadership.

For a long time, I thought leadership had something to do with seeing a solution before other people could see it. I thought it had something to do with mapping out a problem, explaining the way forward, and helping people understand why the path I saw made sense.

There is truth in that.

A leader does often see something. He sees a possible future. He sees a way through a problem. He sees how the pieces might fit together. He sees a path from where things are to where they could be.

But I am becoming convinced that leadership is not manipulation.

I will say that more softly, because manipulation is an ugly word.

Leadership is not even always about being so persuasive that you can bring everyone else to your point of view.

That sentence exposes something in me.

I like to be persuasive. I like to explain. I like to make the case. I like to take a complicated situation and reduce it to a set of steps that seem obvious. I like to believe that if I can just lay the facts out clearly enough, the people around me will understand what I see and join me in the direction I think we should go.

But many times leadership is something quieter and harder.

Many times leadership is seeing a solution to a problem, mapping it out, explaining it to others, and then waiting patiently while they do not understand, do not agree, or do not move at the speed you hoped they would move.

That is not my favorite kind of leadership.

It is much easier for me to lead when people are inspired by the plan.

It is much easier when they say, "That makes sense."

It is much easier when they trust the spreadsheet, see the opportunity, share the urgency, and respond to the same pressure I feel.

But that is not always what happens.

Sometimes people I love are not persuaded.

Sometimes they are cautious.

Sometimes they are tired.

Sometimes they are processing something that I am not processing.

Sometimes they simply do not work the way I work.

That is where the lesson begins.

This book is an example.

At this point I have created about 140 pages of manuscript. That number surprises me a little. I have been writing subjects, fragments, memories, reflections, and chapter seeds. I send them to Jean, and Jean integrates them into something that looks more and more like a coherent book.

That process has been helpful to me.

It lets me think in the way I naturally think. I can send a subject. I can dictate a thought. I can put something down before it disappears. Then the material gets shaped into chapters, and themes begin to connect.

But that is not the whole book I imagined.

I would like Jenny to have been doing the same thing the whole time.

Jenny is interested in the book. She is involved in the stories. She has a point of view, and often her point of view is different from mine. That difference is not a small thing. It may be one of the things the book needs most.

I see the business story through one set of eyes.

She sees it through another.

I tend to see the opportunity, the structure, the path forward, and the potential. I can get caught up in the analysis. I can imagine what a deal could become. I can think in systems and steps and possible outcomes.

Jenny often sees the burden. She sees the strain. She sees the caution. She sees how a decision will land in the family, in the house, in the schedule, in the emotional weather around us.

That is not opposition.

That is perspective.

If Jenny had been writing alongside me from the beginning, our points of view might already have merged into a conversational story. The book might have become a story of individual and mutual lessons learned. It might have carried both of our voices from the start.

I would like that.

I want that.

I believe she will eventually contribute a lot. She has a very substantial story to tell.

But so far she has not written her part.

So at this point the book is not yet a collaborative effort in the way I hoped it would be.

That sentence could become frustration in me if I let it.

It could become pressure.

It could become a leadership test that I fail while writing a chapter about leadership.

My leadership vision believes Jenny will become collaborative. But I cannot make her write. Any effort in that direction would probably be counterproductive. I could remind her. I could push. I could explain why it matters. I could tell her how much better the book would be with her voice in it.

Some of that may even be true.

But truth used as pressure can still become manipulation.

That is the part I am learning.

I am writing this chapter now, but I think when Jenny gets involved the flavor of it will change. That is probably true of the whole manuscript. Her voice will not simply add details. It will change the way some of the stories are understood.

I want the benefit of that contribution now.

But that is not how she works.

Leadership may require me to wait and let it unfold naturally.

That is hard for me.

It is hard because I am not only waiting for a document. I am waiting for a process that I can see in my mind. I can imagine how good it could be. I can imagine the dialogue between us. I can imagine one chapter where I tell the business side and she tells the relational side. I can imagine the reader seeing both ambition and caution, both vision and concern, both risk and wisdom.

I can see it.

But seeing it does not give me permission to force it.

That same leadership test is present in the unfinished story of the Orphaned Property.

The story has not finished yet, and that frustrates me.

Tensity began as one of those properties that seemed almost hidden. The owner had died. The house was vacant. The property was in foreclosure. We had trouble finding heirs. Eventually we found a distant heir and were able to get the property through a quitclaim deed, reinstate the loan, and bring the property back into use.

There was favor in that.

There was also work.

There was the searching, the phone calls, the uncertainty, the title questions, the risk, the reinstatement, and the practical work of making the property useful again.

Then the story connected to Rose Place. Our Tensity tenant became a buyer for Rose Place through a contract for deed. That solved a problem for him and for us. It helped us address pressure from a note coming due. It allowed Tensity to become available for sale.

Then another possibility opened.

Our tenant connected us with people from church who wanted to buy Tensity. Through conversation and interpretation, we learned that they had properties they might trade for equity in our property.

To me, that was interesting.

More than interesting.

It looked like the kind of creative real estate opportunity I am drawn to.

We had a willing buyer. He wanted to trade some property for equity in our property. I had done a lot of documentation, analysis, and presentation work. I had material that could be used to present a proposal and eventually create closing documents. In my mind, it was a profitable, timely deal ready to happen.

But Jenny did not want the properties.

I could not convince her otherwise.

That sentence carries more weight than it looks like on the page.

I could have strong-willed my way into making an offer. I could have pushed the process forward. I could have told myself that I was leading. I could have reasoned that she would eventually see it once the numbers were clear or once the structure was explained.

But she would not have been in agreement.

That matters.

When it came time to meet the buyer with his English-speaking associate, I had no room to negotiate in that direction. From what I could tell, that was his requirement. Left to myself, I probably would have tried to find enough common ground to make a deal.

But that did not happen.

So what am I left with?

Am I left with resentment because we lost a deal Jenny did not want?

That is the dangerous question.

The truth is that I usually think I am right about such things.

But so does she.

I still value her opinion, even when it is hard for me to show it because I do not agree with it.

That may be the most honest sentence in this chapter.

It is easy to value someone's opinion when it confirms yours. It is easy to praise caution after the danger becomes obvious. It is easy to say someone was wise after the deal falls apart in a way everyone can see.

But caution is different from action.

When action is wrong, the evidence often ends up laying all around me. A bad purchase has repairs. A bad structure has payments. A bad tenant has phone calls. A bad title issue has legal fees. A wrong business decision can become visible in bank accounts, calendars, stress, and paperwork.

When caution is wrong, it is harder to prove.

If we do not do a deal, there may be no pile of evidence. There is only the possibility of what might have happened. Maybe we missed a profit. Maybe we avoided a problem. Maybe we lost an opportunity. Maybe we were spared from a burden.

That is hard for me because I can feel the missed opportunity more easily than I can feel the avoided problem.

If Jenny is wrong, the mistake may not be fatal.

But overriding her point of view could have serious consequences.

That is where leadership becomes more than persuasion.

Leadership is not simply getting the deal done. It is not merely pushing the opportunity across the finish line. It is not proving that my analysis was right. It is not using urgency to make someone else carry a decision they did not have peace about.

Leadership allows events to unfold patiently without reacting.

That is especially true for the Christian who actually believes that all things work together for good for those who love God and are called according to His purpose.

I say I believe that.

But do I believe it when a deal stalls?

Do I believe it when Jenny's caution interrupts my momentum?

Do I believe it when I can see a path forward and the person closest to me does not want to walk that path?

Do I believe it when the story is unfinished?

That is the test.

Maybe Jenny's slowness to begin writing is the correct path.

Maybe her resistance to selling Tensity through the trade was for our good.

I do not know.

But I know that I do not know.

That may not sound like a strong leadership conclusion, but maybe it is stronger than it appears.

There is a kind of leadership that has to know. It has to decide quickly. It has to reduce uncertainty. It has to explain every delay. It has to call hesitation unbelief and caution fear. It has to keep the machine moving because stopping feels like failure.

But there is another kind of leadership that can acknowledge uncertainty without surrendering responsibility.

It can say, "This is what I see."

It can say, "Here is the opportunity."

It can say, "Here is the risk."

It can say, "Here is the proposal."

And then it can wait.

Not passively.

Not bitterly.

Not with a hidden campaign to make the other person feel wrong.

It can wait in faith.

That is not natural for me.

I would rather solve the problem. I would rather build the spreadsheet. I would rather make the presentation. I would rather find the structure that makes the deal work. I would rather keep moving.

But sometimes God is not only working through the deal.

Sometimes He is working through the delay.

Sometimes He is not only testing whether I can see opportunity.

Sometimes He is testing whether I can honor caution.

Sometimes He is not only asking whether I can build something.

Sometimes He is asking whether I can let Him build it.

That brings me back to the book.

If this book is something God is building, then I do not have to force Jenny's contribution. I can invite it. I can make room for it. I can value it before it arrives. I can keep writing what I am responsible to write and trust that her part will come in the right way and at the right time.

If the Orphaned Property story is something God is using, then I do not have to turn an unfinished deal into a finished accusation. I can tell the truth about the frustration. I can tell the truth about the opportunity. I can tell the truth about Jenny's caution. I can tell the truth about my uncertainty.

And then I can wait.

Maybe that is leadership.

Not the absence of vision.

Not the absence of planning.

Not the absence of conviction.

But the submission of vision, planning, and conviction to love, unity, patience, and the lordship of God.

I am not writing this because I have mastered it.

I am writing it because I can see it.

And sometimes seeing the lesson is the first mercy before learning it.

The gracious millionaire cannot only be gracious when the deal closes.

He has to become gracious while the deal is unresolved.

He has to become gracious when his wife sees something differently.

He has to become gracious when the book is not yet the collaboration he hoped it would be.

He has to become gracious when leadership means waiting instead of winning.

That is where I am.

The story is unfinished.

So is the lesson.
