# Jenny: Three Babies Under Two

Twenty-three months after our son was born, we were blessed with twin girls.

They were a surprise.

Number One Husband said I wanted and prayed so hard for a girl that the Lord gave me two. There was truth and humor in that. I had wanted a girl. I had prayed for a girl. And then suddenly there were two of them, along with a little boy who was not yet two years old.

Three babies under the age of two is not a season a person can fully explain to someone who has not lived it. The days were full before they even began. There were diapers, feedings, crying babies, laundry, dishes, and all the small urgent needs that arrive one after another until time becomes hard to measure. I was not thinking in terms of self-care or balance. I was trying to get through the day faithfully.

Overnight, we outgrew our starter home.

We also outgrew our car. We had a Cutlass Sedan, our family car, but three car seats could not be wedged into the back seat. That sounds almost funny now, but at the time it was immediate and practical. Babies have to ride somewhere. Cribs have to go somewhere. A family of five with three very small children needs space.

So we bought a larger, newly built house for $99,000.

Again, that was a lot of money. It was a major step up from our $37,000 starter home. But the need felt obvious. The new house could accommodate three cribs. It could hold the family we now had. We also bought a used Dodge Ram Van with seating for eight.

I want to be clear: I had no intention of filling all those seats. I wanted no more babies.

The van was not about a dream of a larger family. It was about survival. It was about being able to buckle in the children we already had and go where we needed to go. It was about solving a practical problem.

But practical solutions still create payments.

That was a time when banks were charging big interest rates. I remember that at one point we had a 13 3/4 percent rate. That kind of rate changes the feeling of a mortgage. It makes the payment heavier. It makes the cost of the house more than the price on the paper. It makes every month matter.

My days were filled with changing diapers and nursing crying babies. I had little time or energy for anything else, and if I am honest, for anyone else. Looking back, I can speculate that it was a difficult time for Number One Husband. I imagine he felt neglected. He was also saddled with large expenses: a big monthly mortgage and a sizable used-car loan.

At the time, I was inside the pressure. I was not looking at it from above. I was not analyzing how the marriage was absorbing the financial weight. I was simply tired. I was needed constantly. I was doing what mothers do when small children depend on them for everything.

But stress does not wait politely until a mother has energy to understand it.

The numbers were tight. The house was bigger, but the margin was smaller. The family was growing, but the money was strained. We could barely make ends meet, and that created a lot of stress in the marriage.

I still believe buying the larger home made sense from the standpoint of space. We needed room. We needed a vehicle that worked for three car seats. We needed to function as a family.

But this is where caution and reality met each other in a painful way. A decision can be understandable and still become heavy. A purchase can be necessary and still expose weakness. A family can be blessed with children and still suffer under the pressure of supporting them.

Those years taught me that safety is not only about making the obvious practical choice. Safety also requires margin, unity, and dependable work patterns. Without those, even a carefully chosen home can become part of a strain that reaches every room.
