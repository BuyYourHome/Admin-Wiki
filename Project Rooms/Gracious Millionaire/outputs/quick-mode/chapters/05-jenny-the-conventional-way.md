# Jenny: The Conventional Way

I was twenty-three years old when I married my first husband. I will call him Number One Husband here, because that is the simplest way to tell the truth without making this section about names.

Neither one of us expected divorce to be part of our future. I certainly did not. I was young, married, and serious about building a life that made sense. Both of us wanted to own our own home, so during that first year of marriage we did what responsible young people were supposed to do. We lived in an apartment, worked two jobs each, and saved for a down payment.

That sounds simple when it is written in one sentence, but it was not effortless. It meant long hours. It meant saying no to things. It meant deciding that owning a home mattered enough to organize our lives around it for a season. To me, that kind of sacrifice made sense. If the goal was important, then the steps should match the goal.

We bought a small starter home in Northern Virginia in 1978. The price was $37,000.

That number may not sound large now, but it was a lot of money to me then. It was enough money that I did not take it lightly. We bought the home the conventional way. We borrowed from a bank. We took out a thirty-year mortgage. We looked at what we could afford with both of us working full time, and I was confident that the payment fit inside the life we were living.

That was the kind of decision I understood. Bank. Mortgage. Payment. Income. Budget. If the numbers worked, then the step felt reasonable.

I did not know then how much life can change around numbers that once looked reasonable.

At the time, owning that starter home felt like progress. It felt settled. It felt like the beginning of adult responsibility in a form I could recognize. We were not trying to be creative. We were not trying to be aggressive. We were not trying to become investors. We wanted a home of our own.

There is something honorable about that desire. I still believe that. A home can be a place of stability. It can be a place where a family grows, where children learn what normal feels like, where the routines of life are held together by familiar rooms and familiar walls.

But the house itself cannot guarantee the stability. The mortgage cannot guarantee the marriage. The careful beginning cannot guarantee the ending.

That was the part I did not yet understand.

In those early years, I trusted the conventional path because it appeared predictable. Work hard. Save money. Buy what you can afford. Make the payment. Build equity. Stay responsible.

I still value those things. I still believe in doing what is honest, measured, and thought through. But I have learned that a conventional purchase can carry unconventional pressure when the life around it changes. A mortgage that is affordable with two full-time incomes may feel very different when one income goes away. A house that fits a young married couple may suddenly feel very small when children come. A responsible plan can become strained without anyone setting out to be irresponsible.

That first house was not a mistake. It was part of our story. It gave us a beginning. It also taught me that even when the front door is yours, the future is not.
