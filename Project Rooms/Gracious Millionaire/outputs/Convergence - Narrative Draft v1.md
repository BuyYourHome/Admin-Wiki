# Convergence

I have listened to Lance Wallnau talk about convergence, but I do not want to pretend that I can teach his material as though I have mastered it.

That is not what I am trying to do here.

What I understand from him is that God uses the way He made a person. He uses natural ability. He uses history. He uses work. He uses the thing you keep doing over and over again until, after years of it, you begin to realize that it was not just a job skill. It was part of your assignment.

Lance calls that convergence.

At least that is how I understand it.

It is where ability, calling, work, history, and timing begin to come together. It is where a person is not only trying to make a living anymore. He is beginning to recognize the pattern God has been building into him for a long time.

That thought matters to me because my real estate story did not begin with real estate.

It began with systems.

I did not know that at the time.

When I was in my thirties, I shut down the sign business I had been operating for nearly ten years. That business had been my life for a long season. I had been self-employed. I knew how to work. I knew how to figure things out. I knew how to build something with my hands and make a business out of it.

But I also knew that my life was not going in the direction I wanted it to go.

So I started college later than most people start formal education. I was well beyond the age when people normally begin that path. I was not an eighteen-year-old freshman with a clean sheet of paper in front of me. I was a man with a business behind me, a family, bills, and a lot of life already accumulated.

I was studying mechanical engineering.

That was the formal direction.

But while I was studying mechanical engineering, something else began to develop in me. Computer programming caught my attention. I do not know that I would have called it a calling then. I probably would have called it a skill, or a useful thing, or a way to solve a problem.

But looking back, I can see that something was being formed.

I had a part-time job at the University of Florida working for one of the departments as a macro developer inside Excel. That may sound small. It was not a glamorous assignment. I was not building some giant corporate platform. I was building tools inside spreadsheets.

But that is where I began to see something.

A spreadsheet is not just numbers in boxes.

At least not to me.

A spreadsheet is a way to organize thought. It is a way to take a scattered process and make it visible. It is a way to take work that is being done over and over again and give it structure. It is a way to help a person see what is happening, what should happen next, and what the decision might cost.

I did not have those words for it then.

I was just learning.

When I got out of college and moved to North Carolina, I started looking for technical jobs. The first one I got was as a CAD draftsman for an engineering firm. That made sense with the mechanical engineering background. I was drawing. I was working inside technical systems. I was learning how engineers organized their work.

The second job I got was as a programmer for the Lisp language inside AutoCAD for Siemens.

That was another step.

Again, it was not real estate. It was not buying houses. It was not private money, contract for deed paperwork, seller conversations, or equity calculations.

But it was systems.

It was taking a process that someone needed to repeat and turning it into something that could be executed with more order, more speed, and more accuracy.

I quit that job to go back into business for myself. I began developing systems to create mail products for different individuals. That was another attempt at business. It was another attempt to use what I knew to build something that worked.

Some of these seasons did not look connected while I was living them.

That is one of the reasons convergence is interesting to me.

When you are in the middle of it, life can look scattered.

Sign business.

College.

Mechanical engineering.

Excel macros.

CAD drafting.

AutoCAD Lisp.

Mail products.

Database development.

Industry after industry.

It can look like a man trying to find the next thing that will work.

But when you look back, there may be a thread.

After that I got a job as a database developer. The assignment was to convert Excel spreadsheets into a back-end database in SQL Server with a front end developed in Visual Basic. My manager gave me the assignment to create the whole package under his direction.

That was a turning point in my professional life.

I was not just writing code.

I was learning how to develop systems.

That is different.

Code is part of a system, but code by itself is not the whole system. A system has inputs. It has outputs. It has users. It has mistakes that need to be prevented. It has decisions that need to be made. It has data that needs to be trusted. It has reports that need to tell the truth.

A system takes chaos and gives it shape.

That became the thing I did.

One job after another led me from industry to industry developing databases. I worked in different environments, with different people, different managers, different business problems, and different kinds of data.

But underneath all the differences, the work was similar.

Someone had a process.

The process was messy.

The data was scattered.

The spreadsheet had become too big, too fragile, or too important to remain only a spreadsheet.

The business needed structure.

So I built structure.

Thirty years later, the last job I had was as a database developer for First Citizens Bank.

That is a long time to do a thing.

I do not want to overstate this. I am not saying every database project was spiritual. I am not saying I woke up every morning feeling like I was walking in some grand prophetic assignment. Most days I was probably just trying to get through the work, solve the problem, answer the request, fix the bug, and make the system do what it was supposed to do.

But this is what I can see now.

God was building something in me.

He was not only giving me a way to earn a living. He was forming a way of thinking.

I learned to look at scattered facts and see structure.

I learned to look at repeated work and see a process.

I learned to look at numbers and see a story.

I learned to look at a business problem and ask what kind of tool would make the next decision clearer.

Then in 2019, I convinced Jenny to go to a Ron LeGrand real estate conference.

That sentence still makes me smile a little.

I had been studying Ron LeGrand's CD-based conference and his systems for about a decade. I had listened and listened. I had absorbed the language. I had thought about the methods. I had paid attention to how he structured the business of real estate investing.

It was not only that he taught real estate.

He taught systems.

That is probably why I kept listening.

At that conference we met Jay Conner. Jay was a real estate guru based out of North Carolina, and Jenny and I liked him a lot. There was something about him that connected with us. We bought into his program in about May of that year.

Then I started putting all of his systems into action, with my own particular spin, right away.

Of course, my particular spin was to put his system into Excel spreadsheets tied to a CRM that we bought that was particular to real estate.

That was not accidental.

That was me.

That was the same man who had built Excel macros at the University of Florida. That was the same man who had converted spreadsheets into SQL Server databases. That was the same man who had spent thirty years taking business processes and turning them into systems.

Only now the business problem was not bank data.

It was real estate.

It was sellers.

It was leads.

It was houses.

It was follow-up.

It was offers.

It was private money.

It was spreadsheets that could tell us whether a property was worth pursuing, whether the numbers were honest, whether the cash flow made sense, and whether a creative structure might turn a problem into a possibility.

By that September, I had bought three houses that had the potential to replace my very good income as a database developer.

That happened quickly.

From May to September.

I do not say that to make it sound easy. It was not easy. It was work. It was study. It was phone calls. It was risk. It was pressure. It was learning a whole new industry while carrying the weight of a good job and a future that was not guaranteed.

But I could see it.

I could see the system working.

I could see that the skill I had spent my life building could be applied to this new field.

So I resigned my position and started in business for the third time.

This time as a real estate investor.

When I say it that way, it sounds like a career change.

It was more than that.

It was convergence.

My skill was inventing systems. I applied it to real estate investing. God blessed those efforts. He put His hand upon a skill I already had and gave us favor in everything we did.

That last sentence is important because it keeps the story in the right order.

I had a skill.

I used the skill.

I worked.

I built.

I studied.

I acted.

But I do not believe the skill by itself explains what happened.

If skill alone were enough, the earlier real estate season would have produced a very different ending. I had intelligence then. I had energy then. I had ideas then. I had ambition then. I even had some good real estate ideas then.

But I did not understand favor then the way I understand it now.

I did not understand assignment the way I understand it now.

I did not understand that God may take the natural ability He has been developing in a person for decades and breathe on it at the appointed time.

That is what this chapter is about.

It is not a chapter about being clever.

It is not a chapter about technology.

It is not a chapter about how to build a spreadsheet.

It is a chapter about how God wastes nothing.

He did not waste the sign business.

He did not waste the late start in college.

He did not waste the mechanical engineering classes.

He did not waste the Excel macros.

He did not waste the CAD drafting.

He did not waste the AutoCAD Lisp.

He did not waste the mail products.

He did not waste the databases.

He did not waste the bank job.

He did not waste the decade of listening to real estate teaching before Jenny and I finally went to the conference.

He did not waste the failures either.

That is harder to say, but I think it is true.

The failed real estate season taught me things that success alone would not have taught me. It taught me humility. It taught me that ambition can outrun wisdom. It taught me that a good idea can still collapse if the foundation underneath it is not right. It taught me that money pressure exposes things in the heart and in relationships that might otherwise stay hidden.

Then, years later, the same God who was with me in the failure began to use the skill that had been forming all along.

That is grace.

That is also why I think convergence belongs in a book called *Gracious Millionaire*.

Because gracious wealth is not only about how much money comes in.

It is about recognizing that even the abilities we think are ours were gifts before they were skills.

It is about recognizing that work can be part of worship when it comes under lordship.

It is about recognizing that the thing you have done for thirty years may become the tool God uses in the next assignment.

And it is about recognizing timing.

I did not begin real estate investing in my twenties. I did not step into this season before the systems were built into me. I did not have this kind of favor when I first had real estate ambition.

The timing matters.

Maybe that is why Lance's teaching about convergence interests me so much. It gives language to something I have lived but did not know how to name.

There are seasons where you are not behind.

You are being prepared.

There are years that feel scattered while they are happening, but later they reveal a pattern.

There are abilities you take for granted because they come naturally to you, but those may be the very abilities God intends to place His hand on when the assignment comes into view.

That is what I think happened to me.

God did not ask me to become someone else in order to become a real estate investor.

He used the man He had been building.

He used the systems person.

He used the database developer.

He used the spreadsheet thinker.

He used the late-start college student.

He used the former sign-business owner who had already learned something about working for himself.

Then He added favor.

That is the part I cannot reduce to a formula.

I can explain the systems.

I can explain the spreadsheets.

I can explain the CRM.

I can explain the lead flow and the follow-up and the offers.

But I cannot fully explain why the doors opened the way they did, why Jenny and I connected with the mentors we connected with, why the first houses came together when they did, or why the timing gave me courage to leave a very good job.

I can only say what I believe.

God put His hand on what He had already been building.

That is convergence.

And if He did that for me, then the money is not the miracle by itself.

The deeper miracle is that He formed the man before He released the assignment.
