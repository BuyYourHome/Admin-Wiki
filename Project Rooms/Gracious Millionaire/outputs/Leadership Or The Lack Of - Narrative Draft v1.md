# Leadership Or The Lack Of

I have always thought I would be a great leader if I could only get people to follow me.

That sentence is funny to me now, but it is also painfully honest.

For a long time I thought leadership had something to do with seeing farther than other people could see. I thought it had something to do with having a plan, putting that plan on paper, explaining it well enough, and then waiting for everybody else to recognize the wisdom of what I was trying to do.

I have always been a planner.

I can fill pages with plans. I can make lists. I can draw diagrams. I can look at a situation and begin to see what should be done next. That has been true in business, in family life, in real estate, in church work, and in almost every other arena where I have cared deeply about the outcome.

But if I am honest, very few of those plans have unfolded the way I expected them to.

That is not a small statement in a book like this.

I have made three distinct, separate, and unique attempts at being in business. The first two did not pan out the way I planned. I wanted them to. I worked hard. I had ideas. I had energy. I had what I thought was vision.

But the first two attempts did not become what I expected them to become.

This real estate business looks like it might pay off.

Even saying that makes me cautious, because one of the things I am learning is that payoff is not the same as lordship. A business can begin to work and still expose wrong motives in me. Success does not automatically sanctify ambition. Sometimes success simply gives ambition more room to move around.

One of the great motivators for me for a long time has been the desire to be influential to the people around me.

That sounds noble when I say it the right way.

Influence can be a good thing. A father should influence his family. A husband should bring strength and vision into a marriage. A business owner should influence the people who work with him. A board member should bring wisdom into the room. A believer should carry something of the Kingdom of God into every place he goes.

But wanting to be influential is not the same as leadership.

Thinking you know how others should act is not the same as leadership.

That is one of the harder sentences for me to write.

I have often been frustrated when I was trying to make progress toward a certain goal and expected the people around me to understand, participate, and care about that goal the same way I did. In my mind the path was clear. The need was obvious. The timing mattered. The idea made sense.

So why could they not see it?

That question has come up in me more times than I want to admit.

I have sat on different boards at different times in my life. I have been in rooms where decisions needed to be made and where I believed I could see a better way forward. I expected others to see the wisdom of the ideas I was expressing. I would be patient for a little while, because I knew I would have to explain why I thought something was such a good idea.

But as time dragged on, and my companions or associates or family members did not fully embrace what I was engineering, my patience often turned into frustration.

Sometimes that frustration became unveiled anger.

Sometimes it was not even very thinly veiled.

That has shown up in my family. It has shown up when my spouse was not in lockstep with what I wanted to do. It has shown up when my children were not pursuing the same agenda I had in my mind, or when they were not reacting the way I expected them to react in a given set of circumstances.

I could call that vision.

I could call that urgency.

I could call that leadership.

But a lot of the time it was really control wearing leadership's clothes.

That is not easy for me to say.

I do believe God has given me an ability to see patterns. I do believe He has given me a systems mind. I can look at an opportunity and start building a process around it. I can see where a piece of information belongs. I can imagine how a follow-up system should work. I can look at a real estate lead and start thinking through the seller, the buyer, the financing, the repairs, the risk, the timing, and the possible path forward.

Those are gifts.

But a gift that is not submitted to God can become pressure on the people around me.

That is where this chapter belongs in the story.

In the convergence chapter I talked about the systems instinct that has been in me for a long time. I talked about the sign business, the early computer, the engineering training, the database work, the spreadsheets, and the way God has been weaving those skills into the real estate business.

But convergence is not enough by itself.

If God brings a person's ability, history, timing, and assignment together, that person still has to learn how to carry the assignment. He still has to learn how to lead without using frustration as a tool. He still has to learn that people are not raw material for his plan.

That may be one of the hardest parts of leadership.

Leadership is not just having good ideas and expecting everybody to get on board.

Leadership begins with walking in proper relationship with God.

It begins with following His voice. It means learning when to speak and when to be quiet. It means expressing, when appropriate, what God is saying to you to the people around you. But it also means being patient when they do not adopt your plans, your mindset, or your priorities.

That last part is where the formation happens.

It is one thing to say God gave me a plan.

It is another thing to keep walking with God when the plan is not moving at the speed I expected, when the people I love are not responding the way I hoped, when the board does not vote the way I think it should, when the business does not grow according to my spreadsheet, or when a family member does not see what seems obvious to me.

In those moments I have often wanted to push harder.

But pushing harder is not always faith.

Sometimes it is fear.

Sometimes it is pride.

Sometimes it is my attempt to make sure the thing I built does not fall apart.

One of my favorite quotes from Bill Johnson is, "If you build it, you have to support it. If God builds it, He supports it."

That quote has stayed with me because it speaks directly to the kind of man I have been. I know how to build things in my mind. I know how to imagine a structure. I know how to create systems, plans, processes, files, lists, and projections. I know how to look at a business and say, "Here is how this could work."

But if I build something out of my own striving, I have to support it.

I have to keep persuading.

I have to keep defending.

I have to keep explaining.

I have to keep applying pressure.

I have to keep managing everybody's reaction to the thing I am trying to make happen.

That is exhausting.

It is also not the same as Kingdom leadership.

If God builds it, He supports it.

That does not mean I become passive. It does not mean I stop planning. It does not mean I refuse responsibility or sit around waiting for mystical direction before I make every phone call. Real estate does not work that way. Business does not work that way. Marriage and family do not work that way either.

But it does mean the weight changes.

If the plan is God's plan, I can give it back to Him.

I can pray.

I can wait.

I can explain without demanding.

I can be an example of what I am advocating.

That is not easy for me.

Leaders lead by example, not because it is easy but because it is hard.

It is hard to keep a right spirit when people do not do things your way. It is hard to stay gracious when you feel misunderstood. It is hard to keep your voice level when you believe the stakes are high. It is hard to keep trusting God when the timing of your plan does not match the timing of the people around you.

But maybe that is exactly why leadership has to begin with lordship.

If I am not submitted to God, I will eventually try to make other people submit to my plan.

If I am walking with God, I can hold the plan with an open hand.

That does not mean I will never be direct. It does not mean I will never challenge people. It does not mean leadership becomes soft, vague, or passive. Jesus was not passive. But He was completely submitted to the Father. He did not need to manipulate people into obedience. He spoke what the Father gave Him to speak. He did what the Father gave Him to do.

That is the pattern I am still learning.

The real estate business has given me a classroom for this. There are plans everywhere. There are seller calls, buyer calls, lender conversations, title issues, repairs, lease agreements, spreadsheets, projections, insurance questions, and deadlines. Every property can become a little kingdom if I am not careful.

My little kingdom.

My deal.

My system.

My plan.

My timing.

But this book is not about my little kingdom.

It is about the Kingdom of God.

So leadership requires gracious patience when things are not going my way and people are not doing things my way. It requires me to ask whether I am leading by example or merely applying pressure. It requires me to notice when my patience has turned into frustration and when frustration has started looking for a target.

And then it requires repentance.

That word belongs here.

Not as a religious word thrown into a business chapter, but as the honest response when I realize I have been trying to support what I built instead of trusting God with what He is building.

I still make plans.

I still write things down.

I still think on paper.

I still believe planning is part of stewardship.

But I am learning that a plan is not a lord.

A plan is a tool.

God is Lord.

If He gives me something to build, then I want to build it with Him, under Him, and for Him. I want the people around me to experience the grace of that leadership, not just the pressure of my urgency.

That may be one of the great tests of becoming a gracious millionaire.

Can God trust me with increase if I cannot be patient with people?

Can He trust me with influence if I confuse influence with control?

Can He trust me with assignment if I turn every assignment into evidence that everybody should finally listen to me?

Those are not theoretical questions for me.

They are the questions God keeps using to form me.

And maybe that is the mercy in all the plans that did not unfold the way I expected.

God was not only interested in whether the plan worked.

He was interested in what kind of man would be leading if it did.
