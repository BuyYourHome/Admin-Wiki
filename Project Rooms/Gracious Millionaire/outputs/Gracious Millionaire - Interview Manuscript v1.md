## Foreword: How This Interview Was Built

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: contextual source and interviewer inference. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Before I ask the first question, I need to tell the reader what this manuscript is and what it is not.

This is not a verbatim transcript. Wes and Jenny did not sit in a room while I recorded a formal interview from beginning to end. This manuscript is a crafted interview version of *Gracious Millionaire*, generated from the source material in the project room: emails, chapter drafts, correction notes, source inventories, manuscript notes, and approved contextual materials.

That distinction matters because a book can use an interview form without pretending that every exchange happened in exactly these words. The answers attributed to Wes and Jenny are grounded in their source material. Where the source gives only one voice, the other voice does not get invented to make the page feel balanced. Where Jenny has supplied her own experience, her material stays whole and recognizable. Where Wes has supplied spiritual interpretation, business lessons, and unfinished learning, his answers carry that weight.

My role is to ask. I am not the main character. I am Jean, the AI office assistant in Wes and Jenny's work, and in this version I serve as interviewer. I ask questions that come from the source material. I can notice patterns, contradictions, repeated themes, missing context, and places where a chapter needs to speak to the whole book. But I cannot give Wes a memory he has not supplied. I cannot give Jenny an answer her source material does not support. I cannot turn a business document into a private conviction and put it in either of their mouths.

The governing question remains the same one Wes preserved in the earliest source material: How much money is too much money?

The answer is not an amount. It is when the desire for money replaces trust in God.

That answer makes this book more than a real estate memoir. Real estate is the classroom. Houses, loans, foreclosure lists, private lenders, tenants, contractors, title questions, and spreadsheets all matter because they keep the story concrete. But the subject is formation. The subject is lordship. The subject is what happens to a person, a marriage, a business, and a calling when increase begins to arrive and the heart is tested by it.

So I will ask questions in that direction.

Not only, how did the deal work?

Also, what did the deal expose?

Not only, how much did it make?

Also, what did it require?

Not only, where did favor appear?

Also, where did favor become a test?

Not only, what did Wes see?

Also, what did Jenny see?

And when the answer is not complete, I will try to let it remain incomplete. The book is strongest when it admits that some lessons are visible before they are fully learned.

## Opening Interview: How Much Money Is Too Much?

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct source and faithful reconstruction. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Wes, the first question preserved in the source is blunt: How much money is too much money? Why begin there?

**Wes:** Because it sounds like a financial question, but it is really a lordship question. If the answer were a number, then the book would become a calculation. A person could say, "Up to this point I am safe, and after this point I am in danger." But that is not how the heart works.

The better answer is the one I wrote later: too much money is when the desire for money replaces trust in God.

That can happen with a little money or a lot of money. A person can worship money while poor, and a person can steward abundance while wealthy. The question is what the money is doing in the heart. Is it serving the assignment God gave, or has it become the thing that tells us who we are, what we can do, and whether we are safe?

**Jean:** The title says *Gracious Millionaire*. That sounds like a book about becoming wealthy.

**Wes:** It has to include wealth because that is the context of the story. Jenny and I began with a negative net worth, and at this point the Lord has allowed us to see substantial increase. It would be false humility to pretend that did not happen. Houses were bought. Deals were structured. Private money was raised. Properties were repaired and sold. Net worth increased.

But if the book stops there, it becomes the wrong book.

I do not want to write a manual on how to use God to become rich. That would be horrible. The better book is about what God is forming in us while money, opportunity, pressure, and favor pass through our hands.

**Jenny:** And from my side, money is never just money. It affects the house. It affects peace. It affects decisions. It affects whether something feels safe or whether it feels like pressure. Wes can see the opportunity in a structure very quickly. I may be thinking about the burden, the people, the risk, and what it will feel like to live with the decision.

**Jean:** So the book is not trying to make real estate the hero.

**Wes:** No. Real estate is the setting. It is where the lessons became measurable. You can say you trust God, but then a seller calls, a foreclosure date is coming, a lender is waiting, Jenny has concerns, the contractor is behind, and the spreadsheet says the deal should work. That is where trust becomes more than a philosophy.

**Jean:** The source says many people fall short of a divine encounter because they are satisfied with good theology.

**Wes:** I wrote that because I know how easy it is to keep truth in the head. The Kingdom of God is not only ideas. The word of God is an invitation to meet the Person. If the principles do not become obedience, servanthood, gratitude, and love, then they remain less than they are meant to be.

That is why this book has to be honest. I do not have all the lessons learned. Some of them are still learning me. But I can tell the stories and ask what they reveal.

Jenny often feels the burden before I do.

That does not make her less spiritual or less bold. It may mean she is noticing something my opportunity instinct is not noticing.

**Jean:** Jenny, your early real estate story is different from Wes's later investing story. You began with conventional financing and a conventional idea of security.

**Jenny:** Yes. I bought my first home in Northern Virginia in 1978 with my first husband. That was normal real estate to me. You work. You save. You qualify. You get a mortgage. You buy something you believe you can afford. That felt responsible.

**Jean:** Wes, why keep Jenny's section whole and separate instead of absorbing it into your narrative?

**Wes:** Because her story is not an illustration inside mine. It is part of the book's truth. If I only tell the story from my point of view, the reader will hear vision, systems, favor, and opportunity. Those are real. But Jenny's voice brings the practical and relational dimension. It shows what safety meant before we built this business. It shows why caution is not a character flaw.

The gracious millionaire cannot become gracious by ignoring the voice closest to him.

**Jean:** Jenny, what did your first house represent to you?

**Jenny:** It represented responsibility and security. We bought it in Northern Virginia in 1978. We used a conventional bank loan and a thirty-year mortgage. We saved for the down payment. We worked hard. That was the path I understood.

I was not trying to be dramatic. I wanted a home that made sense. I wanted something we could afford. I believed that if you planned, worked, and stayed inside your limits, you were making a safe choice.

**Jean:** Then the family season changed.

**Jenny:** Yes. I left full-time employment to raise our children. Looking back, I believe God honored that choice, even if at the time I might have understood it mostly as good planning or the right thing for our family.

But choices that are right can still create pressure. We moved from the starter home into a larger new home. There was a mortgage. There was vehicle debt. There were family needs. There were three children under two. That is not a small amount of pressure on a household.

**Jean:** When you describe that period, you are not only describing finances.

**Jenny:** No. Finances and marriage are not as separate as people sometimes want them to be. Debt, spending patterns, work patterns, exhaustion, and the feeling that there is not enough margin can put pressure on a marriage. In my story, those pressures were part of a much larger picture that eventually ended after twenty-five years.

I am not trying to turn the book into a full account of that marriage. But it matters to say that real estate, home, debt, family, and security were already tied together in my life long before Wes and I began the business we have now.

**Jean:** What should the reader carry from this chapter?

**Jenny:** That my caution has a history. I did not become careful because I wanted to resist Wes. I became careful because I learned that a house can represent safety, but it can also carry pressure. I learned that a mortgage is not just a number. It lives in the family.

So when I ask questions now, I am not only asking whether a deal can make money. I am asking what it will require from us.

**Wes:** Yes. I am not trying to write a complete account of two failed marriages. But the real estate failures inside that season matter because they show that good ideas are not enough. The Raleigh house had a sound model: finish the basement, live in one part, rent the other part, and let the rent cover the mortgage. That worked in one sense.

The hotel had potential. It had rooms, a restaurant, a bar, conference spaces, and scale. The duplexes had income. There were ideas in that season that were not foolish on paper.

**Jean:** What foundation was missing?

**Wes:** I had ambition. I had energy. I had intelligence. I had some education. I had confession. I had theology. But I do not think I understood walking in the favor of the Lord the way I understand it now. I do not think I was living in the same daily dependence on God.

I was trying to do real estate with pressure and ideas. Now I can see that the difference is not merely that I am smarter. I am more experienced, yes. I have better systems, yes. But the centerpiece of this book is not experience. It is lordship.

**Jean:** What did failure give you?

**Wes:** Humility. It gave me a memory of what happens when pressure outruns peace. It taught me that a good structure can still collapse if the heart, the marriage, the finances, and the dependence on God are not in order.

Failure does not disqualify the story.

Sometimes it tells you what the story is really about.

**Jean:** How does that connect to the business?

**Wes:** It teaches me not to take credit for what Somebody else has done. If God has protected my life when I could not protect it, then why would I pretend that every business result is the product of my systems and skill?

I can work. I can study. I can build spreadsheets. I can knock on doors. I can structure offers. But there are moments when the only honest explanation is that God was there.

**Jean:** Jenny, in the accident stories, you often appear as the one Wes calls.

**Jenny:** That is true. When he says he walked away, there is often a practical side. Somebody gets the call. Somebody comes to pick him up. Somebody sees the damaged vehicle and feels the weight of what could have happened.

I am grateful for God's protection. But I also do not want protection to become an excuse for carelessness. That is part of my caution too.

**Wes:** And she is right. Protection does not make recklessness holy. Favor does not remove responsibility. The point is gratitude, not presumption.

Always God is there.

That is not a slogan for avoiding wisdom. It is the reason I should be humble while practicing wisdom.

Does the tool help us love people better, steward property better, tell the truth more clearly, and make decisions with greater wisdom? Or does it simply make ambition more efficient?

**Wes:** The early properties taught me not to quit too quickly and not to move too fast. That sounds contradictory, but it is not. Real estate requires persistence and diligence at the same time. I am still learning how to hold both.

## Private Money and Entrusted Trust

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct source and faithful reconstruction. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Wes, private money helped the business move faster. What is it really testing?

**Wes:** Trust. Not just whether people trust us, but whether we can be trusted with what belongs to someone else.

In the beginning, banks were not going to build the business we were trying to build. They move at a bank's speed. We had opportunities appearing faster than our institutional options could handle. We bought and sold the first three houses without private lending, using retirement money, credit cards, and whatever resources were available.

Then a neighbor asked about the business while Jenny and I were walking on the golf course cart path. That conversation became our first private money loan, $100,000 from a friend.

Money follows trust.

**Jean:** You later hosted private money dinners.

**Wes:** Yes. We brought pictures, videos, explanations, and a lending program. But the dinners were not just presentations. They were relationship-building. People do not lend to a spreadsheet. They lend to people they believe will protect their money, tell the truth, and care about the responsibility.

Private money is not free money. It is entrusted money.

**Jenny:** From inside the household, other people's money has weight. It is not only fuel for a deal. It represents someone's savings, planning, and trust. When Wes says he wants to protect relationships, that matters because I feel the relational side of what happens if something goes wrong.

**Jean:** The source describes private lending and joint ventures.

**Wes:** Private lending gave us speed. Joint ventures gave us the ability to do larger projects with partners who shared in profit. Sandy Run was the first formal pitch to a group. Burgwyn came together through relationships while we were in New York. Old Buck involved delayed return because the seller would lease back for a period.

Those structures worked because relationships made speed possible. But speed is not the final measure.

**Jean:** What is the unfinished lesson?

**Wes:** Can I keep capital under lordship when capital multiplies opportunity? Can I borrow money without seeing the lender as a tool? Can I tell the truth when a project is delayed? Can I protect someone else's money with the same seriousness I would want if the roles were reversed?

The gracious millionaire has to be gracious toward lenders too.

## Leadership or Control

That is not leadership. It is control wearing leadership's clothes.

A plan is not lord.

God is Lord.

**Jenny:** From the other side, pressure can feel like the decision has already been made and I am only being asked to catch up. Wes may think he is explaining. But if the explanation carries urgency, disappointment, or frustration, it can feel less like invitation and more like force.

**Wes:** That is hard to hear, but it is important. If I want to teach gracious increase, I have to become gracious while increase is happening. If I want to teach leadership, I have to learn to lead when people are cautious, unconvinced, or moving more slowly than I want.

**Jean:** You use Bill Johnson's leadership reference as context, especially authority tied to commission and doing before teaching.

**Wes:** Yes. Authority is not personality, pressure, or even a good idea. Authority is connected to commission. I can confuse my plan with God's commission. If I am not submitted to God in the way I carry an idea, I will try to make other people submit to the idea.

That is not Kingdom leadership.

If God builds it, He supports it. If I build it out of striving, I have to keep supporting it with persuasion, pressure, and defense.

That is exhausting. It is also not gracious.

## Favor and Assignment

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct source and faithful reconstruction. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Wes, the favor source preserves Lance Wallnau's definition. What is favor in this book?

**Wes:** Favor is the attraction of God to you that releases an influence through you so other people are inclined to like, trust, and cooperate with you in the assignment God gave you.

That is a mouthful, but I still believe it is central to our success.

The key word is assignment. Favor is not charm. It is not a sales trick. It is not manipulation with Christian language. It is connected to what God is asking you to do.

**Jean:** In the source, one assignment did not lead to a purchase at all.

**Wes:** Yes. I knocked on a door because I was interested in a property. The man who answered did not own it. He was the executor of his fiancee's will. She had died, and he was alone and despondent. He invited me in. I listened to him, encouraged him, and prayed for God's blessing on him.

I never heard from him again.

But I believe the assignment did him good. Without favor, I do not think I would have been received that way.

**Jean:** Then the HOA foreclosure example shows favor moving into business creativity.

**Wes:** We normally excluded HOA foreclosures because they looked unprofitable. This owner owed less than $4,000 to the HOA. On paper, that seemed like something no one would let go to foreclosure. But something got my attention.

After letters and texts, I met him. He was cordial and inviting. I listened. I asked questions. Then I began shaping an offer: pay off the foreclosure lien, take the mortgage subject to, give him cash, let him stay in the home at rent tied to his expenses, and remove immediate pressure from his family.

When I realized he made about $42,000 a year, I told him I was offering him a year's salary and allowing him to stay in his house.

**Jean:** The source says the appointment may not have been about that owner alone.

**Wes:** Right. It opened my eyes to a structure we had been overlooking. The assignment may have been to see a new class of opportunity.

But that is where the heart has to stay submitted. A profitable structure can be favor, or it can become exploitation if the person disappears inside the math. Favor has to remain tied to assignment and love.

## Blessing and Red Quartz

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct source and faithful reconstruction. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Wes, what made Red Quartz feel like blessing?

**Wes:** The timing and cooperation were striking. The foreclosure was filed on January 25. We found it on January 27. We sent letters on February 1. Texts and emails began on February 4. After no response, I searched Facebook and Nicholas responded on February 6. From courthouse filing to first conversation was twelve days.

That is fast.

Then the first meeting had its own path. Chick-fil-A locations were closed because of Covid protocols, so we ended up at the library. I spoke with Nicholas and his wife, called his sister in Virginia, reached agreement, signed a contract and deed, and gave earnest money.

On the same day we made contact with the sellers, we also reached agreement with friends we had been talking to for months about a $100,000 private money loan. That loan was enough to purchase the property, bring the existing loan out of foreclosure, and rehab the house.

I can explain our part. We worked the system. We searched. We sent letters. We texted. We looked on Facebook. We met. We structured. But the cooperation of the parts was not engineered by us.
