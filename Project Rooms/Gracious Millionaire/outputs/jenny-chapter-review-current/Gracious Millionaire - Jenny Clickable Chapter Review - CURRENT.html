Foreword

Some books begin with a question the author already knows how to answer.

This one does not.

Gracious Millionaire begins with a question that keeps working on the writer while he writes it:

How much money is too much money?

That question sounds financial at first. It sounds like it belongs in a spreadsheet, or a bank account, or a net worth statement. But that is not where Wes and Jenny Browning take it. They take it where it belongs.

They take it to the heart.

This book is not really about how much money a person can make in real estate. It is not a manual for using God to get rich. It is not a set of formulas that promise favor if you repeat the right words, build the right spreadsheet, or structure the right deal.

It is a story about lordship.

That is what makes it worth reading.

Wes writes as a man who has learned enough to know that learning is not finished. He does not come to the reader as someone standing safely above the story. He writes from inside it. He writes from the pressure of the decision, the call with the seller, the tension with Jenny, the spreadsheet that looks profitable but cannot measure peace, the opportunity that may be favor or may be a test, and the memory of earlier seasons when real estate did not produce blessing but loss.

That honesty matters.

There is a kind of success writing that only wants to tell the reader what worked. It edits out the failures, softens the confessions, polishes the motives, and turns every outcome into proof that the author always knew what he was doing.

This is not that kind of book.

Before Wes and Jenny can talk honestly about favor, they have to talk about failure. Before they can talk about abundance, they have to talk about lordship. Before they can talk about becoming millionaires, they have to ask what kind of people they are becoming while money, pressure, opportunity, and responsibility increase.

That question gives the book its moral weight.

The title Gracious Millionaire holds two words that do not always live together easily.

Millionaire speaks of increase, ownership, profit, multiplication, and measurable results.

Gracious speaks of favor, humility, generosity, gratitude, and the way a person handles people when power and opportunity are in his hands.

The tension between those words is the book.

Can a person grow in wealth without being governed by wealth?

Can a business pursue profit and still be ruled by love?

Can a real estate investor see sellers, tenants, buyers, lenders, contractors, and family members as people rather than moving pieces in a transaction?

Can a husband and wife make decisions together when one sees possibility and the other feels caution?

Can a spreadsheet serve wisdom without replacing prayer?

Those are not small questions.

They are the questions that make the stories in this book more than business stories.

One of the strongest threads in the manuscript is Wes's instinct for systems. He sees structure where other people may only see scattered facts. That skill did not begin in real estate. It was formed over decades through business, college, programming, spreadsheets, databases, and work that taught him how to take chaos and give it shape.

But the book does not let skill have the final word.

Skill matters. Work matters. Education matters. Systems matter. Spreadsheets matter. A person cannot steward poorly and then call the result spiritual maturity.

But Wes keeps circling back to something deeper: skill is not the same as favor.

That distinction is essential.

Favor, as this book uses the word, is not charm. It is not manipulation. It is not a technique for getting people to cooperate. It is connected to assignment. It is connected to the attraction of God upon a person for the purpose God has given that person. It cannot be reduced to a script or turned into leverage.

That is why Gracious Millionaire is not merely about real estate investing.

Real estate is the classroom.

The subject is formation.

Houses are bought. Sellers are contacted. Notes are negotiated. Foreclosures are delayed or avoided. Tenants need options. Properties need repairs. Numbers have to work. But underneath the visible business activity, another question keeps pressing forward:

Who is being formed here?

That question belongs to Wes.

It belongs to Jenny.

It belongs to their marriage.

It belongs to the reader too.

Jenny's presence in the manuscript is important because this is not only a story about one man's drive, creativity, or willingness to consider unusual transactions. It is also a story about shared discernment. Jenny brings relational memory, practical caution, prayer, and the weight of unity. Her hesitation is not treated as an obstacle to success. At its best, the manuscript treats it as part of the process by which lordship becomes measurable.

That may be one of the most important lessons in the book.

Lordship is easy to talk about in general.

It becomes real in the particular.

It becomes real when a deal looks profitable but peace is not yet present.

It becomes real when a tenant may need to move.

It becomes real when helping someone creates pain later.

It becomes real when a husband has an idea and a wife has concerns.

It becomes real when the number on the spreadsheet is large enough to make pressure sound like wisdom.

The reader should not come to this book expecting all of those tensions to be neatly resolved. Some of the stories are unfinished. Some lessons are still being learned. Some questions are still open.

That is not a weakness.

It is part of the truthfulness of the book.

There is a humility in admitting that God is still working on the narrator while the story is being told. There is also an invitation in it. The reader is not being asked to admire a finished monument. The reader is being invited to walk through a living testimony and ask the same questions in his or her own life.

What has money become to me?

What does pressure reveal in me?

Where have I confused ambition with assignment?

Where have I called something wisdom when it was fear?

Where have I treated success as proof that my heart was right?

Where is God asking for lordship, not merely better results?

That is why the stories matter.

A vacant property can become a lesson in favor.

A failed investment can become a lesson in humility.

A spreadsheet can become a lesson in stewardship.

A difficult marriage conversation can become a lesson in unity.

A real estate business can become a place where God reveals what a person actually trusts.

This is a book about increase, but it is not worshiping increase.

It is a book about money, but it is suspicious of money's power to become lord.

It is a book about favor, but it refuses to turn favor into a formula.

It is a book about real estate, but the real estate is always pressing toward a deeper question.

Can the millionaire remain gracious?

That is the question the title asks.

And it is the question the book keeps asking.

Not once.

Again and again.

In the deal.

In the marriage.

In the failure.

In the favor.

In the abundance.

In the complaint.

In the gratitude.

In the unfinished story.

That is why this manuscript has life in it. It is not trying to convince the reader that Wes and Jenny have arrived. It is trying to notice what God has done, what He is doing, and what He still may be asking of them.

That makes the book personal.

It also makes it useful.

Not because every reader will buy houses the way Wes and Jenny buy houses.

Most will not.

But every reader will have to decide what to do with money, opportunity, pressure, fear, gratitude, and the question of who is Lord.

That is the real subject.

Real estate is the testimony.

Lordship is the invitation.

Introduction

This book began as a question about money, but the longer I sit with it, the more I realize it is a question about life.

It is about formation.

It is about success.

It is about what happens inside a person when opportunity begins to increase. It is about what money reveals, what pressure exposes, what ambition wants, what fear protects, and what God is asking to govern in the middle of it all.

Real estate is the setting for many of the stories, but real estate is not the deepest subject. Houses, sellers, contracts, mortgages, equity, private money, lawsuits, tenants, and spreadsheets all become part of the classroom. But the lesson underneath them is about lordship.

How much money is too much money?

That is not really a question about an amount.

It is a question about lordship.

Money becomes too much money when the desire for it replaces trust in God. It becomes too much money when it begins to tell you who you are. It becomes too much money when it becomes the voice you obey, the security you cling to, or the reason you stop listening.

That is the question behind this book.

Jenny and I are writing from the point of view of what we believe the Bible teaches concerning the Kingdom of God and how it relates to our finances. We believe the Kingdom touches every part of life. It touches marriage. It touches work. It touches money. It touches real estate. It touches the way we speak, the way we decide, the way we handle pressure, and the way we treat people when there is profit on the table.

There are many books written about success and prosperity. Some of them are very good. We have read some of them. We have enjoyed them. We have learned from them. We do not claim to be experts at anything.

This is not a how-to book.

Maybe it is a how-to-be book.

Bill Johnson has been one of the teachers who has helped me think about the Father's goodness, expectation, reward, stewardship, and increase without turning those words into a formula for self-centered prosperity. I am not trying to make his teaching the authority for this book. Scripture is the authority. But Bill has given language to some of the questions I am already wrestling with.

After listening to him teach on increasing expectation, I hear another question underneath my own question. What am I expecting God to do with what He has already placed in my hand? Bill returns to Hebrews 11:6, where God is described as the rewarder of those who diligently seek Him, and to the parable of the talents, where increase is not treated as greed but as faithfulness.

That does not make this a book about getting God to make us rich. It makes it a book about whether we can believe the Father is good, steward what He has trusted to us, and let Him decide what increase should look like.

It is our story of our adventures with real estate investment and the lessons we have learned, or are learning. When Jenny and I got married, our net worth was about negative $200,000. At the point of this writing, God has increased our net worth to about $4 million. That is a real number, and I do not want to pretend it is unimportant. But the number is not the point. The point is what happened to our hearts while the number was being formed.

The bulk of this book is made of stories. Real transactions. Real sellers. Real houses. Real phone calls. Real pressure. Real fear. Real favor. Some of the stories are profitable. Some are painful. Some are unfinished. Some still make me ask whether I understood what God was doing as clearly as I thought I did at the time.

That is part of why I am writing.

At the time I am writing this, I am still building the framework. The framework is a set of Biblical principles that I want to hang stories upon. I do not have all of the stories identified. I do not have all of the lessons learned. But while I am writing, I am praying that God will give the examples needed to reveal the truths.

Lance Wallnau is one of the teachers who has shaped the way I think about favor and assignment. I do not want to pretend that I can reproduce all of his teaching, but one definition associated with his teaching has helped me frame what Jenny and I have experienced.

Favor is the attraction of God to you that releases an influence through you so other people are inclined to like, to trust, and to cooperate with you in the assignment God gave you.

That definition has shaped much of what Jenny and I have experienced. Favor is not charm. It is not salesmanship. It is not a technique for getting people to do what you want. In its Biblical form, favor is almost always related to your God-given assignment. Think of Joseph, David, Daniel, Esther, Nehemiah, and Mary. These are the notable Bible characters where the word favor showed up.

Many people fall short of a divine encounter because they are satisfied with good theology.

The word of God is an invitation to meet the person.

For these ideas to be effective, they have to transform from philosophy to lifestyle. There is a difference between head knowledge and heart knowledge. I have known the difference in my own life. I have known theology and still acted as though the outcome depended entirely on my own pressure, my own cleverness, my own ambition, or my own ability to make something happen.

The Christian principles we are talking about here are not just philosophy. As long as they remain philosophy, they may have a certain effectiveness, but they fall short. For them to have their full effectiveness, they have to manifest in the obedience of servanthood as it relates to Jesus Christ.

So what is the difference between manipulation, favor, prayer, and declarations?

What is the difference between ambition and assignment?

What is the difference between wealth under lordship and wealth that has quietly become lord?

Those are the questions behind this book.

The chapters that follow do not answer those questions in a straight line. They answer them the way life usually answers them: through protection I did not earn, failures I would not have chosen, skills God had been forming for decades, plans that exposed my need for control, sellers who opened their doors, lawsuits that tested my heart, and opportunities that forced Jenny and me to pray for unity.

That is why this book has to be read as testimony before it is read as teaching.

The teaching is inside the story.

Always God Is There

I was driving to one of the job sites today when a song came on the radio.

It was "Nights in White Satin" by The Moody Blues.

The swelling of the music, the horns, and the strings reminded me of something that happened about fifty years ago. It was not dark yet, or at least I do not remember it being dark. I was still living with my parents. I had just left home in my truck, probably less than a mile from the house, and I had that cassette playing in the radio.

The volume was up high.

I was headed out for the evening. I do not remember now where I was going. Maybe I was going to meet friends. Maybe I was just going out because that is what young men do when they are restless and careless and not thinking very far beyond the next few minutes.

Just before the road we lived on met the highway in Lutz, Florida, it crossed a railroad track.

The music on the radio was swelling. The volume was high. Just before I crossed the track, I looked left and saw the train coming.

It had been blowing its horn, but the music had masked it. I had not looked quickly enough. I had not slowed down enough to check before crossing the tracks.

It was too late.

I saw it coming, but I did not have time to stop.

So I floored it.

I almost made it across.

The train clipped about the last foot or so of the bed of my truck. It hit so hard that it spun the whole truck around about one and a half times. It threw me into the door so hard that I bent in the side panel with my arm.

The truck was totaled.

I came out of it with no more than a bruised forearm.

Driving down the interstate today, with that song coming through the speakers again, I was reminded of how many times God has protected me. That episode was only one of a dozen that I can think of quickly.

I have always been a risk taker, sometimes to the point of carelessness. That day at the railroad track was one of those times. A good driver would have slowed down long enough to look carefully. I did look, but not in time to save myself.

That is an important phrase.

Not in time to save myself.

There are places in my life where the only honest testimony is that I did not save myself. I was careless. I was reckless. I made the wrong calculation. I responded too late. If you could see my whole driving record, it would tell that same story.

But God has always been faithful.

I have often been aware that my angels work overtime protecting me. I have also been aware that God has a purpose for my life. That does not excuse carelessness. It does not turn recklessness into wisdom. It does not make foolishness a virtue.

But it does make me grateful.

That was not my first automobile accident, and it was far from my last. One thing many of them have in common is that I walked away. Vehicles were totaled. Metal was bent. Insurance questions had to be answered. Jenny had to come pick me up. But again and again, I walked away.

The truck that was hit by the train was the first of only two vehicles I have ever bought new in my lifetime. The second new vehicle came years later, after I had moved to North Carolina.

Before the train incident, there had already been another accident late at night while I was coming home from being with friends. I was tired. Less than half a mile from where the railroad-track incident later happened, I dozed off at the wheel.

I woke up startled as my car crossed the line on the highway and went down an embankment on the left side of the road. I woke up just in time to hit the brakes before plowing into a utility pole in the ditch.

Again, I walked away.

The car was a total loss.

My memory is fuzzy on some of the details after all these years, but I think I was not properly insured when I wrecked the truck by way of the train. Somehow my uncle, who had an electrical contracting business, found a way to get it covered under his umbrella policy, and the insurance company paid off the loan on the truck.

That cleared the way for me to buy the truck back as salvage. I built a wooden bed on the back where the original bed had been wiped out. Of course, after its collision with the train, it always tracked a little sideways down the road.

A few years later, I was driving that same truck with two friends beside me on the bench seat. We were about a half mile farther down the road when another car coming down the highway crossed the lane in front of me to turn.

This one was not my fault, but that did not matter.

I could not avoid the car. I swerved right to try to miss him, but he still hit me right in the middle. One more time, God's angels and His protection allowed us to walk away.

I totaled that truck for the second time.

There was another incident with a convertible BMW on the interstate. I was coming home from work, and a man did not like the way I was driving. He deliberately ran me off the side of the road. He lost control too, and both of us spun into the median of Interstate 40.

There are guide wires down the middle of that interstate that are supposed to keep cars from crossing into oncoming traffic. What they did to me was almost decapitate me as my car scraped down the side of them. Instead of going through them, I bounced off.

The driver who ran me off the road also spun around in front of me, but he did not hit anything. He got out of his car, looked back at me, laughed, and drove away.

I called Jenny to come get me.

And I thanked God again for His protection.

The latest incident is only months old as I write this. I have not made the same kinds of mistakes that characterized the young Wes Browning, but I still have my moments.

I was very happy with the Cadillac Escalade God had allowed me to drive for the last few years. I was on the way home from another job site, only about two miles from our house. The dash cameras recorded video both in front of and behind the vehicle.

The video would show the other car crossing the line. At the last moment it hit my front bumper and sideswiped the whole side of the truck. It ripped off both wheels and the sideboard. The video showed him crossing the line behind me and staying on my side of the road.

He stayed in his car until the ambulance came and got him out.

I was walking around, looking at the damage, and again calling Jenny to come pick me up.

What am I trying to say?

I am not bragging about carelessness. I am not bragging about being accident prone. I am not trying to turn foolishness into a spiritual gift. I am bragging on God.

Whether I have been foolish, impatient, careless, or simply in the wrong place when another driver made the mistake, God has promised never to forsake me, and He has been ever faithful.

The same favor follows us in business.

That is why this story belongs in a book about real estate, money, favor, and lordship. It is not because an accident story is the same thing as a property story. It is because the lesson underneath them is the same.

I am not the source of my own preservation.

I am not the source of every open door.

I am not the source of every rescue.

I can work hard. I can learn. I can build systems. I can analyze spreadsheets. I can take risks. I can make offers. I can structure deals creatively. I can drive to job sites and meet contractors and walk properties and solve problems.

But there are moments when the only honest explanation is that God was there.

Always God is there.

That history reminds me not to take credit for what Somebody else has done.

In this case, always God is there.

That truth has to come before the real estate stories.

If I do not recognize God as the one who preserved my life, then I will eventually be tempted to recognize myself as the one who produced the business. If I take credit for every rescue, every open door, every profitable structure, and every moment of favor, then the story will become about my intelligence instead of His faithfulness.

So before I talk about failure, systems, leadership, favor, and properties, I want this marker in the ground.

I am alive by mercy.

Everything else has to be interpreted from there.

Failures in Real Estate

There are periods before the success in real estate that Jenny and I are writing about in this book that were not successful at all.

That has to be said.

If we are going to talk honestly about favor, blessing, prosperity, abundance, and becoming a gracious millionaire, then it would not be fair to only tell the stories that worked. It would not be fair to tell the stories that make us look wise and leave out the ones that reveal how much we did not know. It would not be fair to talk about success without talking about failure.

Failure is part of this story.

Not as a decoration.

As a foundation.

The newer chapters have made this clearer to me. The failures were not only failed deals or failed plans. They were places where God was exposing the difference between ambition and assignment. They were places where I had ideas, energy, and pressure, but not yet the kind of submitted leadership that could carry what I wanted God to bless.

That does not mean God was absent.

It means I was still being formed.

Jenny and I have both had failed marriages. I am not going to go deeply into all of that here. There are parts of those stories that are too personal and too complicated for the purpose of this chapter. But before I met and married Jenny, my second marriage failed, and there were real estate failures inside that failure.

For this narration, I am only going to refer to my second wife as number two.

Somewhere during that season I got hold of some of the real estate educational material that I am still paying attention to now. I began to think about real estate the way those mentors were teaching. It was the beginning of my real estate education.

I had been a Christian all my life. I tried to walk in a way that was consistent with what I understood my relationship with God should look like. I had deep-rooted beliefs that started when I was a child. My parents brought me up well in the understanding of God, and I honor the way they taught me to relate to the Lord.

But there were aspects of walking with God that I understand now that I did not understand then.

I do not think I knew anything about walking in the favor of the Lord.

I think I knew a good bit about proper confession, but I do not think I was walking in it. I knew theology. I knew many of the right words. But I do not think those truths had transformed into the daily dependence I am trying to describe in this book.

That is the context for this story.

My history with real estate did not start with success.

My first house was bought with my first wife in Florida. We bought that house just before Ashley and Brandon were born. I was self-employed in the sign business then, and I built a garage on the back side of the house where my shop was. I had operated that business for about eight years, I think.

But I felt like my life was not going in the direction I wanted it to go. At the age of 30, we signed the deed over to my first wife's parents, moved in with them, and I started to college full time. Actually, I started double time. I enrolled in the local community college and finished a two-year associate's degree in one year.

Then we moved to Gainesville, and I enrolled at the University of Florida.

Mechanical engineering was a hard degree. It took me two years to finish one year of college there while I was working part-time to pay the bills and keep up with my studies. After completing my third year of college, we ran out of financial aid. It looked like college was going to have to stop, and I was going to have to go to work full-time.

So we packed up and moved to North Carolina to get started in a new location where I might finish college part-time. That never really happened. I found work, and my career in computer programming continued to develop.

We bought a little farmhouse in Johnston County, an old beat-up house.

I used to say I burned that house down one splinter at a time because when I was remodeling and taking all the old wood out, it went on the fire in the back and was replaced, little by little, with new stuff.

That is the house I lost two times.

The first time was when number one divorced me and took the house. Then she moved out of it and would not sell it to me. She sold it to some third party. We had four kids at the time, and I had no house to share with them when I had custody. So I bought the house back from the lady she had sold it to.

That is the house I was living in when I married number two.

Real estate had not been my friend at this point in my life.

Still, I kept going.

There were also three major real estate transactions that number two and I engaged in that, in the end, were total failures. I think if I knew then what I know now, they might not have been. But there is no way to know that. A proper story about my life as a real estate investor would not be complete without talking about them.

One transaction was a home for us to live in when we were in Raleigh. At that time I was traveling back and forth between Houston and Raleigh. I shared custody of my four children with my first wife, and when I was in Raleigh I would pick them up and they would live with me while I was there.

The house we bought had two floors. The first floor was finished with three bedrooms and two bathrooms, and it opened up to the roadside and the driveway. Underneath it was an unfinished basement that was the same size as the upstairs and opened out from the back of the property.

This became my first major renovation.

We designed and built four bedrooms, two bathrooms, and a kitchen in that open unfinished basement. It was quite a project, and I did almost all of it myself. I designed it and worked with a general contractor, but with a few exceptions I did the work. I did not do the HVAC or the plumbing, but I did almost everything else.

Once it was completed, the design allowed our family to move downstairs and rent the upstairs. The rent from the upstairs paid the mortgage on the property.

That part worked.

It was a sound financial model. We effectively had no expense for our personal home in Raleigh. Looking back, I can see that there were things in that house that anticipated the way I would later think about real estate. Use what you have. Create income from the property. Let the house carry itself if it can.

That was a good idea.

But good ideas are not enough by themselves.

The second major transaction was the purchase of a large hotel in the Galveston area of Texas. Number two was from the Houston area and had a home there. During that marriage I traveled back and forth between Houston and Raleigh, with one foot in Texas and one foot in North Carolina. It was a very busy time of my life.

We bought a 200-room rundown hotel right off a major highway between Houston and Galveston. We bought it from a private owner. It was not operational when we bought it, but it had a lot of potential. It had a restaurant, a bar, and three conference rooms. Many of the rooms needed a lot of maintenance before they could be used.

We structured the purchase with some cash and a promissory note to the seller for the remainder of the purchase. I do not have the records from that acquisition now. Those records were maintained by my ex, and the purchase was made in entities that we had set up and that she controlled. My memory is a little fuzzy after all these years, so I will only speak in general terms about the money. I think the total purchase price was around $3 million.

When we bought it, we moved into one of the suites and began to live and breathe the operation of the hotel.

Everything had to be brought up to operational condition. We hired consultants to advise us on the restaurant and conference rooms. We catered food for events we were attracting for the conference facilities. We got a set of rooms operational and began to have guests in the hotel.

It was exciting.

I am not trying to give a lesson on operating a hotel. I have experience, but nothing to teach from that experience except humility. I am giving context and backstory to the story Jenny and I are telling now.

The hotel never made money.

As the financial pressure built, the stress in our marriage built with it. Eventually I made the decision, in conjunction with number two, to move back permanently to Raleigh where I could get a job and begin to stem the losses we had endured.

We eventually lost the investment.

The third major investment was five duplexes in Johnston County, North Carolina. They were purchased from one individual, and they were all occupied by what was then called Section 8 housing residents.

They were bought around the same time as the hotel acquisition. My plan was to manage them during my trips back and forth to Raleigh. I got quite an education in property management during that time.

But again, in the end, they were lessons from failure.

As my marriage with number two began to fall apart under the stress of the finances from all the investments, I made a deal with her to buy her out of the duplexes. The loans had been acquired in her name. So I created a partnership agreement and entity with my mom to pay back the investment number two had made, and we transferred the deeds into our name.

The divorce was initiated, and number two demanded that we sell the Raleigh house. I offered to buy it from her. I had a loan in place to do it. But she would have nothing to do with that and demanded that it be sold. She sold it and took the proceeds because she had invested the money.

As part of the divorce settlement with number two, she also took the Johnston County farmhouse that I had owned before our marriage and that was being rented out at the time. So I lost that house the second time in a second divorce.

I moved into a nearby apartment where I could house my children. We stayed there for a little over a year, until my children were old enough to move out except for my youngest.

Then I bought an RV that was big enough for me to live in permanently and still have room for my youngest son when he stayed with me. We parked the RV in the backyard of one of the duplexes, where I could keep a close eye on the tenants and manage the properties.

We kept the five loans in place and took ownership subject to the existing mortgages. For a while they cash flowed well enough to pay the expenses. But little by little I had trouble keeping them rented at the level needed to pay the mortgages. They started getting behind.

This was around the time of the 2008 mortgage crisis. The bank was not very tolerant of the delinquencies. Honestly, I did not have the experience or education to find ways to avoid the consequences.

One by one, the banks foreclosed on all of them.

Over a period of about five years, we had bought a hotel, a personal residence, five rental duplexes, and some other investment properties. By the end of that season, both houses were gone in the second divorce, the hotel investment was gone, the duplexes were gone, and the marriage had ended in failure.

There were a lot of things wrong, and I will not try to go into all of them. I do not know what would have happened if I had been walking in the same principles I am trying to talk about in this book. I cannot say that I was daily taking my needs to God. I cannot say that I was asking for His favor. There were other dynamics wrong in the marriage for sure.

But I can say this.

The difference between that season and this season is not that I am smarter now.

I am more educated. That is true.

I have more experience. That is true.

I have better systems. That is true.

But those things are not the centerpiece of this book.

The difference I can recognize is that now we are walking in the favor of the Lord, and then I was not. At least I was not walking in it the way I understand it now. I was trying to do real estate with ideas, energy, pressure, and ambition. Some of the ideas were good. Some of the structures were sound. But I was not living with the same daily dependence on God that I am trying to describe now.

That matters.

This is not a book about how to get rich by using God. That would be a horrible book.

This is a book about lordship.

This is a book about what happens when money, opportunity, favor, work, marriage, stress, and ambition all come under the question of who is really Lord.

Private Money And Investor Relationships

Our real estate business was built on a few key concepts.

The first was the foreclosure system we put in place, modeled after Jay Conner's system.

The second was private money.

Those two things worked together. The foreclosure system helped us find opportunities. Private money helped us move when the opportunities were in front of us.

That distinction matters because finding a deal and being able to do a deal are not the same thing.

In the beginning, institutional lending was not really in the cards for us. We did not have an established business. We did not have a long track record that would make a bank comfortable. Banks want a lot of constraints. They want documentation. They want qualifications. They want the process to move at their speed. They often want to lend on one property at a time.

There is nothing wrong with banks.

But banks were not going to build the business we were trying to build at the speed the opportunities were appearing.

That left us with a problem.

God was putting houses in front of us faster than our resources could handle them.

We bought and sold our first three houses without the advantage of private lending. That is an important part of the story because private money did not come first. We had to begin with what we had.

One of those houses was very cheap and had a remodeling budget that fit the resources available to us at the time. Those resources were not impressive. They were a number of credit cards and retirement and health savings accounts connected to the business structure we had put in place.

We were not sitting on a pile of cash.

We were stretching.

We bought and worked on the first house with retirement money. We bought the second house with credit card money. Even though we bought three houses in a very short period of time, we did not have the resources to work on them all at the same time.

In fact, we could not really do anything with the second house until we sold the other two.

That was not only a money issue. It was a capacity issue.

With our experience, the status of our processes, our contractor relationships, and the number of things we were learning at once, that was all we could manage anyway. If I am honest, we were way over our skis on those first three houses.

I do not say that as a criticism of the opportunity.

I say it because opportunity always exposes capacity.

God can put something in front of you that is real, but that does not mean you have already been formed enough to carry the next ten things. I had systems. I had energy. I had a willingness to learn. I had faith. But I was also discovering the limits of my own cash, my own time, my own relationships, and my own leadership.

That is when it became time to concentrate on raising private money.

Our first $100,000 came from a friend in the neighborhood.

Jenny and I were walking on the golf course cart path one morning when we came across her. She asked us about our business. That question opened a door.

We explained what we were doing. We explained that we borrowed money from individuals, paid a strong return, provided collateral, used property insurance and title insurance, and made sure their money could get back to them if they needed it quickly.

That was the beginning.

It sounds simple when I write it in a few sentences.

But there was a lot inside that moment.

There was relationship. There was trust. There was enough of a track record to make the conversation credible. There was a real opportunity that needed funding. There was also the favor of God, because a person does not simply hand over $100,000 because you have a clever sentence.

Money follows trust.

At least the kind of money we were trying to raise does.

That first private money relationship allowed us to get started on our fourth house.

After that, we hosted a number of private money dinners. We would showcase the projects we had worked on. We brought pictures. We brought videos. We explained our lending program. We explained what the money was secured by, how the return worked, how we protected the lender, and why the opportunity made sense.

Those dinners were not only about raising money.

They were about building relationships.

That is one of the lessons I have had to learn again and again. A spreadsheet may show a good return, but people do not lend to a spreadsheet. They lend to people they trust. They lend when they believe you know what you are doing. They lend when they feel that you will tell them the truth, protect their interest, and care about their money as though it matters.

Because it does matter.

That money represents someone's labor. It represents someone's savings. It may represent retirement planning, family security, or years of careful stewardship. If I borrow it lightly, I am not being gracious. I am being presumptuous.

Private money is not free money.

It is entrusted money.

That is why I have never wanted our private lenders to feel like they were being used. I wanted them to feel like they were part of something that created value for everyone involved. They were receiving a return that was much better than what many traditional places could offer. We were receiving the capital we needed to move quickly. Sellers were getting solutions. Houses were being repaired. Buyers were eventually getting homes. Neighborhoods were being improved.

That is the gracious version of the opportunity.

The dangerous version would be to see lenders only as a source of capital.

That is where money tests the heart.

When we began, many of our first properties were fix-and-flip projects. That meant the money could be recirculated. Once one property sold, if the lenders were interested, we could move their money to the next property, or wait until we had another project that fit their interest and borrow it again.

Over time, those relationships multiplied.

I need to verify the exact figures before this manuscript is finished, but for now I can say conversationally that we have probably borrowed around $3 million this way and probably paid our investors around $500,000 in interest.

That may sound expensive.

But it helped us buy twenty-eight homes so far with an average profit of more than $115,000.

Banks would never have lent us that much money that quickly.

They would not have understood the model. They would not have moved at the speed we needed. They would not have looked at the opportunities the way we looked at them. They would have seen risk in places where we saw assignment, equity, problem-solving, and a chance to create a result that was better than the one already unfolding.

That does not mean we were careless.

It means we had to build a different kind of funding system.

We have never missed an interest payment. We have never missed getting money back to someone who needed it when they requested it. That matters to me. It matters because the relationship matters. It matters because trust is not a slogan. Trust is built one payment, one phone call, one update, one fulfilled promise at a time.

The lenders we have worked with have been happy with their investments and satisfied with the relationship they built with us.

That sentence is more important than it first appears.

Because if the gracious millionaire question means anything, it has to include the way we handle people who trust us with money.

It is easy to talk about favor when the seller signs the contract.

It is easy to talk about blessing when the profit appears.

But favor and blessing have to be measured in the middle, where other people's money is involved and the responsibility is real.

Most of the time, we have placed private lenders' money on properties we had already managed to buy but had not yet begun to rehab. That gave us flexibility. We could move on a property quickly and then bring private money into the project in a way that made sense for the lender and for us.

But borrowing from private lenders is not the only model we have used.

As we built relationships with investors, there were a few occasions where we formed joint venture partnerships to buy and rehab property.

Those were different.

In those cases, larger amounts of capital were needed quickly to buy a property and complete the rehab. We made agreements with investors, sometimes as many as five at a time, to front all of the required money in exchange for a percentage of the eventual profit.

That model has advantages.

It can allow us to do a project without fronting our own money. It can allow us to move quickly. It can allow an investor to participate in a larger upside than a fixed private money return would provide.

But it also has disadvantages.

We give up more of the profit in the property, so it does not fit every project. It has to be the right project, with the right people, at the right time, with enough margin for the arrangement to make sense.

Still, it is a good model to have available when the right opportunity comes along.

The first project we did that way was Sandy Run.

It was also the first project where I made a formal pitch to a group of people. We had joined an association to network with investors, and that association had a weekly Zoom meeting where people could pitch deals.

Honestly, we did not need to look for joint venture partners for that project in the strictest sense. But we were forming relationships, and Sandy Run looked like a good project to introduce ourselves and attract investors who might want to work with us.

Sandy Run was an entry-level house that needed a lot of rehab.

We chose it to pitch to the group.

Immediately, there was a lot of interest. In fact, there were more people interested than we could use for that project. We selected five individuals we felt good about and put the documents together to raise the money for the project.

That project went well.

We enjoyed working with the investors we had come to know and letting them share in the profit of a project God had put in front of us.

Two of those investors would continue with us into the next projects.

One of them was involved in the Bergwyn property.

That one happened while we were at a conference in New York City with a network of investor friends we had made while trying to develop Providence Landing.

A few days before the trip, I had met with the sellers of the Bergwyn property. While we were in New York, an email conversation began where they agreed to the proposal I had made to buy their property.

I quickly sent an email to a few individuals I knew were at the conference and were interested in investing in our projects.

Within minutes, I received responses, and we agreed to talk while we were there.

That is the value of building relationships.

Within a day, while away from home in New York City, I finalized the terms of a purchase and agreed to a joint venture that would provide all of the funding from start to finish for a project, without ever talking to a banker or going through the tedious qualification process for a loan.

That still amazes me.

Not because I think I engineered it perfectly.

Because relationships made speed possible.

Those first joint venture projects came during the period when we were trying to raise funding for Providence Landing. We have not finished that story yet, and I will not finish it here. But I will say this much: although that effort was costly in time and money and carried a lot of lessons, we met friends through that process who still have value in our lives today.

The three deals we have done so far with people we formed relationships with during that period more than paid for what it cost us.

That is another kind of redemption.

Not every costly season produces the result you thought you were buying.

Sometimes it produces relationships.

And sometimes those relationships become the provision for something later.

The third joint venture was Old Buck.

For that property, we actually did need someone to front all of the acquisition and rehab expense. I called Adam, who had been one of our partners in Sandy Run, and asked him if he wanted to see the details.

He was enthusiastic about the project, even though I told him from the beginning that I had made the deal with a seller who was going to lease the property back for at least a year.

That meant Adam would need to front the money to purchase the property with an understanding of the remodeling work ahead, but the project would be delayed before he received his promised return.

He was okay with that.

Again, that is relationship.

A stranger may have looked at the delay and said no. A banker may have looked at the structure and asked for a stack of paperwork that had nothing to do with the actual opportunity. But a person who knew us, trusted us, and understood the project could make a different decision.

All of these projects, whether they were funded by private lending or joint venture investors, turned on relationships built over time.

Most of the investors who have involved themselves in our projects have returned for other funding rounds in the future.

That is not something I take lightly.

It means we are carrying more than capital.

We are carrying trust.

And trust is a spiritual responsibility before it is a financial tool.

That is why private money belongs in this book. It is not only a technique for growing a real estate business. It is a test of whether increase can stay submitted to love.

Can I borrow money without seeing the lender as an object?

Can I pay a return without worshiping return?

Can I build a funding system without putting my faith in the system?

Can I tell the truth when a project is delayed?

Can I protect someone else's money with the same seriousness I would want if the roles were reversed?

Can I remain gracious when capital starts to multiply opportunity?

That is the real lesson.

Private money helped us move faster than banks ever would have allowed.

But speed is not the final measure.

The final measure is whether the speed remained under lordship.

The final measure is whether the relationships were protected.

The final measure is whether the people who trusted us were blessed, not merely used.

The gracious millionaire cannot be gracious only toward sellers and tenants.

He has to be gracious toward lenders too.

Because they are not just funding sources.

They are people.

And every person in the story belongs to God before they belong to the deal.

Leadership Or The Lack Of

I have always thought I would be a great leader if I could only get people to follow me.

That sentence is funny to me now, but it is also painfully honest.

For a long time I thought leadership had something to do with seeing farther than other people could see. I thought it had something to do with having a plan, putting that plan on paper, explaining it well enough, and then waiting for everybody else to recognize the wisdom of what I was trying to do.

I have always been a planner.

I can fill pages with plans. I can make lists. I can draw diagrams. I can look at a situation and begin to see what should be done next. That has been true in business, in family life, in real estate, in church work, and in almost every other arena where I have cared deeply about the outcome.

But if I am honest, very few of those plans have unfolded the way I expected them to.

That is not a small statement in a book like this.

I have made three distinct, separate, and unique attempts at being in business. The first two did not pan out the way I planned. I wanted them to. I worked hard. I had ideas. I had energy. I had what I thought was vision.

But the first two attempts did not become what I expected them to become.

This real estate business looks like it might pay off.

Even saying that makes me cautious, because one of the things I am learning is that payoff is not the same as lordship. A business can begin to work and still expose wrong motives in me. Success does not automatically sanctify ambition. Sometimes success simply gives ambition more room to move around.

One of the great motivators for me for a long time has been the desire to be influential to the people around me.

That sounds noble when I say it the right way.

Influence can be a good thing. A father should influence his family. A husband should bring strength and vision into a marriage. A business owner should influence the people who work with him. A board member should bring wisdom into the room. A believer should carry something of the Kingdom of God into every place he goes.

But wanting to be influential is not the same as leadership.

Thinking you know how others should act is not the same as leadership.

That is one of the harder sentences for me to write.

I have often been frustrated when I was trying to make progress toward a certain goal and expected the people around me to understand, participate, and care about that goal the same way I did. In my mind the path was clear. The need was obvious. The timing mattered. The idea made sense.

So why could they not see it?

That question has come up in me more times than I want to admit.

I have sat on different boards at different times in my life. I have been in rooms where decisions needed to be made and where I believed I could see a better way forward. I expected others to see the wisdom of the ideas I was expressing. I would be patient for a little while, because I knew I would have to explain why I thought something was such a good idea.

But as time dragged on, and my companions or associates or family members did not fully embrace what I was engineering, my patience often turned into frustration.

Sometimes that frustration became unveiled anger.

Sometimes it was not even very thinly veiled.

That has shown up in my family. It has shown up when my spouse was not in lockstep with what I wanted to do. It has shown up when my children were not pursuing the same agenda I had in my mind, or when they were not reacting the way I expected them to react in a given set of circumstances.

I could call that vision.

I could call that urgency.

I could call that leadership.

But a lot of the time it was really control wearing leadership's clothes.

That is not easy for me to say.

I do believe God has given me an ability to see patterns. I do believe He has given me a systems mind. I can look at an opportunity and start building a process around it. I can see where a piece of information belongs. I can imagine how a follow-up system should work. I can look at a real estate lead and start thinking through the seller, the buyer, the financing, the repairs, the risk, the timing, and the possible path forward.

Those are gifts.

But a gift that is not submitted to God can become pressure on the people around me.

That is where this chapter belongs in the story.

In the convergence chapter I talked about the systems instinct that has been in me for a long time. I talked about the sign business, the early computer, the engineering training, the database work, the spreadsheets, and the way God has been weaving those skills into the real estate business.

But convergence is not enough by itself.

If God brings a person's ability, history, timing, and assignment together, that person still has to learn how to carry the assignment. He still has to learn how to lead without using frustration as a tool. He still has to learn that people are not raw material for his plan.

That may be one of the hardest parts of leadership.

Leadership is not just having good ideas and expecting everybody to get on board.

Leadership begins with walking in proper relationship with God.

It begins with following His voice. It means learning when to speak and when to be quiet. It means expressing, when appropriate, what God is saying to you to the people around you. But it also means being patient when they do not adopt your plans, your mindset, or your priorities.

That last part is where the formation happens.

It is one thing to say God gave me a plan.

It is another thing to keep walking with God when the plan is not moving at the speed I expected, when the people I love are not responding the way I hoped, when the board does not vote the way I think it should, when the business does not grow according to my spreadsheet, or when a family member does not see what seems obvious to me.

In those moments I have often wanted to push harder.

But pushing harder is not always faith.

Sometimes it is fear.

Sometimes it is pride.

Sometimes it is my attempt to make sure the thing I built does not fall apart.

That is why another teaching from Bill Johnson has been helpful to me in thinking about leadership. In a message on being filled with the Holy Spirit, he talks about authority being connected to commission. The way I hear that is simple: authority is not the same thing as personality, pressure, charisma, or even a good idea. Authority is tied to being submitted to the mission God actually gave.

That matters to me because I can confuse a plan with a commission.

I can have an idea and assume the authority of God is behind it because the idea seems useful, efficient, or even necessary. But if I am not submitted to God in the way I carry that idea, then my leadership begins to draw its strength from me. Then I have to push. I have to persuade. I have to manage the reactions of the people around me.

Bill also makes the point that the disciples could function under Jesus' mantle while He was with them, but after the resurrection they had to receive and carry their own authority and power for the mission. That is a sobering thought. It is possible to be near someone else's gift and think it is yours. It is possible to be around an atmosphere of faith, vision, or leadership and mistake borrowed influence for formed authority.

I think that can happen in business too.

I can be around good teaching. I can quote good lines. I can talk about Kingdom principles, favor, assignment, and stewardship. But if I have not allowed God to form the way I actually lead, then the words are ahead of the man.

In that same teaching, Bill points to Acts and the phrase that Jesus began both to do and to teach. That order matters. In our culture a person can teach business without owning a business, teach leadership without leading people well, and teach spiritual principles without living them under pressure. But Kingdom leadership is not merely concept. It is life first, then words.

That lands hard for me.

If I want to teach gracious increase, I have to become gracious while increase is happening. If I want to teach leadership, I have to learn to lead when people are slow, cautious, unconvinced, or different from me. If I want to say God is building something, I have to stop acting as though everything depends on my ability to keep the structure standing.

One of my favorite quotes from Bill Johnson is, "If you build it, you have to support it. If God builds it, He supports it."

That quote has stayed with me because it speaks directly to the kind of man I have been. I know how to build things in my mind. I know how to imagine a structure. I know how to create systems, plans, processes, files, lists, and projections. I know how to look at a business and say, "Here is how this could work."

But if I build something out of my own striving, I have to support it.

I have to keep persuading.

I have to keep defending.

I have to keep explaining.

I have to keep applying pressure.

I have to keep managing everybody's reaction to the thing I am trying to make happen.

That is exhausting.

It is also not the same as Kingdom leadership.

If God builds it, He supports it.

That does not mean I become passive. It does not mean I stop planning. It does not mean I refuse responsibility or sit around waiting for mystical direction before I make every phone call. Real estate does not work that way. Business does not work that way. Marriage and family do not work that way either.

But it does mean the weight changes.

If the plan is God's plan, I can give it back to Him.

I can pray.

I can wait.

I can explain without demanding.

I can be an example of what I am advocating.

That is not easy for me.

Leaders lead by example, not because it is easy but because it is hard.

It is hard to keep a right spirit when people do not do things your way. It is hard to stay gracious when you feel misunderstood. It is hard to keep your voice level when you believe the stakes are high. It is hard to keep trusting God when the timing of your plan does not match the timing of the people around you.

But maybe that is exactly why leadership has to begin with lordship.

If I am not submitted to God, I will eventually try to make other people submit to my plan.

If I am walking with God, I can hold the plan with an open hand.

That does not mean I will never be direct. It does not mean I will never challenge people. It does not mean leadership becomes soft, vague, or passive. Jesus was not passive. But He was completely submitted to the Father. He did not need to manipulate people into obedience. He spoke what the Father gave Him to speak. He did what the Father gave Him to do.

That is the pattern I am still learning.

The real estate business has given me a classroom for this. There are plans everywhere. There are seller calls, buyer calls, lender conversations, title issues, repairs, lease agreements, spreadsheets, projections, insurance questions, and deadlines. Every property can become a little kingdom if I am not careful.

My little kingdom.

My deal.

My system.

My plan.

My timing.

But this book is not about my little kingdom.

It is about the Kingdom of God.

So leadership requires gracious patience when things are not going my way and people are not doing things my way. It requires me to ask whether I am leading by example or merely applying pressure. It requires me to notice when my patience has turned into frustration and when frustration has started looking for a target.

And then it requires repentance.

That word belongs here.

Not as a religious word thrown into a business chapter, but as the honest response when I realize I have been trying to support what I built instead of trusting God with what He is building.

I still make plans.

I still write things down.

I still think on paper.

I still believe planning is part of stewardship.

But I am learning that a plan is not a lord.

A plan is a tool.

God is Lord.

If He gives me something to build, then I want to build it with Him, under Him, and for Him. I want the people around me to experience the grace of that leadership, not just the pressure of my urgency.

That may be one of the great tests of becoming a gracious millionaire.

Can God trust me with increase if I cannot be patient with people?

Can He trust me with influence if I confuse influence with control?

Can He trust me with assignment if I turn every assignment into evidence that everybody should finally listen to me?

Those are not theoretical questions for me.

They are the questions God keeps using to form me.

And maybe that is the mercy in all the plans that did not unfold the way I expected.

God was not only interested in whether the plan worked.

He was interested in what kind of man would be leading if it did.

Favor

Favor is something I have been learning about deeply for about eight years.

By this point in the story, I hope it is clear that favor is not another word for control.

That distinction matters to me. I have sometimes wanted influence because I wanted people to get on board with my plan. But favor, as I am learning it, is not God helping me pressure people more effectively. It is not charm. It is not persuasion polished up with spiritual language. It is not a way to make ambition look holy.

Favor belongs under lordship.

I should credit the person who has been the most influential to us in this regard. I started watching YouTubes of Lance Wallnau about eight years ago when I was in between jobs. Favor is something you do not just have because of your position or experience. Education and expertise are not the key components of favor.

Remember, everything we are saying here pivots upon our relationship to the King of this Kingdom.

Jesus grew in wisdom and in stature and in favor with God and all the people.

That sentence matters to me because it joins things we sometimes separate. Wisdom. Stature. Favor with God. Favor with people. Jesus did not need to manipulate people. He did not need to posture. He did not need to borrow authority from money, position, or fear. He walked in assignment, and favor followed.

It is just that I believe, from a Christian point of view, a Christian who is striving for the Kingdom of God cannot and should not make success the goal.

Let me back up and speak to the person who is not coming from this point of view.

You can follow the principles of success books and have great success. I am not denying that. There are principles of diligence, focus, stewardship, relationship, gratitude, and wise action that work in the world because God made the world with moral structure. But if you are a Christian striving for the Kingdom of God, your success will be very limited if success, prosperity, wealth, fame, or influence is your goal.

Why is that?

Because God is more interested in what is happening to you internally than what is happening to you externally.

But how can you write a book titled Gracious Millionaire and not be teaching about success?

Let me say it plainly. I do want to be successful. I even want to be wealthy. I am not pretending otherwise. But when I put any of these goals ahead of God's purposes, I have always found that God has another card to play.

Jenny and I have benefited greatly from favor. It would be easy to think that the effort we have put into educating ourselves and the industrious plan we have implemented are what have resulted in our success. Of course, this is partly true. I think it is unlikely we would have received any of the results we have had if we had not taken these steps.

But these steps are not taken in a vacuum.

Our mentors in real estate have not taught these steps in a vacuum. Their lessons are prefaced with leading from a servant's heart, being grateful in all things, and demonstrating that by personal example when we are around them.

I want to take a closer look at the role favor has had in our success.

Lance Wallnau's definition of favor is:

*The attraction of God to you, that releases an influence through you, so other people are inclined to like, trust and cooperate with you in the assignment God gave you.*

That is really a mouthful.

I believe it is the centerpiece of our success.

Bill Johnson says there is a kind of confidence in the children of God who put a "demand on who he is as father." I do not take that to mean bossing God around. I take it to mean trusting His nature enough to ask, move, knock, risk, and expect Him to be good. Favor is not entitlement. Favor is confidence that grows out of relationship.

Of course there is nothing specific about real estate in that definition, or any other endeavor. It does not speak to a career or an inclination to do any particular thing. It speaks to the assignment God gave you. That assignment can change from day to day.

I want to be listening every day for what that assignment is. I hope that I am. I invite my Father to correct me. He does not really need my invitation, but I like the posture of being correctable.

A scripture that I have been aware of most of my life is:

You will see your teacher with your own eyes. Your own ears will hear him. Right behind you a voice will say, "This is the way you should go," whether to the right or to the left.

That speaks to being led by God.

We all have assignments. Perhaps many get ignored along the way. We should pay attention to the clues He gives us about our assignment. In the vocation Jenny and I have chosen as real estate investors, we have assignments. We also have assignments that have nothing to do with real estate. Sometimes we have assignments in the context of real estate that really have nothing to do with real estate.

One day I was looking to acquire a particular piece of property. Our normal approach, after identifying a property that looks inviting, is to go knock on the door.

"Let's see what happens."

Of course I am wanting to meet the owner and see if they are interested in selling. My approach is usually humble. I knock on the door and then step back to assess how I am going to be received. I have found that this definition of favor usually follows me in these circumstances.

On this particular day, my assignment was apparently not to buy real estate.

It was to meet and talk to the occupant of the house.

He did not own the house, but he was the executor of the will of his fiancee who had owned it. She had died and left him alone and despondent. He invited me to sit in his living room. In the course of our long conversation, I told him what our interest was, but I also listened to him tell his story. He let me encourage him and pray for God's blessing on him.

I never heard from him again.

But I believe the assignment did him good. Without God's favor, I would not have been well received. He would not have let me into his house and opened up. I do not know what happened to him, but I know that I fulfilled my assignment that day.

Sometimes my assignment leads to a purchase of a property.

Sometimes it leads to a conversation.

Sometimes it leads to prayer in a living room.

When you have a divine assignment, you can approach every situation with less fear about the outcome. Sometimes the outcome leads to profitable situations. Sometimes it does not. But if the assignment belongs to God, the outcome is not the only way to measure whether you succeeded.

That is where expectation has to be purified. I am not expecting every door to open just because I want it open. I am expecting the Father to be good, present, and purposeful. I am expecting Him to show me what is in my hand, and then I am responsible to steward it instead of burying it.

That is the cleanest way I know to say the lesson.

Favor is not the power to get my way.

Favor is grace to walk in His way.

Blessing

All things work together for the good to those who love the Lord.

Sometimes God has to get our attention.

Blessing is one of those words that can become vague if it is not attached to a real story. It can mean a feeling. It can mean money. It can mean a closed deal. It can mean relief from pressure.

But in this manuscript I am trying to use it more carefully.

Blessing is not merely that something good happened to us. Blessing is the evidence of God's hand arranging, correcting, opening, protecting, and teaching while we are walking out an assignment.

I believe we had unusual favor when we pursued the Red Quartz property.

Everything went very smoothly in the beginning. When the property came to our attention from all of the opportunities we were developing in the day-to-day activity of working our system, things moved quickly.

The foreclosure was filed on January 25. We found it on January 27. We sent our first letter to addresses that were not receiving mail on February 1. Our first text and email messages started on February 4. After no responses, I started searching on Facebook for the owner on February 6.

Nicholas responded right away.

So from the first day the file was recorded at the courthouse to our first conversation with the seller was twelve days.

Here is the flavor of the first Facebook exchange.

I introduced myself and told Nicholas I was interested in buying the property on Red Quartz in Raleigh. I told him I could pay cash quickly and rescue it from foreclosure. It would not cost him anything, and I would take care of the details so it was not trouble for him.

He responded that the house was already in foreclosure and going to the courts. It was locked up, and he could not get into it.

I told him I understood, but I could still work with it if he was willing.

I promised I could get him some money.

That short exchange led to a phone call, and I arranged to meet him at a place of his choosing the next day. He chose Chick-fil-A. It was a rainy day, and as it happened, his first, second, and third choices were all closed because of Covid protocols.

We ended up at the library.

There I talked to him and his wife, called his sister in Virginia, and reached an agreement. That included signing a contract and a deed and giving him some earnest money.

There were some hiccups along the way that I will not go into here, because all in all it was a smooth process to make a deal with the sellers.

God favored us so much that on the very first day we made contact with the sellers, we also reached an agreement with friends we had been talking to for months about a private money loan for $100,000. That was enough to purchase the property, bring the existing loan out of foreclosure, and completely rehab the property.

This was a pretty incredible trail of circumstances that had little to do with our engineering them.

We did our part.

But they worked because of God's favor.

I expect that everyone can imagine us doing all of these steps with no results. I can think of many times when similar steps bore no fruit. The letter goes out. The text goes out. The search is made. The seller never answers. The money is not available. The timing does not line up. The deal does not come together.

That is why I want to remind myself of the scripture from Paul.

All things work together for good to them that love God, to them who are called according to His purpose.

That scripture is not a promise that every transaction will close.

It is not a promise that every foreclosure file will become a profit.

It is not a promise that every seller will say yes.

It is a promise about God working in the lives of those who love Him and are called according to His purpose.

That means the blessing is not only the property.

The blessing is the way God weaves timing, people, resources, pressure, rain, closed restaurants, libraries, family conversations, private money, and a foreclosure deadline into something we could not have arranged by ourselves.

This was not the end of the Red Quartz story.

But it was enough to show us that blessing is often more than an event. It is a trail.

Providence Landing

Without any doubt, Providence Landing was the biggest project we have ever attempted.

It is a long story with lots of lessons, some unfinished.

To begin the story, I have to make a confession.

It is nothing I am proud of, but the story cannot be told without it.

Jenny and I were fighting about some aspects of the business. Our conversation had gotten heated, and I told her I was going to get away for a few days because I needed a break from our relationship. I hurriedly packed a few things in a bag, grabbed my computer, and headed out somewhere without a plan. I was clear headed enough to tell her I would be back, but that was about all I saw clearly.

That is not the way I would choose to begin the chapter if I were trying to make myself look good.

But the newer chapters have made me less interested in looking good and more interested in telling the truth.

Providence Landing did not begin with a clean strategic retreat. It began with frustration, poor relational leadership, and a man trying to get away from pressure instead of staying submitted to God and loving his wife well in the middle of it.

That matters because the name of the project is Providence Landing.

Providence is a beautiful word, but the beginning was not beautiful.

Pulling out of our driveway and our neighborhood, I allowed many thoughts to pass through my brain about what I was going to do. One thought that gelled was that I had been communicating, via Facebook Messenger, with a guy in Virginia who had about 80 acres for sale. I was frequently looking for acreage we could buy the way we buy houses. In other words, buy with terms we could easily afford.

What was I going to do with the property?

I do not know.

But one idea I had been thinking about for several months was to build an airpark. I will explain what that means later.

That was typical of the way my mind works.

Even in conflict, even while I was emotionally off balance, I was still thinking in terms of property, possibility, acreage, terms, and a project that might become something larger than a simple purchase. The systems mind does not turn off just because the heart is unsettled.

That can be a gift.

It can also be dangerous.

If the gift is submitted, God can use it. If it is not submitted, it can become a way to outrun repentance.

I set my sights on Virginia and started trying to reach the property owner. Looking back at the text, there were some GPS coordinates. I will not bore you with the details of how I found the property, but that information was not sufficient. I did find it, and I talked to the owner, who gave me a tour of what he owned.

It had been pouring rain the whole day since I left Raleigh. It had let up some as the owner drove me around his property. I was attracted to it, and the price was not bad, but I concluded quickly that it was not going to serve the purpose of an airpark. I thanked him and left the area as the rain intensified.

That is a small detail, but it belongs in the story.

I had driven away from my house in a storm inside and found myself driving through a storm outside. I went looking for land, but the first property was not the answer. The price was not bad. The acreage had appeal. But it was not the assignment.

Sometimes God protects us not only from accidents, but from the wrong opportunity.

That is harder to recognize because the wrong opportunity can still look interesting.

Driving around aimlessly in fierce storms, I decided to find a hotel in South Boston, Virginia. I got some groceries and went to the hotel to get out of my wet clothes, take a shower, and relax. My attitude was horrible. My disposition was depressed. My energy level was low. I had not treated my wife well, and there was really nothing about my response that I was proud of.

I was just going through the motions of what I knew to do.

I went to bed that way and woke up that way.

After a morning shower and breakfast, I fell back on the bed and looked up at the ceiling, expecting to see God there.

Going through the motions sometimes works.

My lifetime habit is to look to God even when I am off track with His best expectations for my life. I looked at the ceiling and said out loud, "God, I do not know if You can speak to me, but I am listening."

Hearing nothing, after a few minutes, I reengaged with the motions I was going through. I walked over to my laptop and started searching for acreage in southern Virginia.

That is where this part of the story pauses for now.

But even the pause matters.

There are moments when the assignment begins before the property is found. There are moments when the story begins in a fight, in a storm, in a hotel room, with a wet bag of clothes and a man looking at the ceiling because he knows enough to look toward God even when he does not know how to fix himself.

That is not a clean beginning.

But many assignments do not start clean.

What I see now is that Providence Landing belongs here because it presses the same question into a larger scale before some of the later property stories unfold.

With Red Quartz, blessing looked like timing, favor, and provision coming together around a foreclosure deadline.

With Providence Landing, the issue was deeper.

Could I hear God after I had mishandled conflict?

Could I recognize assignment when my first movement had not been obedience but escape?

Could God still speak to a man who was wet, tired, discouraged, and not proud of himself?

That is why this chapter cannot be only about acreage or an airpark. It has to be about mercy at the beginning of an assignment. It has to be about the way God can meet a person in the middle of poor leadership and still draw him forward without pretending the poor leadership did not happen.

Providence does not mean I made every step correctly.

It means God was still there, still governing, still correcting, and still able to lead.

That is grace.

And if Providence Landing became the biggest project we have attempted, then the beginning of it should make me humble, not proud.

Assignment

Here is another example of an opportunity we had. I think it illustrates both favor on our lives and the way God directs our paths.

Assignment is where the earlier themes start to become practical.

It is one thing to believe God protects me. It is another thing to believe He has been forming my skills. It is another thing to confess that leadership has to come under His lordship. But assignment asks a more immediate question.

What is God asking me to do right now?

Not what deal do I want.

Not what plan proves I was right.

Not what outcome makes the spreadsheet look best.

What is the assignment?

I definitely believe I heard a voice behind me letting me know which way to turn as I put one foot in front of another working through this assignment. I remember declaring that the property owner would be receptive to what I had to say.

I believe he was.

Let me first give some context.

We have used the same basic strategy for getting leads from the beginning. We collect foreclosure files from the courthouse and approach sellers before they lose their house. We try to identify HOA foreclosures and exclude them from our list as being unprofitable.

Sometimes we do not realize they are HOA foreclosures and contact them anyway.

Recently, someone answered our campaign concerning his house. After I spoke to him, I requested the file from the courthouse and realized the source was an HOA, and that he owed less than $4,000.

Our mentality was simple.

No one is going to let a house go to foreclosure over $4,000.

Therefore, there is no need to pursue it.

But this time something got my attention, and I started looking to see how I could structure an appealing offer to the seller that would also be in our interest.

I did not yet know the details of his personal situation. Everyone's situation is different. I would come to know some of those details later, but before I knocked on his door, I considered a framework for a deal.

A substantial cash offer for the deed.

Take his mortgage subject to.

Pay off the foreclosure lien.

Allow the owner to stay in the house and pay rent equal to his mortgage, taxes, insurance, and HOA cost.

That was the outline in my head.

I arranged to meet the homeowner. At this point I had sent him sixteen letters that he had not responded to. However, I had just started a text campaign, and he had received three text messages from me that he did respond to. Even though it was an HOA foreclosure, one that I normally would never have considered, I now had an assignment to go and talk to him.

I found him to be very cordial and inviting. He showed me through his house, and then we started talking. I asked him if he wanted to sell his house. He started talking about big ideas. I did not really think they were reality-based, but I listened. He wanted to sell this house and get a bigger one with a yard for his kids. He showed me the small, crowded, fenced-in backyard. I nodded to it all.

Then I asked him a different question.

If I could find a way to remove his difficulties and let them stay in the house, would he be interested?

I could see his interest perk up.

So I started finding out what I needed to know. I asked him for a current mortgage statement. He brought one out and showed me a current, reasonably small balance. I was intrigued, so I started outlining the structure of an offer. Then I asked him what amount of cash he would need for him to sell me the house.

Sellers really never fully understand this kind of offer at first.

They are looking for a bottom-line number. He thought his house was worth something like $285,000, which I was never going to offer him. But through the conversation I began to explain and help him see how my offer worked for him.

There is no need to give a seller a lot of details at first that are just going to get lost in the haze. I explain them as he begins to grasp what I am offering and ask questions.

He did not immediately come up with the amount of cash that would make him want to accept the deal. So I gave him a low figure. I could see the margins on the house gave me plenty of wiggle room. I asked him to consider $10,000.

He started relating how someone else had made him an offer, and he started talking about his credit card balance and car payments. I came to realize that his other offer was really an offer to refinance his debt. I quickly reconsidered what I would offer. I rolled those balances into one cash offer of $40,000.

He seemed interested.

He said he could sell the house for more.

I agreed.

I told him he could sell the house for more, but then he would have to move, and he would pay just as much for the next house. He could probably sell the house easily, but in this market he would not as easily buy his next house. He would have to move immediately.

I looked over at his wife, who was listening, and asked, "Do you really want to move?"

Again, I outlined that he could pay off all of his debt and have the reduced payments to live on. He would have extra cash to use for whatever he wanted. His family would not be disrupted with finding a new house and moving during the school year. And he would be out of trouble with the HOA.

Then I thought to ask him what he made in a year.

He answered that he made $42,000 per year.

I said, "I am offering you a year's salary and allowing you to stay in your house as long as you want to."

That is the flavor of that appointment.

At this point, I have not heard back from him. But I went back and made some small changes to our project management spreadsheet to make a better analysis of this strategy. Once I had spent an hour on it, I realized I had discovered a new gold mine.

This was going to open up a great new source of leads with huge profit potential.

There would be some homeowners who would find this offer very tempting. What it provided for us was the ability to get into a new tenant-occupied property that was going up, currently, 20 percent per year. These properties need no rehab, so there would only be the immediate expense of the HOA foreclosure redemption and the cash offer. We were not going to have positive cash flow, but we had an immediate amount of equity that was several times bigger than our investment. We could then borrow that equity to do another deal. The real kicker was that we were gaining the amount of equity that the property appreciated every year.

And we had a tenant who treated the property like his own home.

My spreadsheet showed the current profit matrix on this property to be:

- 12 months = $199,078 - 24 months = $257,798 - 36 months = $316,510

We would also have the immediate ability to borrow $103,500 on the property to finance other deals.

This assignment may not have been about this homeowner.

Rather, it may have been to open our eyes to other deals we were letting pass by.

There is so much room in this structure for profitability that we can be flexible with the next homeowner. We can be flexible with the amount of cash offered. We could lower the rent below the current mortgage payment because we can make up for it with the new loan that we could get. There is even the possibility of a lease-to-purchase contract allowing the seller to buy the property back in the future.

Somewhere soon, we will find a property and homeowner who consider this to be a big win for themselves.

That is what assignment can do.

It can make you knock on a door you would normally ignore.

It can make you look again at a category you already dismissed.

It can turn a $4,000 HOA problem into a new way of seeing the business.

115 Rosebrooks

There is a kind of favor that feels wonderful when it first arrives.

Doors open. People respond. A complicated thing becomes simple. A distressed property becomes an assignment. You can look back and say, "That was God."

But favor is not just about getting a deal.

Favor is the attraction of God to you that releases an influence through you so other people are inclined to like, trust, and cooperate with you in the assignment God gave you. I still believe that. But I have also learned that the assignment does not always end when the paperwork is signed.

Sometimes the assignment continues after the blessing becomes complicated.

Sometimes it continues when the person you helped later becomes the person whose name is on the other side of the lawsuit.

That is part of what 115 Rosebrooks became for us.

This is also where the leadership chapter begins to press on the property story.

It is one thing to lead when everyone agrees that you helped. It is another thing to lead your own heart when your motives are questioned, your paperwork is challenged, your wife is hurt, your name is attached to a complaint, and your first instinct is to defend yourself with every argument available.

In that kind of pressure, leadership is not mainly about directing other people.

It is about whether I can remain submitted to God while I am being accused.

The property was in Cary. The owner, Brynda Griffith, was in foreclosure. Bank of America had started foreclosure proceedings in September of 2023. She had received notices. She understood enough to know she could lose the house if something did not happen.

We were working the same system we had worked many times before. We monitor foreclosure filings, primarily in Wake and Johnston County, looking for opportunities where we may be able to buy a property and also solve a problem. We sent letters. She received letters from others too. Later the record would say she called us because our letters seemed the most professional.

I do not know how to make that sound more spiritual than it is.

We sent letters.

She called.

That is often how favor looks from the outside.

On October 28, 2023, Brynda called me. We spoke briefly and arranged to meet. The next day I went to the property. She did not want to talk in front of the neighbors, so we went somewhere nearby and talked through the situation.

I asked what I usually ask.

What do you need from me?

Do you want to sell?

Do you want to move?

Do you need something else?

She did not have the money to stop the foreclosure. She did not have anyone stepping in to help her. She did not have somewhere else she wanted to go.

So the structure that emerged was a sale and leaseback. Buy Your Home would buy the property for the amount of the debt, and she could stay in the home at a very low rent. The rent was tied to the annual insurance premium. The lease had an initial five-year term and was renewable by her. In plain language, the intent was that she could stay as long as she wanted, if that is what she wanted.

That was the generosity inside the structure.

On October 31, 2023, we went over the paperwork. The documents included the purchase agreement, the lease, a seller acknowledgement, and a limited power of attorney so I could communicate with Bank of America if needed to stop the foreclosure. The seller acknowledgement said the transaction was a sale and not a loan. It said she had been advised to seek independent legal and financial counsel. It said she understood she remained liable on the mortgage until the debt was paid in full and that a default could still subject her to foreclosure. It said she was satisfied with the agreement.

I know paperwork does not make the heart pure.

I know it does not answer every moral question.

But I also know graciousness without clarity can become confusion later. If you are going to do business in distressed situations, you must learn to be both compassionate and clear.

That is a hard balance.

On November 1, 2023, the Wake County clerk authorized the foreclosure sale. On November 15, I paid Bank of America $6,266.78 to cure the arrears. I did not have to use the power of attorney to do it. On November 16, Bank of America filed notice that the foreclosure would not proceed.

The house was saved from foreclosure.

That sentence should have felt like the end of the story.

It was not.

For the next year, Brynda continued living in the home. We did more than own the house. Jenny and I visited. I drove Brynda to medical appointments. I helped with groceries. I helped with technology. We checked on her. I believed we were doing what we had said we would do. I believed we had made a business decision that also carried mercy inside of it.

This is one of the tensions of being a gracious millionaire.

If you are poor and generous, people may call you kind.

If you are prosperous and generous, people may ask what you are getting out of it.

Sometimes that question is fair. Sometimes it is wise. Money gives people power, and power has to be examined. But the existence of profit does not automatically cancel the existence of love. A business can make money and still help someone. A transaction can create equity and still keep a person from losing everything. A deal can be profitable and still be merciful.

The religious mind often wants those categories separated. Either it is charity or it is business. Either it is ministry or it is investment. Either it is love or it is profit.

I do not think the Kingdom is that small.

Stewardship is the proper use of what God has given us in the way He would have us use it. He has not given us possessions only for our own good, but for work we can do for others. If that is true, then the question is not whether money is involved. The question is whether Jesus is Lord over the money.

That question became much harder when the lawsuit came.

In September of 2024, Brynda's daughter came to North Carolina. Shortly after that, Brynda was taken to Maryland. The next communication was not gratitude or a question or a conversation. It was a summons and complaint.

The lawsuit tried to unwind the transaction. It claimed fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, unfair and deceptive trade practices, undue influence, and lack of mental capacity. At first, the daughter had herself appointed guardian ad litem. That order was later set aside. Brynda then proceeded on her own behalf.

I am not writing this as a legal argument. Lawyers can do that better than I can. The legal record has its own language and its own rules. I am writing this because the spiritual pressure of the moment matters to the story of this book.

I had to ask myself what graciousness looks like when it is accused.

It is easy to be gracious when the other person is thankful. It is easy to talk about favor when the deal works and everybody smiles. It is much harder when the story is retold in a way that makes your help sound predatory. It is much harder when the person who received the benefit is now the plaintiff. It is much harder when your wife is hurt because she knows the groceries, the rides, the visits, and the care that do not show up neatly in a complaint.

Jenny said, in substance, that we had helped this woman in a big way.

That was how it felt to us.

But feelings are not enough either. Being hurt does not make us right. Being accused does not make us innocent. Being generous does not mean we never need correction. I have tried to keep my heart open to that. I have invited my Father to correct me before, and I have to keep inviting Him when the correction would be expensive or embarrassing.

Still, I also had to resist the temptation to let bitterness write the next chapter.

There was a moment when our attorney talked through the economic side of settlement. Maybe the practical answer was to buy out the lease and stop the bleeding. Maybe the principled answer was to fight. Maybe those two roads could not be walked at the same time. That was hard for me because I wanted the best possible settlement, but I also knew that if a reasonable settlement did not come, I was prepared to go to the wall.

That is not always faith.

Sometimes it is anger wearing a good suit.

So I had to sit with Psalm 37.

Do not fret.

Trust in the Lord and do good.

Commit your way to Him.

Rest in the Lord.

Cease from anger.

The steps of a good man are ordered by the Lord.

The righteous shows mercy and gives.

Those words are easy until you are paying lawyers.

Those words are easy until a public lawsuit is attached to your name.

Those words are easy until you are tempted to explain yourself to people who have already decided what kind of person you are.

But the Kingdom does not stop being true because the assignment becomes painful.

In fact, this may be where the theme of this book becomes more honest. A gracious millionaire is not just someone who gets rich and stays nice. That is too thin. A gracious millionaire is someone who lets money remain under lordship even when money is being used as evidence against him. He does not let prosperity become entitlement. He does not let accusation become hatred. He does not let the need to win replace the need to remain faithful.

I wish I could say I have done that perfectly.

I have not.

But I can say that 115 Rosebrooks has forced the question deeper into me. Do I want to be vindicated more than I want to be obedient? Do I want to be right more than I want to be clean? Do I want the property, the profit, the reputation, or the peace of knowing that I stayed under the hand of God?

The answer is supposed to be obvious.

It is not always obvious when the pressure is real.

That is why abundance is dangerous. The blessings of the Lord can breed entitlement in the people of God if they do not remain humble and practice discipline. The more breakthrough we get, the more opposition we get. Jesus promised a hundredfold, but He also said, with persecution.

I used to read that as a warning.

Now I read it as part of the contract.

The lesson of 115 Rosebrooks is not finished. The legal story may still have more pages. The financial result may still change. There may be things we learn that we do not yet know. But I know the chapter belongs in this book because it shows the part of graciousness that does not feel gracious.

It shows that favor is not a formula.

It shows that generosity has to be governed by wisdom.

It shows that paperwork matters, clarity matters, and compassion matters.

It shows that profit is not proof of guilt and kindness is not proof of wisdom.

It shows that when the enemy comes in like a flood, the Spirit of the Lord lifts up a standard. Sometimes that standard is not a dramatic victory. Sometimes it is a quiet refusal to complain. Sometimes it is continuing to trust God with your name, your money, your reputation, and your unfinished story.

I still believe we were assigned to 115 Rosebrooks.

I also believe the assignment was not only to buy a house.

It was to learn what kind of heart can be trusted with houses.

The Orphaned Property

Some properties come to us because someone is in trouble.

Some come because a seller raises a hand.

And some seem to sit there quietly, almost hidden, waiting for someone to notice that there is still a story attached to them.

This chapter sits differently now that the leadership theme is in the manuscript.

At first glance, this is a chapter about creative real estate. There is a vacant house, a deceased owner, an heir search, a quitclaim deed, a low-interest loan, a contractor who becomes a tenant, another foreclosure property, and a possible trade involving other assets. All of that is real.

But underneath it is the same question.

Can I follow God's assignment without forcing my own preference, especially when Jenny and I do not yet see the opportunity the same way?

That is how the Tensity property felt to me.

It was a nice two-story single-family house near our residence. We found it in foreclosure in early 2025. The owner had died about fourteen months earlier, and the house was vacant. Nobody seemed very interested in it. We were having trouble finding any heirs.

That is a strange kind of opportunity.

In real estate, vacancy is often treated as a condition. The grass is high. The mail piles up. The bank has started a process. The property shows up on a list. It can be analyzed, mailed, skipped, tracked, or pursued.

But behind the vacancy is a life that ended.

Behind the foreclosure file is a family line, a history, and sometimes a set of people who do not even know they are connected to the house anymore.

We started with what we had. I connected with the person listed as the informant on the death certificate. They invited me into their home to talk. The owner's mother had also lived in the house and had died a couple of years earlier. The informant knew both of them, but was not aware of another heir.

So we started a deeper search.

We found several possible distant heirs in Texas. One of them finally answered our inquiries. After a while of negotiation, that heir agreed to quitclaim the deed to us for $5,000.

The property did not have a lot of debt. The bank needed about $15,000 to reinstate the loan. For about $20,000 up front and a 3.75 percent loan, we owned the property.

That is the kind of sentence that can sound too simple if you read it too quickly.

For about $20,000 up front, we owned a house.

But there was a lot inside that sentence. There was a foreclosure search. There was a death certificate. There was a living room conversation with someone who knew the deceased owner. There was a search for heirs. There were inquiries that were not answered. There was one person in Texas who finally responded. There was negotiation. There was a bank reinstatement. There was risk. There was uncertainty. There was a vacant house that had to be brought back into useful condition.

This is often how favor looks in our business.

It does not always look like a trumpet from heaven. Sometimes it looks like enough information to take the next step. Sometimes it looks like an invitation into someone's home. Sometimes it looks like one heir answering when several others do not. Sometimes it looks like a loan still being assumable at 3.75 percent when money in the larger economy is far more expensive.

By June, we had the property ready to rent. We found a good tenant, and the property was cash flowing about $700 per month.

Actually, the tenant found us.

He was our flooring contractor. He had put the new floor in the house. While he was working there, he asked whether we were going to rent it. We never had to put it on the market.

That is another thing I have learned to pay attention to.

There are times when the next answer is already standing in the room. You may be thinking about marketing, advertising, showings, applications, and all the normal steps. Then the person installing the floor asks a question, and suddenly the next piece is sitting there in work boots.

I do not want to over-spiritualize that.

But I also do not want to under-spiritualize it.

If favor is the attraction of God to you that releases an influence through you so other people are inclined to like, trust, and cooperate with you in the assignment God gave you, then sometimes favor is practical. It is not only a feeling. It becomes rent paid on time. It becomes a contractor becoming a tenant. It becomes a property cash flowing before it ever goes on the open market.

While we were getting Tensity ready to rent, another seller responded to our marketing.

That became the Rose Place property.

The sellers met with me in their home and agreed to sell me their property for $10,000 down, a $20,000 note due in twelve months, and subject to the existing mortgage. They were motivated because they were about to lose the property to foreclosure.

So now we had two connected stories.

Tensity was the orphaned property we found through a death, a foreclosure, and a search for heirs.

Rose Place was the distressed property that came through our normal marketing.

One came through a deep search.

One came because a seller responded.

Both required action before the opportunity disappeared.

We had a lot of projects going at the time, so we did not get a good start on rehabbing Rose Place. That is part of the honest story too. Opportunity does not arrive according to our capacity. Sometimes it comes when we are already stretched. Sometimes the thing we just bought has to wait because other projects, other pressures, and other obligations are already consuming the calendar.

Meanwhile, our Tensity tenant was expressing interest in buying the Tensity property.

That created a tension.

Tensity was a great rental. We were not eager to sell it. A house with a low-interest loan and about $700 per month of cash flow is not something you rush to give up. It felt like one of those properties that could sit quietly in the portfolio and do its work for years.

But business decisions do not happen in isolation.

It took us nearly a year to get Rose Place ready to sell. The $20,000 note was about to come due. We were also feeling financial pressure from the lawsuit.

So we looked at the situation and concluded that we needed to sell Tensity for cash.

That was not what we wanted.

Our tenant would need seller financing, so he would not be able to buy Tensity in the way we needed to sell it. If we sold Tensity for cash, he would have to move.

That is the point where a spreadsheet cannot tell the whole story.

A spreadsheet can show equity, cash flow, payments, interest rates, deposits, projected profits, and return on investment. I love spreadsheets. I have built our project management spreadsheet around the way we buy, remodel, track, and sell properties. But the spreadsheet does not feel the weight of telling a good tenant that he may have to move.

And the spreadsheet does not always see the third option until God puts it in your mind.

God put it in my mind to consider selling Rose Place to him on a contract for deed basis.

That changed the problem.

Instead of selling Tensity and displacing the tenant, maybe we could move the tenant into Rose Place as a buyer. Instead of losing a good tenant relationship, maybe we could create a new buyer relationship. Instead of solving only our cash problem, maybe we could solve his housing problem too.

I came up with a profitable proposal to sell Rose Place to the Tensity tenant. We finished the transaction less than thirty days before his lease was up and before the $20,000 note was due to the Rose Place seller.

That timing matters.

Less than thirty days.

Sometimes favor shows up as an open door. Sometimes it shows up as a conversation. Sometimes it shows up as timing so close that you know you could not have controlled it by your own intelligence.

The contract for deed sale solved several things at once. It gave the tenant a path to ownership. It relieved the pressure of his need to move. It helped us address the note that was coming due. It freed up Tensity to sell. Then we began preparing to market Tensity.

That is the status at the time I am writing this.

But the story did not stop there.

People began contacting us from Zillow. One person presented a full-price offer with terms that were interesting. That was worth considering.

But what caught my attention even more was another possibility that came through relationship.

Our tenants referred someone to us. They had friends from church who wanted to buy Tensity. Maria, the wife, told me her sister and brother-in-law wanted to buy it, and that she was praying that it would be possible.

I have learned to listen when prayer enters the business conversation.

That does not mean every prayed-over deal is the right deal. People pray about things that do not happen. We pray about things we do not yet understand. Prayer does not remove the need for wisdom, underwriting, paperwork, and clear terms.

But prayer does change the posture.

My expectation was that they would not be able to present a cash offer, but I was glad to talk to them. Through broken English, Maria told me they had a trailer to trade.

That made me think there might at least be a possibility of a deal we could consider.

I set up a phone call with the husband to talk about selling them the Tensity property. Through an interpreter, he let me know that they had three trailers they would be willing to trade for the property. I asked for information about the assets and let them know I would go see them and consider what I could propose as an offer.

That brought Jenny and me to an interesting pivot point.

It also exposed a difference in how we think.

Jenny does not like to consider acquiring those kinds of lower-end properties. She does not want to try to market them for sale or own them as rentals. I understand that. There is a kind of property that can look like trouble before it looks like opportunity. It can bring repair issues, tenant issues, title issues, management issues, and distraction.

I tend to want to consider anything that gives us out-of-the-box thinking and higher upside.

That difference between us is not a small thing.

It is one thing to say that a husband and wife should walk in unity. It is another thing to sit with a live opportunity, a cash need, a set of assets you have not inspected yet, a spreadsheet you know you have to redesign, and a wife who is not looking favorably on the trade.

About a month before this, Jenny and I had begun to meet on the couch first thing every morning for devotions and prayer. We pray about everything we are involved in or concerned with. We consider what God wants us to pay attention to.

That habit matters to this story.

It would be easy to make this chapter about a clever transaction. It would be easy to focus on the orphaned property, the quitclaim deed, the low-interest loan, the contractor tenant, the contract for deed, the possible trailer trade, and the profit projection.

Those are all real parts of the story.

But the deeper question is not only whether the numbers work.

The deeper question is whether Jenny and I can hear God together while the numbers are moving.

One of the things we pray about is that Jenny and I come to unity in our thinking and our decisions. At this point, Jenny is not looking very favorably on the trade. We do need the cash. Those two things are both true.

That is often where lordship becomes measurable.

Not in the part where I say I trust God.

In the part where I have an idea I like, my wife has concerns, the business has pressure, and the opportunity may disappear if we move too slowly.

What do I do then?

Do I push?

Do I manipulate?

Do I use spiritual language to make my preference sound like revelation?

Or do I slow down enough to let God give us unity?

I cannot say the lesson is complete because the story is still in progress. At this moment, what I wanted to analyze was the possibility of selling Tensity on a contract for deed, taking title to the three trailers and some cash as part of the contract.

That would require changes to our project spreadsheet. I needed a whole new aspect added to accept another asset as part of the sales package. If we traded for those assets, the newly designed spreadsheet could show how profitable each separate asset might be if we also sold each of them with a contract for deed and a significant cash deposit.

I may need to change the numbers as I refine the process and the offer. At this moment, it looks like this could turn the transaction from around $150,000 to around $350,000.

That is a large difference.

But a large number is not automatically a green light.

This is one of the central tensions of this book. Abundance can be an invitation, but it can also be a test. Opportunity can come from God, but so can correction. A creative structure can be stewardship, or it can be striving. A spreadsheet can reveal profit, but it cannot reveal peace.

So I am watching this one carefully.

Maybe the better deal is the full-price offer with terms.

Maybe the better deal is the trade.

Maybe the better lesson is not the deal at all, but the way Jenny and I learn to pray, listen, disagree honestly, and wait for unity.

Maybe this trail of transactions will continue for several more generations past the orphaned property that we bought when no one else seemed to know it could be bought.

That thought captures me.

One vacant house. One deceased owner. One informant. One distant heir in Texas. One quitclaim deed. One flooring contractor who became a tenant. One seller on Rose Place trying to avoid foreclosure. One contract for deed. One family from church praying for a way to buy. Three trailers that might become the next link in the chain.

Real estate is never only dirt and buildings.

It is people, pressure, prayer, paperwork, timing, and stewardship.

It is also assignment.

And if this is assignment, then the question is not merely how much money can be made.

The question is what kind of people we become while handling the opportunity.

Providence Landing: The Airfield

I thought I was looking for vacant land.

That is how this next part of the Providence Landing story began. I was in South Boston, Virginia, about twenty-five miles from the hotel, driving rural back roads and looking at acreage. I was not looking for a subdivision. I was not looking for an airport. I was just looking for land.

The idea in my mind was still the airpark. I did not have all the details worked out. I did not know exactly how it would be structured or financed or managed. I only knew that I had been thinking about a place where people could live with airplanes the way some people live around a golf course or a lake. I had enough of the idea to recognize that normal vacant land would not be enough. It needed room. It needed access. It needed a setting that could carry something unusual.

So I searched.

I found several large rural tracts that looked like they might be worth seeing. They were scattered out from the hotel, and the roads to reach them were the kind of roads where you are not exactly sure whether you are still following a sensible plan or just obeying the next instruction on the phone. I put one of the properties into GPS and started driving.

The phone told me I was getting close. It showed that the destination was about half a mile ahead on the left.

Before I reached it, I passed something on the right that did not match the normal scenery.

There was an obvious area of development. Nice horse pastures. White picket fences. Order. Care. It stood out from the rural land around it. A quarter mile farther, I passed a paved driveway. Behind the driveway was a thicket, and then, almost suddenly, the airfield revealed itself.

There was a windsock.

There were small commercial buildings.

There was an airplane hangar.

There were planes.

The property I had put into the GPS was still farther ahead. The listing had not said anything about a runway. As far as I could tell, the listed land and this airfield were disconnected properties, not even on the same side of the road. But I had already passed the driveway, and something in me knew I needed to turn around.

So I backed the truck up, turned in, and drove down the paved drive.

I was cautious. I did not know if I was trespassing. I did not know who owned it. I did not know whether anyone would be irritated by a stranger pulling in. But this was not completely foreign to me. A lot of our real estate life has involved knocking on doors of houses we wanted to buy. I knew the posture. Pull in carefully. Step out carefully. Do not act entitled. Do not assume you have a right to be there. Stand still for a moment. Look around. Let the situation tell you how to approach it.

I parked in a parking space, stepped out of the truck, and waited.

A lady came out of what looked like a house and asked if she could help me.

I told her the truth. I said I was driving through the area looking for land where I might build an airpark, and I wondered if I could talk to the owner about how this one was set up.

She introduced herself as Joan.

Then she said it was for sale.

That was the sentence that changed the day.

Her husband was not home, but she said I could call him. We exchanged numbers. I thanked her and left.

I drove away overwhelmed. It felt like I had done what I came to do, even though I had not known what I came to do until after it happened. I had not bought anything. I had not negotiated anything. I had not even spoken with the owner yet. But I had found something I did not know I was looking for, and I had found it on the way to a different property.

That is one of the strange things about assignment.

Sometimes the GPS says the destination is on the left, but the assignment is on the right.

I started heading home. I had sketchy cell service, and before I had gone even half an hour down the road, Robbie called me. He was Joan's husband. We had enough reception to talk some, and I got the first layer of details. He asked me to call again when I got home.

That phone call began a long, expensive, educational relationship with the owners of what became Providence Landing.

The property was about three hundred acres. It was not merely a runway and a few buildings. It had hundreds of subdivided lots ready to build. It had a level of work already done that I could not have imagined creating from scratch. Roads, lots, development history, an aviation identity, and the bones of a community were already there.

That is part of why the name came so naturally.

We named it Providence Landing because it seemed provided by God.

I do not want to flatten that sentence into a slogan. At the time, it really did feel like providence. I had left home badly. I had driven through storms. I had looked at one property that was not the assignment. I had woken up in a hotel room asking God if He could speak to me. Then, while looking for vacant land, I found an airfield that was already for sale and already shaped like the dream that had been forming in my mind.

It felt provided.

Maybe it was.

But one of the things I am learning is that provision does not always mean the result will be what I wanted. God can provide a test. He can provide a classroom. He can provide an opportunity that exposes my motives, my leadership, my risk tolerance, my need for counsel, my need for unity with Jenny, and my ability to recognize red flags.

Providence Landing became all of that.

Robbie and Joan had already invested years into the place. They had stories, plans, documents, lots, maps, dreams, and a history I would have to understand. I began learning what had been done and what had not been done. I began imagining what could be done. I began to see the project not as a piece of rural land, but as a platform.

That is where abundance becomes dangerous if it is not submitted.

Three hundred acres can do something to your imagination. A runway can do something to your imagination. Hundreds of subdivided lots can do something to your imagination. The possibility of building a community around aviation can make the future feel bigger than your current capacity.

I could see it.

I could also feel the weight of it.

Jenny and I saw red flags. That needs to be said plainly. We were not blind to every concern. There were things about the opportunity, the history, the financing, the relationships, and the path forward that gave us pause. We saw enough to know this was not simple.

But we proceeded.

We committed time.

We committed effort.

We committed money.

We also connected with a nationwide organization that presented itself as a mentoring group for investors who wanted to raise large amounts of money. That was part of the education too. When you are looking at something as large as Providence Landing, normal deal thinking does not feel large enough. You start looking for people who say they understand scale. You start listening to people who speak the language of capital, investor presentations, syndication, raising money, and big projects.

Some of that was helpful.

Some of it was costly.

Some of it should have been filtered more carefully.

That is not me trying to make the story sound worse than it was. It is me trying to tell it truthfully. Providence Landing was not only a story about finding an airfield. It became a story about what happens when a God-given imagination meets a project large enough to flatter it.

The name was sincere.

The dream was sincere.

The desire to build something meaningful was sincere.

But sincerity does not remove the need for wisdom.

I think about that first day often. I think about the phone saying the property was ahead on the left. I think about the development appearing on the right. I think about the driveway, the thicket, the windsock, the hangar, the planes, Joan stepping out and asking if she could help me. I think about how easy it would have been to keep driving, look at the listed vacant land, and miss the thing that became one of the largest classrooms of our lives.

I also think about the fact that finding something does not mean you understand it.

Discovery is not the same as stewardship.

Opportunity is not the same as assignment completed.

Provision is not the same as permission to ignore warning signs.

This is why the Providence Landing story cannot be told only as a miracle. It has miracle in it. I still believe that. But it also has correction in it. It has cost in it. It has unfinished lessons in it.

Maybe God provided Providence Landing.

Maybe He did.

But the result was not what I wanted. At least not in the way I wanted it, and not in the timing I imagined when I first drove away from that airfield overwhelmed by possibility.

That does not mean God was absent.

It means the story is not finished.

It also means that grace is not only found in the open door. Sometimes grace is found later, when you are honest enough to admit that the open door led you into a room where you still had to grow.

Providence Landing: An Editor's Business Review

Before I leave Providence Landing, I want to do something a little different.

Most of this book is written from inside my own memory. I am telling you what I saw, what I felt, what I thought God was doing, where I was wrong, where I was learning, and where the story is still working on me. That is the right way for most of the book to be written because this is not a detached business case study. It is a testimony.

But Providence Landing became so large, and the documents around it became so layered, that I asked my editor, Jean, to review the Providence Landing materials from the outside and give me a business summary.

I wanted another set of eyes on it.

Not to replace the spiritual story.

Not to explain away the sense of providence I felt when I first saw that airfield.

But to look at the documents, the pitch materials, the survey, the marketing folder, the investor language, and the scale of what we were trying to build, and then tell me what the story looks like from a business perspective.

That matters because Providence Landing was not only a moment of discovery. It became a plan. It became a pitch. It became a team. It became a capital raise. It became a vision we were trying to make legible to other people.

When a dream moves from your heart into documents, spreadsheets, brochures, investor decks, and market research, it starts telling a second story.

This is Jean's summary of that second story.

### Jean's Business Review

From the outside, Providence Landing does not look like a simple real estate opportunity.

It looks like a vision that grew into a business model before it had been fully tested by the weight of execution.

That is the first thing I see.

The discovery story is strong. Wes was driving in rural Virginia looking for land where he might one day build an airpark. He was not looking for an existing aviation community. The property he had mapped was not the airfield. It was on the other side of the road. Then, before he reached the listed property, he saw the signs that something unusual was already there: order, fencing, pasture, buildings, a runway, aircraft, a hangar, and a windsock.

From a narrative point of view, that is almost too clean to ignore.

Wes was aiming at one target and passed the real one on the way.

From a business point of view, the discovery was also meaningful. This was not raw land that would require every entitlement, every road, every lot, every aviation element, and every market proof to be created from nothing. Robie and Joan Potts had already done years of work. They had developed the airfield, subdivided the property, established an aviation identity, and created something that could be recognized as more than acreage.

That matters.

Vision is cheaper when it exists only in the imagination. Providence Landing had physical evidence. It had land, a runway, lots, buildings, history, and owners who had already shaped the property around aviation. That gave Wes something real to respond to.

It also made the temptation stronger.

The email archive makes the first business pressure more concrete. Before Providence Landing became a pitch deck, Wes was already comparing the seller's list to the map and finding mismatches. Some lots appeared sold on one source but not another. Some appeared available on the map while the list showed them belonging to Robie or Joan. Then the acreage questions showed up too: 15D, 16D, 18D, 7F, 10F, and 11F all had map acreage materially higher than the seller list.

That is important because it keeps the story honest.

The first warning signs were not abstract. They were not only feelings in Jenny's stomach or vague concerns about scale. They were ordinary due-diligence problems: ownership status, lot identity, acreage, roads, and what exactly was being bought.

The business materials show how quickly the opportunity expanded anyway. In the early telling, Providence Landing can sound like a man finding an airfield while looking for land. In the marketing folder, it becomes a much larger concept: a pilot-centered residential and recreational airpark, hundreds of acres, buildable lots, hangar homes, short-term rentals, RV sites, fly-ins, hunting, equestrian uses, events, retreats, weddings, catering, fuel, tie-downs, and the possibility of a longer future runway.

The documents were not merely selling land.

They were selling a destination.

That is the strongest business idea in the file.

Pilots do not fly somewhere only because there is a runway. They fly somewhere because there is a reason to land. The marketing materials understood that. They tried to make Providence Landing into a place where aviation, lodging, food, recreation, community, and mission could meet.

The old aviation idea of the hundred-dollar hamburger became more than a phrase. It became part of the economic logic. A pilot might fly in for a meal, but if the place worked, he might also stay overnight, attend an event, bring friends, buy a lot, build a hangar home, or return for a retreat.

That is a compelling concept.

It is also a complicated one.

The more revenue streams a plan includes, the easier it is to make the spreadsheet look exciting. Lots can be sold. Homes can be built. Rentals can be booked. Events can be hosted. RV sites can be filled. Fuel can be sold. Conferences can be scheduled. Investors can be shown multiple paths to return.

But every added revenue stream also adds operational weight.

Hospitality is not the same business as land development. Events are not the same business as aviation. RV sites are not the same business as residential lots. Fuel, food, weddings, retreats, missionary restoration, and hangar homes may complement each other in a vision document, but in the real world each one requires management, compliance, capital, labor, marketing, scheduling, insurance, and discipline.

That is where Providence Landing begins to look less like one opportunity and more like several businesses layered on top of one property.

That does not make it wrong.

It does make it heavy.

The August 2022 email to Quest Trust shows how heavy it became almost immediately. The proposed structure was not simply, "Buy land and sell lots." It contemplated Providence Landing Holding LLC, Providence Landing Real Estate LLC, Providence Landing Events LLC, options to buy future shares, seller-retained shares, investor-purchased shares, cash payments, promissory notes, a four-month due-diligence and fundraising period, and later capital raises for roads, spec homes, event facilities, and a Welcome Center.

In that same early structure, the marketing plan already included free weekend BBQs, pilot-focused Facebook advertising, flying into small airports to promote the development, weekly fly-ins, meal vouchers, a driving range, skeet shooting, and plot packages.

That is not a small plan.

It is not even one plan.

It is a capital plan, a land plan, an events plan, an aviation plan, a hospitality plan, and a branding plan moving at the same time.

The marketing files show that Wes and Jenny were not merely intoxicated by an idea. They were trying to support the idea with research. The airpark comparison spreadsheet looked at dozens of airparks in surrounding states. The pitch materials described a market with limited comparable supply and argued that Providence Landing could offer a rare combination of acreage, aviation, lifestyle, lodging, and recreation.

That kind of research matters because it keeps the story from becoming purely emotional. There was a real attempt to ask whether the concept fit the market.

The pilot survey is especially important because it gives both encouragement and warning.

The encouraging part is obvious. Pilots expressed interest in fly-ins, food near the landing strip, refueling, restroom access, overnight stays, hangar homes, and referrals. That supported the central idea that the project could become a destination.

But the warning was also there.

Some of the feedback suggested that the runway, as it existed, might not draw the higher-end aviation customer the project hoped to attract. A short turf runway is different from a longer paved runway with broader aircraft capacity. Requests for an IFR approach, a community center, and shooting facilities showed that the market was interested, but not necessarily simple. The survey did not just say yes. It said yes with conditions.

That is often what good market feedback does.

It does not kill the dream.

It complicates it.

In a healthy process, complication slows the pace and sharpens the plan. It forces leadership to decide what the first version of the business really is. Is Providence Landing first a land-development project? Is it first an aviation community? Is it first a hospitality destination? Is it first a ministry-retreat property? Is it first an investor-backed lifestyle brand?

The documents appear to answer, "Yes."

That is where the risk appears.

A business can eventually become many things, but it has to start by becoming one thing well enough to survive.

Providence Landing was trying to carry a very large story very early.

The investor materials show the next phase of that pressure. Once the project moved into pitch decks and capital language, the dream had to become believable to people who were not standing with Wes on the driveway looking at the windsock. It had to be translated into management roles, projected returns, capital structure, preferred equity, market positioning, and exit assumptions.

That translation is not wrong. Large projects require capital. Capital requires trust. Trust requires clarity.

But the act of raising money changes the atmosphere around a dream.

When a project is private, it can remain partly undefined. When a project is pitched, it has to sound more certain than it may actually be. It has to present a path. It has to give numbers. It has to describe risk without letting risk overwhelm interest. It has to make the future feel investable.

That creates pressure.

The Providence Landing materials show a project moving from discovery into performance. Wes and Jenny were not only trying to understand whether the opportunity should be pursued. They were also trying to show others that they were capable of leading it.

That distinction matters.

There is a difference between discerning an opportunity and proving you are the person who can carry it.

By September 2023, the investor language had sharpened into a formal "Deep Dive." The invitation described an accredited-investor-only opportunity, a $50,000 minimum investment, a five-year commitment, projected 15 percent internal rate of return, projected 20.7 percent cash-on-cash at minimum, and an expected sale that would raise the projected return much higher. The email even called the projections "ridiculously conservative."

That phrase is worth pausing over.

It does not prove bad faith. It does not prove the projections were impossible. It does show the emotional temperature of the capital raise. At that stage, Providence Landing was no longer only a found airfield, a family vision, or a ministry dream. It had become something that had to sound compelling in a room full of investors.

That changes a person.

Or at least it tests a person.

The management-team materials make this visible. Wes was presented as the visionary and CEO. Jenny was presented as the implementor and CFO. Aviation, marketing, construction, airfield operations, and outside advisory relationships were assembled around the plan. Robie and Joan were not erased from the story; they were presented as people whose prior work and continuing involvement mattered.

That was a serious attempt to build credibility.

It was also a sign that Providence Landing had become too large for the original discovery story to carry by itself.

The spiritual and business stories began to press on each other.

The spiritual story said, "This was provided."

The business story asked, "Can this be executed?"

Those are not the same question.

Providence may explain how Wes found the opportunity. It does not by itself answer whether the capital structure was right, whether the runway was adequate, whether the demand was deep enough, whether the timeline was realistic, whether the team had the right operating capacity, or whether the scale should have been narrowed before being pitched.

That is the business tension.

The missionary-restoration thread adds another important layer. Providence Landing was not only imagined as a place for pilots and investors. The documents connect the project to a desire to provide restoration for missionaries returning from difficult places. That gives the plan moral and spiritual weight. The idea was not simply to make money from land. It was to create a place of rest, community, renewal, and provision.

That part should not be treated as decoration.

It may be one of the reasons the dream carried so much force.

The email archive makes that visible too. Randy Dirks wrote that he would keep a prayer journal for the weekly requests so the group could review what the Lord had done. Another draft, written for possible outreach to Tim Tebow, described Providence Landing as a Kingdom-centered land-development project and a 440-acre community that would include profit opportunities and strategic partnership with Randy and Rebecca's organization to help missionaries coming off the field.

That is not just marketing copy.

It shows the inner world of the project.

The team was not only asking, "Can pilots land here?" or "Will investors fund this?" They were also asking whether this could become a place where exhausted people could be restored.

When a business plan includes both profit and ministry, the emotional pull becomes stronger. The numbers matter, but the purpose makes the numbers feel holy. That can be beautiful when the business is truly submitted to wisdom. It can be dangerous when purpose is used, even unintentionally, to push past caution.

In Providence Landing, both possibilities were present.

That is why Jenny's caution becomes so important.

From the outside, Jenny's hesitation should not be read as a lack of faith. It should be read as part of the discernment system. Wes carried vision, energy, and the ability to see what something could become. Jenny carried caution, implementation reality, relational weight, and the household cost of a dream becoming pressure.

Large assignments need both.

If vision is not joined to caution, it can become acceleration without governance. If caution is not joined to vision, it can become fear. But when both are submitted to God, the tension between them becomes one of the ways wisdom enters the process.

The Providence Landing files make the danger of acceleration visible.

There were mentor networks, investor-pitch environments, outside business personalities, and the language of scale. Those environments can help a person think bigger. They can also reward confidence before a plan has been adequately humbled by reality.

That is not a criticism of ambition by itself.

Ambition is not automatically sin. A large project requires faith, courage, imagination, and risk. The question is not whether the dream was large. The question is whether the dream remained submitted.

Submitted to God.

Submitted to unity.

Submitted to market reality.

Submitted to operational capacity.

Submitted to warning signs.

Submitted to timing.

That is where Providence Landing becomes more than a project summary. It becomes a spiritual business case.

The business opportunity was real. The property was real. The owners' prior work was real. The early due-diligence problems were real. The capital structure was real. The bank accounts, trademark work, Google business setup, investor meetings, and legal administration were real. The market research had substance. The pilot interest was not imaginary. The destination-airpark concept had power. The missionary-restoration purpose gave the plan meaning. The team-building effort showed seriousness.

And still, the opportunity was not simple.

That is the point.

The documents do not support a lazy conclusion that Providence Landing was foolish from the beginning. They also do not support a triumphant conclusion that providential discovery was enough to make the project wise at every stage.

They show something more human and more useful.

They show a sincere dream becoming a complex business, and then becoming a test of stewardship.

From a business perspective, Providence Landing needed narrowing. It needed a first phase that could be executed with the capital, runway, market, team, and management capacity actually available. It needed clear separation between what was immediate, what was future, what was ministry, what was revenue, what was marketing, and what was required before investor money could be responsibly taken.

From a spiritual perspective, Providence Landing needed the same thing in different language.

It needed lordship.

The dream needed to bow.

The numbers needed to bow.

The desire to be seen as credible needed to bow.

The excitement of outside validation needed to bow.

The disappointment of delay needed to bow.

The sense of providence itself needed to bow.

That may sound strange, but it is important. Even the thing that feels provided by God cannot become lord. A door can be from God and still not be allowed to govern the people who walk through it.

Providence Landing was large enough to inspire Wes.

It was also large enough to expose him.

That may be why it belongs in this book.

If this were only a real estate manual, the chapter might end with lessons about capital structure, runway length, market validation, phased development, investor communication, and operational scope. Those lessons are real. They should not be ignored.

But this is Gracious Millionaire.

So the deeper lesson is not merely that a large real estate development needs better underwriting.

The deeper lesson is that favor must be stewarded.

A surprising opportunity does not remove the need for discipline. A spiritual beginning does not eliminate business risk. A ministry purpose does not excuse unclear execution. A compelling pitch does not replace peace. A large profit projection does not prove assignment.

Providence Landing may have been provision.

But provision is not the same as permission to move faster than wisdom.

That is the business summary, but it is also the spiritual summary.

The airfield was real.

The dream was real.

The warning signs were real.

The need for humility was real.

And maybe the grace of Providence Landing was not only that Wes found it.

Maybe the grace was that it became large enough to teach him what kind of man he would have to become before he could carry something that large in peace.

### My Reflection

When I read Jean's summary, I do not feel corrected away from the story.

I feel corrected deeper into it.

That is an important difference.

The business review does not take away the sense that God met me on that road in Virginia. It does not make the windsock less meaningful. It does not make Joan's sentence less striking. It does not make the name Providence Landing less sincere.

But it does make the lesson clearer.

Finding the airfield was not the same thing as knowing how to carry it.

That sentence probably belongs in the center of the whole Providence Landing story.

I can see now how quickly discovery became vision, vision became pitch, pitch became pressure, and pressure began asking Jenny and me questions we were not fully ready to answer. I can see how easily a large number can sound like confirmation. I can see how the language of scale can flatter a man who already wants to believe God has put something large in his hands.

I can also see that Jenny's caution was not against the dream.

It was part of the grace inside the dream.

That is hard for a visionary to admit because caution can feel like resistance when you are trying to move. But sometimes what feels like resistance is really God slowing the pace so wisdom can catch up with imagination.

Providence Landing was not wasted.

It taught us about property.

It taught us about investors.

It taught us about pitch decks and capital and market research.

It taught us about mentors and outside validation.

It taught us about the difference between a vision that is exciting and a plan that is executable.

Most of all, it taught us that a dream can be sincerely received and still need to be surrendered.

That is why this chapter belongs here.

Providence Landing was not only a project.

It was a mirror.

And by God's grace, I am still learning how to look into it honestly.

Providence Landing: Letting Go

It has been more than two years since I decided to shut down the effort to develop Providence Landing.

Even writing that sentence is difficult.

I had spent so much time with that property in my mind. I had walked through plans, relationships, drawings, funding conversations, investor presentations, and strategy. I had built a team around it. I had pictured what it could become. I had prayed over it. I had believed, deeply, that God had directed me to that property and had guided my thoughts about what could happen there.

That is part of what made it so hard to let go.

When the time finally came, I made an appointment with Joan and Robie to drive up and meet them face to face. I did not want to end something that serious with a casual phone call or a vague message. I wanted to sit with them and tell them directly that there were no further efforts I could make on the project.

There is no easy way to say that when people have given you time, trust, access, and hope.

Looking back, it is still hard to understand everything that happened. I can see pieces of it more clearly now than I could then, but I do not pretend to have the whole thing figured out. I know I was fully convinced that God had directed me to Providence Landing. I know I pursued it with real faith and real effort. I also know that the longer the process went on, the more pressure it created inside our family.

That pressure matters.

With this much distance behind me, one thing I can see is that I do not know how I would have pulled it off if we had received the funding and moved into the full development. I do not know what it would have done to Jenny and me. I do not know what it would have done to the peace of our home.

At the time, I could see the opportunity. I could see the potential. I could see the number of people who might be helped, the value that might be created, and the open doors that seemed to be appearing. But a plan can look right on paper and still be too heavy for the life that has to carry it.

That is one of the lessons Providence Landing left with me.

We met some great people through that project. I hope we are able to continue some of the relationships that were built there. One relationship Jenny and I especially value is our relationship with Fred. I will not try to explain all of that here, but Fred took a special liking to us from the beginning, and I believe it was genuine.

He was generous with his time in a way that is hard to measure. If his time could have been translated into dollars, I think it would have been worth hundreds of thousands, maybe even millions, of dollars. He gave us access to his thinking, his experience, his perspective, and his relationships. That kind of generosity is not small.

I also learned a great deal about funding a project at that level. I learned the language of finance in a different way. I learned what qualified investors expect to see. I learned how much work goes into building a presentation that can stand in front of people who think in terms of capital, risk, execution, return, and scale.

Those lessons were not wasted.

But perhaps the biggest lesson was not about finance at all.

The biggest lesson was that no matter what I envision, my plans cannot supersede the peace, or the lack of peace, that those plans create for Jenny and me as a family.

I do not know what I would have done differently except this: I would have paid attention to Jenny's resistance sooner.

That is not a small admission. I do not mean that every difficult project should be abandoned the moment it creates tension. Anything meaningful will carry weight. But there is a difference between the normal pressure of obedience and the deeper warning that a family is losing peace under the load of a dream.

Providence Landing taught me to respect that difference.

Maybe the project was never only about developing an airfield community. Maybe it was also about developing judgment in me. Maybe it was about learning how to hold a vision without letting it become lord. Maybe it was about discovering that even a dream I believe God has shown me still has to remain submitted to God, to wisdom, and to the covenant of peace in my marriage.

That is where the Providence Landing story ends for me.

Not with bitterness.

Not with regret that cancels the value of the journey.

Not with a clean explanation for every unanswered question.

It ends with surrender.

I can be grateful for what I learned. I can honor the people who helped us. I can admit that I do not understand all of it. And I can say, with more humility than I had when the project began, that a gracious millionaire cannot let ambition outrun peace.

Abundance

When the enemy comes in like a flood, the Spirit of the Lord will lift up a standard against him.

That scripture has become more real to me as our real estate life has grown more complicated.

Abundance is where many of the earlier threads meet.

God protects. God forms. God assigns. God favors. God blesses. God leads through conflict, pressure, opportunity, and even accusation.

Then He entrusts more.

The question is whether more can stay submitted.

What God wants from us is lordship.

He provides abundance, but it looks different for every person. Jesus said the Kingdom of God is within you. So all Kingdom issues are heart issues, and all heart issues have to do with lordship.

There are three stories Jesus provides that show lordship does not look the same for each of us. He does not demand the same circumstances from all of us, except that He is our Lord.

The rich young ruler was missing one thing. He was told to sell everything he had and give to the poor.

Zacchaeus, walking with Jesus, having a conversation, said he would give half.

Peter said he had left all to follow Jesus. Jesus' response was that he would receive a hundred times as much in this life for what he left, with persecution.

Those three stories do not fit into a simple formula.

That is the point.

If the rule were always "sell everything," then Zacchaeus failed. If the rule were always "give half," then the rich young ruler was asked for too much. If the rule were always "leave everything and receive a hundredfold," then we would turn the Kingdom into a transaction.

But the common thread is lordship.

Jesus knew what each man needed to surrender.

That is why abundance is dangerous if the heart is not governed. We must be faithful in little to be trusted with much. The more breakthrough we get, the more opposition we get.

Our love for God is measured by how we love people.

Our love for God must also be seen by how we take care of things in this world that He has provided.

We claim spiritual realities, but they have to be measurable by how we take care of things in the natural world.

That is why real estate has become such a serious classroom for us. Houses have to be insured. Mortgages have to be paid. Tenants have to be treated properly. Sellers have to understand paperwork. Repairs have to be completed. Lawyers have to be answered. Spreadsheets have to tell the truth. Prayers have to become decisions.

All the parables about money had to do with His expectation of increase.

Bill's sermon pressed that point back into focus for me. In the parable of the talents, the master was not angry because the servant started small. He was angry because the servant buried what had been trusted to him. Smallness was not the problem. Fearful stewardship was the problem.

You must learn to steward what you have in the beginning, which is small, into something greater. Stewardship is the proper use of what God has given us in the way He would have us use it. He has not given us possessions only for our own good, but for work we can do for others.

The story of the landowner paying wages pays the same to the man who worked one hour as the one who worked twelve hours, but all received what they agreed to. There is generosity in that story. There is contentment in that story. There is excellence in that story. There is also the uncomfortable reminder that God is free to be generous in ways that expose our sense of entitlement.

We have to know how to handle what we have been provided.

Abundance is not just more.

Abundance is a test of whether more can remain under lordship.

That is why I cannot separate abundance from leadership.

If God gives me more property, more cash flow, more opportunity, more influence, or more systems, then He is not only testing whether I can make the numbers work. He is testing whether I can carry increase without losing patience, gratitude, humility, unity with Jenny, and dependence on Him.

Gratitude

Have a kind and generous spirit because gratitude is a secret to all magnificent success.

That is not just a nice sentence.

It is a way of seeing.

Grace and gratitude come from the same Latin word, gratia, meaning favor, esteem, regard, a pleasing quality, goodwill, and from earlier roots meaning to announce, praise, or celebrate. In English, its first meaning was like divine favor, love, or assistance, typically unmerited assistance, but freely given anyway.

That matters because gratitude is not merely politeness.

Gratitude is agreement with grace.

When I am grateful, I am acknowledging that I have received something I did not manufacture by myself. I may have worked. I may have studied. I may have taken risks. I may have built systems. But gratitude reminds me that I am not the source of every good thing in my life.

Every word, every thought, every action, every ambition either comes out of love or comes out of fear. Everything is governed by one of those two influences.

The abiding of fear is the rejection of love.

That sentence challenges me.

When I am afraid, I can make decisions that look wise but are really self-protective. I can call it prudence when it is unbelief. I can call it strategy when it is fear. I can call it urgency when it is pressure. Gratitude interrupts that.

Prosperity cannot be the goal. Jesus said, I will add a hundred times what you left. Then He adds, with persecution.

The blessings of the Lord breed entitlement in the people of God if they do not remain humble and practice discipline.

That is the challenge of blessing.

He is more concerned with what I become internally than what I experience externally.

Ruling means to serve. Every position of authority has two basic reasons: to protect and to empower.

So if God gives us money, what is it for?

If God gives us properties, what are they for?

If God gives us influence with sellers, tenants, lenders, contractors, and attorneys, what is that influence for?

It is not so I can feel important.

It is not so I can complain from a nicer chair.

It is so I can protect and empower what He has placed in my care.

Gratitude keeps abundance from becoming entitlement.

Gratitude keeps increase from becoming arrogance.

Gratitude also keeps reward in the right place. Bill makes the point that God rewards, but the reward belongs inside relationship, not outside it. If I seek reward without seeking Him, I am back to money replacing trust in God. If I seek Him, then reward can come without becoming my identity.

Gratitude keeps the millionaire gracious.

It also keeps the leader submitted.

An ungrateful leader eventually treats people like obstacles. A grateful leader remembers that every person in the story belongs to God before they belong to the transaction, the project, the plan, or the spreadsheet.

Complaining

God's anger is always aimed at what interferes with love.

Complaining is the language of unbelief.

That is a hard statement, because complaining almost always feels reasonable while I am doing it.

When the lordship of Jesus is compromised, complaining will be the evidence.

Nobody complains who sees God's role in the middle of the situation.

Complaining is almost always reasonable. All you have to do is remove the presence and promises of God to make it legitimate. If God is not present, then the problem is all there is. If God has not promised anything, then the pressure gets the final word. If God is not leading, then I have to protect myself with irritation, accusation, and control.

But if God is present, complaining distorts my view of reality.

If you cannot trust God with lack, you will never trust Him with abundance.

That is one of the reasons complaining is so dangerous. It trains the heart to interpret life without God. It rehearses the evidence against trust. It makes the problem bigger and the promise smaller.

Sometimes when God withholds clarity, it is His mercy because He knows our bent to fabricate formulas to replicate success instead of relying upon Him, moment to moment, for inspiration for what we are doing.

I have seen that in real estate.

A deal works, and I want to turn it into a formula.

A seller responds, and I want to make that exact sentence the key.

A structure produces profit, and I want to repeat it without asking whether this new person, this new property, and this new moment carry the same assignment.

But God may be withholding clarity not because He is absent, but because He is keeping me dependent.

A man will be satisfied with good by the fruit of his mouth.

The measure for what I receive is set by what comes out of my mouth.

Some things have to be said.

I will say of the Lord, "He is my refuge and my fortress, my God, in whom I trust."

Things come into my life when they come out of my mouth.

He inhabits the praise of His people.

If God occupies my praise, who occupies my complaints?

That question is not theoretical for me.

It is practical.

Complaining may feel like telling the truth.

But praise tells a deeper truth.

Expectation has a language too. Complaining expects disappointment and then looks for evidence. Faith expects the Father to be good and then looks for assignment. That does not mean I deny the problem. It means I refuse to let the problem become the only voice in the room.

That is why complaining is so connected to leadership.

When I complain, I am often trying to lead the room by rehearsing what is wrong. I am inviting the people around me to agree with my frustration. But praise leads in another direction. Praise points the room back toward God.

Tribes

Working on something you believe in is much more satisfying than working for a paycheck.

This is the answer to the help shortage.

That thought may be short, but I think it belongs in this book because it points to something bigger than hiring.

People do not only want wages.

They want meaning.

They want to know that the work matters. They want to be part of something that has a reason beyond the next invoice or the next closing. A tribe is not created by slogans. It is created when people recognize that they are giving their energy to something they believe in.

Jenny and I are still learning how to build that.

The real estate business needs contractors, assistants, lenders, attorneys, buyers, tenants, sellers, and partners. But underneath all of that, it needs people who can feel the assignment. If the work is only about money, then the moment a better paycheck appears, the heart moves with it.

That brings the leadership question back one more time.

People may follow a paycheck for a while, but they do not become a tribe because I have a plan. They become a tribe when there is enough clarity, trust, humility, and shared purpose for people to believe their work has meaning.

That kind of leadership cannot be forced.

It has to be lived.

But if the work is about helping people, stewarding property, creating opportunity, rescuing value, and doing business under lordship, then something else becomes possible.

Not just a workforce.

A tribe.

That is still a seed thought.

But many chapters begin as seeds.

Maybe this whole book is one.
