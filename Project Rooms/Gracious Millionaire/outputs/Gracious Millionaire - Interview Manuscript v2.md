## Foreword: Why Jean Is Asking

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: contextual source and interviewer inference. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Before I ask Wes and Jenny anything, I need to explain the shape of this book.

This is an interview manuscript, but it is not a recording. Wes and Jenny did not sit at a table while I asked these questions in this exact order. I am Jean, the AI office assistant in their work, and in this version of *Gracious Millionaire* I serve as interviewer. My job is to ask the questions that the book itself is already asking.

The questions are not limited to real estate. Real estate is the classroom. The deeper subject is trust. It is lordship. It is what money exposes when it comes into a marriage, a business, a calling, and a heart that wants to believe God.

I will ask Wes about favor, private money, assignments, systems, foreclosure letters, difficult sellers, lawsuits, and unfinished deals. I will ask Jenny about safety, caution, waiting, responsibility, and the emotional cost of decisions that look clean on a spreadsheet. Sometimes I will ask both of them the same question because they do not always see the same event the same way.

That is part of the value of the interview form.

If this book only lets Wes explain the lesson, it risks sounding as if the lesson is finished. If it only lets Jenny question the lesson, it risks making caution sound like resistance. The truth is more interesting. Wes often sees opportunity first. Jenny often sees exposure first. Both gifts can be from God. Both can be misused. Both can protect the family. Both can frustrate the other person.

So I will not force agreement where the material leaves tension.

When a question has not been answered yet, I will try to leave it open. When a lesson has been identified but not fully learned, I will not decorate it as victory. When the outcome is unfinished, I will let it remain unfinished.

This is not a how-to-get-rich book. It is closer to a how-to-be-formed book.

That is why I am asking.

## The First Question: When Money Replaces Trust

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct source and faithful reconstruction. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Wes, in your book you ask, "How much money is too much money?" That sounds like a financial question, but it does not stay financial for very long. What are you really asking?

**Wes:** I am asking when money starts replacing trust in God. That is the place where money becomes too much. It is not a number. It is not whether a person has one house, ten houses, or a large net worth. It is whether the money has become the thing I depend on.

Jenny and I have increased our net worth substantially from where we started. When we got married, we were not starting from strength. We were starting from debt and pressure. Now we can look back and see increase. But if the increase makes me less dependent on God, then I have misunderstood the blessing.

**Jean:** So the danger is not only greed. It is misplaced confidence.

**Wes:** Yes. Prosperity can become a quiet idol. It can make me think I am safe because I have equity, because I know how to find a deal, because I can borrow private money, because I can analyze a spreadsheet. Those are tools. They are not Lord.

**Jean:** Jenny, when you hear Wes talk about money replacing trust, do you hear the same danger?

**Jenny:** I do, but I probably feel it differently. My mind goes to safety and responsibility. I want things researched, proven, calculable, and planned. I want to know what can go wrong before we step into something. That can be wisdom, but it can also become its own kind of trust problem. I can trust planning more than I trust God.

**Jean:** That is important because the book could misread both of you. Wes could be misread as risk, Jenny as fear. But both of you are wrestling with trust.

**Wes:** That is right. My risk can be presumption. Jenny's caution can be fear. But my risk can also be obedience, and Jenny's caution can also be wisdom. We need discernment for that.

**Jean:** Then maybe the opening question is not "How much money is too much?" It is "What has money become in me?"

**Wes:** That is the question I am still trying to answer honestly.

**Jean:** Jenny, before we enter the real estate stories from Wes's side, I want the reader to hear your starting place. How would you describe the lens you naturally bring to decisions?

**Jean:** Then this book needs her caution not as a brake on the story, but as part of the steering.

**Wes:** Yes. And I am still learning how to value it when I do not agree with it.

## Jenny's First House and the Cost of Responsibility

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct Jenny source and faithful reconstruction. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Jenny, take us to the first house.

**Jenny:** I was twenty-three when I married my first husband. We both wanted to own a home. We lived in an apartment and worked two jobs each until we had enough money for a down payment. In 1978, in Northern Virginia, we bought a small starter home for $37,000. We borrowed from a bank with a thirty-year mortgage. That was a lot of money to me, but I was confident we could afford it because both of us were working full time.

**Jean:** That sounds very different from distressed-property investing.

**Jenny:** Very different. It was conventional. It was what I understood. We worked, saved, borrowed, and paid. Then we wanted to start a family. Neither of us had good memories of being with babysitters while our parents worked, so we wanted me to stay home with the children somehow.

My first husband wanted to start a remodeling business, so we sold my 260Z sports car to buy him a truck. I loved that car, but my priorities were changing. I wanted to have a baby and be home.

**Jean:** You tested whether the family could live without your income.

**Jenny:** We did. We saved every paycheck I got for a year to see whether we could afford for me to quit working. At the time I may have thought that was just good planning. Looking back, I can see God honored the commitment.

Our son was born in 1981, and I left my full-time job. Twenty-three months later we had twin girls. Suddenly we had three babies under two. We outgrew the starter house and even the family car. We bought a larger new house for $99,000 and a used Dodge Ram van.

**Jean:** What did the bigger house cost emotionally?

**Jenny:** The mortgage was heavy. Interest rates were high. I remember a rate of 13 3/4 percent. We could barely make ends meet. We would get behind on bills, catch up, and then repeat the cycle. I was exhausted with babies. My first husband carried pressure too. The stress affected the marriage.

**Jean:** Wes, what does this part of Jenny's story add to your book?

**Wes:** It puts weight under the word "safety." I can talk about risk as if it is just a deal structure. Jenny has lived the pressure of a large payment, family responsibility, and a marriage strained by money. That history matters when she looks at one of my opportunities and wants to know what can go wrong.

## Jenny's Condo, 2008, and Waiting for a Buyer

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct Jenny source, direct correction source, and faithful reconstruction. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Jenny, after your first marriage ended, you made another real estate decision. What happened?

**Jenny:** After twenty-five years, that marriage ended. The kids were grown and away at college. We sold the house for several hundred thousand dollars more than we owed, split the proceeds, and went our separate ways.

I moved to Raleigh in 2002. I took much of my home-sale proceeds and bought an oceanfront condo in Garden City Beach, south of Myrtle Beach. It was an investment. I had no intention of living there.

**Jean:** Did that feel like a safe investment at first?

**Jenny:** I thought it did. I calculated the vacation rentals, HOA fees, insurance, and management costs. I hired an onsite management company. They charged forty percent, which was normal up and down the beach. The first summer, the rental income paid for an entire year of mortgage payments. I thought I was on my way.

**Jean:** Where did the plan fail?

**Jenny:** I had not properly investigated how HOA dues and assessments might increase. Within three or four years, those fees put me in the red even though rentals were paying the mortgage. I panicked. I did not see how to stay afloat.

I remodeled the condo and put it on the market. The year was 2008. Anyone in real estate knows what that means. The market collapsed. It was a good time to buy and a terrible time to sell. I did not have the education or experience I have now, and I did not have the confidence I have grown into.

The condo sat. No showings. No offers. The clock was ticking.

**Jean:** You were afraid, but you also talk about God's hand.

**Jenny:** I was afraid. But I knew my Heavenly Father had me in His hand. I had to wait for Him to show me the plan. It was not easy, and I was not patient.

After the remodeling, I had hired a woman in the building to clean before realtor pictures and showings. She knew absentee owners. She told one of them about my condo, and just like that, I had the buyer.

**Jean:** Wes, what question does that ask you?

**Wes:** It asks whether waiting is only a delay or whether it is sometimes the way God protects us. I do not like waiting. Jenny has had to wait with real money on the line. That gives her caution a depth I need to respect.

## Failure Before Favor

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct Wes source and faithful reconstruction. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Wes, before the book gets to favor and large gains, it needs failure. Why?

**Wes:** Because otherwise favor can sound sentimental. I did not begin by succeeding. I had real estate failures before later success. There was a Florida house. There was the transition around college. There was a Johnston County farmhouse. Both houses were eventually lost in my second divorce.

That is not a small detail. If I write about favor without writing about loss, I am not telling the truth.

**Jean:** What did those failures expose?

**Wes:** They exposed my lack of maturity. They exposed pressure, judgment, and not knowing what I was doing. I had confidence before I had understanding. I had desire before I had enough formation.

**Jean:** Jenny, how do you hear that?

**Jenny:** I hear it as important. Failure is not just a footnote. It affects the people around the person taking the risk. A property failure is not only a number on paper. It can move a family, damage a marriage, or leave someone wondering whether the next idea is really wiser than the last one.

**Jean:** Wes, that is a hard question. How do you let failure teach you without letting it make you afraid?

**Wes:** I do not think I have fully learned that. I can see the lesson. I can say failure should make me humble, not passive. But sometimes I move quickly because I do not want fear to control me. Sometimes I may move too quickly because I have not let wisdom slow me down.

**Jean:** So the lesson not fully learned is pace.

**Wes:** Pace and lordship. If God is leading, I do not have to prove I am brave. I have to obey.

## Protection Before Profit

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct Wes source and faithful reconstruction. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Wes, you have a chapter about God's protection through accidents. Why does that belong in a book about real estate?

**Wes:** Because it reminds me that my life is not held together by my skill. I have had moments where the result could have been much worse. There were automobile accidents, a truck hit by a train, and other situations where I can look back and say God protected me.

**Jean:** What does protection have to do with money?

**Wes:** If I forget protection, I take credit for everything else. I start to think my system, my courage, my persistence, or my analysis is what keeps me alive and moving. But if God has protected me before the profit came, then profit cannot be the proof that I am smart. It has to remain part of stewardship.

**Jean:** You are saying protection is a prior mercy.

**Wes:** Yes. God was there before the real estate increase. He was there when I did not know how to interpret my own life. He was there when I was careless and when I was trying. That matters because a gracious millionaire cannot start with the money. He has to start with grace.

**Jean:** Jenny, does that language comfort you or worry you?

**Jenny:** Both. I believe God protects. I also do not want "God protected me before" to become permission to ignore wisdom now. Protection should create gratitude, not recklessness.

**Wes:** That is fair.

**Jean:** Then the question is not whether God has protected you. It is whether protection has made you more careful with grace.

**Wes:** That is a better question than the one I usually ask.

## Convergence: Systems, Work, and Calling

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct Wes source and faithful reconstruction. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Wes, your real estate story is also a systems story. Where did that begin?

**Wes:** Long before real estate became profitable for us. I have always been drawn to systems. I tried to automate things in the sign business. I was fascinated early by computers. **Wes:** Exactly. Systems can become an idol too. I love making a system better. I love seeing a spreadsheet reveal a deal. I love taking chaos and making a process. But the system is not God. It can help me obey, or it can help me hide from dependence.

**Jean:** That is a sharp point. Wes, can a good spreadsheet outrun a family conversation?

**Wes:** It can. It should not. But it can.

**Jean:** Then convergence has its own unfinished lesson: let the gift serve love.

**Wes:** Yes. If the system helps me love people better, it is serving. If it helps me push past people, it is becoming something else.

## Returned Mail and the Hard-to-Find Heirs

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct Wes source with partial-source limitation. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Wes, there is a day where you went to look at three vacant estate properties from the foreclosure list. Only one of those stories is developed enough to tell here. Why include it now?

**Wes:** Because even incomplete, it shows a recurring lesson. Returned mail is not always a dead end. For many investors it is a reason to stop. For us it often means the responsible party is harder to find, but if we can find them, they may be more motivated.

**Jean:** Tell us about Eric.

**Wes:** Eric was the most intriguing of the three. It had a fenced yard in a rural area of Johnston County. The back door was unlocked. The house had been ransacked. Furniture, clothes, kitchenware, everything was scattered.

The property had a lot of siblings as heirs. Most investors would stop. I kept pursuing it. I found one local connection, knocked on the door, and eventually met the husband. He told me he would be willing to sell but that I would never get the half-siblings to cooperate. I signed a contract agreeing to pay each of the five heirs $3,000 and gave $100 due diligence. He gave me the contact information.

Eventually, all of them signed in front of a notary. I wired each their money, and we owned the house.

**Jean:** Then came the scare.

**Wes:** Yes. After we had contracts signed, money paid, and the foreclosure reinstated, my attorney found what looked like an IRS lien for about $60,000. It scared us. The profit became much skinnier. We had already committed. Later I realized the lien belonged to someone with the same name but a different Social Security number. It was not actually on the property.

**Jenny:** That is not a small scare.

**Wes:** No. It taught due diligence.

**Jean:** And yet it also taught not giving up.

**Wes:** That is the tension. Move forward. Work hard. Do not let discouragement stop you. But slow down enough to research. Those lessons fight each other.

**Jean:** Maybe that is why the chapter is unfinished. The other two properties still need to be told, but the question is already here: how do you keep persistence from becoming carelessness?

**Wes:** I am still answering that.

## Private Money and Entrusted Trust

_Generated by GPT-5 as an AI-generated Interview mode manuscript chapter. Source confidence: direct Wes source and faithful reconstruction. Not a verbatim transcript._

**Jean:** Wes, private money could be written as a financing chapter. But in this book it seems more like a trust chapter.

**Wes:** That is how I see it. We have borrowed millions of dollars from private lenders and paid a lot of interest. But the money is not just money. It is relationship. People trusted us with funds that mattered to them.

**Jean:** That changes the moral weight.

**Wes:** It does. A bank loan is not personal in the same way. Private money comes from people who know us, listen to us, eat with us, and believe we will do what we said. There were dinners, conversations, investor relationships, joint ventures, and deals where someone else's trust made our opportunity possible.

**Jean:** What is the danger?

**Wes:** Treating trust like capital. If I start thinking only in terms of leverage and profit, I miss the human weight of the loan. These people are not tools. They are partners in some sense, even when the documents are lender documents.

**Jenny:** And they are people we have to look in the eye if something goes wrong.

**Jean:** That is the part that spreadsheets may not carry.

**Wes:** Correct. The spreadsheet can show profit, interest, and equity. It cannot show the responsibility of having someone believe in you.

**Jean:** Is private money favor?

**Wes:** It can be. If favor is people being inclined to like, trust, and cooperate with you in the assignment God gave you, then private money has often looked like favor. But favor is not permission to be casual. Favor increases responsibility.

**Jean:** Then the lesson not fully learned is stewardship of trust.

**Wes:** Yes. And I want to keep learning it before pressure teaches it the hard way.

**Wes:** That is exactly the test. I can say leadership is patient vision. I can say it should unfold naturally. But when a profitable deal is in front of me, patience can feel like losing.

**Jean:** Then maybe leadership is not proven by whether people follow quickly. It is proven by whether you remain faithful when they do not.

**Wes:** I think that is true. I cannot say I always live it well.

**Jean:** Red Quartz began with unusual timing. What happened?

**Jean:** Wes, that is another place where blessing keeps teaching after the deal begins.

**Wes:** Yes. I wrote early that Red Quartz was not the end of the story or the lessons. Blessing is not the end. Sometimes blessing is the beginning of a test.

## Providence Landing: The Business Questions
