# Providence Landing - Marketing Folder Narrative Additions

Source set: SharePoint/Teams folder `Corp-Providence Landing Management LLC/Marketing`, inspected 2026-06-29 under Wes's explicit exception to the normal project-room boundary.

## Bottom Line

The marketing folder adds a second layer to the Providence Landing narrative: not just "Wes found an airfield while looking for land," but "Wes and Jenny tried to turn that providential discovery into a fully capitalized public vision." The best manuscript use is not to overload the chapter with investor math. The chapter should use these files to show the scale of the dream, the seriousness of the planning, the outside validation being sought, and the tension between faith, ambition, business coaching, investor pressure, and warning signs.

## Narrative Additions

1. The discovery story has a stronger visual structure.

   `Orign Story - Wes & Jenny.docx` frames Providence Landing as a hidden-target story: a map full of targets, then a smaller map with no visible target, then the surprise of finding the airpark across the road from the property Wes originally drove to see. This gives the chapter a clean narrative device: Wes was aiming at one tract of land, but the real target was hidden beside it.

2. Joan and Robie Potts should be named as originators, not just sellers.

   The 2023 proposal identifies Robie and Joan Potts as the people who developed Potts Landing, subdivided the property into 90 multi-acre lots, established the FAA-registered airfield, and were expected to remain involved as airport and back-office managers. This makes them part of the Providence Landing story rather than background sellers.

3. The project scale was larger than a vague "airpark dream."

   The materials describe a 440+ acre pilot-centric residential and recreational airpark built around FAA designation `3GV9`, a 2,000 to 2,100 ft turf runway, 51 buildable lots for custom hangar homes, an existing hangar, 6 existing residential buildings, short-term rentals, planned new rental buildings, RV sites, hunting acreage, equestrian uses, conference/wedding facilities, restaurant/catering, fuel, and a future longer runway.

4. The business plan treated Providence Landing as an integrated lifestyle ecosystem.

   The strongest repeated idea is not simply "sell lots." It is a fly-in destination where pilots could land, eat, refuel, stay overnight, use recreation facilities, attend events, and eventually live or own a second home. The "hundred-dollar hamburger" idea appears as a vivid hook: pilots would fly in for a meal and a destination, not just a runway.

5. The investor pitch can serve as a temptation-and-tension thread.

   The pitch decks and ROI material show an ambitious capital raise: about $5.25 million, accredited investors, preferred equity, targeted yields, capital distribution priority, 7-year projections, and a hypothetical sale outcome. This is useful narratively as pressure: the dream began as provision and favor, then quickly entered the world of investor expectations, high projections, and formal securities-style language.

6. The competitive-airpark research gives factual weight to the dream.

   `Airparks.xlsx` and the pitch decks compare 65 airparks across surrounding states, including available lots, occupancy, runway surface, amenities, and price markers. The marketing claim was that Providence Landing would hold a large share of available comparable lots and offer a rare combination of amenities. This supports the idea that Wes was not imagining demand out of thin air.

7. The pilot survey adds both validation and warning.

   `Air Park Survey-Export.csv` shows a small survey of aircraft owners. It supports interest in fly-ins, eating by the strip, refueling/restrooms, short-term rentals, hangar homes, and referrals. It also includes warnings: one respondent said the short turf runway would not appeal to the DC flying crowd with money and suggested accommodating larger aircraft or a Pilatus; others requested a pistol/rifle range, IFR approach, and community center. This belongs in the chapter because it shows both confirmation and friction.

8. The management team was part of the sales story.

   The pitch materials present Wes as CEO/visionary, Jenny as CFO/implementor, Timothy and Tammy Reather as aviation/marketing experts, Jonathan and Walter Brothers as general contractors, Robie and Joan Potts as airfield/back-office managers, and Adam Coffey as an outside advisor. That adds an important phase: Providence Landing became a team-building and credibility-building exercise, not just a personal dream.

9. Missionary restoration was not a side note.

   The marketing playbook and origin story connect Randy and Rebecca Dirks and a missionary-restoration vision to the project. Providence Landing was imagined as a restorative place for pilots, residents, guests, and missionaries, with off-season occupancy partly supported by that mission-aligned use. This should stay in the chapter because it ties the business vision back to spiritual purpose.

10. The outside-business network was a major emotional accelerant.

   The origin-story document and folders around JT Foxx, Fred, George Ross, BNI, and the large presentations show that Wes and Jenny were actively seeking momentum, mentorship, and investor-network access. The narrative should treat this carefully: it may represent favor and open doors, but also the place where excitement and external validation could outpace sober discernment.

## Suggested Manuscript Use

- Add the hidden-target/arrow image to the opening of the Providence Landing chapter or the continuation after `The Orphaned Property`.
- Expand Joan and Robie from incidental sellers into originators whose prior work made the opportunity real.
- Add one compressed paragraph showing the dream's full scale: homes, runway, fly-ins, rentals, RV, events, mission renewal, and future runway.
- Add a short "this is where the dream became a pitch" section to transition from providential discovery to investor-world pressure.
- Use the pilot survey as the evidence-and-warning beat: enough interest to keep the dream alive, enough concerns to show that Providence Landing was not simple.
- Keep the investor numbers out of the main manuscript except as selective context. They are useful for tension, not for proving the story.
