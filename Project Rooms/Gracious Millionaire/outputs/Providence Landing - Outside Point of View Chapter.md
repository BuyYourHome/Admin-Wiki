# Providence Landing: What I See

The Providence Landing story is not just the story of a man finding an airfield.

That is the first thing I see.

If I only tell it that way, the story becomes too small. It becomes a surprising real estate anecdote. Wes was looking for land, he drove down a road in Virginia, he saw a windsock, he met Joan, and suddenly there was an airpark for sale.

That is true, but it is not enough.

Providence Landing is the story of a dream that arrived wearing the clothes of provision. It looked like favor. It felt like favor. It had the timing, the surprise, and the almost theatrical precision of favor. But then it grew into something more complicated. It became a test of vision, wisdom, ambition, unity, leadership, money, mentorship, and discernment.

That is why it belongs in this book.

The discovery itself is almost too clean to ignore. Wes was not looking for an existing airpark that day. He was looking at raw land, carrying an idea that had been forming in him for years: a place where pilots could live with their aircraft, a community built around a runway instead of a golf course or a lake.

He had already looked at one property that was not right. He had spent the night in South Boston, Virginia, carrying the weight of a bad day, a strained conversation with Jenny, and the uneasy feeling that he was driving without much of a plan. Then, in a hotel room, he said out loud that if God could speak, he was listening.

That matters.

It matters because what happened next cannot be separated from that posture. He opened the computer, searched for acreage in Virginia, and found a tract of raw land to go see. That was the visible destination. That was the property on the left side of the road. That was the thing the GPS was taking him toward.

But the hidden target was on the other side.

Before he reached the listed property, he saw signs of something unusual. There were fences, pastures, order, buildings, and then the unmistakable evidence of aviation: a runway, hangars, planes, and a windsock. The property he came to see was not the airpark. It was across the road from the airpark.

That is the image the chapter needs.

Wes was aiming at one target and passed the real one on the way.

I do not think that is just clever structure. It is the spiritual center of the story. Many people talk about favor as though it is merely God giving us the thing we already wanted. Providence Landing shows something more unsettling. Sometimes favor appears beside the thing we thought we were pursuing. Sometimes God lets us drive toward one address so we will notice another one.

Wes turned around.

That turn matters too.

He did not own the property. He did not have an appointment. He had no right to assume that anyone would welcome him. But real estate had already trained him in the strange humility of approaching property owners. He knew how to pull in carefully, wait, speak honestly, and not act entitled.

Joan came out and asked if she could help him.

He told her he was looking for land where he might build an airpark.

Then she told him this one was for sale.

That sentence is the hinge.

From there, Providence Landing stopped being an idea and became a possibility. Robie and Joan Potts were not simply people on the other side of a transaction. They were originators. They had already done what Wes had only imagined doing. They had purchased the land, developed the airfield, subdivided the property, established the aviation identity, and given the place a form that could be recognized.

That changes the story.

Wes did not discover empty potential. He discovered someone else's years of work. He found a dream that had already been partially built by another couple. That is important because it keeps the story grounded. Providence Landing was not a fantasy projected onto raw land. It had roads, lots, buildings, an FAA designation, a runway, and history. It also had complexity.

Provision is rarely simple.

The marketing files make that obvious. They show Providence Landing becoming larger and more defined with every document. It was not just a runway. It was not just 300 acres. The vision expanded into more than 440 acres, with a pilot-centered residential and recreational airpark, a turf runway, buildable lots for hangar homes, existing residential buildings, short-term rentals, RV sites, hunting acreage, equestrian uses, events, conferences, wedding venues, restaurant and catering kitchens, fuel, hangars, tie-downs, and even the possibility of a longer future runway.

There is a kind of energy in those documents.

You can feel the dream trying to stand up.

The repeated idea is not merely that pilots would buy lots. The idea is that pilots would have a destination. They could land, eat, refuel, stay overnight, meet friends, attend an event, shoot skeet, ride horses, fish, hunt, use the RV park, rent a house, or build a second home with a hangar attached. The old aviation joke about the hundred-dollar hamburger becomes a business model. A pilot does not fly in only because there is a runway. He flies in because there is a reason to land.

That is a powerful idea.

It is also a dangerous idea if it becomes bigger than the wisdom needed to steward it.

This is where Providence Landing moves from discovery into testing.

The earliest version of the story has the feeling of a miracle. The later documents have the feeling of a pitch. Neither feeling is false. They are just different phases of the same story. First there was the road, the hidden target, Joan, Robie, the airfield, the sense of providence. Then came the decks, the spreadsheets, the investor language, the profit centers, the capital raise, the market research, the executive summary, the offering size, the preferred equity, the projected returns, the management team, and the pressure to make a vision sound fundable.

I do not say that critically.

I say it because that is exactly where many dreams are tested.

It is one thing to receive an idea. It is another thing to package the idea for investors. The moment a dream is put into a deck, it has to be translated into numbers. It has to be made legible to people who are not moved by the same internal sense of providence. It has to answer questions about supply and demand, returns, risk, capital structure, management, and exit strategy.

The Providence Landing files show that effort in detail.

There was research. Sixty-five airparks in surrounding states were compared. Available lots, runway surface, amenities, websites, restaurants, golf, RV camping, horses, fishing, hunting, and other features were examined. The materials argued that Providence Landing would offer a rare combination of amenities in a market with limited supply. That matters. It shows that Wes was not merely intoxicated by a spiritual impression. He was trying to test the idea against the market.

There was also a pilot survey.

That survey may be one of the most useful pieces of the whole folder, not because it proves the project would have worked, but because it holds confirmation and warning in the same hand.

The responses supported several parts of the vision. Pilots showed interest in organized fly-ins, eating by the landing strip, refueling and restroom access, short-term rentals, hangar homes, and referring others. That would have encouraged the dream.

But the survey also pushed back. One respondent said that to draw the flying crowd with money from the D.C. area, a short turf runway would not be enough. Larger aircraft would need more runway. Others wanted an IFR approach, a community center, and a pistol or rifle range. In other words, the market did not only say yes. It said, "Yes, but..."

That is often how wisdom sounds.

It does not always slam the door. Sometimes it says, "Slow down. This is real, but it is not simple."

That is the tension I see throughout Providence Landing. The opportunity was real. The dream was real. The market logic had real support. The property had real assets. Robie and Joan had done real work. Wes and Jenny had a real track record in real estate. The management team being assembled had real experience.

And still, none of that removed the need for discernment.

This is where the chapter should be careful. It should not mock the dream because the result was not what Wes wanted. It should not flatten the story into "we should have known better." That would be too easy, and probably untrue. A person can have legitimate favor, legitimate evidence, legitimate opportunity, legitimate relationships, and still move into a season where motives and methods have to be purified.

Providence Landing appears to be exactly that kind of season.

The management-team materials are especially revealing. Wes and Jenny were not presenting themselves as lone dreamers. Wes was framed as the visionary and CEO. Jenny was framed as the implementor and CFO. Timothy and Tammy Reather were positioned as aviation and marketing experts. Jonathan and Walter Brothers were the general contractors. Robie and Joan Potts were to remain involved with the airfield and back office. Adam Coffey appeared as an outside advisor. The broader folders around JT Foxx, Fred, George Ross, BNI, and the large presentations show a deliberate attempt to enter a larger business ecosystem.

That adds another layer to the story.

Providence Landing was not just about land. It became about credibility.

Could Wes and Jenny be seen as people who could do something this large? Could they move from Raleigh rehabs into a multi-million-dollar development? Could they speak the language of investors? Could they stand in rooms with people who talked about scale and capital and exits? Could they take a faith-born idea and make it survive in the world of investor scrutiny?

Those are not small questions.

They also touch pride.

I do not mean pride in the cartoonish sense. I mean the subtler kind. The kind that grows when a dream is so big that you begin to need other people to see it. The kind that wants confirmation. The kind that looks for mentors and platforms and rooms where the scale of the opportunity will be recognized. The kind that can confuse acceleration with approval.

That is not unique to Wes. That is human.

Every large assignment tests the difference between faith and momentum.

The spiritual element in Providence Landing is not only in the discovery. It is also in the missionary-restoration thread. Randy and Rebecca Dirks appear in the origin story as people who had carried a vision to serve missionaries returning from hard places. The marketing files connect that mission to the business plan. Providence Landing was imagined as a place where pilots, residents, guests, and missionaries could be restored. The off-season occupancy strategy was not only a business tactic. It was tied to people who needed rest, safety, and understanding.

That is one of the reasons the dream should not be dismissed.

There was ministry inside it.

There was an attempt to make the economics serve something larger than economics. The property could host church conferences, retreats, corporate gatherings, weddings, fly-ins, and missionary renewal. That is not a normal subdivision pitch. It shows the desire to build a place where commerce, aviation, hospitality, and spiritual restoration could meet.

That is beautiful.

It is also a lot to carry.

And that may be the lesson.

Providence Landing was too large to be carried by excitement alone. It needed unity. It needed sober counsel. It needed clean financial structure. It needed legal clarity. It needed market discipline. It needed patience. It needed the humility to let warning signs slow the pace. It needed the ability to distinguish between God's provision of an opportunity and God's permission to pursue every possible version of that opportunity.

Those are not the same thing.

Finding an airfield may have been providence.

Turning it into a capital raise was stewardship.

And stewardship is where the test came.

I keep coming back to the hidden target image because it holds both wonder and warning. Wes saw something he was not looking for. He turned around. He met Joan. Robie called. A name was born. Providence Landing felt provided.

But a hidden target is not the same as a finished assignment.

A target can tell you where to aim. It does not shoot the arrow for you. It does not account for wind, distance, strength, timing, or whether you should be the one holding the bow. It does not mean the first impulse after discovery is the correct plan.

In that sense, Providence Landing is a chapter about the difference between revelation and execution.

The revelation was the airfield.

The execution was the pitch decks, spreadsheets, surveys, investor calls, mentor networks, team-building, negotiations, financing structures, and all the unseen pressure that follows when a dream becomes public.

The documents show a sincere attempt to execute. They also show how quickly the dream became heavy. That heaviness is not failure by itself. Some dreams are supposed to be heavy. But if the weight of a dream begins to exceed the weight of peace, unity, and wisdom, something has to be examined.

That is what I think this chapter adds to *Gracious Millionaire*.

It is not merely "God showed me an airpark."

It is "God showed me an airpark, and then the airpark showed me me."

It showed ambition. It showed imagination. It showed courage. It showed the ability to see value where other people might not. It showed the desire to build something meaningful and profitable and restorative. It showed the power of relationships. It showed the danger of external validation. It showed the limits of enthusiasm. It showed the need for Jenny's caution, not as resistance to vision, but as part of the wisdom required to carry vision safely.

That may be the most important sentence I can write from the outside.

Jenny's caution was not the enemy of Providence Landing.

It may have been one of the graces inside it.

In stories like this, the visionary often remembers the open door more vividly than the warnings. That is understandable. The open door is dramatic. The warnings are usually quieter. They arrive as a question, a hesitation, a spreadsheet that does not quite settle, a conversation that leaves unease behind, a spouse who is not as excited, a survey response that complicates the plan, a mentor whose advice sounds big but not necessarily grounded.

Those quiet things are easy to resent when you are trying to move.

But in the Kingdom, restraint can be favor too.

That is what Providence Landing teaches from my point of view. Favor is not only the surprising driveway. Favor is also the uneasy feeling that asks for another look. Favor is not only the person who says, "This is for sale." Favor is also the person who says, "Are we sure?" Favor is not only the room full of investors. Favor is also the spreadsheet, the survey response, the spouse, the red flag, the delay, and the eventual disappointment that keeps a man from confusing scale with obedience.

Providence Landing did not become what Wes imagined.

At least not in the way he imagined.

But that does not make the story empty.

Some dreams are given to be built. Some dreams are given to expose what still needs to be built in us. Providence Landing may have been both. It was a real opportunity with real beauty inside it. It was also a mirror. It showed where faith and ambition can stand so close together that only testing can separate them.

That is why the chapter should not end in bitterness.

It should end in formation.

Wes found the airfield while he was looking for something else. Later, through the airfield, he found other things he was not looking for: the seduction of big numbers, the pressure of investor language, the need for better counsel, the importance of unity with Jenny, the difference between opportunity and assignment, and the humbling truth that a providential beginning does not guarantee an easy ending.

That is a gracious lesson.

Not an easy one.

But gracious.

Because grace does not only give us doors to walk through. Grace also teaches us how to walk. Sometimes it teaches us by letting us move forward. Sometimes it teaches us by letting us feel the cost of moving too fast. Sometimes it teaches us by giving us a dream large enough to reveal what still has to be surrendered before we can carry the next one.

Providence Landing was large enough to do that.

It was large enough to inspire.

It was large enough to tempt.

It was large enough to require God.

And maybe that is the simplest way to tell the story.

Wes thought he was looking for land.

He found an airfield.

Then, through the airfield, he found a classroom.
