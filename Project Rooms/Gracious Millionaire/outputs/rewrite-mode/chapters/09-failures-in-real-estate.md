# Failures In Real Estate

There were years before the success in real estate when real estate did not look like a calling, a classroom, or a blessing. It looked like another place where my life could come apart.

That belongs in this book.

Jenny and I have both lived through failed marriages. I do not want to expose every private detail or pretend that real estate explains what happened in those relationships. It does not. But it would be dishonest to tell the later stories of increase without acknowledging that property, debt, ambition, and loss were woven into some of the hardest years of my life.

My first house was in Florida with my first wife. I was self-employed in the sign business, and I built a garage behind the house for my shop. I had been operating that business for close to eight years when I began to feel that my life was not going in the direction I wanted. At thirty, I made a decision that must have looked reckless from the outside. We signed the deed over to my wife's parents, moved in with them, and I went to college full time.

Actually, I went double time. I finished a two-year associate degree at the community college in one year. Then we moved to Gainesville so I could study mechanical engineering at the University of Florida. The work was hard. While working part time and trying to pay the bills, it took me two years to complete what amounted to one year of the engineering program. When the financial aid ran out, we moved to North Carolina with the thought that I might finish school part time. That never happened. My career in computer programming began to develop instead.

In Johnston County, my first wife bought a worn little farmhouse. I remodeled it piece by piece. Someone used to say I burned the house down one splinter at a time, because the old wood went into the fire out back while new material slowly took its place.

That was the house I lost twice.

The first time was in my first divorce. My former wife took the house, moved out, and would not sell it to me. She sold it to someone else. We had four children, and I needed a home where they could stay when they were with me, so I bought the house back from the woman who had purchased it. I was living there when I married my second wife, whom I will call Number Two.

During that marriage, I began listening seriously to real estate teachers. The education mattered. It was the beginning of learning to see properties as more than buildings and mortgages. I began to see structures, possibilities, income, and leverage. But education is not maturity, and an idea that works on paper does not carry itself through pressure.

I had been a Christian all my life. My parents raised me with a deep respect for God and a clear sense that my life should be lived in relationship with Him. I knew the importance of confession and had beliefs rooted in childhood that remain with me. Yet I did not understand then what I now call walking in favor, and I was not consistently bringing my needs and decisions to God with the dependence I am trying to describe in this book. I do not say that to blame every loss on a deficient prayer life. I say it because the interior condition of the person making a plan matters, even when the plan itself is financially intelligent.

Number Two and I bought a home in Raleigh that had a finished three-bedroom, two-bath main floor and an unfinished basement of roughly the same size. I designed four bedrooms, two bathrooms, and a kitchen downstairs. Other than the HVAC and plumbing, I did nearly all the work with a general contractor. When it was finished, the family could live downstairs while we rented the upper floor. The rent covered the mortgage. Financially, the model worked.

But a sound model does not make a sound life by itself.

At the same time, we bought a rundown hotel near the highway between Houston and Galveston. It had about two hundred rooms, a restaurant, a bar, and three conference rooms. It was not operating when we bought it. I no longer have the acquisition records because they were maintained by Number Two through entities she controlled, so the figures in my memory are only approximate. I believe the purchase price was around three million dollars, with some cash paid and a promissory note to the seller for the rest.

We moved into one of the suites and lived inside the work. Rooms had to be repaired. Consultants helped us think through the restaurant and conference facilities. We catered events and opened enough rooms to begin receiving guests. It was exciting, demanding, and much larger than anything I had operated before.

The hotel never made money.

We had consultants helping us think about the restaurant and conference rooms, and we catered food for the events we attracted. We brought a portion of the guest rooms back into service while many others still needed work. Every improvement seemed to uncover another demand. We were not passive owners watching a manager rescue the asset. We were living in a suite, breathing the operation every day, and discovering that potential is not the same as an operating business.

As the losses grew, so did the stress in our marriage. We decided I would return permanently to Raleigh, find work, and try to slow the financial bleeding. Eventually we lost the hotel investment. I learned something about hotel operations, but I did not emerge qualified to teach anyone how to run one. Experience and expertise are not the same thing.

We also bought five duplexes in Johnston County around that period. All were occupied by tenants receiving what was then commonly called Section 8 assistance. I intended to manage them while moving back and forth between Raleigh and Houston. I did receive an education in property management, but much of it came through mistakes.

When the marriage began to fail under the weight of finances and other problems, I agreed to buy Number Two out of the duplexes. The loans were in her name. My mother and I formed an entity, repaid the investment Number Two had made, and took the deeds subject to the existing mortgages.

The divorce also cost me both the Raleigh house we had occupied and the farmhouse I had owned before the marriage. The farmhouse had been rented during our marriage. In the settlement, Number Two took both. That was the second time I lost the farmhouse.

I moved into an apartment where my children could stay with me. After a little more than a year, when all but my youngest were old enough to move out, I bought an RV large enough for me to live in and for my youngest son to stay with me. I parked it behind one of the duplexes so I could watch the properties and manage them closely.

For a while, the five duplexes produced enough cash to cover their expenses. Then vacancies and rent problems began to pull them behind. This was during the 2008 mortgage crisis. The bank had little tolerance for delinquency, and I did not have the experience or education to find another path. One by one, the duplexes went into foreclosure.

Over about five years, we had acquired a hotel, a residence, five rental duplexes, and other investments. By the end of that season, the investments were gone and the marriage had ended.

I can look backward and see choices I would not make now. I can see ambition without enough order, financial exposure without enough margin, and education that had not yet become judgment. I can also see that my relationship with God was different.

I used to think the later success could be explained by one great contrast: then I was not walking in favor, and now I am. I still believe favor matters, but the contrast has become more demanding as I have examined it. The later business also had better systems, clearer processes, more developed skills, and relationships we did not have before. Grace does not require me to deny those natural differences. It requires me to hold them without making myself the author of every good result.

Failure does not prove that God was absent. Success does not prove that I have finally learned how to make Him cooperate.

The years that followed would include better systems, stronger relationships, private money, profitable properties, and what I understand as favor. But those later results cannot be told honestly unless the losses stay in the story. I had already learned how to work hard, build things, and see potential. I had not learned how little those abilities could guarantee.

That lesson is still not finished. When a plan works now, I still feel the temptation to explain the outcome by the quality of the plan. When it fails, I still want a clean diagnosis. The earlier years warn me against both habits. Some losses can be studied. Some can be repaired. Some remain losses.

Real estate was not my friend then. Perhaps it was already becoming a classroom.
