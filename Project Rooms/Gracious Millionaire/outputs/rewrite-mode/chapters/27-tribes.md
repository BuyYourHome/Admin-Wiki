# Tribes

Years ago, I wrote one sentence under the heading *Tribes*: "Working on something you believe in is much more satisfying than working for a paycheck. This is the answer to the help shortage."

It sounded like a conclusion. It was really a beginning.

People do want meaningful work. A paycheck can compensate time and skill, but it cannot by itself create belonging. It cannot make a person believe that the work matters. It cannot turn a group of people into a tribe.

A paycheck is not unimportant. People have obligations, and meaningful language does not pay a mortgage or buy groceries. Work that claims a noble purpose while treating compensation as beneath discussion is not more spiritual. If the work matters, then the people doing it matter in practical ways. Meaning has to include fairness, clarity, and respect for the life a person has outside the assignment.

What the sentence was reaching for was the difference between labor that is merely exchanged and work in which a person can recognize part of a calling. When people believe the outcome serves someone beyond the person receiving the profit, effort takes on another dimension. Creativity appears. Responsibility becomes more than compliance. The work can become a place where gifts are discovered and strengthened.

But that kind of belonging cannot be demanded. It grows when purpose and practice agree.

Yet belief alone is not enough either. People have been asked to sacrifice for visions that mainly served the person casting them. A leader can speak about mission while expecting everyone else to absorb the cost. A business can call itself a family when what it really wants is loyalty without mutual obligation. Meaningful language does not guarantee meaningful work.

The stories in this book have made that distinction harder to ignore. Vision matters. So do systems, records, agreements, patience, and the effect of our decisions on the people closest to us. A cause does not excuse disorder. A calling does not remove the need to listen. Favor does not make every idea an assignment. Abundance does not belong to the leader alone.

If a tribe is worth belonging to, the work must be larger than one person's ambition and honest enough to survive questions.

It must also remain open enough to receive people who do not resemble the leader. A tribe built around one personality eventually rewards imitation. A healthier community makes room for different strengths, different histories, and different ways of recognizing what the work requires. Agreement on purpose does not require sameness of temperament.

That is especially important for us because this is not Wes's story with Jenny standing nearby. Jenny's experience did not begin when she entered Wes's real estate work. Her caution, losses, prayer, planning, and endurance were already forming her. Wes's vision and systems did not make those qualities valuable. They were valuable before the two stories converged.

The same principle applies beyond a marriage. People do not become supporting characters because they join someone else's assignment. They bring their own histories, gifts, limits, and ways of seeing. The person who notices danger may be serving the mission as surely as the person who notices opportunity. The person who asks for documentation may be protecting what enthusiasm would otherwise expose. The person who waits may understand something the person who moves has not yet seen.

Belonging should make those differences useful rather than inconvenient.

This may be part of the answer to the help shortage, but it is not a staffing technique. You cannot manufacture a tribe by attaching purpose language to a job description. People recognize the difference between being invited into meaningful responsibility and being recruited to carry someone else's dream. They recognize whether authority protects and empowers or merely directs.

Meaningful work has to become measurable. Does the work serve people? Are promises kept? Are burdens acknowledged? Can disagreement be spoken without threatening belonging? Is increase shared in a way that reflects the values being preached? Do the systems support the people, or do the people exist to feed the systems?

Those questions belong at the end of this book because the book does not offer a finished model. Real estate gave us work we believed in. It also gave us enough pressure to reveal that belief is not the same as maturity. We have seen vision produce energy, and we have seen the need to surrender a vision. We have experienced the value of caution and the frustration of delay. We have wanted to serve people and have had to examine the places where service, influence, and control can become entangled.

So the tribe we are looking for is not a circle of people who agree with us. It is a community in which calling and character are formed together. It is a place where work can matter without becoming an idol, where increase can be pursued without replacing trust, and where unfinished people can tell the truth about what they have not yet learned.

Such a community is not held together by constant success. If belonging depends on the project working, the profit arriving, or the leader always being right, it will break at the first serious test. The stronger bond is a shared willingness to serve the purpose, tell the truth, and remain teachable when circumstances expose what enthusiasm concealed.

That includes us.

We believe work can be more than a paycheck. We believe property, money, systems, and influence can be used for the good of others. We also know that every one of those things can become a substitute for lordship. The final lesson is not that we found the perfect way to build a business or a community. It is that belonging requires the same surrender as abundance.

The work must not merely be ours.

It must be something we can faithfully belong to together.
