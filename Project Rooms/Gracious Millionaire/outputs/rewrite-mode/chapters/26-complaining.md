# Complaining

I have written some severe sentences about complaining.

Complaining is the language of unbelief. When the lordship of Jesus is compromised, complaining will be the evidence. Nobody complains who sees God's role in the middle of the situation. Complaining is almost always reasonable if we remove the presence and promises of God. Complaining distorts our view of reality.

Those sentences are easier to admire than to live.

They can also be used badly. A person in pain can be silenced by someone who calls every honest description of suffering a complaint. A spouse can avoid listening by turning a spiritual principle into a verdict. A leader can use positive language to keep other people from naming the cost of the leader's decisions. If this chapter becomes a way to deny fear, grief, injustice, or practical danger, then the chapter itself will distort reality.

The stories in this book give us no permission to do that. Financial pressure was real. Marriages ended. Plans failed. Projects strained relationships. A lawsuit created expense and fear even after much of the exposure was reduced. Waiting did not become easy because we believed God was present. Jenny's caution was not unbelief merely because Wes could see a possibility beyond it.

So the distinction cannot be between speaking and silence, or between pleasant words and unpleasant ones. Some things have to be said.

The deeper question is what our words train us to see.

Complaint can begin with an accurate fact and still become a false account of the whole. The door closed. The money is not there. The person did not keep a promise. The risk is larger than expected. The plan is not working. Any of those statements may be true. Distortion begins when the true statement becomes the only reality we will acknowledge.

There is another distortion that can hide inside complaint: it can make us the innocent center of every story. If the problem is always what someone else did, what circumstances withheld, or what God has not yet explained, then complaint protects us from examining our own part. It can sound like discernment while functioning as self-justification.

The alternative is not false blame. We should not accept responsibility for another person's conduct simply to sound humble. But we can ask what remains ours to govern. Did we move too quickly? Did we listen? Did we document what needed to be documented? Did we make an opportunity carry more of our identity than it was meant to carry? Did we speak about another person in a way that made reconciliation harder?

Those questions return agency without pretending we control the outcome. Complaint often leaves us powerful enough to accuse and powerless to change. Responsibility is more demanding. It asks us to act faithfully within our actual authority and release what is outside it.

That is why complaining is so often reasonable. It can assemble evidence. It can explain exactly why discouragement makes sense. It can produce a detailed account in which God is absent, grace is irrelevant, other people are reduced to obstacles, and our own interpretation is beyond question. Nothing in the account has to be fabricated for the account itself to be incomplete.

Fear has the same narrowing effect. It makes one possible outcome feel inevitable and one danger feel ultimate. We have both experienced circumstances where fear had evidence. Gratitude did not erase that evidence. Trust did not make risk imaginary. The work was to keep the danger from becoming lord.

This is where words matter. "I will say of the Lord, He is my refuge and my fortress" is not a magical technique for changing circumstances. It is a declaration of where confidence belongs while circumstances remain capable of hurting us. Praise does not bribe God. It reorders attention.

I have also written that the measure of what I receive is set by what comes out of my mouth. That line can sound like a formula, and formulas are one of the dangers this book is trying to resist. Words are not a machine for producing money, favorable rulings, or successful properties. We cannot speak a preferred ending into existence and call the result faith.

But speech does have consequences. What I repeat becomes easier to notice. What I name shapes the atmosphere in which a decision is made. What I say about Jenny can either honor the caution she brings or reduce her to resistance. What I say about a difficult owner, investor, attorney, or project can either preserve that person's humanity or make contempt feel justified. What we say in private eventually affects how we act in public.

Some things have to be said because silence can also distort reality. A concern left unnamed does not disappear; it often becomes resentment. A mistake hidden behind spiritual confidence cannot be corrected. A burden one spouse carries alone does not become lighter because the other spouse prefers peace. Truthful speech is part of stewardship.

The test is not whether the words are negative. The test is what the words are serving. Are they seeking understanding, protection, repentance, or a wise next step? Or are they rehearsing injury until injury becomes identity? The same facts can be spoken toward responsibility or spoken toward contempt. Tone alone does not decide the difference. The posture of the heart does.

The fruit of the mouth is not only an external result. It is also the person our words are forming us to become.

This is why complaint can compromise leadership. A leader who continually narrates other people as the problem will eventually stop listening to them. A person who narrates every delay as opposition may force an outcome that required patience. A person who narrates every success as favor may become unable to recognize correction. The language can remain religious while the heart becomes less submitted.

The question at the end of the early notes was blunt: If God occupies my praise, who occupies my complaints?

I do not think the answer is that we should never speak about what is wrong. The Psalms do not read that way, and neither do our lives. The answer is that honest speech should remain open to the presence of God, the dignity of other people, and the possibility that our own interpretation is incomplete.

That kind of honesty sounds different from accusation. It can say, "This has hurt us," without saying, "Nothing good is possible." It can say, "I am afraid," without enthroning fear as prophet. It can say, "We made a mistake," without turning confession into hopelessness. It can say, "I do not understand what God is doing," without deciding that God is doing nothing.

We have not mastered this. The pressure described throughout this book did not always call forth our best words. The point is not to rewrite those moments as if we passed every test. The point is to notice that language reveals what is governing attention.

Complaining distorts reality not because every complaint is factually wrong, but because it reduces reality to the part we resent. Praise restores proportion. Gratitude restores memory. Honest lament keeps pain in the conversation without giving pain the final word.

The unfinished work is learning to speak truthfully when fear has a reasonable case. Not positively. Not mechanically. Truthfully. That means naming what is wrong, remembering who is present, and refusing to make our worst interpretation the only story available.
