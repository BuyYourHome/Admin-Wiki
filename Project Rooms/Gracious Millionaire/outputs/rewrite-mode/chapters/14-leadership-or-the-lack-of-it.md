# Leadership Or The Lack Of It

I have often thought I would be a great leader if I could only get people to follow me.

That sentence is funny because it is true, and troubling for the same reason.

I have always been a planner. I put plans on paper. I see systems, sequence, opportunity, and what I believe could happen if the right people would do the right things at the right time. Very few of those plans have unfolded the way I expected.

I made three separate attempts at being in business. The first two did not become what I planned. The real estate business has produced better results, but success in the third attempt does not retroactively prove that every instinct behind it was leadership.

For much of my life, I wanted to be influential. I sat on boards and expected other people to recognize the wisdom of ideas I presented. I could be patient for a while. I understood that a good plan needed explanation. But when associates, family members, or people serving with me did not embrace what I was engineering, patience often became frustration. Sometimes the anger was thinly veiled. Sometimes it was not veiled at all.

The sequence was predictable. I would begin by explaining the destination. If that did not work, I would provide more detail. Then I would answer objections, restate the benefits, and wait for the other person to see what seemed obvious to me. With every round, I could tell myself I was being patient because I had not yet demanded agreement. But patience that exists only until another person reaches my conclusion is a form of pressure with a timer on it.

This showed up most painfully at home. When my spouse was not in lockstep with a goal, or when my children did not respond as I expected, I could treat their independence as resistance to something obviously right. I was measuring leadership by agreement with me.

Thinking I know how another person should act is not leadership.

Leadership begins with being led. For me, that means being in right relationship with God, listening for His voice, and holding my plans open to correction. It may include explaining what I believe He is saying. It does not give me the right to attach God's authority to my preferences.

That distinction is especially important in a marriage. Saying, "I believe this is what God wants," can end a conversation that ought to remain open. The spiritual language may be sincere and still place an unfair burden on the person who disagrees. If I am truly listening, I have to allow that God may use Jenny's lack of peace, a practical concern, or an inconvenient delay as part of His leadership of me.

That is where vision becomes dangerous. A detailed plan can feel like confirmation because so much thought has gone into it. A spreadsheet can make an outcome look inevitable. An ability to answer objections can feel like evidence that the objector simply does not understand. But persuasion is not the same as submission, and pressure is not the same as leadership.

Bill Johnson has said, "If you build it, you have to support it. If God builds it, He supports it."

The thought challenges the way I carry plans. If an idea is truly God's, I do not have to force every person around me into immediate agreement. I can explain it, work faithfully, pray, and wait. If the plan collapses the moment I stop applying pressure, I need to consider what was actually holding it together.

The development of this book became a small but revealing example. At one stage I had produced a large amount of manuscript material while Jenny had not yet begun submitting her own writing. She was interested. She lived inside many of the stories. She had a point of view that often differed from mine. I wanted the book to become a conversation in which our individual and shared lessons could meet.

I also wanted that collaboration on my schedule.

I could not make her write. Any sustained attempt to push her into my process would have worked against the book I said I wanted. If her voice arrived under pressure, it would not be a free contribution. Leadership required me to believe in the value of her story while allowing her to enter the work in her own way.

Now her chapters can stand in her voice. That change exposes the weakness in the earlier structure. When only my material was present, it was easy for the manuscript to make me the narrator of our shared life and Jenny the person I described. Better leadership did not mean finding a more skillful way to place her inside my version. It meant making room for a version that could correct mine.

This is a lesson identified, not mastered.

The same tension appeared in the unfinished trade involving the Orphaned Property. A willing buyer wanted to exchange other properties for equity in ours. I had done extensive analysis and prepared documentation for a proposal and eventual closing. From my perspective, the transaction looked profitable and timely.

Jenny did not want the properties being offered in trade.

I could not persuade her otherwise. I might have pushed through with an offer, but she would not have been in agreement. When it was time to meet the buyer and his English-speaking associate, I did not have room to negotiate in the direction they seemed to require.

Left alone, I believe I might have found enough common ground to make a deal. That belief is part of the problem. I usually think I am right about these things. Jenny often thinks she is right too. I value her judgment, even when I have difficulty showing that value because I disagree with her conclusion.

There is an asymmetry between caution and action. When caution is wrong, the opportunity simply does not happen, and it can be difficult to prove what would have followed. When my action is wrong, the evidence may be visible in losses, broken relationships, unfinished projects, or obligations that remain after the excitement passes.

That does not make caution automatically right. It makes overriding it more consequential.

The unfinished trade belongs later in this book, where its details and waiting can be allowed to remain unresolved. Here, its leadership question is enough. What does a leader do when he sees a solution, explains it, and still does not receive agreement from the person whose life will also be changed by the decision?

Manipulation can be forceful, but it can also be soft. It can sound patient while arranging the conversation so that only one answer counts as reasonable. It can invoke urgency, lost profit, preparation already completed, or the disappointment of other people. It can call resistance a lack of vision.

Real estate supplies all of those arguments. A foreclosure has a date. A buyer may walk away. A lender may be waiting. Analysis may show that every month of delay costs money. Those facts can be real and still become tools of control when I use them to make another person's consent feel impossible to withhold. Urgency may require a quick decision. It does not decide what that decision must be.

Leadership has to be something else.

It may be seeing ahead and accepting that others do not yet see what I see. It may be building an example instead of winning an argument. It may be waiting while an opportunity changes or disappears. It may be discovering that the person slowing me down noticed something I missed.

Christian leadership rests on a conviction that I am not the final protector of God's plan. If all things can work together for good for those who love Him and are called according to His purpose, then delay is not automatically defeat. Another person's disagreement is not automatically sabotage. My inability to complete a transaction is not proof that the transaction was stolen from me.

I do not know whether the delayed trade will prove Jenny's caution wise. I do not know whether the time it took for her voice to enter this book was the correct path. I know that forcing either outcome would have created a different problem.

Leadership requires patience when things are not going my way and people are not doing things my way. It requires enough confidence to speak and enough humility to stop speaking. It requires me to ask whether I am following God or merely asking God to endorse a plan I am determined to lead.

I can see the lesson clearly.

Living it is harder.
