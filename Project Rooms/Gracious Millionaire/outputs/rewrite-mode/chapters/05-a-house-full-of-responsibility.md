# A House Full Of Responsibility

In 1981, our son was born, and I left the full-time job I had never especially liked. I did not return to life as an employee during the years I was raising our children.

Twenty-three months later, I gave birth to twin girls.

They were a surprise. Number One joked that I had wanted and prayed so hard for a girl that the Lord gave me two. I could laugh at that, but the practical reality arrived immediately. We had three babies under the age of two. Our starter home no longer fit us, and neither did our Cutlass sedan. Three car seats would not fit across the back seat of what had been our family car.

We bought a larger, newly built house for $99,000, with room for three cribs. We also bought a used Dodge Ram van with seating for eight. I had no ambition to fill the remaining seats. Three babies were more than enough.

The size of our life had changed almost overnight. So had the financial pressure. Interest rates were high, and at one point I remember our mortgage rate being 13 3/4 percent. The number looks startling now, but at the time it was attached to the place where our children slept and where most of my days unfolded.

Those days were filled with diapers, nursing, crying babies, meals, laundry, and the constant work of keeping very small people safe. I had little time or energy left for anyone else. Domestic responsibility can disappear when a business story is told. It should not. This was work, and it was formation. It taught me to organize, endure, notice risk, and carry needs that did not wait until I felt rested.

There was no clean boundary between financial life and family life. The van loan existed because three car seats would not fit in the sedan. The larger mortgage existed because the starter home could not easily hold three cribs. The expenses were not luxuries detached from the children; they were attached to the practical shape of caring for them. That did not make the payments smaller. It made the pressure more personal.

Looking back, I can also imagine how hard that season was for Number One. He was carrying a large mortgage, a sizable used-car loan, and the pressure of providing while trying to establish himself in remodeling. I was absorbed by three babies because they required me to be. He may have experienced that necessary absorption as neglect.

We could barely make ends meet. We fell behind on bills, caught up, and then repeated the cycle. Number One responded to the pressure in ways that made the problem worse: not working full time and spending money we did not have. That pattern did not end quickly. Over time, it contributed to the ending of the marriage.

The cycle itself became a kind of instability. Catching up brought relief, but not safety, because the next shortfall was already possible. For someone who values predictability, living that way keeps the mind working even when the body needs rest. A bill is not only a bill. It is another sign that the plan may not hold.

It is tempting to arrange this period into a simple lesson about living within one's means. The numbers certainly mattered. A large mortgage at a high interest rate creates pressure whether or not a person has good intentions. Income instability and uncontrolled spending have consequences.

But the story was not only a budget failure. It was a house full of people whose needs collided. Babies required care. A young business required work. A marriage required attention. Bills required money. Exhaustion narrowed what each of us could see in the other.

I can now acknowledge Number One's pressure without changing what his choices did to us. Compassion does not require pretending that inconsistent work and spending beyond our means were harmless. At the same time, assigning all failure to one person would give me a cleaner story than the one I actually have. I was depleted. He was under pressure. The children were appropriately demanding. The marriage absorbed what none of us knew how to resolve.

My instinct was to create safety through planning and responsibility. When the household became less predictable, I tightened my grip on what I could organize. I do not know that I had another way then. I do know that good planning did not make me unlimited. I could not meet every need, control another person's choices, or guarantee that sacrifice would produce the future I expected.

Years later, real estate would present responsibility in different forms: association assessments, market collapse, tenants, records, and business decisions. The house with three babies came first. It taught me that stewardship is not an abstract virtue. It is what a person does when several legitimate needs arrive at once and the available money, time, and strength do not seem large enough for all of them.

The lesson I still have not fully learned is how to carry responsibility without believing everything depends on my ability to hold it together. Responsibility is real. So are human limits. Trust does not remove the mortgage or quiet three babies. It does change where I look for the strength to live inside what I cannot control.
