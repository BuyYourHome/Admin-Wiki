# Foreword

This is not a how-to book. Maybe it is a how-to-be book.

There are already many books about success, prosperity, investing, and the habits that are supposed to produce them. We have read some of those books. We have learned from them. This book is not an argument against practical wisdom, education, planning, or disciplined work. It is simply not our attempt to add one more formula to the shelf.

The problem with a formula is not always that it is false. Often it contains something useful. The problem comes when a principle that should lead us into relationship becomes a substitute for relationship. Good theology can describe God and still leave a person satisfied without encountering Him. A sound business principle can improve a result and still leave the person using it unchanged. Head knowledge and heart knowledge are not enemies, but they are not the same thing. A philosophy becomes a life only when it reaches our obedience.

*The Gracious Millionaire* is our story of real estate and the lessons we have learned, or are still learning, while living it. Real estate is the setting because that is where so many of the questions became concrete. Money had to be borrowed or repaid. Houses had to be bought, repaired, managed, or sold. People had to decide whether to trust us. We had to decide whether to trust one another. Opportunity had to be weighed against peace. A profitable idea could still be the wrong assignment. A loss could expose something more valuable than the money it took away.

The Kingdom of God, as we understand it, touches every part of life. It does not stop at the door of a church or remain safely inside a devotional thought. It reaches our finances, our ambitions, our marriages, our fears, our work, and the way we treat people when something valuable is at stake. That is the point of view from which we write. A reader does not have to begin from the same place to enter these stories, but it would be dishonest for us to hide the place from which we are looking.

That point of view makes the ordinary choices important. Love and fear can both produce activity. Prayer, declaration, influence, and manipulation can sound similar when a person is determined to reach an outcome. The difference is not merely the language being used. It is who is Lord, what spirit governs the action, and whether the people involved are being served or controlled. Real estate gave those questions names, dollar amounts, deadlines, and consequences.

One of the questions behind this book is simple: How much money is too much money?

We do not believe the answer is an amount. Money becomes too much when the desire for it replaces trust in God. That can happen to someone who has very little, and it can happen to someone who has more than enough. The number itself does not settle the question of lordship.

We want to be successful. We have wanted to be wealthy. Pretending otherwise would make the book less honest before the first property appears. The question is what happens when success, prosperity, influence, or even a good opportunity moves ahead of God's purpose. We have found that external increase does not settle the internal question. Triumph and disaster can both misname us if we let the outcome tell us who we are.

That is why this book cannot be a celebration of our cleverness. Skill matters. Systems matter. Research, caution, persistence, and experience matter. Yet none of them explains a life by itself. We have lived through success and failure, protection and carelessness, gain and loss, confidence and fear. At times we recognized grace while it was happening. At other times we called our own planning the explanation until experience forced us to look again.

We are also writing as two people, not as one central character with a spouse standing in the background. Some stories belong chiefly to Wes because he lived them and supplied them. Some belong to Jenny. Some cannot be told truthfully unless both perspectives remain visible. Our instincts are not identical. Vision and caution can pull in different directions, and either one can be wise or distorted. Part of our formation has been learning that unity is not produced by one person telling a convincing story until the other person agrees.

Even the word *our* has to be earned. It is easy to place two names on a cover and continue telling the story through one set of eyes. Shared authorship asks more of us. It requires us to let a remembered event change when the other person describes the burden she carried, the warning he missed, or the meaning neither of us could see alone. We do not need identical voices. We need truthful ones.

The most important lessons in these pages are not lessons we claim to have mastered. In every chapter we want to ask not only, "What did this teach us?" but also, "What can we see now that we still do not consistently live?" A tidy ending can be a kind of dishonesty. Some transactions remain unresolved. Some relationships do not return to the form we wanted. Some decisions can be understood only in part. Faith does not require us to pretend otherwise.

So we offer these stories as testimony, not proof of a formula. We will tell you about houses, mortgages, private money, systems, negotiations, lawsuits, development plans, and the strange turns by which an ordinary business decision can become a spiritual examination. We will also tell you where memory is incomplete, where motives became mixed, and where we are still waiting to understand what obedience required.

Real estate may be the classroom, but the subject is trust. The examination is not merely whether a deal worked. It is what the deal revealed about the people carrying it.

That is the story we are trying to tell.
