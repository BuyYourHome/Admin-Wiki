# Providence Landing: The Vision

Providence Landing was the biggest project Jenny and I had ever attempted. It eventually became a development plan, an investor presentation, a set of companies, a network of advisers, a capital model, and more work than I knew how to measure. But it did not begin with any of those things. It began with a fight.

Jenny and I had been arguing about the business. The conversation became heated, and I told her I was going away for a few days because I needed a break from our relationship. I packed hurriedly, took my computer, and left without a real plan. I told her I would come back, but that was about the clearest thing I did in that moment.

I am not proud of that beginning. It would be easier to tell the Providence Landing story as a clean sequence of inspiration, opportunity, and vision. That version would make me look better. It would also be false. I left home carrying frustration, not peace. Whatever I later believed God was showing me, I first had to admit the condition I was in when I went looking.

For several months I had been thinking about an airpark, a residential community built around a runway where pilots could live near their airplanes. I was also frequently looking for acreage that might be bought with terms we could afford, much as we had learned to buy houses. A man in Virginia had told me through Facebook Messenger that he had about eighty acres for sale. With no better plan for where to go, I aimed the truck toward Virginia.

It rained hard most of the day. The directions I had were incomplete, but I eventually found the owner and toured his land. The price was not unreasonable, and the property interested me, but it would not work for an airpark. I thanked him and drove away as the rain intensified.

By then I was wet, tired, and depressed. I found a hotel in South Boston, bought some groceries, changed clothes, and tried to settle myself. I knew I had treated Jenny badly. I knew leaving the way I had left was not leadership. I knew I was not in a condition to trust every strong thought that entered my mind.

The next morning I ate, showered, and fell back on the bed. I looked at the ceiling as if God might be visible there and said aloud, "God, I do not know if You can speak to me, but I am listening."

I heard nothing.

So I did the next ordinary thing. I opened my laptop and began searching for vacant land in southern Virginia.

Several large rural parcels looked worth seeing. They were about twenty-five miles away, reached by back roads through country I did not know. I put an address into the GPS and started driving. When the phone showed that I was about half a mile from the destination, I began watching the left side of the road.

Instead, something on the right caught my attention. I passed an obvious development with good-looking horse pastures bordered by white fences. A quarter mile later I passed a paved driveway. As I cleared a thicket beyond it, the view opened. There was an airfield. I saw a windsock, small commercial-looking buildings, a hangar, and airplanes.

Nothing in the listing I was following had mentioned a runway. The vacant land I intended to inspect was farther down the road and on the opposite side. The two properties were not connected. I had already passed the driveway, so I backed the truck, turned in, and pulled cautiously into a parking space.

I got out and stood still, looking around the way I often did when approaching a house whose owner I hoped to meet. A woman came out and asked whether she could help me. I explained that I was looking at land because I had been thinking about building an airpark, and I wondered whether I could speak with the owner about how this one had been developed.

She introduced herself and told me the property was for sale. I would have to speak with her husband, who was not home, but she could ask him to call. We exchanged numbers, and I left overwhelmed by the coincidence. Before I had driven half an hour, he called. The cell service was poor, but I learned enough to ask whether we could continue the conversation after I reached home.

That call began a long, expensive, and deeply educational relationship.

The property was not simply an airstrip with a few houses. It covered roughly three hundred acres in the early descriptions, with dozens of subdivided building lots, existing structures, and roads that had been laid out or partly started. Later promotional language described an even larger community and broader mission. The exact scope shifted as ownership lists, maps, acreage, and plans were examined. Those discrepancies should have taught us early that the vision would be easier to describe than to execute.

The early records made that problem concrete. One description identified more than ninety subdivided plots, with room for additional development. Some roads had been laid out and begun as tar-and-chip roads, but substantial work remained. When I compared the sellers' property list with the development map, lots shown as sold in one place appeared available in another. Other lots appeared on the sellers' list while the map suggested different ownership. Acreage figures also varied between the map and the list. These were not philosophical objections to the vision. They were ordinary diligence questions about what land was actually included and what could legally and economically be developed.

I responded the way I usually respond to disorder: I made lists. I marked the mismatches, asked questions, and tried to turn uncertain information into a structure we could evaluate. That work was necessary. It also fed my confidence that any problem could be solved if I organized it carefully enough.

Still, the possibilities were hard for me to resist. I could see homes for pilots, fly-ins, gathering spaces, events, trails, and places where meaningful work and ministry might meet. The plan expanded to include a Kingdom-centered community and a place of rest for missionaries returning from the field. Prayer became part of the team's regular rhythm. The mission language was not decoration added after the business plan. We were trying to understand whether the land could support both economic return and service.

The business structure grew quickly. We considered holding and operating companies, options, shares, promissory notes, investor-funded development phases, roads, model homes, a welcome center, and event facilities. We sought advice from people with development, legal, investment, and operating experience. We studied other airparks and built databases of airplane owners. At one point our market work included thousands of aircraft-owner records and comparisons across dozens of airpark communities.

The vision also acquired numbers large enough to make confidence sound like certainty. A capital model showed millions of dollars in first-year expenditures and substantial negative cash flow before later revenue was expected to mature. Investor materials eventually described minimum investments, projected returns, and a five-year horizon. We built presentations sophisticated enough to sit in front of accredited investors. The project became real enough to have bank accounts, legal entities, agreements, reporting obligations, and all the administrative drag that accompanies an actual enterprise.

The marketing ideas were equally tangible. We considered weekend barbecues, fly-ins, meal vouchers for visiting pilots, a driving range, skeet shooting, model homes, and a welcome center. We assembled information on Virginia aircraft owners and compared other airpark communities. The more specific the ideas became, the easier it was to picture families arriving by plane, walking the land, and imagining a home there.

Alongside that commercial picture, members of the team kept a weekly prayer rhythm. One friend proposed recording prayer requests so we could later review what God had done. Draft outreach described a place where missionaries returning from the field might rest, walk trails, meet with trained people, and recover before returning to service. We were not merely trying to attach a charitable contribution to a subdivision. We were imagining the land itself as a setting for restoration.

That mixture made discernment harder. A project offering profit alone can be judged largely by risk and return. A project carrying prayer, community, and care for missionaries can begin to feel morally protected from ordinary questions. Yet the more sacred the stated purpose, the more carefully we needed to test whether the operation beneath it could carry the promise.

We named it Providence Landing because it seemed provided by God.

That name carried both faith and danger. Once I had interpreted the discovery as providence, every next step could feel like confirmation. The runway appeared while I was searching for unrelated land. The owners called. Advisers came. Relationships formed. Plans became more detailed. Each development made the original moment feel more significant.

But detail is not the same as direction, and momentum is not the same as permission.

Jenny and I both saw red flags. We proceeded anyway. We committed time, money, energy, and imagination. We met remarkable people and learned things we could not have learned from a smaller project. Yet even at the beginning, the story held a tension I did not know how to resolve: I had found the property while running from conflict with my wife, and I was soon asking my wife to help carry the largest vision I had ever brought home.

The airfield was real. The opportunity was real. The work was real. The sense of providence was real to me. None of that, by itself, answered the question that would govern everything that followed.

Was this vision ours to carry?
