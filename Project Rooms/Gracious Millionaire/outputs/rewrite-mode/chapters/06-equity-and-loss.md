# Equity And Loss

I never expected to be divorced.

The marriage to Number One lasted twenty-five years. By the time it ended, our children were out of the house and away at college. We sold the home we had once bought because three cribs would not fit in the starter house. After all those years, the sale produced several hundred thousand dollars more than we owed. We divided the proceeds and went our separate ways.

The children had grown beyond the rooms we once needed so urgently. The van with eight seats and the season of three car seats belonged to the past. Yet the house remained tied to that whole period: the high interest rate, the bills we fell behind on and caught up, the remodeling business, the marriage under strain, and the years in which a family became older inside its walls.

That is the financial summary. It is accurate, but it is not the whole event.

A house can hold equity and history at the same time. The sales price can measure what the market will pay. It cannot measure the years of raising children, the strain of unpaid bills, the work that kept a household functioning, or the expectations that were present when the mortgage was signed. It cannot calculate what it means to leave a marriage a person believed would last.

The equity was real. We had bought the house for $99,000 and eventually sold it with substantial value above the debt. That increase gave each of us something tangible at the end. But the language of gain becomes misleading if it is allowed to describe the whole transaction. I did not enter the sale as an investor harvesting a successful return. I entered it as a woman leaving a twenty-five-year marriage.

The money mattered. It gave me resources with which to begin another season. But proceeds are not the same as restoration. Dividing equity did not divide the past into clean, equal portions. It did not turn the end of the marriage into a successful investment outcome.

In 2002, I moved to Raleigh. I was no longer the twenty-three-year-old woman buying a first home with two incomes and a conventional mortgage. I had raised three children. I had lived through years of financial pressure. I had watched patterns in a marriage become strong enough to help end it. I had money from the home sale, but I also had the knowledge that plans could fail in ways a calculation could not predict.

I had once believed divorce would never be part of my life. I could not know then that another marriage would also end. From where I stand now, both belong to my story, but they should not be collapsed into one generic statement about failure. Number One included twenty-five years, three children, and a home full of responsibility. Number Two would be brief, abrupt, and frightening in different ways.

My instinct was still to make careful decisions. Security still mattered to me. So did integrity, predictability, and understanding the numbers. Those traits had not disappeared because life had proved their limits. If anything, loss can make a cautious person more determined to avoid the next mistake.

What I did next was both independent and familiar. I took much of the home-sale proceeds and invested it in an oceanfront condominium in Garden City Beach, South Carolina. I did not buy it as an escape or as a future home. I bought it as an investment, and I approached it as one. I calculated vacation rental income, mortgage payments, insurance, management costs, and homeowners association fees.

That decision belongs to the next chapter. What matters here is the threshold.

The equity from the family home became capital for a new property. On paper, one asset had been sold and another acquired. In life, the movement was not so clean. The money came out of a marriage that had ended. It carried both provision and loss.

That complexity matters to the rest of this book. Money is rarely only money. It can be stored labor, delayed desire, family history, freedom, fear, or the means to begin again. The same dollar can be evidence of increase and a reminder of what had to end before it became available.

I still prefer categories that are black and white. Equity is measurable. Loss is not. One can be entered into a spreadsheet; the other changes the person holding the spreadsheet.

I cannot make the end of that marriage into a tidy lesson. I can say that God did not abandon me in it. I can say that the proceeds gave me room to act. I can also say that financial provision did not erase grief or prove that every choice that followed would be safe.

Sometimes the most honest thing we can do is refuse to call survival victory too quickly.
