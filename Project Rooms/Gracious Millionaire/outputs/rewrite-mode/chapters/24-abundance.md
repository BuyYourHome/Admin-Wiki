# Abundance

For much of this book, money has been present even when it was not the subject. It was present when there was not enough, when a property looked promising, when an investor trusted us, when a plan became larger than our ability to control it, and when the cost of a decision could no longer be measured on a spreadsheet. By the time we arrive at abundance, the easy temptation is to treat it as the reward at the end of the story.

I do not think that is what abundance is.

How much money is too much money? I once answered that question this way: it is not an amount. It is when the desire for money replaces trust in God. The same answer works from the other direction. Abundance is not proved by an amount either. It is revealed by what governs us when we have something to protect, increase, enjoy, or give away.

What God wants from us is lordship. That sentence is simple enough to say when the bank balance is small and there is little to surrender. It becomes more searching when the business is working, when people are depending on us, when an opportunity appears unusually favorable, or when we have accumulated enough to imagine that we are secure because of what we own. Abundance can be provision, but it can also become an argument against dependence.

Jesus did not give every person the same instruction about possessions. The rich young ruler was told to sell what he had and give to the poor. Zacchaeus, after meeting Jesus, volunteered to give half of his possessions and repay those he had wronged. Peter said that he and the other disciples had left everything to follow Jesus. Jesus answered with a promise of multiplied return in this life, and then included the words we are inclined to omit: with persecution.

Those stories do not give us one financial formula. They give us three different encounters with the same Lord.

The rich young ruler's wealth occupied a place he was unwilling to surrender. Zacchaeus's encounter produced generosity and restitution. Peter's leaving did not make increase morally suspect, but neither did the promised increase make following Jesus comfortable. The circumstances were different. The question underneath them was the same: Who has the right to decide what happens to what you have?

That question keeps abundance from becoming a prosperity manual. It also keeps poverty from becoming a sign of superior spirituality. God provides differently for different people. He does not demand identical circumstances from all of us. He does demand that we not confuse provision with ownership in the deepest sense.

The Kingdom of God is within us, which means the issue eventually reaches the heart. A property can be held in a trust, a company, or an individual's name. The deeper issue is where it is held inside us. Can it be used, sold, shared, improved, or released without our identity collapsing with it? Can we receive increase without entitlement? Can we experience loss without deciding that God has abandoned us?

Those are not questions we have answered once and for all. The projects in this book have exposed them repeatedly. Some opportunities rewarded preparation. Some revealed haste. Some gave us the satisfaction of seeing a plan work. Others required waiting, surrender, or the admission that a vision could have been sincere and still become too heavy to carry in the way we were carrying it. Abundance did not remove those tests. In some cases, it made the tests more exact.

Jesus spoke often about faithfulness in little and the responsibility that comes with more. I have tended to hear the promise in that progression: faithful with a little, trusted with much. The more difficult reading is that "much" means more responsibility. It means more people affected by a decision, more resources that can be wasted, more relationships that can be strained, and more opportunities to mistake capacity for permission.

Increase is not simply more to possess. It is more to govern under someone else's authority.

That is why stewardship has to become visible. Our love for God is measured in how we love people. It is also seen in how we care for the things placed in our hands. Spiritual claims have to become measurable in the natural world. If we say we are generous, there should be a person who was treated generously. If we say we value excellence, the property, record, promise, or responsibility in front of us should show evidence of care. If we say people matter more than money, that conviction should survive a decision in which money is actually at risk.

Stewardship is the proper use of what God has given us in the way he would have us use it. That definition includes increase. The parables about money do not celebrate burying what has been entrusted to us. They carry an expectation that what is given will be put to work. Learning to turn something small into something greater can be faithful work. Planning, repairing, documenting, saving, investing, and building systems can all be forms of stewardship.

Small beginnings matter because they reveal our habits before the consequences become large. It is possible to imagine that we would be generous if we had more, careful if the asset were more valuable, or attentive if the assignment were more important. The teaching about faithfulness in little removes that excuse. Care is not something we postpone until the opportunity feels worthy of us. The little thing is where we learn whether our spiritual language will become natural-world behavior.

That includes ordinary maintenance and uncelebrated responsibility. Taking care of what has been provided is not as dramatic as acquiring it. There is no announcement when records are kept accurately, when a repair is handled before it becomes a crisis, or when a commitment is honored even though no one else would have known the difference. Yet those choices make stewardship measurable. They show whether we regard provision as a tool for service or merely as evidence that we have been favored.

Love for God cannot remain invisible at the point where people and possessions require something from us. If the things entrusted to us deteriorate through neglect while we speak about increase, our claims are out of order. If the people affected by our work are treated as costs to control, the same is true. The natural world does not contain the whole spiritual reality, but it often reveals whether we are living the reality we claim.

But increase alone does not prove stewardship. A thing can grow while the person responsible for it shrinks. A business can become more efficient while becoming less humane. A project can make money while consuming a marriage, a family, or the peace that should have governed the work. We can become excellent at multiplying what we were never meant to make central.

This is one reason abundance must include contentment. Contentment is not the refusal to build. It is freedom from needing the next increase to tell us who we are. Generosity is not carelessness. Excellence is not control. These qualities belong together because each corrects a distortion in the others. Generosity without stewardship can waste what might have served people for years. Stewardship without generosity can become fear with good records. Excellence without contentment can become endless striving.

The landowner who paid the workers in Jesus's parable unsettled the people who had worked all day because he gave the late arrivals more grace than comparison could tolerate. The early workers received what they had agreed to receive, yet another person's generosity made their own wage feel smaller. That story reaches beyond payroll. Abundance becomes hard to recognize when we are measuring it against someone else's portion.

Comparison can turn provision into grievance. Entitlement can make grace look unjust. The more we have received, the easier it is to assume that we understand why we received it and what should happen next. Yet the story of grace keeps refusing to fit our formulas.

There is a tension here between justice and grace. We should keep agreements, pay what is owed, and deal honestly. The landowner did that. But after justice had been satisfied, he remained free to be generous. The workers who had carried the day interpreted another person's grace as a reduction of their own worth. I recognize that impulse. I can be content with an agreement until I see what someone else received.

Abundance exposes whether I can celebrate provision that does not pass through my hands. It asks whether I want God's generosity or merely want to control its distribution. That question reaches beyond money. Opportunity, influence, recognition, and the pace at which another person advances can all provoke the same comparison. Contentment is tested not only by what I lack, but also by the grace I see given elsewhere.

There is also opposition. The April notes that became this chapter began with the image of a flood and the Spirit of the Lord raising a standard against it. I do not read that as a promise that increase protects us from pressure. Peter's promise included persecution. Greater breakthrough can bring greater resistance, and greater responsibility can expose weaknesses that were easier to hide when the work was small.

The answer is not to fear abundance. It is to refuse to make abundance our lord.

We want to handle what has been provided with generosity, contentment, and excellence. We want to increase what should be increased and release what should be released. We want the natural evidence of our lives to agree with the spiritual language we use. Those desires are real. So is the distance between desiring them and living them consistently.

Perhaps that is the honest place to end. Abundance is not the point at which the tests stop. It is the point at which the tests reveal what our success has taught us to trust. If money, property, influence, or systems have made us more able to serve, then increase has found part of its purpose. If they have made us more defended, more entitled, or less willing to listen, then abundance has uncovered work still to be done.

The question is not merely how much we have.

The question is who governs us in what we have.
