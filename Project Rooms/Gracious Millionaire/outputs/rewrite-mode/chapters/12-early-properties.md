# Early Properties

The first properties did not give us one lesson. They gave us a series of corrections.

By deed execution date, the sequence began with Marthonna, Roffler, Metacomet, Hazelnut, and Patricia. Memory does not always preserve acquisition order accurately, especially when several projects overlap, so the deed sequence matters. The lessons, however, did not arrive in an orderly line. We were learning about sellers while learning about managers, learning about renovation while learning about contracts, and learning about profit while discovering how many relationships stand behind a single house.

Marthonna taught me that I could do this at all.

I could walk up to a stranger's door, explain why I was there, listen to a difficult property situation, and make an offer far below what a retail buyer might pay. The low price did not have to require contempt for the seller. I could treat people with empathy, give them choices, and offer direction without pretending that my offer was their only path. Sometimes the property had become a dead end in their mind. Our offer gave them a way out they were free to accept or reject.

That first acceptance changed something in me. It moved real estate from education into experience.

Marthonna also showed us how unprepared we were. Jerry and I had no established attorney, contractor network, or business manager. The house was purchased through Sell Your Home, a company owned by our retirement funds. Because of that structure, we could not simply do the work or sign documents ourselves. We needed an independent manager.

We hired a friend who also performed much of the work. The boundaries were poorly defined. Communication became strained, and the relationship soured before the property was sold. We completed the project, put it on the market, considered several offers, and earned what seemed like a good first profit. It would be the smallest profit we made on a project for some time, apart from the property where we eventually lost money.

The retirement-account ownership made those blurred lines more serious. Because Jerry and I could not perform the work or sign for the company ourselves, the manager was not a ceremonial title. The role carried actual authority. We were learning that a legal structure designed for a particular purpose changes how ordinary decisions must be made. Enthusiasm about the property could not replace the discipline required by the entity that owned it.

The money was encouraging. The damaged relationship was instructive.

A profitable project can still reveal bad organization. We needed qualified people, clear roles, and better communication. Friendship did not remove the need for boundaries. In fact, unclear boundaries can place more pressure on a friendship because no one knows where goodwill ends and responsibility begins.

Roffler was a manufactured home on about an acre. The only heir was a young man named Tony, who had struggled with the law and did not know how to handle the home, the estate, or the legal process pressing in on him. Time was running out. I met him at a Bojangles with his uncle, a sensible man who helped steady the conversation.

The home itself was in good condition. It required little more than an agreement, yard cleanup, and a sale. I offered Tony something new for us: a share of the profit when the property sold. The structure could have paid him more than a simple immediate purchase.

But trust has a clock inside it. Tony became impatient and began threatening violence. I could not pretend that the original partnership structure still existed merely because it looked better on paper. I made him a lower cash offer to settle early. We met, I paid him, and he signed the necessary papers returning the full interest to us.

Roffler taught me that a creative agreement is only as durable as the people, expectations, and patience supporting it. More potential profit does not always create more security. Sometimes a smaller certain outcome is the price of ending a relationship that can no longer carry the original plan.

I had entered the arrangement believing that sharing the upside aligned our interests. On paper it did. In life, Tony's need for certainty and speed became more powerful than the future profit we had offered. The experience did not teach me to avoid creative structures. It taught me that the best mathematical outcome may not be the best human fit, especially when an heir is under legal and financial pressure he does not know how to navigate.

It also changed how we sold property. We had used a flat-fee listing service for the first projects, managing inquiries and seller communication ourselves. That approach was inexpensive and had worked. At Roffler, however, we were not getting the traffic we expected. Our friends Ellen and her husband had helped us buy our first house and sell our daughter's house in Clayton. Ellen suggested painting over the wallpaper and brought in professional staging. She listed the property, and offers began arriving. We soon accepted one we liked.

We learned to appreciate what a good real estate professional could add. Doing something ourselves because we can is not the same as doing it ourselves because we should.

Metacomet taught us about sequence in another way. We had a signed contract, earnest money paid, keys, and permission to enter the house. We did not have the deed. In my eagerness, I began cleaning out the property before closing.

The owner lived in New England and came to believe we were trying to cheat her. I returned from a weekend trip to learn that the sheriff had contacted my neighbor after she made a complaint. She was trying to back away from the agreement. Our new attorney wrote a firm letter requiring her to honor the contract. We also sent her money earlier than the agreement required so we could turn the page and move forward.

It worked out. That does not make starting early wise.

Possession, permission, and a contract felt close enough to ownership that I acted as though the final legal step were a formality. It was not. The deed mattered. The mistake could have cost us the deal and more. God's gracious protection in a foolish situation should produce gratitude, not a method for repeating the foolishness.

Hazelnut was our largest rehabilitation to that point. The house was small, in a neighborhood that did not make the project easy, and built around a poor floor plan. It had four bedrooms and one and a half baths, with the master bedroom limited to the half bath. The rooms were dark and closed in. There were holes in the drywall and cracks beneath walls where daylight came through. A large stand of bamboo had taken over the backyard, with roots pressing around the foundation.

We reworked the layout. Two bedrooms became a larger master suite with a full bath. The laundry and bathroom were relocated and enlarged. The kitchen and living room were opened instead of remaining separate dark boxes. This was the first time we hired a general contractor after several rounds of interviews.

The project taught us to see beyond the condition we were buying. A bad floor plan was not fixed by paint. The house needed to be reimagined. It also deepened relationships with people who would remain part of our business. Work with Jonathan and Walter grew beyond one job. Contractors, attorneys, sellers, managers, agents, and lenders were becoming a network of trust rather than names collected for a transaction.

Patricia may have required the greatest act of looking past what was visible.

It was another estate property. The yard was buried under weeds and brush about six feet high, hiding rubbish in front and back. The house had been broken into. Mirrors were shattered, and holes had been punched in the walls. The property was also flooded. Water from neighboring yards seemed to drain toward it and had nowhere to go. We could not properly clear the yard until we lowered the water.

We put sump pumps at opposite corners of the house to drain the crawl space. Then we ran drainage lines through the yard to feed the pumps and carry water toward the ditch in front. Mowers had been getting stuck; once the system worked, the ground began to release the water. My son Drew worked on landscaping, fencing, and clearing, while our contractor managed the rehabilitation.

The order of that work mattered. Clearing brush before understanding the drainage would have left people and equipment fighting the same water. Cosmetic work inside the house would not have answered what was happening beneath it. Patricia was another systems lesson in physical form: first identify where the water is coming from and how it can leave, then build the rest of the recovery on ground that can actually support it.

Finding the responsible heir had been difficult. The deceased owner's daughter was living somewhere near Asheville, perhaps in the mountains, and using temporary addresses. I do not now remember exactly how I found her. I believe the contract and certified funds had to be sent through those temporary locations. That uncertainty belongs in the account. I know we persisted when the obvious paths failed. I do not want to invent the missing step because the result turned out well.

We remodeled Patricia and listed it as the market was heating up. Institutional investors made multiple offers, and the property produced a good return.

The visible lesson was that a jewel can be hidden under extraordinary disorder. The harder lesson was that recognizing potential is not enough. Someone has to find the person with authority to act, solve the water problem before the yard can be cleared, bring in people who know their work, and stay with the project long enough for possibility to become a house someone wants.

These early projects increased in profit, but profit was not the only progression. Marthonna exposed weak boundaries. Roffler tested trust and patience. Metacomet punished haste. Hazelnut required design and professional help. Patricia demanded persistence amid missing information and physical disorder.

None of those lessons stayed confined to one street. We carried them into the systems we built, the people we hired, and the money we later borrowed. We also carried forward habits that still needed correction. Early success can teach confidence before it teaches restraint.

The houses proved that we could buy, repair, and sell property. They did not prove that we knew what every future property would ask of us.
