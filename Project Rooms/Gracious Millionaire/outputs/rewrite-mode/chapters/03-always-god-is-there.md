# Always God Is There

Long before I could point to success in real estate, I could point to protection.

I was driving to a job site when "Nights in White Satin" came on the radio. The swelling music carried me back about fifty years, to a road near my parents' home in Lutz, Florida. I had left the house for the evening and had gone less than a mile. The road crossed a railroad track just before it met the highway.

I had the music turned up high. The train was sounding its horn, but the car radio masked it. I had not slowed down enough to check the track carefully. When I finally looked left, the train was already there.

I do not remember exactly where I was going. I may have been meeting friends. I do not even remember with certainty whether darkness had fallen. What remained was the music rising, the late look to the left, and the sudden knowledge that I had almost used up the distance between an ordinary evening and disaster. The gaps in memory do not change the part I know: the horn was sounding, and I had made it impossible for myself to hear.

There was no time to stop. I pushed the accelerator and almost cleared the crossing. The train caught roughly the last foot and a half of the truck bed. The impact spun the truck around about one and a half times and threw me against the driver's door hard enough to bend its side panel.

The truck was totaled. I walked away with a bruised forearm.

It would make a better picture of young me to say that I had been alert and acted decisively in an unavoidable emergency. That is not the story. I had been careless. A responsible driver would have slowed down, looked, and listened before crossing railroad tracks. I looked too late and then did the only thing left to do.

I have taken risks throughout my life, sometimes past the point where risk became recklessness. My driving record would make that plain. Yet again and again, God protected me from the full consequences of my own carelessness and from dangers I did not create.

Those are two different kinds of stories, and I do not want to confuse them. At the railroad track, my own failure to slow down put me in danger. In later collisions, another driver crossed in front of me, forced me off the road, or entered my lane. Grace met me in both, but honesty requires me to distinguish rescue from consequences I helped create and protection from harm imposed by someone else.

The truck was the first of only two new vehicles I have bought in my lifetime. My memory of the insurance details is not clear enough to state them confidently. I believe I may not have had the right coverage when the train hit me, and I remember that my uncle, who owned an electrical contracting business, found a way for the loss to be covered under his umbrella policy. The loan was paid. I bought the truck back as salvage and built a wooden bed where the original bed had been torn away. After that, it always traveled a little sideways down the road.

A few years later, two friends were sitting beside me on the bench seat when another vehicle crossed in front of us to turn. I swerved right but could not avoid the collision. The truck was totaled a second time. All three of us walked away.

There had been another accident near the same area. Driving home late after being with friends, I dozed off. The car crossed the highway line and went down an embankment on the left side of the road. I woke in time to brake before hitting a utility pole in the ditch. The car was a total loss. I walked away.

Years later, in North Carolina, I was driving a convertible BMW home from work on Interstate 40. Another driver apparently did not like the way I was driving and deliberately forced me off the road. Both cars spun in the median. The guide wires intended to keep vehicles from crossing into oncoming traffic caught the side of my car. As the BMW scraped along them, they came frighteningly close to my head before the car bounced away.

The other driver spun to a stop in front of me without striking anything. He got out, looked back at me, laughed, and drove away. I called Jenny to come get me.

More recently, I was returning from another job site in a Cadillac Escalade, only a couple of miles from home. The dash cameras recorded another vehicle crossing the center line. It struck the front bumper and tore along the side, ripping off both wheels and the running board. The recording showed that vehicle continuing on my side of the road behind me. The other driver remained in his vehicle until an ambulance arrived. I was walking around looking at the damage and calling Jenny once again to pick me up.

By then the young man at the railroad crossing was decades behind me. I had not continued making every mistake that characterized him. Still, age and experience had not made me independent of protection. The vehicles changed. The roads changed. The phone call to Jenny became part of the pattern. I remained the person standing beside wreckage, aware that metal had absorbed violence my body had not.

I do not tell these stories to celebrate being accident-prone. They are not evidence that carelessness is harmless if a person has enough faith. Protection is not permission to ignore wisdom. Grace should make me more grateful and more responsible, not more entitled to another rescue.

That is one of the lessons I can see more clearly than I always live. I know God has been faithful. I also know how quickly a person can turn God's protection into a story about his own toughness, reflexes, or luck. The train crossing leaves no room for that interpretation. I did not save myself by being a good driver. I was a careless young man who saw danger too late and lived.

I have sometimes said that my angels work overtime. The phrase carries gratitude, but it must not become a joke that excuses me. If I believe God preserved my life for a purpose, then preservation creates responsibility. The right response is not to assume I will always be spared. It is to ask what I am doing with the life that was spared.

Jenny's place in the later accidents also reminds me that protection is rarely private. She received the phone calls. She came to get me. She saw the damaged vehicles and the man who walked away from them. Grace preserved my life, but the consequences and gratitude belonged inside our shared life.

The same temptation to take credit follows me into business. Planning, persistence, systems, and judgment all have their place. But when I review the moments in which a transaction turned, a relationship opened, or a danger passed, I have to be careful not to claim authorship of what I did not do.

Before the wealth, before the properties, before the business systems, there was this prior fact: I was kept.

I do not understand why every person is not protected in the same way. I cannot turn these accidents into a formula or make them explain suffering. I can only testify honestly about my own life. I was foolish more than once. I was endangered by others more than once. I walked away.

Always God is there.
