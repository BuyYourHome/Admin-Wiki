# The Faces In The Picture

Every author is tempted to arrange the light.

We choose which memories to include, where to begin a story, and what to leave outside the frame. We know the ending when we describe the beginning, so even an honest memory can become cleaner than the life that produced it. A moment of confusion starts sounding like destiny. A questionable decision becomes courage because something good eventually followed it. Failure becomes a brief dramatic pause on the way to success.

Artificial intelligence adds another layer to that temptation. It can help shape sentences, find repetition, organize a growing body of material, and make a rough presentation look finished. It can also make an attractive version of a story feel true before anyone has looked closely enough to see what changed.

The danger is not limited to an invented fact. A machine can smooth a contradiction until the tension disappears. It can give uncertainty a confident ending, make a mixed motive sound noble, or place a clean lesson over an experience that still hurts. Each sentence may sound reasonable. The distortion appears only when the polished account is compared closely with the people and events that gave rise to it.

I encountered that problem while working on the cover for this book. I submitted a photograph for Jean, our AI office assistant, to enhance. The setting improved. The resolution improved. The posture and clothing still felt familiar. At first glance, the image looked like Jenny and me.

Then I looked at the faces.

They were pleasant faces. They belonged to attractive people. They just did not belong to us.

The image had preserved enough of the original photograph to feel convincing while replacing the detail that made it personal. It was not an obvious fantasy. That was what made it useful as a warning. If the picture had looked absurd, we would have rejected it immediately. Instead, it looked good. It looked almost right. Only close attention revealed that the people on the cover of our story were strangers.

The clothing and posture made the substitution harder to notice. The picture still carried the outline of a real moment. That is how a flattering story often works. It retains the property, the transaction, the marriage, the prayer, or the crisis, but quietly replaces the human face. The author becomes more certain, more courageous, more patient, or more spiritually perceptive than the person who actually lived the event.

We went back to the drawing board because resemblance mattered more than polish. A cover should invite a reader into a book, but it should not do so by quietly replacing the authors.

The same standard has to govern the manuscript.

It would be easy to describe our history as a steady rise: two people of faith apply sound principles, work hard, receive favor, build wealth, and learn a few lessons along the way. Pieces of that sentence are true. Taken together without the difficult details, they could still create faces that are not ours.

The true story includes caution that was wise and caution that may have been fear. It includes vision that opened possibilities and vision that could become pressure. It includes planning, mistakes, carelessness, prayer, divorce, financial strain, unexpected provision, unresolved projects, and conflicts that do not become simple merely because we can now write about them. It includes places where we do not agree on the meaning of an event, and places where one of us owns memories the other cannot narrate.

The chapters that follow begin before the real estate success that could make the title sound impressive. They begin with Wes being protected in accidents he did not always avoid wisely. They continue through Jenny's conventional first home, the pressure of raising three small children, the loss of a marriage, an investment caught in the 2008 collapse, and a night on the coast when no plan seemed to work. Those stories keep the later wealth from becoming the only light in the picture. Grace was present before achievement, and dependence was present inside preparation.

That last point matters. Early versions of this book grew mostly from Wes's telling. As Jenny began writing her part, the book could no longer remain Wes's first-person account with Jenny's memories introduced as additional evidence. An attractive shared memoir can still be false if one person's voice controls the frame and the other person's experience appears only when it supports that frame.

Some chapters in this book therefore speak in Wes's voice. Some speak in Jenny's. Some require both. The change is more than a matter of fairness in presentation. Voice is part of factual accuracy. The person who carried the responsibility, felt the fear, made the calculation, prayed the prayer, or lived through the loss should not be displaced by a narrator who sounds more familiar to the drafting process.

We cannot promise perfect memory. No memoir can. We can promise to resist improving the people until they become strangers. Where a fact is uncertain, uncertainty belongs in the story. Where an outcome is unfinished, we should not manufacture resolution. Where grace met us in our own foolishness, we should not retell foolishness as strategy. Where money was made, we should not assume profit proved that every motive was pure.

This also means resisting a different kind of improvement: making every painful event useful too quickly. A loss does not become worthwhile merely because an author can attach a lesson to it. A lesson may emerge, but the loss must first be allowed to remain a loss. Otherwise even our humility becomes part of the flattering picture.

The faces in the picture gave us a standard: look again.

Look past the composition. Look past the attractive language and the satisfying lesson. Look closely enough to ask whether the details still belong to the people whose names are on the cover.

That kind of looking can make a story less flattering. We hope it also makes the story worth telling.
