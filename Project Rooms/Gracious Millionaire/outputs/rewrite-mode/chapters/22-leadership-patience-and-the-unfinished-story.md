# Leadership, Patience, And The Unfinished Story

The Orphaned Property brought the leadership problem out of theory and into our marriage. I saw a creative trade with significant potential. I had analyzed it, documented it, and redesigned systems to support it. Timing mattered, financial pressure made action attractive, and I believed I could see a path that Jenny could not yet see.

Jenny did not want the properties. We did not have unity, and the deal stalled.

The broader history of my leadership is already visible: plans, explanations, boards, businesses, family frustration, and the temptation to measure influence by agreement with me. This transaction made the next question unavoidable. What does leadership look like after the vision has been explained and the other person still says no?

I could call her resistance a failure to understand the opportunity. I could call my persistence a failure to understand the risk. Neither conclusion is proven by an unfinished story. What is proven is that overriding her would have required me to treat later cooperation as inevitable and use urgency, profitability, and my prior work as pressure.

Sometimes leadership is seeing a possible solution, mapping it, explaining it, and then waiting while another person does not agree. That waiting leaves room for facts, motives, timing, and the person with the vision to be corrected. It did not give me what I wanted on my schedule. It did keep me from making agreement with me the price of peace in our marriage.

That does not prove Jenny's resistance to the property trade was correct. It shows why leadership must allow an unfinished process to remain unfinished.

One of Bill Johnson's statements that has stayed with me is that if I build something, I have to support it; if God builds it, He supports it. I do not take that to mean we avoid work or planning. Providence Landing required enormous work. Our business requires systems, contracts, follow-up, and responsibility. The statement challenges my assumption that strain and control are evidence of commitment.

Another leadership lesson in the teaching we have drawn from is that disappointment tests whether a leader can strengthen himself in the Lord before trying to lead others again. That sequence matters to me. When a plan stalls, my first impulse is often to repair the plan by increasing pressure outside myself. But if frustration has already taken control of me, stronger persuasion will reproduce that frustration in the people around me. Recovery of purpose has to come before renewed influence.

Prayer on the couch is therefore not a ceremonial pause before I return to the argument. It is a place where the argument itself can be surrendered. If I pray only for Jenny to agree with me, I have brought manipulation into devotion. Unity may come through her changing her mind, through me changing mine, or through both of us discovering a path neither had proposed.

If I must continually overpower the people around me to preserve the plan, I should ask what I am actually supporting.

Christian leadership begins in relationship with God before it becomes influence over people. It includes listening, speaking when appropriate, acting by example, and remaining patient when others do not adopt my priorities. If a plan belongs to God, I can give it back to Him. I can pray, wait, and allow Him to work in other people without recruiting anger as the Holy Spirit's assistant.

That is easier to write than to live.

When the potential buyer for Tensity needed the trailers to be part of the transaction, I had no authority from our marriage to negotiate in that direction. The deal stalled. My immediate inner story was that Jenny's resistance had destroyed a profitable opportunity. Resentment stood close by, ready to interpret every delay.

But I did not know the end. I still do not.

Perhaps the properties would have multiplied value exactly as my spreadsheet projected. Perhaps title, condition, tenants, repairs, or marketability would have turned the projection into a burden. Perhaps a different transaction will emerge. Perhaps the need for cash will force a simpler decision. Perhaps the benefit is not in the eventual transaction at all, but in what the disagreement exposes in us.

Leadership allows those possibilities to remain open without punishing the person who would not agree.

The promise that all things work together for good does not mean every idea I have is good. It means God is not trapped by the outcome. If the trade was missed, He is not impoverished. If the trade was dangerous, He is not unable to protect us. If the opportunity returns in another form, He does not need my resentment to keep it alive.

This is a lesson identified, not mastered. I still usually think I am right. Jenny usually has reasons for thinking she is right. I value her judgment even when I do not display that value well in the moment.

Patience also requires me to stop demanding that uncertainty justify itself immediately. When a deal does not happen, I want to know whether we were protected or deprived. When a plan stalls, I want a verdict on who saw it correctly. Leadership may require living without that verdict long enough for relationship, truth, and timing to matter more than being declared right.

The unfinished story is therefore part of the lesson, not a defect in the chapter. I cannot tell the reader that patience saved us. I cannot say it cost us a fortune. I can say that forcing agreement would have cost something in the marriage, whether the transaction later succeeded or failed.

For now, leadership looks less like moving everyone toward my vision and more like holding vision with an open hand. It looks like doing the analysis, speaking clearly, listening seriously, and refusing to make another person's caution into disloyalty.

I know that I do not know how the Orphaned Property ends. That may be the most honest leadership statement I can make.
