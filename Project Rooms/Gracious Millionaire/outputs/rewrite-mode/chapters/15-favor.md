# Favor

Jenny and I have benefited greatly from favor.

It would be easy to explain our success by the education we pursued and the disciplined processes we put into practice. Those things mattered. We probably would not have experienced the same results without them. We studied, gathered foreclosure files, sent letters, built spreadsheets, knocked on doors, made offers, and kept following up.

But none of those steps happened in a vacuum, and none of them guaranteed an outcome.

Lance Wallnau describes favor as the attraction of God to a person that releases influence through that person, inclining others to like, trust, and cooperate in the assignment God has given. I use that definition because it keeps favor connected to assignment. Favor is not charm with religious language added. It is not proof that I am better than the person whose phone call was not returned. It is not a technique for making people agree with me.

Favor can help open a door. It does not give me ownership of the person on the other side.

Isaiah describes a teacher no longer hidden and a voice behind us saying, "This is the way; walk in it," whether the path turns right or left. I have known that passage most of my life. In real estate it became concrete. We had a repeatable process, but every file still required attention. A lead that looked promising could go nowhere. A lead we normally would have discarded could become an assignment.

One day I approached a property hoping to meet its owner. The man who answered did not own it. He was the executor of his fiancee's will. She had died, and he was alone and despondent.

He invited me into the living room. I explained why I had come, but the conversation became something other than an acquisition meeting. He told me his story. I listened, encouraged him, and prayed for God's blessing on him.

I never heard from him again. I did not buy the house.

If favor is measured only by transactions, that appointment was a failure. If the assignment that day was to sit with a grieving man who was willing to open his home and his pain, the outcome looks different. Favor made room for trust, but the trust was not mine to convert into a deal.

That story protects the business stories that follow. It reminds me that an open door does not always lead to profit, and profit is not the only evidence that I was where I should have been.

Another lead began in a category we usually excluded. Our system collected foreclosure files from the courthouse and contacted homeowners before they lost their property. We tried to identify homeowners-association foreclosures and remove them because the balances were often too small to create a realistic purchase opportunity. We assumed no one would lose a house over a few thousand dollars.

This homeowner owed less than four thousand dollars to an association. After speaking with him, I requested the courthouse file and saw that the association had initiated the foreclosure. Under our normal rules, I would have stopped. Instead, the situation held my attention.

I began building a possible structure before I understood all of his circumstances. We could make a substantial cash payment for the deed, take the existing mortgage subject to its terms, satisfy the foreclosure lien, and allow the family to remain in the house while paying rent tied to the mortgage, taxes, insurance, and association cost.

He had not responded to sixteen letters. He did respond after three text messages. I went to meet him.

That contrast is one reason I resist reducing favor to method. Sixteen letters were part of a disciplined campaign, but they did not produce a conversation. Three texts did. The lesson is not that texts are spiritual and letters are not. It is that a system keeps us present long enough for a door to open, while the opening itself remains something we receive rather than command.

He was cordial and showed me the house. When I asked whether he wanted to sell, he described plans for a bigger home with a yard for his children. I did not think those plans fit the immediate reality, but I listened. Then I asked whether he would be interested if I could remove the crisis and let the family stay where they were.

His interest changed. I asked for a mortgage statement and saw a reasonably small balance. Then I asked what amount of cash would make the structure worthwhile to him.

At first he wanted a simple purchase price. He believed the house was worth around two hundred eighty-five thousand dollars, an amount I was not going to offer. I explained the proposal in stages because sellers rarely absorb every part of a creative structure at once. When he did not name a cash figure, I began with ten thousand dollars.

He told me about another offer and began listing credit-card debt and car payments. I realized the other proposal was essentially debt refinancing. I recalculated and offered forty thousand dollars, enough to address those balances.

He said he could sell the house for more. I agreed. He probably could. But a conventional sale would require moving, finding another house in a difficult market, and disrupting the family during the school year. I looked toward his wife, who had been listening, and asked whether she really wanted to move.

Then I asked what he earned in a year. He said forty-two thousand dollars. I told him I was offering roughly a year's salary while allowing the family to remain in the home.

That was the flavor of the appointment. When I first wrote about it, he had not accepted the proposal or called me back.

The unfinished outcome matters. I do not want to turn receptivity in a living room into a completed transaction simply because the meeting helped me see a new strategy.

Afterward, I changed our project-management spreadsheet and spent about an hour testing the structure. The projections showed substantial potential. The model suggested that an association foreclosure could create a tenant-occupied property requiring little immediate rehabilitation, with equity that might support other projects and appreciation that could grow over time. We could vary the cash paid to the seller, adjust rent, or perhaps include a path for the seller to buy the property back.

For that particular property, the spreadsheet projected profit of about $199,078 after twelve months, $257,798 after twenty-four months, and $316,510 after thirty-six months. It also suggested that roughly $103,500 of the equity might be available to finance other work. Those were model outputs based on assumptions, not money in an account. Their value was in showing me why a lead category we had dismissed deserved closer examination.

The spreadsheet estimates were possibilities, not proceeds. No profit had been earned from that homeowner.

The structure also carried responsibilities that a profit projection could not settle. The family would remain in the house. We would become owners and landlords while the old mortgage remained part of the arrangement. Rent, maintenance, insurance, taxes, association obligations, and the possibility of a future repurchase would all have to be handled clearly. A creative offer can solve several problems at once, but it can also bind people together for years. Favor at the first meeting would not excuse carelessness after closing.

Perhaps the assignment was to open our eyes to leads we had been excluding. Perhaps it was to make a sincere offer to one family. Perhaps the appointment will mean something I cannot yet see. Favor does not require me to choose the most flattering interpretation.

I believe God's favor has made people willing to receive me, trust us, lend to us, work with us, and enter agreements that skill alone could not compel. Our responsibility is not to take credit for that willingness or exploit it. Favor increases the obligation to serve the assignment faithfully.

The offer also had to remain a real choice. I could explain that forty thousand dollars approximated the homeowner's annual income and that staying might spare his family a difficult move. I could not use those truths to erase the value he believed he had in the house. Favor did not make my analysis the only valid view of his situation. He retained the right to say no, to sell another way, or to do nothing until the foreclosure forced the issue.

If I turn favor into a formula, I have already misunderstood it. If I use it to explain why my plan should prevail, I have confused favor with control. If I treat every profitable opening as divine approval, I leave no room for correction.

Favor is received. It is also stewarded. Sometimes it produces a property. Sometimes it produces a conversation in a grieving man's living room. The assignment decides what counts as success.
