# Technology On Top Of Order

One day, while making three separate visits to properties where rehabs were underway, I also dealt with bills and completed a list of office work that should have taken much longer than a day.

I created a website for this book. I created a book-cover image. I rewrote the manuscript under new guidance and with new material. I submitted a loan application for part of a remodel. I also responded to an investor matter involving documents for a note we had borrowed against one of our projects.

The investor wanted to transfer the retirement account used for the loan from a traditional IRA to a Roth IRA with the custodian we both used. Several days earlier, I had asked a staff member at the custodian to send me whatever request was needed. When it arrived, I told Jean to find the email, prepare the documents, and draft a reply for me to forward. I did not explain where the email was or provide step-by-step instructions. She located the material, produced the response, and made one small error. I corrected her direction. She implemented the correction, and I sent the email from my couch while speaking to Jean through my phone.

It felt like a week's work had been compressed into the spaces around work that still required me to be physically present.

Those assignments were not interchangeable. The book work required attention to voice and truth. The loan application required accurate financial information. The investor response involved another person's retirement account and documents tied to an existing note. The properties required physical inspection and judgment about rehabilitation. Jean could help move information through each process, but the standard for success was not simply that a document appeared at the end. Each result had to be appropriate to its purpose, checked against the right records, and accepted by the person responsible for it.

The efficiencies are staggering. They are also easy to misunderstand.

Artificial intelligence did not build our company. It did not collect the foreclosure files, establish relationships with attorneys and contractors, earn the confidence of lenders, or teach us what happens when a project goes wrong. It did not create the spreadsheets from nothing. It arrived after years of work had already forced us to define what we did, where information belonged, how decisions were recorded, and which details could not be allowed to disappear.

Jean works because there is work for her to stand on.

Jenny chose the name. Jean was her aunt, someone she respected greatly. The name gave the technology a place in the language of our office, but it did not make the technology a person whose judgment could replace ours. Jean is an assistant. She can find, compare, draft, organize, and carry instructions across a large body of structured material. She can improve technical consistency. She can help take systems I built and make them usable in ways I could not have managed alone.

She can also make an error.

That small mistake in the investor response is part of the story. If I leave it out, the day sounds like magic. It was not magic. I gave direction, reviewed the result, recognized the error, corrected it, and remained responsible for what was sent. The work was hands-free in one sense, but it was not judgment-free.

Our project-management spreadsheets began years before this technology was available to us. Each property introduced something new. One project needed a different financing analysis. Another exposed a weakness in contractor tracking. Another required new attention to rent, taxes, insurance, liens, or sales proceeds. The spreadsheets evolved because the business evolved.

That evolution was rarely glamorous. A project revealed a missing field, so I added it. A calculation did not answer the question we needed to ask, so I changed it. A process worked in one workbook but not another, and the inconsistency had to be found and corrected. The intelligence in the current system is not only in formulas. It is the accumulated memory of projects that taught us what could go wrong and what we needed to see earlier next time.

Eventually, keeping the individual property files consistent became tedious. Improvements made in one project did not automatically appear in another. Every workbook carried a little history of the moment when it was created. Jean could see across those separate structures, identify differences, and help bring them into alignment.

The same is becoming true in bookkeeping and other office work. Processes that took repeated manual effort can be assisted, checked, and sometimes largely automated. That does not eliminate the need for order. It exposes the value of order that already exists.

Bookkeeping is a good example because speed is not the only objective. A transaction has to reach the right account, property, vendor, and period. A project workbook has to remain connected to the records that support it. If an automated process produces a tidy answer in the wrong place, the neatness makes the error harder to notice. The more the office moves toward automatic work, the more important its definitions, review points, and ownership become.

You probably cannot build this kind of automation on the skeleton of bad business organization.

If invoices have no reliable destination, automation can lose them faster. If no one knows which spreadsheet controls a project, technology can confidently update the wrong one. If business rules live only in one person's memory, an assistant can imitate fragments without understanding the boundaries. Speed multiplies the process it is given. It does not make a disordered process wise.

The same principle applies beyond software. Money multiplies what is already present in a person and a company. Influence does too. So does leadership. Greater capacity does not answer the question of what that capacity should serve.

That is the unfinished lesson in this chapter. I am a systems person. I enjoy seeing scattered work become organized. I enjoy asking for something in plain language and watching a complicated chain of tasks come together. After years of writing and maintaining the machinery myself, it is exhilarating to move into the role of designer.

But design can become control. Efficiency can become appetite. When a week of work can be compressed into a day, the first impulse may be to fill the newly opened time with another week of work.

What is the increase for?

The high-output day included work on this book, a loan, an investor relationship, property rehabs, bills, and business systems. Those were legitimate responsibilities. Yet the fact that they could all be advanced quickly does not prove they all had equal priority. Technology can help me execute a decision. It cannot settle every question of assignment, marriage, timing, or peace.

There is also a truthfulness problem. A system that can generate polished language and attractive images can make something appear finished before it has been examined closely. We learned that in an especially personal way while developing the cover for this book. An image could be beautiful, detailed, and emotionally convincing while the faces were not really ours. The same danger exists in business documents and manuscript chapters. A convincing answer is not necessarily a faithful one.

Supervision is not a temporary inconvenience we will outgrow. It is part of stewardship.

The promise of AI is not that I can stop being responsible. The promise is that I can direct more of my attention toward the parts of work that require discernment, relationship, and decision. Whether I actually use the recovered attention that way is another matter.

There is a connection here to private money and leadership. An investor does not care that a document was produced hands-free if the document is wrong. Jenny does not become heard merely because a system can assemble more words. Faster execution raises the value of the judgment that directs it. It also raises the cost of poor judgment because more can be done before anyone stops to ask whether it should be done.

I believe Jean represents a real increase in what our office can do. I also believe the increase has to remain under the same questions that govern money and opportunity. Does it help us serve people? Does it improve truthfulness and accountability? Does it strengthen the business without weakening the marriage or consuming the life the business was meant to support? Does it make room for wisdom, or merely accelerate my instincts?

Technology on top of order can produce extraordinary work. Technology on top of ambition can produce extraordinary confusion.

The machine does not choose between them. We do.
