# The Safe Way

I am most comfortable when things are researched, proven, calculable, and foreseeable. I like plans, rules, high standards, and a clear understanding of what can go wrong. I prefer steady and slow to impulsive. I want integrity in the decision and enough information to believe the outcome is manageable.

I also want things to be honest, balanced, organized, and rational. I am guarded when the facts are incomplete. I do not naturally find comfort in someone else's confidence if I cannot see what supports it. For me, caution is not standing still. It is the work of finding out what a promise will require before I make it.

That is not the only way to describe me, but it is important to the way I first approached real estate.

I was twenty-three when I married Number One. Neither of us expected homeownership to arrive through a creative structure or a fortunate break. We wanted a home, so we worked and saved for one. During our first year of marriage, while living in an apartment, both of us worked two jobs. We kept at it until we had enough for a down payment.

In 1978, we bought a small starter home in Northern Virginia for $37,000. We did it the conventional way: a bank loan, a thirty-year mortgage, and payments we believed we could afford as long as both of us remained employed full time.

The home came after a year in an apartment and after two jobs apiece. The down payment represented evenings and hours of work, not money that happened to be available. By the time we signed a thirty-year obligation, we had already practiced delaying what we wanted. That history was part of what made the mortgage feel responsible rather than reckless.

Thirty-seven thousand dollars felt like a great deal of money to me. Confidence did not come from thinking nothing could go wrong. It came from believing we had counted carefully enough to carry the obligation. The mortgage was long, but the path was familiar. We had jobs. We had saved. The bank had approved the loan. The numbers fit the life we were living.

Two years later, we wanted to begin a family. Both of us remembered being taken to babysitters while our own parents worked, and neither of us wanted that for our children. We decided that I would stay home to raise them somehow.

"Somehow" is not usually a word that makes me comfortable. I wanted to know whether the decision could work before we depended on it.

Number One was working for his brother and wanted to begin his own remodeling business. We sold my beautiful 260Z sports car and used the money to buy him a truck. I loved that car. Giving it up was not a casual sacrifice. It was a visible exchange of one season for another. My priorities were changing as I looked toward motherhood and toward a household that would have to function differently.

Then we tested the plan. For one full year, we saved every paycheck I earned. We wanted to see whether we could live without my income before I quit. It was exactly the kind of experiment that made sense to me: do not merely hope the budget works; live as if the income is already gone and see what the numbers reveal.

Saving those checks also turned a value into evidence. We had said we wanted one parent at home because neither of us remembered the babysitter years fondly. The savings year asked whether that desire was strong enough to reorder how we lived before a baby arrived. We were not merely reducing income. We were choosing the kind of household we hoped to build and trying to bear its cost in advance.

At the time, I probably credited our planning. Looking back, I can also see God's kindness in honoring the commitment. Those two explanations are not enemies. Careful preparation mattered. So did provision that cannot be reduced to a spreadsheet.

I still believe in research. I still believe that commitments should be calculated, that obligations should be understood, and that caution can be a form of stewardship. Later chapters of my life would show me what planning could and could not protect. A person can do her homework and still miss an assessment. A marriage can begin with promises and still end. A market can change after the decision is made. A door can remain closed after every responsible step has been taken.

But I do not want to rewrite my early caution as fear merely because I later learned to take greater risks. The safe way gave us our first home. It allowed us to test whether I could leave employment. It helped us prepare for a family we deeply wanted.

It also formed the person who would later examine rents, management fees, mortgage payments, and association costs at the beach. The weakness in that later analysis did not mean the instinct to calculate was wrong. It meant no calculation is complete merely because it feels thorough. The safe way can reduce risk. It cannot make a person all-seeing.

The unfinished lesson is not that planning is wrong. It is that certainty is smaller than trust. I can research a decision faithfully without making research my source of peace. Even now, I am more comfortable when the path is black and white. Life has rarely agreed to remain that clear.
