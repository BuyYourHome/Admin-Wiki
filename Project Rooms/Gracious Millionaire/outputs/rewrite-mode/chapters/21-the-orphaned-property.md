# The Orphaned Property

The first house in this story seemed almost orphaned.

We found Tensity in foreclosure in early 2025. The owner had died about fourteen months earlier, the house was vacant, and no obvious heir was stepping forward. The owner's mother, who had also lived there, had died a couple of years before. Even the person listed as informant on the death certificate did not know of another heir.

We searched deeply and found possible distant heirs in Texas. Eventually one of them responded. After a period of negotiation, the heir agreed to sign a quitclaim deed for five thousand dollars. The mortgage did not carry a large balance, and the bank required about fifteen thousand dollars to reinstate it. With roughly twenty thousand dollars up front and a low-interest existing loan, we acquired the property.

The existing loan carried an interest rate of about 3.75 percent, which made preserving it particularly valuable. Our initial cash was small in relation to the house, but that did not mean the acquisition was effortless. The value came from solving several disconnected problems at once: locating an heir, obtaining a deed, stopping foreclosure, bringing the loan current, and taking responsibility for a vacant house that still needed work.

This is the kind of transaction that can make real estate investing look almost magical when summarized after the fact. A house appears abandoned, twenty thousand dollars is assembled, and a cash-flowing asset emerges. The summary leaves out the uncertainty of searching for someone authorized to act and the possibility that each lead will end without an answer.

That sentence makes the process sound cleaner than it felt. The house had been sitting in a legal and human gap. Someone had died. The most apparent family connections had ended. The bank's process was moving. We had to assemble enough information to find a person with a plausible claim and enough willingness to act. It was a property almost no one seemed to know could be bought.

By June we had Tensity ready to rent. Our flooring contractor, who had installed the new floor, asked whether we planned to lease it. We never had to place it on the market. He became the tenant, and the house produced about seven hundred dollars a month in cash flow.

It was the kind of rental investors say they want to keep. We had found it when others had not, solved the ownership problem, restored it, and placed a tenant who already knew the house.

While Tensity was being prepared, we bought Rose from sellers who had responded to our marketing. They were facing foreclosure and agreed to a structure that included ten thousand dollars down, a twenty-thousand-dollar note due in twelve months, and our taking the property subject to the existing mortgage.

At the time we had many projects underway. We did not begin the Rose rehabilitation as promptly as we should have. Nearly a year passed, and the twenty-thousand-dollar note approached its due date. The Rose project was unfinished, legal expenses from Rosebrooks were adding pressure, and we needed cash.

The obvious answer appeared to be selling Tensity. Our tenant was interested in buying it, but he would need seller financing. Seller financing would not produce the immediate cash we needed, and selling to someone else would require him to move.

The two properties therefore became connected by timing as much as by strategy. Rose carried a twenty-thousand-dollar obligation coming due after twelve months. Tensity carried the equity and cash-flow value that could meet pressure elsewhere. Our tenant needed a home and wanted ownership, but his financing needs did not match our need for cash. Finishing Rose and moving him into a contract-for-deed purchase there allowed one transaction to relieve several constraints without forcing him abruptly out of Tensity.

Then another possibility came together. We completed Rose and arranged to sell that house to the Tensity tenant on a contract-for-deed basis. The transaction was finished less than thirty days before his lease ended and before the note to the Rose seller came due. He had a path to ownership, Rose had a buyer, and Tensity became available for a cash sale.

That could have been the end of the chapter. Instead, the story opened into a trade I had not anticipated.

People began contacting us through Zillow. One proposed a full-price offer with terms worth considering. But another possibility interested me more. Our tenants referred friends from their church who wanted to buy Tensity. Maria told me that her sister and brother-in-law hoped it might be possible and that she was praying for it.

I assumed they would not be able to make a cash offer, but I was willing to talk. Through limited English and later an interpreter, I learned that the family had trailers they might trade. The husband eventually explained that they had three trailers available as part of a possible transaction.

Before any responsible offer, we would need more than a count of the trailers. We would need to understand title, location, condition, debt, occupancy, repair needs, and what market existed for each one. The proposed value depended on our ability to take control of three separate assets and then sell or finance them successfully. The interpreter solved the immediate language barrier. It did not solve the diligence problem.

My mind immediately began building a structure. We could sell Tensity on a contract for deed, accept title to the three trailers plus cash as part of the package, and then evaluate each acquired asset separately. Our project spreadsheet had been designed to buy houses and track renovations. I began redesigning it so that a sale could accept other property and follow the profitability of each asset through later transactions.

The early numbers suggested that a transaction originally worth around one hundred fifty thousand dollars might, through several generations of contract-for-deed sales, produce something closer to three hundred fifty thousand dollars. Those were projections, not results. They depended on the condition, title, marketability, financing, and eventual sale of assets we did not yet own.

The spreadsheet could separate the assets, assign costs, and project later sales. It could not tell us whether we wanted to become responsible for three more properties. It could calculate a path to profit, but it could not measure the attention each property might consume or the pressure another complicated chain might place on us. I had built a tool capable of making the opportunity visible. That did not make the tool capable of making the decision.

That distinction mattered greatly to Jenny.

She did not want us acquiring lower-end properties that might be difficult to rent, sell, or manage. I saw an unusual path with a higher upside. She saw assets we did not need, risks that were hard to quantify, and another layer of complexity at a time when cash was already important.

About a month before this opportunity developed, Jenny and I had begun meeting on the couch first thing each morning for devotion and prayer. We prayed about the work, the pressures, and what God wanted us to notice. We prayed for unity in our thinking and decisions.

Unity did not arrive as immediate agreement.

When I met with the potential buyer and his English-speaking associate, I did not have room to negotiate toward taking the trailers because Jenny and I had not agreed to that direction. Left to myself, I probably would have kept searching for enough common ground to make the trade. I had already done substantial analysis, documentation, and presentation work. In my mind, a profitable and timely deal was ready to be shaped.

But the trade did not happen then.

That is why I call Tensity the Orphaned Property. The name began with the house no one seemed able to claim. It expanded into a chain of possible transactions: an orphaned house became a rental; another troubled house became a home for the tenant; the first house became available for sale; three other properties might have entered the chain. Each asset could have produced another generation of provision.

Or it could have produced another generation of problems.

At the time of writing, the larger trade remains unfinished. We needed cash. I wanted the upside. Jenny did not want the trailers. Neither the spreadsheet nor my enthusiasm could settle what we should do together.

The temptation is to complete the story by choosing a lesson before the outcome exists. I could say we missed a remarkable opportunity because caution prevailed. I could say Jenny protected us from a complicated mistake. The evidence does not yet establish either conclusion.

Even the label *missed opportunity* assumes too much. An opportunity is not merely a transaction that could produce a large number on paper. It must also fit our capacity, timing, relationships, and actual assignment. Until those questions are answered, the trade is better described as a possibility we did not pursue than as wealth we lost.

What the story establishes is that we reached another place where profit, creativity, marriage, and prayer met without producing an immediate answer. The orphaned property taught me how much value can hide inside a neglected problem. The unfinished trade is teaching me that seeing value does not give me permission to force a decision.

For now, the ending is not a closing statement. It is two people on a couch, asking God for unity and waiting to see whether the opportunity disappears, changes shape, or proves that waiting was part of the deal.
