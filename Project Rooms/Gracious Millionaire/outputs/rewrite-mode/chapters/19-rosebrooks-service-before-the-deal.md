# Rosebrooks: Service Before The Deal

## Jenny

I remember when Wes told me Brynda had called. She had received our pre-foreclosure letters, six of them according to the records I checked. The CRM and spreadsheets showed that the letters had gone out over about six weeks. She had not responded until the foreclosure was close enough that the court could grant it within weeks.

I reviewed the file before Wes went to meet her. Brynda had inherited the townhouse at Rosebrooks after her sister died. We had bought other properties from heirs, and an estate sale could be straightforward when the heir lived somewhere else. In that situation, whatever money came from the property might feel like an unexpected benefit. Rosebrooks was different. Brynda was living in the house. Losing the property meant losing her home.

After Wes met with her, we talked through what he had learned. She wanted to stay. She did not have the money to bring the mortgage current, and she told him she had nowhere else to go and no one to help her.

We had already made lease-back arrangements with two other older owners. We would buy the property, and they could remain as tenants for as long as they wanted. Those arrangements were going smoothly. I remember saying to Wes, half joking and half serious, "This isn't just a business. It's a ministry."

That statement felt good because service was what we wanted the business to be. We wanted sellers, buyers, contractors, realtors, and tenants to be better because they had dealt with us. We had often discussed how many people and families passed through the work we had been trusted to do. I thanked God for that trust. I did not want us to jeopardize it.

## Wes

Rosebrooks came to us through the same pre-foreclosure process we used to identify other owners who might need an alternative before a forced sale. Brynda called near the end of October 2023. We arranged to meet, and I prepared documents for her to consider, including a purchase agreement and a lease-back arrangement because of her age and her desire to stay in the home.

The surviving records make the pace visible. Brynda called on October 28. We met the next day. I left proposed documents with her after we discussed the foreclosure and the possibility of her remaining in the house. On October 31, the purchase and lease-back documents were signed. The court permitted the foreclosure sale to proceed on November 1. I paid the mortgage arrears on November 15, and the foreclosure firm issued notice the next day that the sale would not go forward.

Later in November, I discovered that a signature needed for the deed had been missed. Brynda signed the deed to the trust holding Rosebrooks on November 28. That correction became part of the later documentary record. At the time, I experienced it as fixing a closing detail after the emergency had passed. In hindsight, the compressed sequence shows why a transaction made under foreclosure pressure requires especially careful documentation and review.

When we met, we discussed the foreclosure and the options as I understood them. She told me she could not pay the bank enough to stop the foreclosure and had nowhere to go if she sold. I proposed that Buy Your Home purchase the property for the mortgage-related amount we would assume or pay and allow her to remain under a lease-back.

Two days after our in-person meeting, documents were signed. The arrangement required us to address the arrears and take responsibility for the mortgage payments. Brynda transferred the property and received the right to continue living there under the lease. The foreclosure was later withdrawn after the arrears were paid.

At the time, I saw the transaction as a way to solve the immediate problem. The bank was moving toward sale. Brynda wanted to stay. We were willing to take on the debt and ownership responsibilities. The value of the property was not what drew us. Rosebrooks may have been among the lower-value properties we had purchased. The human problem was in front of us, and we believed we had found a structure that addressed it.

## Jenny

The relationship did not remain a normal landlord-tenant relationship. Brynda did not drive, and she began asking Wes for transportation to medical appointments. He took her often, perhaps a dozen times or more. We brought her hot meals on several occasions. When it became clear that getting food was difficult, Wes helped her use online ordering through stores such as Walmart and Amazon.

I want to tell this honestly. Brynda could seem demanding to me. She had needs, and it appeared that no one else was meeting them, so we helped. At the same time, she managed her own medications, bills, finances, and appointments. She knew what she needed and asked for it. Wes encouraged her to send a text each day so he would know she was all right. Some of those messages were funny observations about sports or holidays, written in complete sentences with careful grammar and punctuation.

We visited on Christmas. We brought flowers on Mother's Day. Wes helped with transportation and technology. What began as a property transaction became a form of personal care we had not expected when the phone rang.

The care extended into a medical emergency. In August 2024, Wes found Brynda in need of assistance and called emergency services. He notified her daughter, visited Brynda during the days she was hospitalized, and brought her home after she was discharged. These were not tasks described in the lease. They were the kind of things we believed a family member or close friend would do.

That is precisely why boundaries became difficult to describe later. Our help went far beyond collecting rent or maintaining a property. Brynda relied on Wes for transportation and practical technology support, while the records also show her directing her own appointments, finances, purchases, and daily affairs. Dependence in some areas and independence in others existed at the same time.

Service can create a warm story about the people providing it. That is one reason we have to look at Rosebrooks carefully. We remember meals, rides, appointments, and daily messages because those actions mattered to us. They do not, by themselves, settle every question about the transaction. Good intentions are important, but good intentions do not eliminate the need for sound documents, clear boundaries, and honest review.

## Wes

I experienced Brynda as someone who knew what she wanted help with and what she did not. I assisted with transportation, food delivery, internet, email, television, and a phone. She continued to manage her own affairs. I did what she asked in practical areas where she needed help.

That distinction later became legally important. At the time, I was not thinking like a future defendant building an evidentiary record. I thought I was helping an older woman remain in her home. The very closeness that made the arrangement feel like service would later be described by others in a far different way.

Nearly eleven months after our first meeting, Brynda's daughter came to North Carolina. Jenny and I met her for lunch with Brynda. In the weeks afterward, Brynda's communication changed. Eventually, we went to Rosebrooks and found the house closed up with no answer at the door. We later learned that Brynda had gone to Maryland.

## Jenny

The lunch had felt awkward to me, and Brynda was quiet. When the regular contact later stopped, it hurt. We did not understand what had happened, and we did not get to say goodbye. Soon afterward, a lawsuit arrived with Brynda named as plaintiff through her daughter acting as guardian.

I had noticed a change in Brynda's texts before the contact ended. The complete sentences and careful punctuation were replaced by short, informal messages that did not sound like the writing I had come to expect from her. That observation did not tell me who was writing, why the style changed, or what Brynda wanted. It told me only that something in the communication was different. At the time, the change added to my unease. Later, it became one more detail I wanted the legal process to explain.

The accusations transformed the meaning of the preceding year. Conduct we had understood as service was presented as evidence of wrongdoing. A transaction we believed had prevented foreclosure and kept Brynda in her home became the center of claims involving her capacity, our influence, our duties, and our motives.

The lawsuit did not erase what we remembered doing. Our memories did not erase the seriousness of the allegations. Both realities had to be faced.

That is the hard beginning of the Rosebrooks story. We wanted our business to be ministry. We wanted to solve a foreclosure problem without displacing the person living there. We became more involved in Brynda's daily life than we had with any prior seller.

Then we were forced to ask questions no generous version of the story had prepared us to ask. Can service become entangled with control? Can help create obligations and appearances we do not recognize while we are helping? Can we be sincere and still need our actions examined?

Rosebrooks would not let us answer those questions with slogans. The next part of the story moved into depositions, briefs, hearings, legal fees, and a judge's ruling. It also moved deeper into our marriage, because Wes and I did not experience the danger or imagine the proper response in exactly the same way.
