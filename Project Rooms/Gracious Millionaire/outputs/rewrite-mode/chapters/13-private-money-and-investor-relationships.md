# Private Money And Investor Relationships

Our business was built around a few ideas that had to become more than ideas. One was a foreclosure system modeled after what we learned from Jay Conner. Another was private money.

Going to a bank for every property was not a practical beginning for us. We did not have an established real estate business. Institutional lenders brought constraints, documentation, delays, and an appetite for handling one property at a time. We were trying to buy distressed houses quickly, often before the opportunity waited for a conventional approval process.

We bought and sold our first three houses without private lenders. The properties were inexpensive enough, and the first renovation budgets fit resources we could reach through credit cards and retirement and health-savings accounts connected to Sell Your Home. Even so, we did not have enough capital to move all three projects forward at once.

We bought and worked on the first house with retirement money. The second was acquired with credit-card money. Work on one property sometimes had to wait for another to sell. Capital was only one limitation. Our systems, contractor relationships, and experience were also young. We were over our skis with three houses, even if we did not fully recognize it at the time.

That is important because private money can sound like the solution to a funding problem. It is not only money. It is another person's confidence placed inside your unfinished business.

Our first private loan was one hundred thousand dollars from a friend in our neighborhood. We were walking on the golf-cart path one morning when we came across her, and she asked about the business. We explained our model: an individual could lend money, receive a strong return, and have the loan supported by collateral, property insurance, and title insurance. We also told her that when she needed her money returned, we would make that happen.

That last promise shaped the way we thought about liquidity. A project might still be underway when a lender's circumstances changed. Their money could not become so absorbed into our plans that we treated their request as an interruption. We wanted lenders to understand the security and return, but we also wanted them to know the relationship mattered more than squeezing every possible day of use from their capital.

She agreed to lend to us, and that money allowed us to begin our fourth house.

The conversation was simple. The responsibility it created was not.

After that, we hosted private-money dinners. We showed photographs and videos of completed projects and explained how the lending program worked. Those evenings were not merely pitches. They allowed people to see what we had done, ask questions, and decide whether they trusted both the projects and us.

Pictures helped because they made the work visible. A lender could see the condition in which we acquired a house, the rehabilitation, and the finished result. The presentation connected an interest rate and a note to actual walls, yards, kitchens, and work performed by real people. We were not asking friends to fund an abstract claim that we knew real estate. We were showing them evidence while still asking them to make their own decision.

As fix-and-flip properties sold, the money could circulate. A lender might move principal into the next property or wait until another opportunity felt right. The same dollars could participate in multiple projects, but the relationship could not be treated as a revolving account we controlled. Every use carried a promise to document, protect, communicate, pay interest, and return principal.

I have made rough cumulative estimates of what we may have borrowed, paid in interest, and earned across the portfolio. Those figures still need confirmation, so I will not use them as trophies here. The reliable point is more important: private lenders gave us access to speed and flexibility that banks would not have provided, and we understood that never missing an interest payment or a requested return of funds was central to the relationship.

Expensive money is not necessarily bad money. Cheap money is not necessarily good money. The question is whether the structure fits the project and whether the promise can be kept.

Most of our private loans were placed on properties we had already managed to acquire but had not yet been able to rehabilitate. The loan turned a waiting property into an active project. In that sense, money did not create the opportunity. It released work that was already sitting in front of us.

Private lending was not the only model. Sometimes a larger amount of capital was needed quickly, and we formed joint ventures. Investors could provide acquisition and renovation funds in exchange for a share of the eventual profit. That reduced the amount of our own cash required and allowed us to move without bank timing. It also meant giving away more of the upside, so the structure did not fit every deal.

The first formal joint-venture presentation I made was for Sandy Run. We had joined an association while building relationships with investors, and the group held a weekly video meeting where deals could be presented. We did not necessarily need partners for Sandy Run, but it looked like a good project through which to form relationships.

That choice carried its own test. Raising money simply because people were interested would have been easy. We selected five participants we felt good about rather than taking everyone who responded. Capacity to raise capital is not permission to accept every dollar. A joint venture creates voices, expectations, reporting duties, and a division of profit. The group has to fit the property, and the property has to be able to carry the group.

Interest came quickly. More people wanted to participate than the project required. We selected five people we felt comfortable working with and prepared the agreements. Sandy Run was an entry-level house needing substantial rehabilitation. The project went well, and the investors shared in the profit.

The word *shared* matters. Their capital was not a fee we paid to make them disappear. They had accepted risk with us. A good outcome belonged to the agreement we made together.

Two of those investors continued into later projects. One participated in Burgwyn. I had met with the sellers shortly before traveling to a conference in New York City. While I was there, an email conversation moved the purchase toward agreement. I reached out to investor friends attending the same conference. Within minutes, responses began arriving, and we talked through the opportunity while away from home.

Within about a day, the purchase terms and joint-venture funding were taking shape without a bank application. That speed was possible because the relationships were not created that day. The conference conversation was the visible moment. Trust had been developing before the property appeared.

Another relationship carried Old Buckhorn. Adam had participated in Sandy Run, and I called to ask whether he wanted to review the new project. I told him from the beginning that the seller would lease the property back for at least a year. His capital would be needed for the purchase and eventual renovation, but his return would be delayed. He understood and was willing.

That deal required more than enthusiasm about a projected profit. It required patience with a structure that could not move immediately from acquisition to rehabilitation and sale.

Some of these investor relationships grew while we were pursuing Providence Landing. That effort consumed time and money and did not unfold as we first imagined. Yet the friendships formed in that season later supported real projects. I do not want to use those later deals to declare every cost of Providence Landing worthwhile. That would tidy a complicated story too quickly. I can say that relationships formed during an expensive effort retained value after the original plan changed.

Capital is often described as though it were a substance flowing toward the best return. In our experience, it moved through people. People listened, watched, assessed our work, and decided whether they wanted to join us. They remembered whether we communicated when a project was delayed. They noticed whether documents were clear and whether payments arrived. They brought not only money but their own needs, timelines, retirement plans, and tolerance for risk.

That changes the spiritual question. Stewardship is not simply maximizing the spread between what money costs and what a property earns. It is carrying another person's trust without treating it as raw material for my vision.

Private money expanded what we could do. It also expanded the number of people affected by our decisions. Every additional dollar increased capacity and accountability at the same time.

I am grateful for the lenders and partners who have worked with us repeatedly. Their return participation says something about the relationships we built, but it does not give us permission to become casual. Familiarity can make promises feel routine. They are not routine to the person whose money is secured by the property.

The lesson is not that relationships are a clever way to avoid banks. The lesson is that money travels on trust, and trust has to be stewarded long after the dinner, the presentation, or the exciting first conversation is over.
