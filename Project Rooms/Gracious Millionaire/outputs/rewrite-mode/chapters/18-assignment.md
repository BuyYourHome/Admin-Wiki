# Assignment

Some opportunities are obviously wrong. Assignment becomes difficult when the opportunity is good.

Providence Landing was not a scheme scribbled on a napkin and abandoned the next week. The land existed. The runway existed. The development work already completed by others existed. We could identify real markets, real advisers, real investors, and real uses for the property. We could imagine economic value and Kingdom purpose occupying the same ground.

The question was not whether something worthwhile could be done there. The question was whether Jenny and I had been assigned to do it.

## Wes

I am drawn to possibility. Once I see a solution, I begin mapping it. A map leads to a spreadsheet, the spreadsheet leads to a presentation, and the presentation leads to people who might help make it real. Work can create its own evidence. The more effort I invest, the more unreasonable it feels to stop.

Providence Landing gave that part of me almost unlimited room. Every problem invited a system. Ownership questions could be organized. Capital could be modeled. Aircraft owners could be identified. Investor communication could be improved. A team could be built. Legal entities could be formed. The mission could be explained. Each new layer seemed to answer the last objection.

But assignment is not proved by my ability to build a process around an idea.

I believed God had directed me to the property. I believed He was guiding my thoughts about what it might become. Those beliefs mattered to me, and I do not want to rewrite them now merely because the result disappointed me. Yet faith does not excuse me from examining how I heard, what I assumed, and what the plan was doing to the people closest to me.

The discovery itself carried emotional weight. I had prayed in a hotel after leaving home angry, then encountered an airfield while driving toward unrelated vacant land. That sequence felt too precise to dismiss. But a meaningful encounter can be an invitation to learn rather than an instruction to possess. It can reveal a subject without assigning the whole project. At the time, I did not clearly distinguish those possibilities.

## Together

We proceeded despite red flags that both of us could see. The early diligence contained mismatched ownership information and acreage questions. The proposed financial structure became complicated quickly. The capital needs were far beyond an ordinary house purchase. The team would have to manage development, investor relations, construction, sales, events, reporting, and a mission larger than any operation we had previously run.

We did not ignore those things entirely. We asked questions, sought advice, built models, studied comparable communities, and developed increasingly professional materials. Caution and diligence were present. So was momentum.

The team structure made the question more serious. Roles were being imagined for management, investor communication, operations, events, sales, and construction. A missionary-restoration purpose was being tied to the land. Once other people began making room in their lives for the project, my private sense of direction carried public consequences. Calling something an assignment could encourage sacrifice from people who trusted that I had heard clearly. That meant the spiritual language needed more examination, not less.

The larger the circle of trust became, the less freedom I had to confuse personal conviction with collective certainty.

One early proposed structure shows how quickly conviction was becoming obligation. A holding company would control separate real-estate and events companies. Each operating company was proposed to have ten thousand shares. The sellers would retain 1,236 shares, leaving 8,764 to be sold or otherwise used in the capital plan. We discussed four-month due-diligence and fundraising periods, small option payments, cash at closing, promissory notes, and later rounds of partner funding for roads, model homes, a welcome center, and event facilities.

Those details were provisional, not completed outcomes. But even a proposal changes the moral weight of a vision. Share counts, notes, options, and investor money create people to whom answers will be owed. If the project failed, we would not be the only ones disappointed. Assignment had to mean more than my willingness to work hard or my confidence that the plan could eventually be funded.

This is where a married couple can discover that agreement is not a box to check at the end of a proposal. A plan may be technically sound and still produce no peace in the family asked to carry it. One spouse may see risk that the other experiences as resistance. The other may see possibility that caution experiences as pressure. If we reduce either response to a personality flaw, we lose information God may be giving us through the marriage.

Shared assignment does not require identical temperaments. It does require enough unity that the work does not depend on one person continually overcoming the other.

We were trying to combine several serious purposes. We wanted to create value. We wanted to build a community. We wanted to honor investors. We wanted to make room for service, prayer, and restoration. None of those desires was shameful. The danger was that a collection of good purposes could make the project feel automatically authorized.

The word *assignment* helps us because it puts ownership back where it belongs. An assignment is received. It is not seized merely because we are capable of imagining it. If God assigns something, our responsibility is obedience, not self-importance. If He has not assigned it, excellence and enthusiasm cannot convert it into obedience.

The word also changes how we understand provision. Money raised is not necessarily proof. An adviser who helps is not necessarily proof. An open door is not necessarily proof. Those things may support an assignment, but they can also test our motives. Opportunity reveals what we want badly enough to call confirmation.

The same is true of opposition. Difficulty does not automatically mean we should stop, but neither does it automatically prove that the work matters so much the enemy must be resisting it. Sometimes a barrier is simply information. Assignment requires discernment about both open and closed doors. Otherwise every favorable event becomes confirmation and every warning becomes something to overcome.

The original four-month diligence window quietly illustrates the problem. A deadline can focus attention, but it can also make uncertainty feel like an enemy. Ownership mismatches, acreage questions, capital gaps, and marital unease did not become less important because an option period was running. If the only way to preserve the opportunity was to outrun the questions, then the questions were already telling us something about what carrying the project would require.

## Wes

I wanted Providence Landing to work. I wanted the discovery on that Virginia road to mean what I thought it meant. I wanted the project to justify the time already spent and the promises implied by our presentations. I wanted to lead a team toward something larger than any of us had done alone.

I also wanted Jenny to see what I saw.

That desire is where vision can become dangerous. Explaining a plan is not manipulation. Answering questions is not manipulation. Inviting someone to consider a future is not manipulation. But when my peace depends on another person eventually surrendering to my conclusion, persuasion can become pressure without my admitting it.

If Jenny's hesitation existed only because she could not yet understand the model, then more explanation seemed like the answer. If her hesitation carried wisdom, more explanation could become a way of talking past the very warning I needed.

Assignment must survive honest questions. It must leave room for the people carrying it to speak without being classified as obstacles. It must also survive waiting. If a vision can be preserved only by immediate action, mounting pressure, and a refusal to reconsider, then what we call faith may be fear of losing the opportunity.

We did not answer these questions neatly while we were inside the project. We moved forward, learned, invested, presented, prayed, and strained. Only later could I see that Providence Landing was testing more than our ability to raise capital. It was testing whether I believed God's purpose could withstand scrutiny, delay, and even surrender.

The project may have been provided by God. The meeting may have been providential. The relationships may have been assignments even if the development was not. We still do not have a sentence that resolves every part of that.

What we have is a better question than the one I began with. I once asked, *Can we make this work?* Now I am learning to ask, *What, exactly, has God asked us to carry together?*
