# Blessing

"All things work together for good to those who love God, to those who are called according to His purpose."

I have known Romans 8:28 for a long time. It is easy to quote when circumstances move quickly in the direction I hoped. It is harder to resist turning the verse into a promise that every opportunity will become profitable on my schedule.

Red Quartz moved quickly at the beginning.

The foreclosure was filed on January 25, 2022. We found the file two days later. Our first letter went out on February 1 to addresses that were not receiving mail. We began text and email messages on February 4. When those efforts produced no response, I searched Facebook for the owner on February 6.

Nicholas answered almost immediately. From the courthouse filing to our first conversation with the seller, twelve days had passed.

I introduced myself and told him I was interested in buying the property on Red Quartz. I said I could pay cash, move quickly, and help rescue it from foreclosure without placing the work and expense on him. He replied that the property was already in foreclosure, the matter was going to court, and the house was locked so he could not enter.

I told him I understood and could still work with the situation if he was willing. I promised I could get him some money.

His next question was simple: what needed to be done?

I asked whether I could meet him that day. He was leaving for work, so we arranged a phone call and then a meeting for the next day at a place he selected.

It was raining. His first choice was closed because of COVID protocols. So was his second. So was his third. We finally met at a library. There I spoke with Nicholas and his wife, called his sister in Virginia, and reached an agreement. The meeting included a contract, a deed, and earnest money.

The sequence felt unusually smooth. The file appeared, the search found the right person, he responded, family members participated, and an agreement came together despite locked doors, returned mail, rain, and closed meeting places.

On that same day, friends we had been talking with for months agreed to lend us one hundred thousand dollars. The loan was enough to purchase the property, bring the existing mortgage out of foreclosure, and complete the rehabilitation.

The phrase *on that same day* carries much of the weight. The seller agreement and the funding did not develop in isolation. One answered the question raised by the other. A deed without the ability to cure the foreclosure and repair the house would not have been enough. Available money without a property and a signed agreement would have had nowhere to go. Provision arrived in a form fitted to the responsibility.

I cannot look at that sequence and credit only our efficiency. We did our part. Our system found the filing. We sent letters, texts, and emails. I searched Facebook, asked for a meeting, adjusted when locations were closed, and worked through the family details. The lender relationship had also been developing for months.

Yet the pieces came together with a timing we did not engineer.

That is what I mean by blessing here. Not a reward for cleverness. Not a declaration that every fast-moving deal carries God's endorsement. Blessing is received provision that creates responsibility.

The one hundred thousand dollars was not simply an answer to prayer. It was a friend's money. It came with documents, security, repayment, communication, and the obligation to use it as represented. The signed deed was not simply access to equity. It came out of a family under foreclosure pressure. The speed of the agreement did not reduce our duty to be accurate, patient, and fair.

That is one reason blessing cannot be separated from stewardship. Receiving the property meant stepping into deadlines and obligations that had already become urgent. Receiving the loan meant that our decisions would affect friends who trusted us. The same event that increased our opportunity increased the number of promises we had to carry. If I celebrate the provision while ignoring the obligations attached to it, I am celebrating only the part of blessing that serves me.

Blessing increases what has been placed in our hands. It also increases what can be mishandled.

I can imagine the same system producing no response. We have sent letters that disappeared into silence. We have searched for owners we could not find. We have built proposals that were never accepted. We have had plans delayed by title problems, people, money, weather, and our own mistakes. Red Quartz did not work because our process had finally become irresistible.

The failures earlier in my life keep this point from becoming theoretical. I had worked hard on the Raleigh house, the hotel, and the duplexes. Some parts of those plans were financially sound. Hard work did not protect them from loss. Remembering that season does not require me to distrust the good that happened at Red Quartz. It requires me to receive the good without using it as a verdict on my superiority or someone else's lack of faith.

That distinction keeps blessing from becoming entitlement. If I decide the result proves I know how to make God bless a deal, I have moved from gratitude into control. If I decide a failed deal proves God withheld blessing, I have reduced His work to the outcome I preferred.

Romans 8:28 does not say every event is good. It says God works in all things for good within love and calling. The verse can hold a fast agreement at Red Quartz, but it also has to be large enough to hold the projects that did not resolve quickly, the relationships that strained, and the properties we lost.

When I first wrote about Red Quartz, this was not the end of the story. I had recorded the opening, the agreement, and the funding, but not the completed rehabilitation, disposition, profit, or repayment. I will not supply those details from assumption. A testimony does not become stronger when its missing ending is polished into place.

The opening is enough to reveal the lesson available at that moment. Provision arrived alongside assignment. The response should have been gratitude and careful stewardship, not a private sense that we had become unusually deserving.

There is another temptation in the word *blessing*. Because this is a book about real estate and wealth, blessing can begin to sound like increase measured in dollars. Money is visible. A loan of one hundred thousand dollars can be entered into a spreadsheet. A foreclosure deadline can be placed on a calendar. A deed can be recorded.

But blessing also appeared in access to people. Nicholas answered. His wife listened. His sister joined the conversation. Friends trusted us with capital. A library gave us a place to meet when every planned location was closed. None of those details can be reduced to profit, even though they helped make a profitable possibility real.

The responsibility is to notice the provision without worshiping the result.

When the pieces come together quickly, I want to move even faster. That is often when attention is most needed. Urgency can make me skip questions because the path seems confirmed. Favor can make resistance feel irrelevant. Funding can make a project feel inevitable. But receiving a blessing well may require slowing down long enough to remember whose money, whose house, whose family, and whose assignment are involved.

I believe God got our attention through Red Quartz. The speed and coordination were difficult for me to dismiss as our own achievement. At the same time, the unfinished record keeps me from using the story as proof of a formula.

Blessing is not the same as getting everything I want. It is the grace to receive what has been entrusted, the discipline to carry it responsibly, and the humility to admit that I did not arrange every door that opened.

The question is not only whether I can recognize blessing.

It is whether I can be trusted with it.
