# Convergence

For most of my life, the work I was doing did not look as though it belonged to one story.

I began in the sign business, an artistic trade that depended on hands, judgment, timing, and relationships. I had no formal business education. I was trying to learn how to operate a company while I was operating it, and I was not always wise. Even then, though, I kept looking for processes that could be repeated or automated.

One of the first Radio Shack computers fascinated me. A watch now can do more than that machine could do, but at the time it represented an entirely new possibility. I imagined that if I organized the sign business well enough, employees could continue the work while I operated the company from a distance. I put that little computer in my Datsun pickup and drove across the country toward Oregon, confident that I could run the business remotely.

The picture still makes me smile: a little computer riding across the country in a pickup, carrying my confidence that distance could be solved by information. I had built instructions for the employees I left behind and believed the structure would substitute for my presence. What I learned was that a process is not transferred merely because its designer can describe it. People need context. Exceptions arise. Responsibility has to be clear. The first remote-work experiment in my life was less a technological failure than an early lesson in the human side of systems.

I was wrong.

The technology was not ready, but that was not the only problem. I had confused having a system in my head with having a system other people could reliably use. I had not yet learned how much communication, training, accountability, and relationship have to surround a process before the process can travel without its creator.

When I closed the sign business after nearly ten years, I was in my thirties. I entered college later than most students and studied mechanical engineering. The degree was difficult, but computer programming began to draw me. At the University of Florida, I worked part time for a department writing macros inside Excel. I was supposed to be becoming a mechanical engineer. In practice, another line of development was already taking shape.

After moving to North Carolina, I looked for technical work. My first position was as a CAD draftsman for an engineering firm. Then I programmed in the LISP language inside AutoCAD for Siemens. I left that job to go into business for myself again, this time developing systems that produced mail products for different clients.

That business did not become the lasting answer either. Eventually I took a position converting Excel spreadsheets into a back-end SQL Server database with a Visual Basic front end. Under my manager's direction, I was responsible for creating the whole package.

What I was really learning was how to develop systems.

One job led to another. The industries changed, but the underlying problem remained familiar: information was scattered, people needed to use it, and a process had to be made visible enough to support reliable work. For about thirty years, I built databases and connected pieces that did not naturally stay connected. My last position was as a database developer for First Citizens Bank.

Lance Wallnau uses the word *convergence* for the place where a person's developed abilities meet a divine assignment. His teaching helped me recognize something I could not see while I was living it. The sign shop, the failed experiment with the computer in the pickup, engineering school, Excel macros, AutoCAD programming, mail systems, databases, and banking work had not been random stops. They had been developing a way of seeing.

That does not mean every job was secretly a straight road toward real estate. Life is not that tidy. I made choices, changed direction, failed in business, and sometimes followed opportunities because they were the ones available. But over time, a pattern became visible. I naturally looked for structure. I wanted scattered information to become a working system. I wanted to see not only what happened, but how it could happen again without depending entirely on memory or improvisation.

In 2019, after years of listening to Ron LeGrand's real estate education, I convinced Jenny to attend one of his conferences with me. There we met Jay Conner, a North Carolina real estate teacher whose approach we liked. Around May of that year, we bought into his program.

I began putting the system into action immediately, with my own particular spin. That meant Excel spreadsheets connected to a CRM designed for real estate. The CRM held people and communication. The spreadsheets helped me analyze properties, track projects, and make decisions. I was no longer writing software for a bank or an industrial client. I was applying the same systems instinct to foreclosure files, sellers, rehabs, contractors, financing, and sales.

By October, we had bought three houses with enough potential to replace my good income as a database developer. I resigned and entered business for myself for the third time, now as a real estate investor.

Leaving the bank was not a rejection of the career that came before it. The bank had given me one of the final places to practice the skill that now supported the move. I was not abandoning thirty years of database work for an unrelated adventure. I was carrying those years into a business where the records were foreclosure filings, the users were our small team, and the output was not a report but a decision about a real house and the people connected to it.

The sentence sounds cleaner than the experience. Three houses did not mean we had mastered real estate. We had limited capital, few established relationships, and more simultaneous work than we were prepared to manage. The early properties would teach us how much we did not know about attorneys, contractors, managers, boundaries, timing, and private money. But something had converged. A skill I had spent decades developing had found a new field.

I could see a foreclosure campaign not as a pile of names but as a lead system. I could see a property not only as a house but as a project with acquisition, financing, legal, construction, and sale dependencies. Every property had its own story, so every spreadsheet evolved. A new problem became a new field, a new calculation, a new checklist, or a new warning for the next project.

That is the strength of systems thinking. It is also its danger.

A system makes action repeatable. It can preserve details that memory loses. It can help a small company act with discipline. But a system can also tempt its designer to believe that everything important can be reduced to a process. Sellers are not records. Contractors are not resources on a timeline. Jenny is not another stakeholder to bring into alignment. God is not the final approval step in a workflow I designed.

The failed years had already shown me that technical ability could not carry a life. Convergence did not erase that warning. It made the warning more important because the systems were now producing real results.

I believe God blessed the abilities I had developed and gave us favor as we put them to work. I also know we had to get up, collect the files, send the letters, knock on doors, build the spreadsheets, hire people, and make offers. Favor did not make effort unnecessary. Effort did not make favor unnecessary. The mystery is in refusing to use one truth to cancel the other.

Years later, artificial intelligence would move me another step away from being the person who wrote every line and toward being the person who designed, directed, and supervised systems. Jean, our AI office assistant, would work inside structures that had been developing for years. She would make some of those structures more consistent and more useful. But she did not create the habit of order, and she did not decide what the order was for.

That distinction matters enough to deserve its own chapter.

Convergence is not the moment when everything becomes easy. It is the moment when the accumulated pieces of a life become available for an assignment. Availability is not the same as lordship. A mature skill can serve God, or it can give ambition better tools.

I am still learning the difference.
