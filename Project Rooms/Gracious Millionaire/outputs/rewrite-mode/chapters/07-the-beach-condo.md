# The Beach Condo

When I bought the oceanfront condo in Garden City Beach, I intended it to be an investment and nothing more.

I knew the area from earlier vacations, but I had no plan to live in the unit. I wanted vacationers to rent it, and I wanted the income to carry the property. I carefully calculated the expected rent, mortgage, insurance, homeowners association fees, and other known expenses. I hired an onsite management company to handle the rentals and the practical details.

This was the first property in the story that I approached openly as an investment. The family homes had been purchased as places to live. The condo had a different job. It needed to attract vacationers, generate rent, and justify the portion of my home-sale proceeds I had placed into it. I was not counting on appreciation alone. I expected the property to work.

The company charged 40 percent.

That number may sound extraordinary, but I had done my homework. At the time, 40 percent was the going rate up and down the beach. I did not accept it blindly. I compared the market and built the cost into my expectations.

The first summer seemed to confirm the plan. The rental income paid an entire year of mortgage payments. I thought I was on my way.

Early success can make a calculation feel more complete than it is. The result did not merely please me; it appeared to validate the research. The management percentage had been accounted for. Vacation demand had shown up. The largest recurring obligation, the mortgage, was covered by one season of rentals.

What I had not investigated adequately was the possibility that the homeowners association dues and assessments could rise. Within three or four years, those costs put the property in the red, even though the rental income continued to cover every mortgage payment.

That distinction was frustrating. The condo was not failing in the most obvious way. Guests were renting it. Rent was paying the debt exactly as I had hoped. The pressure came from the shared ownership structure around the unit, from costs whose future movement I had not tested. I had calculated the visible machine and missed a lever someone else could move.

I panicked.

That word is important because it is how the pressure felt. I had researched the investment, but I had not researched all of it. The missing question became the one that threatened the whole calculation. I did not have the education, experience, or confidence I have now, and I could not see how to remain financially afloat.

I hired a contractor to remodel the condo completely and put it on the market.

The year was 2008.

Anyone who worked in real estate through that period understands what that date means. Home prices were falling dramatically as the real estate and subprime mortgage crisis unfolded. It was a time to buy if a person had cash and confidence. It was not a good time to sell. I believed I had no choice.

The condo sat on the market. There were no showings and no offers. The financial clock kept moving.

The remodel had made the unit ready, but readiness did not create a market. Each day without a showing left me with the same ownership costs and less confidence that my solution would work. The careful plan had become a waiting problem, and waiting was not one of my strengths.

During the general period of my condo ownership, I remarried. Number Two was not personally invested in the condo. I want that distinction kept clear because the property was my investment and its pressure was attached to decisions I had made. The story of that marriage and its abrupt end belongs in the next chapter, but the timelines overlap.

Fear and faith overlapped too. I cannot say I was calm. I was anything but patient. Yet I believed my Heavenly Father held me and had a plan I could not see. I clung to Him and to His promises while I waited for some indication of what to do next.

After the remodel, I hired a woman who lived in the same building to clean the unit before the real estate photographs and the showings I expected to follow. Because she cleaned for several absentee owners, she knew people throughout the building. She told one of those owners that my condo was for sale.

She was not hired as a broker or a strategist. She was there to clean. Yet she possessed something the formal listing had not produced: an ordinary relationship with another owner who already understood the building. The help came through work that could easily have seemed peripheral to the transaction.

Just like that, I had a buyer.

The source of the opportunity was not the marketing sequence I had imagined. It came through a person already present in the building, doing ordinary work, speaking to someone she knew. My research had not produced that relationship. My panic had not produced it either.

I do not want to turn this into a formula that says every distressed investment will receive a buyer at the right moment. I also do not want to erase the importance of planning simply because my plan had a hole in it. The chapter holds both truths. I should have investigated the association more carefully. I was also met by provision I could not manufacture.

The faces-in-the-picture test applies here too. A flattering version would omit the panic and present the buyer as confirmation of my investment judgment. A harsher version would ignore everything I researched correctly and make the missed association risk proof that caution failed. Neither face is mine. I prepared carefully, missed something consequential, became afraid, acted, waited badly, and received help through a connection I had not designed.

The lesson I still struggle to live is that waiting is not the absence of action, but neither is frantic action the same as faithfulness. I remodeled. I listed. I hired help. I kept looking for an answer. At the same time, the opening arrived through a conversation I did not control.

I had wanted an investment that was calculable. Instead, the condo became another place where I learned that careful numbers cannot eliminate dependence.
