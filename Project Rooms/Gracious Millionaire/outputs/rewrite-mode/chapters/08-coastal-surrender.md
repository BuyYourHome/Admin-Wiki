# Coastal Surrender

Several years after moving to North Carolina, and after buying the beach condo, I remarried. I genuinely believed Number Two would be my forever relationship.

It was not.

The marriage ended abruptly, almost before it began. I remember it as amazingly great and amazingly horrible. I came away from it with raw emptiness and an enormous sense of failure. I was hard on myself for what felt like poor judgment and a departure from my usual caution. I had built much of my life around being careful, and here was another marriage ending.

The self-accusation reached beyond the relationship. If I was the person who researched, guarded, planned, and tried to see trouble before it arrived, what did this ending say about my judgment? The qualities I relied on for safety could not explain how something I believed would last had changed so quickly.

I became jaded about marriage, if not about men in general. More urgently, I wanted to get away from the pain, the gossip, the reminders, and especially the threats that made me fear for my safety.

My grown children were attending college in three different states, none of them in North Carolina. Returning to Northern Virginia did not feel possible. It was too expensive, and I did not want to return to the winters there. The South Carolina coast near my condo was familiar from years of vacations. I decided I would rent a place inland, move there, and begin again.

The coast offered familiarity without requiring me to return to the life I was trying to leave. I knew the area well enough to imagine living near it, but I did not plan to move into the oceanfront investment. I would find an affordable rental inland, put distance between myself and the reminders in Raleigh, and create a new beginning that still looked financially responsible.

The plan sounded simple. I spent several winter days at the condo while it was not being rented, searching for a place to live. I expected to find something quickly.

Instead, doors closed.

I was rejected repeatedly for one reason or another. My seventy-pound poodle was often the problem. Three long days passed, and I had found nothing. On the final night before I planned to return to Raleigh, the confidence behind my plan was gone. I felt alone, exhausted, and empty.

Each rejection narrowed the future I had pictured. The dog was not an incidental detail I could remove to make an application easier. He was coming with me. By the third day, the problem was no longer simply finding a rental. The closed doors began to feel like evidence that I had misjudged the escape plan too.

I sat on the sofa and opened my laptop to search one more time. It slipped from my hands and struck the tile floor. The screen became a scramble of lines that reminded me of an Etch A Sketch.

That small accident landed on top of everything else. I was at the end of myself. I cried out to the Lord for help. I am ashamed to admit how dark the moment became. I looked toward the fifteenth-floor balcony door and considered what it represented.

I include that detail because leaving it out would make the night safer for the page than it was for me. It would give the reader the cleaned-up face: a discouraged woman prayed, a friend called, and everything improved. The truth is that despair had become dangerous before the phone rang.

Then the phone rang.

The only person I knew in the area was calling at ten o'clock at night. Linda.

I tried to sound fine when I answered. She immediately asked, "Are you OK?"

I said I was. She did not accept it. "I'm coming over there," she told me, almost as an order.

For the next twenty minutes, I stared at the scrambled computer screen and prayed. Linda arrived, saw the laptop, and called her husband. Somehow, together, they restored it. More important, she stayed. She reassured me that things would turn around.

Linda did not wait for me to admit the truth before she acted. She heard something my words were trying to conceal. Her question, her insistence, and her physical presence interrupted the isolation. The repaired screen mattered because I needed the computer, but the deeper rescue was that I was no longer alone in the room.

The next morning, I packed, loaded the car, and began driving back toward Raleigh. I still did not know where I was going to live or what I would do next. As I approached the outskirts of the coastal area, my phone rang again.

One of the management companies where I had left messages was calling.

"We have several condos for you to choose from."

I turned the car around and drove directly to the office in Pawleys Island. I took my poodle inside with me. If they were going to reject us, I wanted them to see from the beginning that he was part of the package.

That small decision carried some of my old practicality back into the moment. I was grateful for the call, but I was not willing to create another false beginning by hiding the condition that had closed other doors. The dog came through the office door with me. Acceptance had to include the life I was actually bringing.

The woman in the office handed me two keys and directions to a new condominium development. She told me to go choose. The units were brand new and beautiful. I still had a practical question: Could I afford the rent?

I returned to the office, applied, and was approved to move in immediately.

The relief and gratitude were beyond what I could express. The previous night I had been staring toward a balcony door, unable to see a way forward. The next morning I was holding keys and being invited to choose between two places to live.

The turn was so quick that it would be easy to describe the three days of rejection as a test arranged merely to produce a dramatic answer. I cannot say that. I know only the sequence. Doors closed. I reached despair. Linda called and came. Morning arrived. A message was returned. I turned the car around.

I do not tell this story as proof that every closed door opens the next day. That would turn one of the most vulnerable moments of my life into a promise my experience cannot support. I tell it because this is what happened to me. Linda called when I was hiding how badly I was doing. A management company returned a message when I had already started driving away. Provision arrived, but not while I was successfully holding everything together.

My surrender was not a polished spiritual act. It came when my grip failed. I had reached the limit of my ability to research, plan, and fix the outcome. I became more dependent on the Lord than I had ever been.

That does not make planning the villain of the story. The management company had my message because I had contacted it. I could evaluate the rent because I still understood my financial limits. I completed an application. Surrender did not erase action; it changed my belief that action gave me control of the answer.

He had been there the whole time. I had to stop and let go.

That sentence is true, but the lesson remains unfinished. I still prefer certainty. I still want to know where a road leads before I turn the car around. Coastal surrender did not erase that part of me or explain everything that came next. It gave me a place to live and a moment I could not deny.

For now, that is where this part of the story ends: not with the whole future resolved, but with two keys in my hand and enough grace for the next step.
