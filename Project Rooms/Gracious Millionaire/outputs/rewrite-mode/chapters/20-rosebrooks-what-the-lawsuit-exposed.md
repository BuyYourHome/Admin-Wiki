# Rosebrooks: What The Lawsuit Exposed

## Jenny

When the lawsuit arrived, I was numb. Fear, anger, and hurt came so close together that I could not always separate them. I wanted Brynda to look us in the face and tell us why. I wanted the year of rides, meals, messages, appointments, and concern to count for something.

Instead, we entered a system I had never wanted to learn. We hired a respected law firm and began the long work of calls, records, depositions, motions, briefs, and hearings. I discovered that I could help our attorneys by finding documents, reconstructing events, and comparing claims with the records we had kept. They welcomed the work. Someone told me I would make a good paralegal. I felt more like a private investigator, a role I had secretly admired when I was younger.

The complaint was filed in November 2024. The court record listed claims or requested remedies involving breach of fiduciary duty, fraud and constructive fraud, civil conspiracy and title, mental competency or incapacity, undue influence, unfair trade practices, punitive damages, declaratory relief, and money allegedly owed. Listing them still does not capture what it felt like to see ordinary moments from the preceding year placed beneath those headings.

Each allegation carried its own legal elements, but emotionally they arrived together. The complaint did not feel to me like a technical disagreement about one document. It felt like a judgment on the entire relationship. That reaction made careful legal work harder and more necessary. Hurt could motivate me to search, but it could not be allowed to decide what the evidence proved.

The work gave me something to do with the fear. It did not remove the fear.

The legal bills were enormous. Hourly rates above six hundred dollars startled me. Eventually I had to stop studying every statement as if vigilance alone could control the cost. I tried to trust that God would provide while continuing to do the next responsible thing.

For about eighteen months, the case occupied our attention. Then both sides asked the court for summary judgment. Each side argued that the judge could decide some or all of the claims without sending them to a jury.

The hearing lasted about two hours. I was unfamiliar with courtrooms and uncomfortable in one. The opposing presentation described events in a way I did not recognize. I had to sit still while allegations about our conduct were argued as legal facts to be decided. When it ended, we walked out and waited.

Summary judgment required the judge to decide which issues could be resolved as a matter of law and which, if any, still depended on disputed facts. That procedural purpose mattered. A surviving claim would not mean the plaintiff had won it. A dismissed claim would not necessarily answer every moral question we had about our own decisions. The court was deciding what could proceed, not writing the final meaning of Rosebrooks for this book.

I thought the decision might come in days. It took ten weeks.

## Wes

During those ten weeks, the lawsuit remained part of our morning prayers. We asked God for a favorable ruling. We also had to continue running the business while a potentially expensive and damaging case remained unresolved.

The judge's response arrived through our attorney in four short sentences. The plaintiff's motion for summary judgment was denied on all claims. Our motion was granted on every cause of action except undue influence. The judge found a genuine issue of material fact on that remaining count and directed counsel to prepare the order.

Our attorney described the result as very good. According to his explanation, the ruling removed, subject to appellate review, the Chapter 75 exposure that could have involved treble damages and attorney fees. He also understood the ruling to reject the asserted fiduciary relationship or duty and the competency issue as matters requiring trial. The undue-influence claim remained because it was fact-specific and, absent settlement, could proceed to a jury.

The Chapter 75 issue had carried a special financial threat because a qualifying unfair-trade-practices award can multiply damages and add attorney-fee exposure. Its removal changed the risk entering mediation. The ruling also narrowed the factual story the parties would be arguing if the case continued. But "narrower" did not mean inexpensive, painless, or predictable. Preparing one surviving claim for trial could still require substantial legal work and place deeply personal events before a jury.

That is the legal position as we understood it when this chapter was written. It is not a final ending. The remaining claim has not been resolved here, and the possibility of mediation, trial, or appellate review means we should not write as though the case is over.

## Jenny

My first response was relief and gratitude. God had moved a mountain that had been in front of us every day. We stopped what we were doing and prayed a prayer of thanks.

Then I began making a list of questions for the meeting with our attorney.

That movement from prayer back to preparation describes much of the case for me. I believed God was with us, but I did not experience faith as the absence of work. I read, gathered, organized, and questioned. I wanted the remaining count gone. When I realized it might require mediation or a jury trial, I wanted to find a settlement and finish the case as soon as reasonably possible.

Was that fear or wisdom? I still do not have a simple answer.

A trial in front of a jury felt almost paralyzing to me. Settlement had its own cost, especially if it required us to pay to end a claim we believed was weak. But legal victory also had costs that could not be recovered simply by being right. There were more fees, more preparation, more time, more exposure, and more strain.

By then I had learned that litigation invoices do not measure only legal activity. They measure hours of our lives redirected into defense. They measure business decisions postponed, records revisited, and conversations in which Wes and I had to discuss the same painful facts again. A settlement could feel like surrender. A trial could feel like refusing to surrender while allowing the case to consume still more of what we were trying to protect.

## Wes

My first strategic reaction differed from Jenny's. I believed what remained of the case was weak. I thought mediation demands might be largely positional and that we should communicate both a willingness to end the matter reasonably and a willingness to proceed to trial if necessary. I was thinking about leverage, cost, and what the other side might do after most of the claims had been removed.

Those were my judgments, not established facts. I could not know what the plaintiff, her daughter, or their attorney would decide. I also could not treat the favorable order as proof that every interpretation I held about the case was correct.

Jenny was more ready to consider settlement because trial could cost more than resolution. I was more ready to resist a settlement that did not meet what I considered reasonable expectations. Neither response could be reduced safely to fear or faith. Her caution might have been wisdom about litigation's real costs. My willingness to proceed might have been resolve, or it might have included pride. We needed time, counsel, prayer, and honest conversation.

That is why we chose not to reach a conclusion in the first hours after the email. We discussed the ruling, praised God, and scheduled time with our attorney. More decisions remained in front of us.

## Jenny

The lawsuit exposed how easily service, money, age, dependence, documentation, and influence can become entangled. It also exposed me.

I learned that I could work hard inside a system I feared. I learned that gratitude and dread can occupy the same day. I learned that I wanted vindication, but I also wanted escape. I learned that a favorable ruling could bring enormous relief without bringing final peace.

The case also forced us to examine the ministry language we had used so confidently. Calling a business ministry does not place it above scrutiny. It raises the standard. If we say we serve vulnerable people, then our agreements, boundaries, records, explanations, and conduct must be able to bear examination. Compassion cannot substitute for clarity. Profit does not prove exploitation, and benevolent intent does not prove that every decision was wise.

## Wes

I asked why God would give us such a broad victory and leave one count unresolved. My first interpretation was that the remaining issue might be another test: would we respond with fear or faith?

I still believe testing is part of the story, but the question may be larger than whether we are brave enough to go to trial. Faith might require standing firm. It might also require accepting a prudent settlement. It might require letting counsel correct my assumptions. It certainly requires that I not turn confidence into a prediction and then call the prediction God's promise.

Rosebrooks is unfinished. The judge's ruling materially changed the case and reduced the claims against us. One claim remained. Mediation and possibly trial remained. Our different instincts remained.

So did the morning prayer.

We can thank God for what has happened without pretending to know what happens next. We can believe the story will be used for good without deciding in advance what that good must look like. For now, that is the most truthful place to stop.
