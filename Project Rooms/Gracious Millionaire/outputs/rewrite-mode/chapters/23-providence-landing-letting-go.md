# Providence Landing: Letting Go

More than two years have passed since I decided to stop trying to develop Providence Landing. Distance has made some parts clearer. It has not made the whole story simple.

I arranged to meet the owners face-to-face. After all the calls, models, presentations, relationships, documents, and plans, I did not want the ending delivered as a brief message. I drove to Virginia to tell them I could not see any further effort I could make that would move the project forward.

Letting go was hard because Providence Landing had become more than a possible purchase. I had spent an enormous amount of time developing plans, pursuing relationships, learning the language of investment, and organizing a team. We had studied airpark markets and aircraft owners. We had worked through ownership lists, acreage questions, legal entities, management roles, investor terms, marketing, roads, construction, events, and mission.

The project had its own gravity. Every spreadsheet, meeting, and relationship made withdrawal feel more expensive. Time already spent became an argument for spending more. Money already committed became an argument for finding the next money. People who had believed in the vision became reasons not to disappoint them.

This is one reason large projects can be dangerous even when no single decision looks reckless. Commitment accumulates quietly. Eventually the leader is not deciding whether the next step is wise. He is trying to protect the meaning of all the prior steps.

I had been fully convinced that God directed me to the property. I believed He guided my thoughts about its development. I do not know how to look back and sort perfectly among providence, imagination, ambition, calling, and desire. I am reluctant to solve the tension by declaring that I never heard God at all. I am equally reluctant to claim that every plan carrying His name was therefore His plan.

The facts deserve their own weight.

Early diligence showed ownership and acreage discrepancies. The structure became complex almost immediately. We considered multiple companies, option agreements, seller-retained interests, promissory notes, investor shares, management obligations, and phased development. The capital model projected roughly $4.69 million in first-year expenditures and negative cash flow of about $2.91 million before later revenue streams matured. The investor materials carried confident projected returns. The organization required development, construction, sales, events, investor compliance, administration, and mission to operate together.

By the later investor-presentation stage, the opportunity was being described to accredited investors with a fifty-thousand-dollar minimum and a five-year commitment. The materials projected a 15 percent internal rate of return and 20.7 percent cash-on-cash performance at the minimum investment level, with still higher projected returns tied to an eventual sale. Those figures were projections prepared to explain a proposed investment. They were not realized returns, and they depended on development, sales, timing, cost, and exit assumptions all working closely enough to the model.

The confidence of the presentation created its own responsibility. A polished prospectus can make an uncertain future feel measured. The spreadsheet may be internally consistent while the land, roads, market, team, and capital remain full of variables. If we accepted investor money, the difference between projected and actual results would no longer be an abstract lesson. It would affect people who had trusted us with at least fifty thousand dollars each.

Those numbers and structures did not prove failure. They did reveal scale. Providence Landing was not simply a larger version of buying a house subject to an existing mortgage. It was a development enterprise that could consume the attention of everyone assigned to it and expose every weakness in the team.

The operating plan confirms that scale. Proposed roles placed Jenny and me in overall management and investor-compliance work. Other team members were assigned operations, events, public relations, sales, and construction. Separate documents tried to define the relationship between the development and the missionary-restoration work, including lodging, laundry, amenities, service expectations, and a first year in which some mission roles would receive no salary. These plans showed genuine effort to make the purpose operational. They also showed how many promises had to be coordinated before the first larger development phase could succeed.

Even the routine records told the story. Banking notices, beneficial-owner documents, electronic banking, legal drafts, subscription concepts, reporting duties, transfer restrictions, and key-person provisions accumulated around the vision. None of those items was dramatic enough for a pitch deck. Together they revealed the organization we would have to sustain after the excitement of raising money.

We met capable, generous people along the way. One relationship became especially valuable to Jenny and me. A man named Fred took a genuine interest in us and gave extraordinary amounts of his time. If that time could be translated into professional fees, its value might have reached hundreds of thousands of dollars or more. We learned about capital, presentations, qualified investors, fund structures, and the language needed to discuss a project at that level.

Those relationships were not wasted because the development stopped. We hope some of them continue. Providence Landing may have assigned people to our lives even if it did not assign the land to our future.

The harder review concerns our marriage.

Looking back, I do not know how I would have carried the project if the funding had arrived and full development had begun. Raising money had felt like the barrier. With distance, I can see that receiving the money might have begun the most dangerous phase. Investor capital would have created obligations, deadlines, reporting, construction pressure, and the need to turn projections into results. The vision would no longer have belonged mainly to my imagination. Other people's savings and expectations would have been attached to it.

The early plan anticipated later capital for roads, model homes, a welcome center, and stronger event facilities. Each phase depended on the prior phase creating enough confidence for the next. Delays in roads could affect lot sales. Delays in sales could affect cash available for construction. Event and mission uses could create operating costs before they generated support. A project can be profitable in the final column and still fail because cash is needed years before that column becomes real.

I do not know what that stress would have done to Jenny and me.

That sentence matters because the story began with me leaving home during a fight about the business. I discovered the airfield while separated from Jenny by my own angry decision. Then I spent years trying to build a project whose success would require more unity, trust, and shared endurance than almost anything we had attempted.

The origin and the ending belong together. The airfield cannot be presented as a pure miracle detached from the condition of the marriage in which I found it. Nor can the marital conflict erase the possibility that God met me in the middle of my failure. Grace often meets us where we are. That does not mean grace endorses every structure we build after the meeting.

Jenny's resistance was information I did not value soon enough. I often treated peace as something that would arrive after she understood the plan. I did not adequately consider that the absence of peace in our family might itself be a limit on the plan.

This does not make Jenny the person who ended Providence Landing. The project carried financial, structural, operational, and execution risks independent of her concerns. It also does not make me the sole visionary who dragged everyone else forward. Jenny worked inside the project, appeared in the planning, carried roles, met people, and shared some of the hopes. The honest account is more complicated: we both participated, we both saw red flags, and the cost did not fall on only one of us.

The clearest lesson I can name now is that my plans cannot supersede the peace of our family. A vision may be profitable, generous, and spiritually appealing, yet still demand more than we have been asked to give. Marriage is not an inconvenience to assignment. If Jenny and I are to carry something together, the way we carry it is part of the assignment.

I do not know exactly what I should have done differently at every stage. I can say I should have paid attention to Jenny's resistance sooner. I should have treated the strain in our marriage as seriously as the gaps in the capital model. I should have been more willing to distinguish discovering Providence Landing from being responsible to develop it.

Letting go did not turn the project into a mistake without value. We learned. We built relationships. We saw how quickly technology, systems, mission, and ambition can combine into a story powerful enough to recruit our whole lives. We learned that a sophisticated plan can still rest on unresolved assumptions. We learned that provision is not always permission to continue.

I once believed the great challenge was finding enough capital to make Providence Landing happen. Now I wonder whether the greater grace was that the capital did not arrive in the form and timing necessary to pull us deeper.

I cannot prove that. I will not manufacture a victory out of disappointment. The development did not become what we hoped. Time and money were spent. Relationships carried expectations. Surrender hurt.

But surrender also returned the future to God.

Providence Landing remains one of the clearest examples in our life of a lesson that refuses to become a formula. Sometimes a door opens because we are meant to walk through it. Sometimes it opens so we can see what is on the other side. Sometimes we begin walking and discover that obedience later requires us to stop.

The name still means something to me. Providence is not proved only when a project succeeds. It may also be present when a plan is interrupted, when a marriage is protected, when relationships outlast a venture, and when a leader finally loosens his grip.

I found the runway while asking God to speak. Years later, I had to listen again when the answer was no longer expansion, but release.
