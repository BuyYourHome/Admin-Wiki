# Providence Landing: An Editor's Business Review

Before I leave Providence Landing, I want to do something a little different.

Most of this book is written from inside my own memory. I am telling you what I saw, what I felt, what I thought God was doing, where I was wrong, where I was learning, and where the story is still working on me. That is the right way for most of the book to be written because this is not a detached business case study. It is a testimony.

But Providence Landing became so large, and the documents around it became so layered, that I asked my editor, Jean, to review the Providence Landing materials from the outside and give me a business summary.

I wanted another set of eyes on it.

Not to replace the spiritual story.

Not to explain away the sense of providence I felt when I first saw that airfield.

But to look at the documents, the pitch materials, the survey, the marketing folder, the investor language, and the scale of what we were trying to build, and then tell me what the story looks like from a business perspective.

That matters because Providence Landing was not only a moment of discovery. It became a plan. It became a pitch. It became a team. It became a capital raise. It became a vision we were trying to make legible to other people.

When a dream moves from your heart into documents, spreadsheets, brochures, investor decks, and market research, it starts telling a second story.

This is Jean's summary of that second story.

### Jean's Business Review

From the outside, Providence Landing does not look like a simple real estate opportunity.

It looks like a vision that grew into a business model before it had been fully tested by the weight of execution.

That is the first thing I see.

The discovery story is strong. Wes was driving in rural Virginia looking for land where he might one day build an airpark. He was not looking for an existing aviation community. The property he had mapped was not the airfield. It was on the other side of the road. Then, before he reached the listed property, he saw the signs that something unusual was already there: order, fencing, pasture, buildings, a runway, aircraft, a hangar, and a windsock.

From a narrative point of view, that is almost too clean to ignore.

Wes was aiming at one target and passed the real one on the way.

From a business point of view, the discovery was also meaningful. This was not raw land that would require every entitlement, every road, every lot, every aviation element, and every market proof to be created from nothing. Robie and Joan Potts had already done years of work. They had developed the airfield, subdivided the property, established an aviation identity, and created something that could be recognized as more than acreage.

That matters.

Vision is cheaper when it exists only in the imagination. Providence Landing had physical evidence. It had land, a runway, lots, buildings, history, and owners who had already shaped the property around aviation. That gave Wes something real to respond to.

It also made the temptation stronger.

The business materials show how quickly the opportunity expanded. In the early telling, Providence Landing can sound like a man finding an airfield while looking for land. In the marketing folder, it becomes a much larger concept: a pilot-centered residential and recreational airpark, hundreds of acres, buildable lots, hangar homes, short-term rentals, RV sites, fly-ins, hunting, equestrian uses, events, retreats, weddings, catering, fuel, tie-downs, and the possibility of a longer future runway.

The documents were not merely selling land.

They were selling a destination.

That is the strongest business idea in the file.

Pilots do not fly somewhere only because there is a runway. They fly somewhere because there is a reason to land. The marketing materials understood that. They tried to make Providence Landing into a place where aviation, lodging, food, recreation, community, and mission could meet.

The old aviation idea of the hundred-dollar hamburger became more than a phrase. It became part of the economic logic. A pilot might fly in for a meal, but if the place worked, he might also stay overnight, attend an event, bring friends, buy a lot, build a hangar home, or return for a retreat.

That is a compelling concept.

It is also a complicated one.

The more revenue streams a plan includes, the easier it is to make the spreadsheet look exciting. Lots can be sold. Homes can be built. Rentals can be booked. Events can be hosted. RV sites can be filled. Fuel can be sold. Conferences can be scheduled. Investors can be shown multiple paths to return.

But every added revenue stream also adds operational weight.

Hospitality is not the same business as land development. Events are not the same business as aviation. RV sites are not the same business as residential lots. Fuel, food, weddings, retreats, missionary restoration, and hangar homes may complement each other in a vision document, but in the real world each one requires management, compliance, capital, labor, marketing, scheduling, insurance, and discipline.

That is where Providence Landing begins to look less like one opportunity and more like several businesses layered on top of one property.

That does not make it wrong.

It does make it heavy.

The marketing files show that Wes and Jenny were not merely intoxicated by an idea. They were trying to support the idea with research. The airpark comparison spreadsheet looked at dozens of airparks in surrounding states. The pitch materials described a market with limited comparable supply and argued that Providence Landing could offer a rare combination of acreage, aviation, lifestyle, lodging, and recreation.

That kind of research matters because it keeps the story from becoming purely emotional. There was a real attempt to ask whether the concept fit the market.

The pilot survey is especially important because it gives both encouragement and warning.

The encouraging part is obvious. Pilots expressed interest in fly-ins, food near the landing strip, refueling, restroom access, overnight stays, hangar homes, and referrals. That supported the central idea that the project could become a destination.

But the warning was also there.

Some of the feedback suggested that the runway, as it existed, might not draw the higher-end aviation customer the project hoped to attract. A short turf runway is different from a longer paved runway with broader aircraft capacity. Requests for an IFR approach, a community center, and shooting facilities showed that the market was interested, but not necessarily simple. The survey did not just say yes. It said yes with conditions.

That is often what good market feedback does.

It does not kill the dream.

It complicates it.

In a healthy process, complication slows the pace and sharpens the plan. It forces leadership to decide what the first version of the business really is. Is Providence Landing first a land-development project? Is it first an aviation community? Is it first a hospitality destination? Is it first a ministry-retreat property? Is it first an investor-backed lifestyle brand?

The documents appear to answer, "Yes."

That is where the risk appears.

A business can eventually become many things, but it has to start by becoming one thing well enough to survive.

Providence Landing was trying to carry a very large story very early.

The investor materials show the next phase of that pressure. Once the project moved into pitch decks and capital language, the dream had to become believable to people who were not standing with Wes on the driveway looking at the windsock. It had to be translated into management roles, projected returns, capital structure, preferred equity, market positioning, and exit assumptions.

That translation is not wrong. Large projects require capital. Capital requires trust. Trust requires clarity.

But the act of raising money changes the atmosphere around a dream.

When a project is private, it can remain partly undefined. When a project is pitched, it has to sound more certain than it may actually be. It has to present a path. It has to give numbers. It has to describe risk without letting risk overwhelm interest. It has to make the future feel investable.

That creates pressure.

The Providence Landing materials show a project moving from discovery into performance. Wes and Jenny were not only trying to understand whether the opportunity should be pursued. They were also trying to show others that they were capable of leading it.

That distinction matters.

There is a difference between discerning an opportunity and proving you are the person who can carry it.

The management-team materials make this visible. Wes was presented as the visionary and CEO. Jenny was presented as the implementor and CFO. Aviation, marketing, construction, airfield operations, and outside advisory relationships were assembled around the plan. Robie and Joan were not erased from the story; they were presented as people whose prior work and continuing involvement mattered.

That was a serious attempt to build credibility.

It was also a sign that Providence Landing had become too large for the original discovery story to carry by itself.

The spiritual and business stories began to press on each other.

The spiritual story said, "This was provided."

The business story asked, "Can this be executed?"

Those are not the same question.

Providence may explain how Wes found the opportunity. It does not by itself answer whether the capital structure was right, whether the runway was adequate, whether the demand was deep enough, whether the timeline was realistic, whether the team had the right operating capacity, or whether the scale should have been narrowed before being pitched.

That is the business tension.

The missionary-restoration thread adds another important layer. Providence Landing was not only imagined as a place for pilots and investors. The documents connect the project to a desire to provide restoration for missionaries returning from difficult places. That gives the plan moral and spiritual weight. The idea was not simply to make money from land. It was to create a place of rest, community, renewal, and provision.

That part should not be treated as decoration.

It may be one of the reasons the dream carried so much force.

When a business plan includes both profit and ministry, the emotional pull becomes stronger. The numbers matter, but the purpose makes the numbers feel holy. That can be beautiful when the business is truly submitted to wisdom. It can be dangerous when purpose is used, even unintentionally, to push past caution.

In Providence Landing, both possibilities were present.

That is why Jenny's caution becomes so important.

From the outside, Jenny's hesitation should not be read as a lack of faith. It should be read as part of the discernment system. Wes carried vision, energy, and the ability to see what something could become. Jenny carried caution, implementation reality, relational weight, and the household cost of a dream becoming pressure.

Large assignments need both.

If vision is not joined to caution, it can become acceleration without governance. If caution is not joined to vision, it can become fear. But when both are submitted to God, the tension between them becomes one of the ways wisdom enters the process.

The Providence Landing files make the danger of acceleration visible.

There were mentor networks, investor-pitch environments, outside business personalities, and the language of scale. Those environments can help a person think bigger. They can also reward confidence before a plan has been adequately humbled by reality.

That is not a criticism of ambition by itself.

Ambition is not automatically sin. A large project requires faith, courage, imagination, and risk. The question is not whether the dream was large. The question is whether the dream remained submitted.

Submitted to God.

Submitted to unity.

Submitted to market reality.

Submitted to operational capacity.

Submitted to warning signs.

Submitted to timing.

That is where Providence Landing becomes more than a project summary. It becomes a spiritual business case.

The business opportunity was real. The property was real. The owners' prior work was real. The market research had substance. The pilot interest was not imaginary. The destination-airpark concept had power. The missionary-restoration purpose gave the plan meaning. The team-building effort showed seriousness.

And still, the opportunity was not simple.

That is the point.

The documents do not support a lazy conclusion that Providence Landing was foolish from the beginning. They also do not support a triumphant conclusion that providential discovery was enough to make the project wise at every stage.

They show something more human and more useful.

They show a sincere dream becoming a complex business, and then becoming a test of stewardship.

From a business perspective, Providence Landing needed narrowing. It needed a first phase that could be executed with the capital, runway, market, team, and management capacity actually available. It needed clear separation between what was immediate, what was future, what was ministry, what was revenue, what was marketing, and what was required before investor money could be responsibly taken.

From a spiritual perspective, Providence Landing needed the same thing in different language.

It needed lordship.

The dream needed to bow.

The numbers needed to bow.

The desire to be seen as credible needed to bow.

The excitement of outside validation needed to bow.

The disappointment of delay needed to bow.

The sense of providence itself needed to bow.

That may sound strange, but it is important. Even the thing that feels provided by God cannot become lord. A door can be from God and still not be allowed to govern the people who walk through it.

Providence Landing was large enough to inspire Wes.

It was also large enough to expose him.

That may be why it belongs in this book.

If this were only a real estate manual, the chapter might end with lessons about capital structure, runway length, market validation, phased development, investor communication, and operational scope. Those lessons are real. They should not be ignored.

But this is *Gracious Millionaire*.

So the deeper lesson is not merely that a large real estate development needs better underwriting.

The deeper lesson is that favor must be stewarded.

A surprising opportunity does not remove the need for discipline. A spiritual beginning does not eliminate business risk. A ministry purpose does not excuse unclear execution. A compelling pitch does not replace peace. A large profit projection does not prove assignment.

Providence Landing may have been provision.

But provision is not the same as permission to move faster than wisdom.

That is the business summary, but it is also the spiritual summary.

The airfield was real.

The dream was real.

The warning signs were real.

The need for humility was real.

And maybe the grace of Providence Landing was not only that Wes found it.

Maybe the grace was that it became large enough to teach him what kind of man he would have to become before he could carry something that large in peace.

### My Reflection

When I read Jean's summary, I do not feel corrected away from the story.

I feel corrected deeper into it.

That is an important difference.

The business review does not take away the sense that God met me on that road in Virginia. It does not make the windsock less meaningful. It does not make Joan's sentence less striking. It does not make the name Providence Landing less sincere.

But it does make the lesson clearer.

Finding the airfield was not the same thing as knowing how to carry it.

That sentence probably belongs in the center of the whole Providence Landing story.

I can see now how quickly discovery became vision, vision became pitch, pitch became pressure, and pressure began asking Jenny and me questions we were not fully ready to answer. I can see how easily a large number can sound like confirmation. I can see how the language of scale can flatter a man who already wants to believe God has put something large in his hands.

I can also see that Jenny's caution was not against the dream.

It was part of the grace inside the dream.

That is hard for a visionary to admit because caution can feel like resistance when you are trying to move. But sometimes what feels like resistance is really God slowing the pace so wisdom can catch up with imagination.

Providence Landing was not wasted.

It taught us about property.

It taught us about investors.

It taught us about pitch decks and capital and market research.

It taught us about mentors and outside validation.

It taught us about the difference between a vision that is exciting and a plan that is executable.

Most of all, it taught us that a dream can be sincerely received and still need to be surrendered.

That is why this chapter belongs here.

Providence Landing was not only a project.

It was a mirror.

And by God's grace, I am still learning how to look into it honestly.
