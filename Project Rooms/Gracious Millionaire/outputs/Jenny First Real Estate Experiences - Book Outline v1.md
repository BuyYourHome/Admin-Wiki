# Jenny's Real Estate Story - Book Outline v1

## Working Title

**Safe Enough To Trust**

## Core Premise

This book or book-section follows Jenny's real-estate formation from the point of view of someone who naturally values safety, order, planning, caution, integrity, and predictable outcomes.

The story is not about becoming reckless or learning to ignore risk. It is about how God can work through a cautious person, how wisdom can grow through pressure, and how waiting can become a spiritual discipline when the numbers no longer feel safe.

## Voice

The voice should stay first-person, practical, and honest.

Jenny's natural posture is security, safety, research, proven facts, integrity, careful planning, high standards, and steady movement. The writing should not apologize for that. It should let the reader see that caution is not unbelief by default. It can be stewardship. It can also become fear when pressure rises. The book should let both truths stand.

## Reader Promise

This is a story for the person who wants to make wise decisions, avoid unnecessary risk, protect family, and still learn to trust God when circumstances move outside the range of what can be calculated.

## Possible Structure

### Introduction - The Safe Way

Open with Jenny's self-description: cautious, guarded, organized, rational, steady, and slow.

Set the tension clearly:

- I wanted life to be safe.
- I wanted money decisions to be understandable.
- I wanted real estate to be researched, calculated, and predictable.
- But real estate, marriage, parenting, debt, divorce, and market collapse do not always stay inside the lines.

This introduction should frame the book as Jenny's side of the story: not the investor who runs toward opportunity first, but the person who asks whether the opportunity is safe, wise, affordable, and good for the family.

### Chapter 1 - The Conventional Way

Tell the 1978 Northern Virginia first-home story.

Key beats:

- Marriage at 23.
- Apartment living.
- Both working two jobs.
- Saving for a down payment.
- Buying a $37,000 starter home with a bank loan and 30-year mortgage.
- Confidence because the numbers made sense with both incomes.

Lesson:

There is dignity in the conventional way. Planning, saving, and affordability are not small things. They are part of stewardship.

### Chapter 2 - Trading The Sports Car For A Truck

Tell the early-family transition.

Key beats:

- Wanting children.
- Wanting Jenny to be home rather than sending children to a babysitter.
- Selling the 260Z sports car so her husband could buy a truck for remodeling work.
- Saving Jenny's paychecks for a full year to test whether the family could live on one income.
- Looking back and seeing that God honored the commitment, even if it felt like good planning at the time.

Lesson:

Sometimes faith looks like planning. Sometimes planning is the form obedience takes before we have language for it.

### Chapter 3 - Three Babies Under Two

Tell the pressure of rapid family growth.

Key beats:

- First child in 1981.
- Twin girls 23 months later.
- Outgrowing the starter home and family car.
- Buying the larger $99,000 home.
- Buying the Dodge Ram van.
- Interest rates around 13.75%.
- Financial pressure, sleep deprivation, and relational strain.

Lesson:

A good decision can still create pressure. A larger house can solve one problem and expose another. The story should be honest about how family stress and financial stress can become tangled.

### Chapter 4 - Behind, Caught Up, Behind Again

Tell the long-term strain in the first marriage.

Key beats:

- Bills falling behind, then catching up, then falling behind again.
- Different ways of coping with stress.
- The weight of large expenses.
- The marriage eventually ending after 25 years.
- Selling the house for several hundred thousand dollars over what was owed.
- Splitting the proceeds and beginning again.

Lesson:

Equity can increase while a marriage weakens. Financial gain is not the same as peace. A house can grow in value while the people inside it are under strain.

### Chapter 5 - The Beach Condo Investment

Tell the Garden City Beach condo purchase.

Key beats:

- Moving to Raleigh in 2002.
- Investing home-sale proceeds into an oceanfront condo.
- Treating it as an investment, not a future residence.
- Calculating rental income, HOA fees, insurance, and management costs.
- Hiring onsite management at 40% because that was the going rate.
- The first summer covering a full year of mortgage payments.

Lesson:

Careful research can still miss the one cost that changes the whole investment. Good numbers at the beginning do not guarantee safety over time.

### Chapter 6 - The Cost I Did Not See

Tell the HOA and assessment pressure.

Key beats:

- Rental income covered the mortgage.
- HOA dues and assessments rose.
- The investment moved into the red within a few years.
- Jenny felt the weight of it being "all on me."
- Remodeling the condo to prepare for sale.

Lesson:

Risk is not only the mortgage payment. It is also the things around the mortgage that can change. This chapter can become a strong practical and spiritual bridge: research matters, but control is limited.

### Chapter 7 - Selling In 2008

Tell the market-collapse season.

Key beats:

- Listing after the remodel.
- The year was 2008.
- Home prices falling.
- Buyers scarce.
- No showings, no offers, and the clock ticking financially.
- Lacking the education, experience, and confidence Jenny has now.

Lesson:

There are seasons when the right answer financially would have been to buy, but life forces you to sell. This is where the book can be honest about fear without making fear the final word.

### Chapter 8 - Waiting For The Father To Show Me

Tell the faith center of the condo story.

Key beats:

- Knowing the Heavenly Father had Jenny in His hand.
- Being afraid but clinging to Him and His promises.
- Believing He had a plan.
- Having to wait for Him to show it.
- Admitting the waiting was not easy and patience was not natural.

Lesson:

Waiting is harder for a cautious person because waiting feels unsafe. But sometimes waiting is where trust becomes more than a word.

### Chapter 9 - The Buyer In The Building

Tell the provision story.

Key beats:

- The cleaner who lived in the building.
- Her connection to absentee owners.
- The conversation that led to the buyer.
- The suddenness of the answer after the long wait.

Lesson:

God's provision may come through ordinary relationships already near the problem. The answer did not come through a dramatic strategy. It came through a person in the building.

### Chapter 10 - To Be Continued

Close this first section by pointing toward marriage number two.

This chapter should not rush ahead. It should let the first real-estate arc stand on its own:

- conventional purchase,
- family pressure,
- divorce,
- investment,
- missed risk,
- market collapse,
- fear,
- waiting,
- provision.

The continuation can then explore what changed when Jenny entered another marriage, another real-estate season, and eventually the work she and Wes are now living out together.

## Through-Line

Jenny's real-estate story is not a story of moving from caution to risk. It is a story of moving from caution as control toward caution submitted to trust.

That distinction matters.

The book should not make Jenny sound like the obstacle to vision. Her caution is part of the wisdom God is using. The unresolved question is how caution, faith, marriage, and entrepreneurial vision learn to walk together without one swallowing the other.
