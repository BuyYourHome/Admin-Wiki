# Failures in Real Estate

There are periods before the success in real estate that Jenny and I are writing about in this book that were not successful at all.

That has to be said.

If we are going to talk honestly about favor, blessing, prosperity, abundance, and becoming a gracious millionaire, then it would not be fair to only tell the stories that worked. It would not be fair to tell the stories that make us look wise and leave out the ones that reveal how much we did not know. It would not be fair to talk about success without talking about failure.

Jenny and I have both had failed marriages. I am not going to go deeply into all of that here. There are parts of those stories that are too personal and too complicated for the purpose of this chapter. But before I met and married Jenny, my second marriage failed, and there were real estate failures inside that failure.

For this narration, I am only going to refer to my second wife as number two.

Somewhere during that season I got hold of some of the real estate educational material that I am still paying attention to now. I began to think about real estate the way those mentors were teaching. It was the beginning of my real estate education.

I had been a Christian all my life. I tried to walk in a way that was consistent with what I understood my relationship with God should look like. I had deep-rooted beliefs that started when I was a child. My parents brought me up well in the understanding of God, and I honor the way they taught me to relate to the Lord.

But there were aspects of walking with God that I understand now that I did not understand then.

I do not think I knew anything about walking in the favor of the Lord.

I think I knew a good bit about proper confession, but I do not think I was walking in it. I knew theology. I knew many of the right words. But I do not think those truths had transformed into the daily dependence that I am trying to describe in this book.

That is the context for this story.

There were three major real estate transactions that number two and I engaged in that, in the end, were total failures. I think if I knew then what I know now, they might not have been. But there is no way to know that. A proper story about my life as a real estate investor would not be complete without talking about them.

The first transaction was a home for us to live in when we were in Raleigh. At that time I was traveling back and forth between Houston and Raleigh. I shared custody of my four children with my first wife, and when I was in Raleigh I would pick them up and they would live with me while I was there.

The house we bought had two floors. The first floor was finished with three bedrooms and two bathrooms, and it opened up to the roadside and the driveway. Underneath it was an unfinished basement that was the same size as the upstairs and opened out from the back of the property.

This became my first major renovation.

We designed and built four bedrooms, two bathrooms, and a kitchen in that open unfinished basement. It was quite a project, and I did almost all of it myself. I designed it and worked with a general contractor, but with a few exceptions I did the work. I did not do the HVAC or the plumbing, but I did almost everything else.

Once it was completed, the design allowed our family to move downstairs and rent the upstairs. The rent from the upstairs paid the mortgage on the property.

That part worked.

It was a very sound financial model. We effectively had no expense for our personal home in Raleigh. Looking back, I can see that there were things in that house that anticipated the way I would later think about real estate. Use what you have. Create income from the property. Let the house carry itself if it can.

That was a good idea.

But good ideas are not enough by themselves.

The second major transaction was the purchase of a large hotel in the Galveston area of Texas. Number two was from the Houston area and had a home there. During that marriage I traveled back and forth between Houston and Raleigh, with one foot in Texas and one foot in North Carolina. It was a very busy time of my life.

We bought a 200 room rundown hotel right off a major highway between Houston and Galveston. We bought it from a private owner. It was not operational when we bought it, but it had a lot of potential. It had a restaurant, a bar, and three conference rooms. Many of the rooms needed a lot of maintenance before they could be used.

We structured the purchase with some cash and a promissory note to the seller for the remainder of the purchase. I do not have the records from that acquisition now. Those records were maintained by my ex, and the purchase was made in entities that we had set up and that she controlled. My memory is a little fuzzy after all these years, so I will only speak in general terms about the money. I think the total purchase price was around $3 million.

When we bought it, we moved into one of the suites and began to live and breathe the operation of the hotel.

Everything had to be brought up to operational condition. We hired consultants to advise us on the restaurant and conference rooms. We catered food for events we were attracting for the conference facilities. We got a set of rooms operational and began to have guests in the hotel.

It was exciting.

I am not trying to give a lesson on operating a hotel. I have experience, but nothing to teach from that experience except humility. I am giving context and backstory to the story Jenny and I are telling now.

The hotel never made money.

As the financial pressure built, the stress in our marriage built with it. Eventually I made the decision, in conjunction with number two, to move back permanently to Raleigh where I could get a job and begin to stem the losses we had endured.

We eventually lost the investment.

The third major investment was five duplexes in Johnston County, North Carolina. They were purchased from one individual, and they were all occupied by what was then called Section 8 housing residents.

They were bought around the same time as the hotel acquisition. My plan was to manage them during my trips back and forth to Raleigh. I got quite an education in property management during that time.

But again, in the end, they were lessons from failure.

As my marriage with number two began to fall apart under the stress of the finances from all the investments, I made a deal with her to buy her out of the duplexes. The loans had been acquired in her name. So I created a partnership agreement and entity with my mom to pay back the investment number two had made, and we transferred the deeds into our name.

The divorce was initiated, and number two demanded that we sell the Raleigh house. I offered to buy it from her. I had a loan in place to do it. But she would have nothing to do with that and demanded that it be sold. She sold it and took the proceeds because she had invested the money.

I moved into a nearby apartment where I could house my children. We stayed there for a little over a year, until my children were old enough to move out except for my youngest.

Then I bought an RV that was big enough for me to live in permanently and still have room for my youngest son when he stayed with me. We parked the RV in the backyard of one of the duplexes, where I could keep a close eye on the tenants and manage the properties.

We kept the five loans in place and took ownership subject to the existing mortgages. For a while they cash flowed well enough to pay the expenses. But little by little I had trouble keeping them rented at the level needed to pay the mortgages. They started getting behind.

This was around the time of the 2008 mortgage crisis. The bank was not very tolerant of the delinquencies. Honestly, I did not have the experience or education to find ways to avoid the consequences.

One by one, the banks foreclosed on all of them.

Over a period of about five years, we had bought a hotel, a personal residence, five rental duplexes, and some other investment properties. At the end of that time, we had lost all of it and the marriage had ended in failure.

There were a lot of things wrong, and I will not try to go into all of them. I do not know what would have happened if I had been walking in the same principles I am trying to talk about in this book. I cannot say that I was daily taking my needs to God. I cannot say that I was asking for His favor. There were other dynamics wrong in the marriage for sure.

But I can say this.

The difference between that season and this season is not that I am smarter now.

I am more educated. That is true.

I have more experience. That is true.

I have better systems. That is true.

But those things are not the centerpiece of this book.

The difference I can recognize is that now we are walking in the favor of the Lord, and then I was not. At least I was not walking in it the way I understand it now. I was trying to do real estate with ideas, energy, pressure, and ambition. Some of the ideas were good. Some of the structures were sound. But I was not living with the same daily dependence on God that I am trying to describe now.

That matters.

This is not a book about how to get rich by using God. That would be a horrible book.

This is a book about lordship.

This is a book about what happens when money, opportunity, favor, work, marriage, stress, and ambition all come under the question of who is really Lord.

In that earlier season, I had real estate ambition before I had real estate favor. I had education before I had assignment. I had confession before I had the kind of dependence that makes confession powerful. I had motion, but I do not know that I had peace.

That does not mean God was absent. I do not believe He was absent. He was with me in the loss, the divorce, the apartment, the RV, the foreclosures, and the rebuilding. But I did not know how to cooperate with Him in the way I am trying to cooperate with Him now.

So this chapter belongs near the beginning.

Before we talk too much about the favor that followed us into profitable transactions, we need to say that we have also known what it is to lose. Before we talk about the blessing, we need to say that not everything we touched turned to gold. Before we talk about being millionaires, we need to say that some of the education came through failure.

Failure does not disqualify the story.

Sometimes it tells you what the story is really about.
