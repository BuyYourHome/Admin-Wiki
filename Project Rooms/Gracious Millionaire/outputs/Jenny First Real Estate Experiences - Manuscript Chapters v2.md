## Jenny's Introduction: The Safe Way

I want my part of this story to sound like me.

That may seem like a small thing, but to me it is not small at all. I am careful with words because I am careful with most things. I like things that are secure, researched, proven, and honest. I like a plan I can understand. I like numbers that can be checked, decisions that can be explained, and steps that make sense before anyone takes them. I am most comfortable when something is calculable, foreseeable, black and white, and well thought out.

That does not mean I have always been right. It does not mean my caution has always protected me from pain. It does not mean I have never made a decision out of fear or pressure. It simply means that when I look back over my life, I can see a pattern. I wanted security. I wanted safety. I wanted to do the responsible thing. I wanted to be able to say, with a clear conscience, that I had done the homework before I moved.

That is part of why real estate has been such an interesting subject in my life. Real estate sounds solid. It sounds practical. You can see it, insure it, improve it, rent it, sell it, borrow against it, and pass it down. It feels like something that ought to reward careful people.

And sometimes it does.

But real estate also exposes what a person does not know. It exposes what a person did not ask, did not see, did not understand, or could not control. It exposes the difference between a good plan and a complete plan. It exposes the difference between being cautious and being wise.

Those are not always the same thing.

When Wes tells real estate stories, he often sees possibility first. He can look at a deal and see hidden value, structure, opportunity, and a way through. My mind usually starts in a different place. I am asking what could go wrong. I am asking whether the numbers are dependable. I am asking what happens if the assumption fails. I am asking whether the family will be safe if the plan takes longer than expected.

That difference between us has not always been easy. But I do not believe it is accidental. I believe the Lord can use both vision and caution when both are surrendered to Him.

My first real estate experiences did not begin with unusual deals or creative financing. They began the way many people begin, with a conventional home purchase, a bank loan, a mortgage payment, and the hope that if we worked hard and made careful decisions, everything would hold together.

At the time, I would not have called that a faith journey. I would have called it being responsible.

Now, looking back, I can see the Lord's hand even in the seasons when I thought I was simply planning well. I can also see places where my planning was not enough. I can see where I was protected. I can see where I was stretched. I can see where I was afraid. And I can see that God was present before I understood how much I needed Him to be.

This part of the story is not written as investment advice. It is not meant to make me look clever. In some places it may do the opposite. It is simply the beginning of how I learned that property can be practical and still unpredictable, that safety can be planned for but not guaranteed, and that waiting on God is sometimes the only safe place left.

## Jenny: The Conventional Way

I was twenty-three years old when I married my first husband. I will call him Number One Husband here, because that is the simplest way to tell the truth without making this section about names.

Neither one of us expected divorce to be part of our future. I certainly did not. I was young, married, and serious about building a life that made sense. Both of us wanted to own our own home, so during that first year of marriage we did what responsible young people were supposed to do. We lived in an apartment, worked two jobs each, and saved for a down payment.

That sounds simple when it is written in one sentence, but it was not effortless. It meant long hours. It meant saying no to things. It meant deciding that owning a home mattered enough to organize our lives around it for a season. To me, that kind of sacrifice made sense. If the goal was important, then the steps should match the goal.

We bought a small starter home in Northern Virginia in 1978. The price was $37,000.

That number may not sound large now, but it was a lot of money to me then. It was enough money that I did not take it lightly. We bought the home the conventional way. We borrowed from a bank. We took out a thirty-year mortgage. We looked at what we could afford with both of us working full time, and I was confident that the payment fit inside the life we were living.

That was the kind of decision I understood. Bank. Mortgage. Payment. Income. Budget. If the numbers worked, then the step felt reasonable.

I did not know then how much life can change around numbers that once looked reasonable.

At the time, owning that starter home felt like progress. It felt settled. It felt like the beginning of adult responsibility in a form I could recognize. We were not trying to be creative. We were not trying to be aggressive. We were not trying to become investors. We wanted a home of our own.

There is something honorable about that desire. I still believe that. A home can be a place of stability. It can be a place where a family grows, where children learn what normal feels like, where the routines of life are held together by familiar rooms and familiar walls.

But the house itself cannot guarantee the stability. The mortgage cannot guarantee the marriage. The careful beginning cannot guarantee the ending.

That was the part I did not yet understand.

In those early years, I trusted the conventional path because it appeared predictable. Work hard. Save money. Buy what you can afford. Make the payment. Build equity. Stay responsible.

I still value those things. I still believe in doing what is honest, measured, and thought through. But I have learned that a conventional purchase can carry unconventional pressure when the life around it changes. A mortgage that is affordable with two full-time incomes may feel very different when one income goes away. A house that fits a young married couple may suddenly feel very small when children come. A responsible plan can become strained without anyone setting out to be irresponsible.

That first house was not a mistake. It was part of our story. It gave us a beginning. It also taught me that even when the front door is yours, the future is not.

## Jenny: Trading The Sports Car For A Truck

By 1980, we wanted to start a family.

That desire changed the way I looked at everything. It changed how I thought about work, money, cars, time, and what kind of life we were trying to build. Neither of us had fond memories of going to the babysitter while our parents worked, and we agreed that when we had children I would be a stay-at-home mother somehow.

That word somehow carried a lot of weight.

We did not have a large cushion. We were not wealthy. We were not stepping into a life where one income would obviously be enough. But we had made a commitment, and I took commitments seriously. If I was going to leave my job, I wanted to know we could survive it. I wanted proof before I moved.

That is my safe nature. I do not like guessing with the stability of a family.

At the same time, Number One Husband was working for his brother and wanted to start his own remodeling business. That meant he needed a truck. We did not have money sitting around for that, so we sold my beautiful 260Z sports car and used the money to buy him one.

I loved that car.

I do not want to pretend I did not. It was not just transportation to me. It was something I enjoyed, something that felt like mine. But priorities have a way of changing when a baby becomes more than an idea. I was looking forward to motherhood. I was looking forward to being home. I was willing to give up something I loved for something I believed mattered more.

That is one of the difficult parts of telling a life story honestly. At the time, sacrifice can feel noble and practical at the same time. Later, a person can look back and see more layers. The truck was connected to his hope for work. The sale of my car was connected to our hope for family. Both were connected to money. Both were connected to responsibility. Both were connected to trust.

I do not remember thinking of it as a spiritual decision. I remember thinking of it as a practical one.

We also did something very much in keeping with my personality. We saved every paycheck I earned for one solid year to see whether we could afford for me to quit working. We did not merely say, "We hope this works." We tested it. We lived as though my income was already gone and watched what happened.

That was my way of trying to remove uncertainty.

Looking back now, I can see how God honored that commitment. At the time, I may have thought it was our good planning. And yes, it was planning. It was careful. It was disciplined. It was the kind of thing I still believe people should do when making a large family decision.

But planning was not the whole story.

In 1981, our first child, a son, was born. I quit my full-time job, which I did not like anyway, and I did not really return to being an employee during the years of raising children.

That decision shaped the rest of my life. It affected our income, our household, our stress level, our marriage, and the way I saw my role. I was glad to be home with my son. I believed that was where I was supposed to be. But joy in the right decision does not mean the decision has no cost.

The house payment still had to be made. The truck still had to be useful. The business still had to produce. The grocery money still had to stretch. A baby brought love into the home, but also responsibility.

I was learning that family decisions and real estate decisions are rarely separate. The home, the mortgage, the income, the vehicle, the business, and the babies were all tied together.

I wanted a safe life. I wanted a responsible life. I wanted a home where my children could be raised with love and stability.

God was kind to me in that season. He let me have the thing I deeply wanted: to be home with my child.

But He did not promise that the rest of it would be easy.

## Jenny: Three Babies Under Two

Twenty-three months after our son was born, we were blessed with twin girls.

They were a surprise.

Number One Husband said I wanted and prayed so hard for a girl that the Lord gave me two. There was truth and humor in that. I had wanted a girl. I had prayed for a girl. And then suddenly there were two of them, along with a little boy who was not yet two years old.

Three babies under the age of two is not a season a person can fully explain to someone who has not lived it. The days were full before they even began. There were diapers, feedings, crying babies, laundry, dishes, and all the small urgent needs that arrive one after another until time becomes hard to measure. I was not thinking in terms of self-care or balance. I was trying to get through the day faithfully.

Overnight, we outgrew our starter home.

We also outgrew our car. We had a Cutlass Sedan, our family car, but three car seats could not be wedged into the back seat. That sounds almost funny now, but at the time it was immediate and practical. Babies have to ride somewhere. Cribs have to go somewhere. A family of five with three very small children needs space.

So we bought a larger, newly built house for $99,000.

Again, that was a lot of money. It was a major step up from our $37,000 starter home. But the need felt obvious. The new house could accommodate three cribs. It could hold the family we now had. We also bought a used Dodge Ram Van with seating for eight.

I want to be clear: I had no intention of filling all those seats. I wanted no more babies.

The van was not about a dream of a larger family. It was about survival. It was about being able to buckle in the children we already had and go where we needed to go. It was about solving a practical problem.

But practical solutions still create payments.

That was a time when banks were charging big interest rates. I remember that at one point we had a 13 3/4 percent rate. That kind of rate changes the feeling of a mortgage. It makes the payment heavier. It makes the cost of the house more than the price on the paper. It makes every month matter.

My days were filled with changing diapers and nursing crying babies. I had little time or energy for anything else, and if I am honest, for anyone else. Looking back, I can speculate that it was a difficult time for Number One Husband. I imagine he felt neglected. He was also saddled with large expenses: a big monthly mortgage and a sizable used-car loan.

At the time, I was inside the pressure. I was not looking at it from above. I was not analyzing how the marriage was absorbing the financial weight. I was simply tired. I was needed constantly. I was doing what mothers do when small children depend on them for everything.

But stress does not wait politely until a mother has energy to understand it.

The numbers were tight. The house was bigger, but the margin was smaller. The family was growing, but the money was strained. We could barely make ends meet, and that created a lot of stress in the marriage.

I still believe buying the larger home made sense from the standpoint of space. We needed room. We needed a vehicle that worked for three car seats. We needed to function as a family.

But this is where caution and reality met each other in a painful way. A decision can be understandable and still become heavy. A purchase can be necessary and still expose weakness. A family can be blessed with children and still suffer under the pressure of supporting them.

Those years taught me that safety is not only about making the obvious practical choice. Safety also requires margin, unity, and dependable work patterns. Without those, even a carefully chosen home can become part of a strain that reaches every room.

## Jenny: Equity And Loss

The financial pressure did not disappear.

We began getting behind on bills, then catching up, then falling behind again. It became a cycle. Anyone who has lived that way knows the emotional weight of it. It is not only the math. It is the feeling that the household is always one step from trouble. It is the stack of bills. It is the conversations no one wants to have. It is the mental calculation that begins before the paycheck arrives.

For a person like me, that kind of uncertainty is exhausting.

I wanted things to be orderly. I wanted rules to work. I wanted if we did this, then that would happen. I wanted work to produce income, income to pay bills, and bills to stay current. I wanted predictable cause and effect.

But marriage brings another person into every plan. So does business. So does stress.

Number One Husband coped by not working full time and by spending money we did not have to spend. That is the simplest way I can say it. I am not writing this to attack him. I am writing it because it mattered. It affected the household. It affected the marriage. It affected what should have been safe.

When a family has a large mortgage, a vehicle loan, three small children, and one parent at home, dependable income is not optional. It is the structure holding everything up. When that structure weakens, the person who needs safety feels it in her body.

The pattern continued long enough that it eventually caused the marriage to end after twenty-five years together.

By then, the children were out of the house and away at college. That mattered to me. I had not planned to divorce. I had not wanted that to be part of my story. But there I was, with one failed marriage behind me and another future I could not yet see clearly.

We sold the house for several hundred thousand dollars more than we owed. That part is important because it shows the complicated nature of real estate. The house had carried pressure. The mortgage had been heavy. The financial stress had been part of the marriage strain. And yet, when the house sold, there was equity.

Real estate had created both burden and value.

We split the payout and went our separate ways.

That sentence holds more than it says. It holds the end of a marriage, the end of a household, the release of a property, the dividing of money, and the strange feeling of walking away with proceeds from a life that had not held together.

I do not want to make the equity sound like victory. It was not that simple. Money from the sale did not erase the loss. It did not make divorce painless. It did not undo the years of stress or the disappointment of a covenant ending.

But it did give me something practical to carry into the next season. That is also true.

I had learned that home ownership can build value over time. I had also learned that value does not protect a marriage by itself. A house can appreciate while a relationship deteriorates. Equity can grow while trust erodes. The financial statement can look better than the emotional reality.

Those are hard lessons, but they are real ones.

When I left that season, I still carried my cautious nature with me. I still wanted decisions that were researched and rational. I still believed in property. I still believed in doing the homework. But I was no longer the twenty-three-year-old woman buying a starter home with simple confidence in a conventional path.

I had seen more.

I had lived long enough to know that a safe-looking plan can still become painful. I had also lived long enough to know that God can let something produce provision even when the larger story contains loss.

That is how I entered the next real estate decision: with proceeds from a house sale, a desire to invest wisely, and still a deep need to feel secure.

## Jenny: The Beach Condo Investment

I moved to Raleigh, North Carolina, in 2002.

After the sale of the house, I took much of the proceeds and invested them in an oceanfront condo in Garden City Beach, South Carolina, south of Myrtle Beach. I did not buy it because I intended to live there. I bought it as an investment.

On paper, it made sense to me.

I carefully calculated how the condo would rent to vacationers. I looked at the mortgage, HOA fees, insurance, and the other numbers I knew to consider. I hired an onsite management company to take care of the rental side. They charged 40 percent, which sounds like a lot because it is a lot. But I had done my homework, and 40 percent was the going rate up and down the beach.

That mattered to me. I did not want to be careless. I did not want to be talked into something without checking. If I was going to put a significant portion of my proceeds into a property, I wanted to know that the numbers were based on something real.

The first summer seemed to confirm the plan. The rentals paid an entire year of mortgage payments.

I thought I was on my way.

That is the dangerous thing about an early success. It can feel like confirmation. It can make a person believe the plan was complete because the first visible result was good. I had calculated the rental income. I had accounted for the management fee. I had considered insurance. I had looked at the expenses I understood.

But I had missed something important.

In all my careful research and planning, I had failed to investigate how the HOA dues and assessments might increase.

That is not a small oversight in a condominium investment. It was a structural oversight. HOA fees and assessments are not optional. They are part of ownership. They can change the entire investment picture even when the mortgage is being paid.

Within three or four years, those fees had put me in the red. The rental money still easily paid every mortgage payment, which made the situation even more frustrating. The property was not failing in the way I had expected a property might fail. It was not sitting empty. Vacationers were renting it. The mortgage was covered by rental income.

But the other costs had moved against me.

That was when I panicked.

I do not use that word lightly. This was all on me. I had made the investment decision. I had used the proceeds. I had believed the numbers. I had no husband beside me sharing the responsibility. I did not see a way to stay afloat.

A person can be cautious and still be caught by what she did not know to ask. That is one of the humbling lessons of my life. I had done homework, but not enough of the right homework. I had calculated, but not completely. I had tried to be wise, but I lacked experience.

That lack of experience was costly.

I hired a contractor to completely remodel the condo. My thinking was that if I could make it more attractive, I could sell it and get out from under the pressure. Again, I was trying to solve the problem in a practical way. Improve the property. Put it on the market. Find a buyer. Stop the financial bleeding.

It was a reasonable plan in one sense.

But timing matters in real estate, and I was about to learn that timing can overpower even a reasonable plan.

## Jenny: Selling In 2008

The year was 2008.

Anyone who remembers real estate in 2008 understands what that means. The market collapsed. The subprime mortgage crisis shook the country. Home prices fell dramatically. People who had planned to sell discovered that buyers had vanished or changed. Financing was harder. Confidence was gone.

And there I was, caught in the crosshairs.

It was a good time to buy, not a good time to sell. But I felt I had no choice. The condo was costing me money. The HOA dues and assessments had changed the math. I had remodeled it. I had put it on the market. I needed a way out.

The problem was that the market did not care what I needed.

The condo sat.

No showings. No offers. The financial clock was ticking.

That kind of waiting is difficult for anyone, but it is especially difficult for a person who wants certainty. I wanted a plan. I wanted a buyer. I wanted a date. I wanted the situation resolved before it could do more damage. Instead, I had silence.

I did not have the education I have now. I did not have the experience I have now. I certainly did not have the confidence I have grown into in recent years. I was facing a problem larger than my preparation.

That is a frightening place to be.

I can look back now and describe it with more calm than I felt then. At the time, I was afraid. I knew enough to understand that the market was bad and that my need to sell did not make the property sellable. I knew I could not force a buyer to appear. I knew the costs were real. I knew the responsibility was mine.

But I also knew my Heavenly Father had me in His hand.

I cannot say I was not afraid. That would not be honest. Faith did not make me fearless. But fear did not mean I was abandoned. I was clinging to Him and to His promises. I believed He had a plan, even though I could not see it yet.

That is where waiting becomes spiritual.

Waiting is not my natural strength. I am anything but patient when the stakes are high and the outcome is unresolved. I want to know what is going to happen. I want to make the responsible move. I want to fix what can be fixed.

But there are seasons when all the responsible steps have been taken and the answer still has not arrived.

In those seasons, a person learns what she is really trusting.

I had trusted my calculations. I had trusted rental projections. I had trusted homework. I had trusted the idea that a remodeled oceanfront condo should be sellable. None of those things was wrong in itself, but none of them was enough to deliver me.

I had to wait for God to show me.

That was not easy. It was not sentimental. It was not the kind of waiting that looks peaceful from the outside. It was the kind of waiting where a person keeps returning to the Lord because she does not know where else to go.

Looking back, I can see that He was not late. But while I was living it, the waiting felt long.

## Jenny: The Buyer In The Building

After the condo remodeling was finished, I hired a woman who lived in the same building to clean before the realtor pictures and the showings that were supposed to follow.

That detail may seem small, but it became important.

She knew many of the absentee owners in the building because she cleaned for a number of them. She had relationships I did not have. She knew people connected to that property in a way I did not. I had hired her for one practical reason, but God used that connection for another.

She spoke to one of the owners about my condo being for sale.

And just like that, I had the buyer.

I do not mean the process suddenly became unimportant or that the stress before it did not matter. I mean that the answer came through a channel I could not have produced by calculation alone. I had remodeled the condo. I had listed it. I had waited through no showings and no offers. I had worried over the financial clock.

Then the buyer came through a woman in the building who knew another absentee owner.

That is the kind of thing a person remembers.

To someone else, it may sound like a normal real estate connection. Buildings have owners. Owners know people. A cleaning woman hears something and mentions it. A buyer appears. But when you are the person under pressure, the person afraid, the person praying and waiting, you know there is more to the story than a convenient contact.

I saw the kindness of God in it.

Not because I had done everything perfectly. I had not. Not because I had been fearless. I had not. Not because my research had been complete. It had not been. I saw His kindness because He made a way when I did not know how to make one.

That experience did not make me careless in future real estate decisions. If anything, it made me more aware of how much I did not know. It showed me that a person can be careful and still need mercy. It showed me that a person can make a mistake in what she fails to investigate and still be held by God. It showed me that waiting is not wasted just because it is uncomfortable.

The Garden City Beach condo taught me more than one lesson.

It taught me to look beyond the obvious numbers. Mortgage coverage is not the same thing as profitability. Rental income is not the whole equation. HOA dues, assessments, insurance, management costs, timing, and market conditions all matter.

It taught me that selling under pressure is very different from selling by choice.

It taught me that the market can change at the worst possible time.

And it taught me that God can use ordinary people in ordinary places to answer prayers that feel very large to the person praying them.

That is why I cannot tell this story as only a mistake or only a rescue. It was both a lesson and a mercy.

My first real estate experiences began with a conventional starter home in Northern Virginia and led through family pressure, mortgage stress, divorce, equity, an oceanfront condo, an investment miscalculation, a market collapse, fear, waiting, and finally a buyer I did not find by myself.

I still value safety. I still value research. I still value plans. I still want decisions to be honest, rational, and well thought out.

But I know now that safety is not found only in the plan.

Safety is found in belonging to the Lord while the plan is being tested.
