# Providence Landing: Letting Go

It has been more than two years since I decided to shut down the effort to develop Providence Landing.

Even writing that sentence is difficult.

I had spent so much time with that property in my mind. I had walked through plans, relationships, drawings, funding conversations, investor presentations, and strategy. I had built a team around it. I had pictured what it could become. I had prayed over it. I had believed, deeply, that God had directed me to that property and had guided my thoughts about what could happen there.

That is part of what made it so hard to let go.

When the time finally came, I made an appointment with Joan and Robie to drive up and meet them face to face. I did not want to end something that serious with a casual phone call or a vague message. I wanted to sit with them and tell them directly that there were no further efforts I could make on the project.

There is no easy way to say that when people have given you time, trust, access, and hope.

Looking back, it is still hard to understand everything that happened. I can see pieces of it more clearly now than I could then, but I do not pretend to have the whole thing figured out. I know I was fully convinced that God had directed me to Providence Landing. I know I pursued it with real faith and real effort. I also know that the longer the process went on, the more pressure it created inside our family.

That pressure matters.

With this much distance behind me, one thing I can see is that I do not know how I would have pulled it off if we had received the funding and moved into the full development. I do not know what it would have done to Jenny and me. I do not know what it would have done to the peace of our home.

At the time, I could see the opportunity. I could see the potential. I could see the number of people who might be helped, the value that might be created, and the open doors that seemed to be appearing. But a plan can look right on paper and still be too heavy for the life that has to carry it.

That is one of the lessons Providence Landing left with me.

We met some great people through that project. I hope we are able to continue some of the relationships that were built there. One relationship Jenny and I especially value is our relationship with Fred. I will not try to explain all of that here, but Fred took a special liking to us from the beginning, and I believe it was genuine.

He was generous with his time in a way that is hard to measure. If his time could have been translated into dollars, I think it would have been worth hundreds of thousands, maybe even millions, of dollars. He gave us access to his thinking, his experience, his perspective, and his relationships. That kind of generosity is not small.

I also learned a great deal about funding a project at that level. I learned the language of finance in a different way. I learned what qualified investors expect to see. I learned how much work goes into building a presentation that can stand in front of people who think in terms of capital, risk, execution, return, and scale.

Those lessons were not wasted.

But perhaps the biggest lesson was not about finance at all.

The biggest lesson was that no matter what I envision, my plans cannot supersede the peace, or the lack of peace, that those plans create for Jenny and me as a family.

I do not know what I would have done differently except this: I would have paid attention to Jenny's resistance sooner.

That is not a small admission. I do not mean that every difficult project should be abandoned the moment it creates tension. Anything meaningful will carry weight. But there is a difference between the normal pressure of obedience and the deeper warning that a family is losing peace under the load of a dream.

Providence Landing taught me to respect that difference.

Maybe the project was never only about developing an airfield community. Maybe it was also about developing judgment in me. Maybe it was about learning how to hold a vision without letting it become lord. Maybe it was about discovering that even a dream I believe God has shown me still has to remain submitted to God, to wisdom, and to the covenant of peace in my marriage.

That is where the Providence Landing story ends for me.

Not with bitterness.

Not with regret that cancels the value of the journey.

Not with a clean explanation for every unanswered question.

It ends with surrender.

I can be grateful for what I learned. I can honor the people who helped us. I can admit that I do not understand all of it. And I can say, with more humility than I had when the project began, that a gracious millionaire cannot let ambition outrun peace.
