## Jenny's Introduction: The Safe Way

If my voice is going to be part of this book, then I need to begin by saying something plainly.

I am most comfortable with security.

I like safety. I like things researched, proven, honest, certain, calculable, foreseeable, balanced, organized, steady, and slow. I like plans. I like high standards. I like rules that make sense and numbers that can be checked. I am cautious by nature. I am guarded. I want to know what we are doing before we do it.

That is not the same thing as unbelief.

Sometimes caution is wisdom. Sometimes it is stewardship. Sometimes it is the part of a marriage or a business decision that keeps everybody from running too fast toward something that looks good but has not been fully counted yet.

But sometimes caution can also become fear.

That is part of my story too.

When Wes talks about real estate, he can talk about opportunity, favor, unusual deals, private money, sellers, terms, and open doors. My first experiences with real estate were not like that. I did not begin with creative financing or distressed-property lists or conversations with sellers who needed help. I began the conventional way.

I began with work, savings, a bank, a down payment, and a thirty-year mortgage.

I began with wanting a home that felt safe.

Looking back now, I can see that God was present in those early decisions, even when I did not have the language for it. At the time, I might have called it good planning. I might have said we were being responsible. I might have said the numbers worked. All of that was true.

But God was in the story too.

He was in the desire to build a home. He was in the sacrifices. He was in the years of raising children. He was in the pressure I did not know how to carry. He was in the failure of a marriage I never expected to fail. He was in the real estate investment that looked safe until the costs changed around me. He was in the market collapse. He was in the waiting. He was in the buyer I could not produce for myself.

That is why this belongs in *Gracious Millionaire*.

Not because I started out as an investor.

I did not.

I started out as a young woman who wanted a home, a family, and a life that made sense.

## Jenny: The Conventional Way

I was twenty-three years old when I married my first husband. I will call him Number One.

I never thought I would be divorced. I do not think most people do, at least not when they are young and standing at the beginning of what they believe will be the rest of their life. I certainly did not imagine that one day I would look back and say I had two failed marriages.

But this is part of the truth of my story.

Number One and I both wanted to own our own home. We were living in an apartment at the time, and owning a house seemed like the right next step. It was the normal dream. It was the safe dream. It was the way responsible people built a life.

So we worked.

We both worked two jobs. We saved until we had enough money for a down payment. We did not have a creative structure. We did not have some unusual real estate strategy. We did it the conventional way. We borrowed from a bank. We took out a thirty-year mortgage. We bought what we believed we could afford with both of us working full time.

The house cost $37,000.

The year was 1978.

It was in Northern Virginia.

That was a lot of money to me.

But I was confident we could afford it. The numbers made sense. We had done what we were supposed to do. We had worked, saved, qualified, and bought a small starter home. There was comfort in that. There was comfort in knowing we had not stretched beyond what both of our paychecks could support.

I look back at that young version of myself and recognize her immediately.

She wanted to be safe.

She wanted to know the house payment could be made.

She wanted the decision to be rational.

There is nothing wrong with that.

Sometimes people who are drawn to unusual opportunities can make ordinary responsibility sound small. But there is dignity in the conventional way. There is dignity in saving. There is dignity in buying a house you can afford. There is dignity in being able to look at the payment and know how it will be paid.

That first house was not just a house. It was the evidence that we were building something.

At least that is what I thought.

## Jenny: Trading The Sports Car For A Truck

By 1980, we wanted to start a family.

Neither one of us had fond memories of going to a babysitter while our parents worked. Because of that, we made a vow that somehow I would be a stay-at-home mom.

That was not a small decision.

Number One was working for his brother, but he wanted to start his own remodeling business. So we sold my beautiful 260Z sports car and used the money to buy him a truck.

I loved that car.

But my priorities were changing. I was looking forward to having a baby. I was looking forward to staying home to raise children. The car represented one season of life, and the truck represented another one.

That is how it felt to me at the time.

As is my safe nature, we did not just jump into the decision for me to leave work. We saved every paycheck I got for one solid year to see whether we could afford for me to quit working. That was my way of testing the decision. Could we live on one income? Could we make it? Could the household function without my paycheck?

I can see now how God honored that commitment.

At the time, I may have thought it was simply our good planning.

That is one of the ways faith can be hidden inside responsibility. Sometimes you do not recognize the spiritual part of a decision until years later. At the time, you are just doing what seems wise. You are saving money. You are counting costs. You are trying not to be foolish.

But God sees the heart inside the plan.

In 1981, our first child was born, a son. I quit the full-time job I did not like anyway, and I never really went back to being an employee during the years of raising children.

I had wanted that.

I had planned for that.

And still, when the child arrives, the plan becomes real in a way the spreadsheet never can.

The house was no longer just a starter home. It was where a family had begun.

## Jenny: Three Babies Under Two

Twenty-three months after our son was born, we were blessed with twin girls.

They were quite a surprise.

Number One said I had wanted and prayed so hard for a girl that the Lord gave me two. I can smile at that now, but at the time it was chaos.

We had three babies under the age of two.

Almost overnight, we outgrew not only our starter home but also our Cutlass sedan. Three car seats could not be wedged into the back seat of our family car. The life we had planned for had multiplied faster than our space, our vehicle, and our energy.

So we bought a larger, newly built house.

It cost $99,000.

That house could easily accommodate three cribs. We also bought a used Dodge Ram van with seating for eight. I will say plainly that I did not want to fill all of those seats. I wanted no more babies.

This was also a time when banks were charging high interest rates. I remember at one point we had a rate of 13.75 percent.

My days were filled with changing diapers and nursing crying babies. I had little time or energy for anything or anyone else. Looking back, I can speculate that it was a tough time for Number One. He no doubt felt neglected. He was also saddled with large expenses, including a big monthly mortgage and a sizable used-car loan.

We could barely make ends meet.

That created stress in the marriage.

This is one of the lessons real estate taught me before I ever thought of myself as a real estate investor: a house can solve one problem and create another. The larger house solved our space problem. The van solved the car-seat problem. But the payments introduced pressure into a marriage that was already carrying sleeplessness, babies, work, and emotional strain.

We would get behind in bills, then catch up, then repeat the cycle again.

That cycle has a way of wearing on people.

It wears on conversation. It wears on patience. It wears on trust. It wears on the way a husband and wife see each other. Financial pressure is rarely only financial. It finds every weak place.

I do not want to pretend that all of this was only about money. Marriage is more complicated than that. People are more complicated than that. But the financial stress was real, and the house was part of the weight we were carrying.

The home that was supposed to hold the family also held the pressure.

That is a hard sentence to write.

But it is true.

## Jenny: Equity And Loss

Over time, the pattern continued.

Bills would get behind. Then they would get caught up. Then they would get behind again.

Number One coped in ways that made the pressure worse. He did not always work full time, and he spent money we did not have to spend. I was trying to keep the family steady, but the cycle kept repeating.

Eventually, after twenty-five years together, the marriage ended.

By then, the children were out of the house and away at college. That matters to me. I am grateful they were not still small children when the marriage finally ended. But divorce is still divorce. I had never thought I would be divorced, and yet that is where I was.

We sold the house.

It sold for several hundred thousand dollars more than what we owed.

On paper, that looks like success. We had bought a house for $99,000, and years later there was significant equity. That is what real estate is supposed to do. It is supposed to grow. It is supposed to build wealth. It is supposed to leave you with something after years of payments.

And it did.

But that equity came at the end of a failed marriage.

That is one of the tensions I want to keep intact in my part of this story. A house can go up in value while the marriage inside it is going down. A family can build equity and still be breaking apart. A sale can produce money and still mark the end of something deeply painful.

We split the payout and went our separate ways.

I do not want to make that sound smaller than it was.

There was financial provision in the sale. There was also loss.

Both were true.

## Jenny: The Beach Condo Investment

I moved to Raleigh, North Carolina, in 2002.

After the home sale, I took the proceeds and invested much of it into buying an oceanfront condo in Garden City Beach, South Carolina, south of Myrtle Beach.

This was my investment.

I did not intend to live there. I intended to rent it to vacationers. I carefully calculated how the condo would rent, what the HOA fees were, what the insurance would cost, and what the rest of the arrangement would look like. I hired an onsite management company to take care of the rental activity.

They charged me 40 percent.

That sounds high, but I had done my homework. Forty percent was the going rate up and down the beach.

That is an important detail because it shows how I made the decision. I did not just get excited and buy a condo. I researched. I calculated. I compared. I tried to understand the costs. I wanted the investment to make sense.

The first summer, the rentals paid for an entire year of mortgage payments.

I thought I was on my way.

There is a kind of relief that comes when the early numbers confirm the plan. The first year made me feel as though I had made a good investment. The rental income did what it was supposed to do. The mortgage was covered. The property was not just sitting there. It was working.

But there was a cost I had not understood deeply enough.

In all my careful research and planning, I had failed to investigate how and whether the HOA dues and assessments might increase.

Within three or four years, those fees had put me in the red, even though the rental money easily paid every mortgage payment.

That is a real estate lesson I did not know yet.

The mortgage is not the whole risk.

The payment you calculate at closing is not the only number that can change your future. The things around the mortgage matter too. HOA dues matter. Assessments matter. Insurance matters. Management costs matter. Market conditions matter. Reserves matter.

I had calculated what I knew to calculate.

But I did not know enough to calculate everything that mattered.

I guess you could say I panicked.

This was all on me, and I did not see a way to stay afloat.

## Jenny: Selling In 2008

I hired a contractor to completely remodel the condo, and I placed it on the market for sale.

The year was 2008.

If you mention 2008 to any real estate investor today, they will most likely cringe at the memory. It was the year the real estate market collapsed and triggered the subprime mortgage crisis. Home prices fell dramatically.

And there I was, caught in the crosshairs.

It was a good time to buy.

It was not a good time to sell.

But I felt as though I had no choice.

I did not have the education I have now. I did not have the experience I have now. I certainly did not have the confidence I have grown into in recent years.

My condo sat on the market.

No showings.

No offers.

The clock was ticking financially.

This is the place where my safe nature met something it could not control. I had researched. I had planned. I had remodeled. I had listed. I had done what seemed to be the next responsible step. But the market did not care about my need to sell.

That kind of waiting is hard for me.

Waiting is hard for a cautious person because waiting feels unsafe. I would rather know the answer. I would rather know the path. I would rather see the numbers and know the plan is working.

But during that season, the plan was not working in any visible way.

I cannot say I was not afraid.

I was.

But I knew my Heavenly Father had me in His hand. I was clinging to Him and to His promises. I believed He had a plan. I had to wait for Him to show me.

That was not easy.

I was anything but patient.

This is one of those places where I can see the lesson more clearly now than I could live it then. Trust sounds beautiful until you are the one making the payment. Waiting sounds spiritual until the condo is still sitting and the market is still falling and the phone is still not ringing.

But God was still there.

He had not left the story.

## Jenny: The Buyer In The Building

After the condo was remodeled, I had hired a lady who lived in the same building to clean before the realtor pictures and the showings that would follow.

She knew many of the absentee owners. She cleaned for a number of them.

She spoke to one of them about my condo being for sale.

And just like that, I had the buyer.

That is how the answer came.

Not through a dramatic strategy.

Not through a marketing plan I had designed.

Not through the kind of certainty I wanted before the waiting began.

The answer came through a person in the building.

It came through an ordinary relationship that was already close to the problem.

I do not want to overstate the story. I do not want to turn every detail into something more dramatic than it was. But I also do not want to miss God in the ordinary. The provision came through someone who was already there, someone I had hired for a practical reason, someone who knew people I did not know.

That buyer was the answer I had been waiting for.

This first real estate story does not end with me becoming bold or fearless. That would not be true. It ends with me seeing that careful planning matters, but it cannot control everything. It ends with me recognizing that I can do my homework and still miss something. It ends with me admitting that fear can rise even when faith is present.

And it ends with God providing a way through.

That is the honest lesson.

My real estate story began with safety, planning, and the conventional way. It moved through family pressure, debt pressure, divorce, an investment that looked good until the hidden costs changed, a collapsed market, fear, waiting, and provision.

It is not the whole story.

It is the first part.

The next part belongs to marriage number two.

For now, this is enough to show where I began.

I began as someone who wanted real estate to be safe.

And God began teaching me that safety and trust are not always the same thing.
