Use this profile when drafting from Wes's routed email submissions and manuscript notes, but do not treat it as a substitute for his actual words. Wes's own submitted text remains the controlling source for tone, facts, emphasis, and meaning.

## What The Current Manuscript Shows

Wes is presented as a reflective narrator trying to understand real estate, money, favor, failure, marriage, and calling under the lordship of Jesus. The manuscript is not written from the posture of a man selling a formula. It is written from the posture of a man tracing the hand of God through business stories that were sometimes profitable, sometimes painful, and often unfinished.

The current manuscript shows Wes as a systems thinker, real estate investor, husband, father, student of mentors, and man of faith. He is willing to name success directly, including the million-dollar results of the real estate business, but he keeps returning to the deeper question: what is God forming in the person while the money is being made?

## Likely Role In The Book

Wes is the primary first-person witness. His role is to tell the story from inside the pressure, not after every lesson has been fully mastered.

He carries the book's main theological questions:

- What makes money too much money?
- What is the difference between ambition and assignment?
- What is the difference between skill and favor?
- What kind of person does abundance form or expose?
- How do Jenny and Wes make decisions together when opportunity, pressure, and prayer all meet in the same room?

Wes should not be portrayed as a detached expert. He should be portrayed as someone learning in public, often with enough candor to admit confusion, failure, fear, frustration, or incomplete understanding.

## Spiritual Posture

Wes's voice assumes a Kingdom-of-God worldview. Money, real estate, work, skill, favor, marriage, and opportunity are all interpreted through lordship.

He is comfortable using Christian language plainly, but the strongest parts of the manuscript are usually not abstract teaching. They are stories where a principle becomes measurable in conduct: how a seller is treated, how a tenant is protected, how Jenny's caution is honored, how a spreadsheet is questioned, how pressure exposes the heart, and how prayer changes the posture of a decision.

The spiritual posture is not triumphalist. Wes repeatedly distinguishes between success as an external outcome and formation as an internal work. He does not want a book about using God to become wealthy. He wants a book about what happens when wealth, opportunity, and ambition come under the question of who is really Lord.

## Business Posture

Wes appears to think in systems before he thinks in isolated deals. His database-development background matters because it trained him to see structure inside scattered facts.

He naturally pays attention to:

- Lead flow and follow-up.
- Seller situations.
- Equity, cash flow, deposits, interest rates, and projected profit.
- Private money structures.
- Subject-to and contract-for-deed possibilities.
- Spreadsheets, CRMs, and repeatable operating systems.
- Out-of-the-box deal structures that can solve more than one problem at once.

This systems posture should be preserved. Wes is not merely chasing transactions. He is often trying to understand how a transaction fits inside a process, how the process fits inside a business, and how the business fits inside an assignment.

## Emotional And Relational Notes

Wes's perspective may naturally emphasize:

- The tension between ambition and obedience.
- The humility learned through real estate failure.
- The desire to see favor without turning favor into technique.
- The pressure of making decisions that affect Jenny, family, sellers, buyers, tenants, lenders, and contractors.
- The way business opportunities can expose marriage dynamics.
- The need to honor Jenny's perspective, especially where caution and unity are required.
- The recurring question of whether a profitable idea is also peaceful, wise, and under lordship.

The manuscript should preserve moments where Wes admits the story is unfinished. That incompleteness is part of the voice. It keeps the book from sounding like a packaged success system.

## Voice And Style Notes

Wes's voice is plain, reflective, direct, and self-examining. It often moves from concrete business detail to spiritual interpretation and then back to a practical question.

Do not confuse Wes's quick instruction messages, dictated punctuation, or bullet-point notes with his finished book voice. Those inputs usually identify the idea, sequence, or concern he wants addressed. They should be shaped into readable narrative unless he explicitly asks for an outline or list.

Common voice patterns:

- Short thesis sentences after longer explanation.
- Rhetorical questions, used selectively.
- Direct admissions of uncertainty.
- Plain transitions such as "Let me back up," "That matters," and "Some things have to be said."
- First-person testimony rather than abstract instruction.
- Practical real estate detail used as evidence for spiritual formation.

Do not polish the voice into corporate nonfiction. The writing should retain the feel of a man thinking, praying, remembering, and interpreting his life in front of the reader. At the same time, avoid overusing short question clusters or repeating the same reflective structure until it becomes predictable.

## Drafting Guidance For Wes's Submissions

When Wes sends material for *Gracious Millionaire*:

- Preserve his first-person voice unless a section clearly belongs in shared "Jenny and I" language.
- Keep his directness and the strongest rhetorical questions, but prune redundant question lists.
- Smooth obvious transcription errors, but do not over-polish the thought pattern.
- Preserve confession, uncertainty, and incomplete lessons.
- Keep concrete business details close to the spiritual point.
- Do not turn every insight into a maxim or sermon outline.
- When Wes names a mentor or teacher, use that as context, but keep Wes's testimony as the controlling story.
- When Wes describes a profitable deal, keep the tension between profit, stewardship, assignment, and lordship.
- When Wes and Jenny differ, do not frame Wes as automatically right because the idea is creative or profitable. Let the manuscript show the need for unity.

## Things To Avoid

- Do not make Wes sound like a prosperity teacher.
- Do not make him sound certain where the source material is exploratory.
- Do not remove the weight of failure, divorce, pressure, or relational tension.
- Do not portray skill as the whole explanation for success.
- Do not portray favor as a technique.
- Do not let spreadsheets or deal mechanics replace the heart question.
- Do not invent private motives, dates, numbers, or outcomes not present in the source material.
- Do not use this profile to override Wes's later corrections.
