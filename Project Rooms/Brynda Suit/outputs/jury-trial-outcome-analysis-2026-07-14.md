# Jury Trial Outcome Analysis - 2026-07-14

This is a source-based litigation-risk analysis for internal review. It is not legal advice and should be reconciled with Keith Johnson's trial strategy and Judge Collins's written order.

## Bottom Line

Based on the current source documents, the defense is favored at a jury trial on the remaining undue influence claim, but the case still carries meaningful jury risk because the surviving claim is fact-heavy and emotionally easy for a plaintiff to frame.

My expected outcome is a defense verdict or directed-verdict-style defense win as the more likely result. A practical risk band is roughly 65-75% defense-favorable outcome and 25-35% plaintiff-favorable or materially adverse outcome, depending heavily on how Brynda appears live or by deposition, how the jury reacts to the price/value spread, and whether the court lets the plaintiff present a broad vulnerability/predatory-transaction story.

The summary judgment ruling materially improves the defense posture: plaintiff's motion was denied, defendants' motion was granted on all claims except undue influence, and the Chapter 75/treble damages/attorney-fee exposure appears removed subject to appeal. That leaves a narrower, cleaner case for trial.

## What The Jury Is Really Deciding

The live issue is not whether the transaction looks inexpensive in hindsight or whether Brynda was vulnerable. The practical jury question is whether Wes or Buy Your Home overcame Brynda's free will at the time she signed the transaction documents.

That distinction favors the defense because the source record includes:

- Brynda personally signed the purchase agreement, leaseback, seller acknowledgments, deed, and related documents.
- The seller acknowledgments expressly say the transaction was final, not a loan, fairly negotiated, not under duress, and that she had been advised to seek legal/financial counsel.
- Keith's post-deposition assessment was that Brynda's inability to remember the key transaction events leaves Wes's account largely uncontested.
- The court has already removed several plaintiff theories that would have made the defense look legally worse to a jury, including Chapter 75 and fiduciary-duty theories.
- The defense has a coherent transaction story: foreclosure was pending, Brynda had nowhere to go, Buy Your Home paid/cured the arrears, foreclosure was withdrawn, and Brynda received a leaseback that let her stay in the property.

## Defense Strengths

The strongest defense fact is the document trail. Jurors may dislike the economics, but signed acknowledgments and a leaseback are hard to overcome unless plaintiff can show that the signatures were not meaningful.

The second strongest defense fact is plaintiff's proof problem. Keith's April 3, 2025 discussion focused on Brynda's deposition problem: if she does not remember the transaction and Natalie cannot testify in her place, plaintiff has a burden-of-proof gap. A jury does not get to rewrite a transaction just because it now feels harsh; plaintiff must connect Wes's conduct to undue influence at signing.

The third defense strength is timing. The deepest care/dependence facts largely appear after the sale: rides, groceries, technology help, medical appointment support, Christmas/Mother's Day visits, and emergency/hospital help. Those facts can be spun both ways, but for the legal question they are less powerful if they arose after the October 31, 2023 sale documents and November 28, 2023 deed.

The fourth defense strength is the daughter's distance from the original transaction. Natalie lived in Maryland, had limited direct involvement before the transaction, and appears to become central after the transaction and after Brynda's later health decline. That gives the defense a clean theme: the lawsuit is driven by a later family reaction, not by what actually happened at signing.

## Plaintiff Strengths

The plaintiff's strongest trial theme is not technical law. It is optics.

The plaintiff can tell a simple story: an elderly woman in foreclosure, living in difficult conditions, with health and cognitive vulnerabilities, sold a house worth far more than the cash paid, after receiving multiple foreclosure-pressure letters from a professional real estate buyer. That theme can move a jury even if several legal claims are gone.

The price/value spread is the biggest emotional risk. Plaintiff sources emphasize sale consideration around the BofA debt/arrears against alleged assessed/listed values in the high six figures. The defense can explain the leaseback and foreclosure rescue economics, but a jury may still ask why an elderly owner gave up the house for that number.

The plaintiff's second best fact cluster is vulnerability: age, illness, isolation, difficulty using phones/technology, later pneumonia/hospitalization, and texts showing Brynda needed help with groceries, medication, phone access, and appointments. Some of this is later than the transaction, but it helps plaintiff paint the whole relationship.

The plaintiff's third risk point is the professional-versus-layperson contrast. Wes is the repeat real estate investor; Brynda is an elderly homeowner in foreclosure. Even without a fiduciary duty, that imbalance can matter to jurors.

## Most Likely Trial Path

If the plaintiff's live proof remains as reflected in Keith's April 2025 assessment and the summary judgment briefing, the plaintiff likely struggles to prove undue influence. The most likely defense path is:

1. Plaintiff presents vulnerability, foreclosure pressure, low price, and after-the-fact dependence.
2. Defense emphasizes signed acknowledgments, leaseback benefit, foreclosure cure, lack of POA use, lack of fiduciary duty, lack of competency issue, and Brynda's inability to contradict Wes's transaction account.
3. Defense seeks a directed verdict after plaintiff's evidence or carries the issue to the jury with a strong burden-of-proof theme.
4. If the jury stays focused on whether Brynda's will was actually overborne at signing, defense likely wins.

## Main Trial Risk

The main risk is a jury that decides the transaction feels predatory even if the remaining legal claim is narrow.

That risk increases if:

- Brynda appears sympathetic and credible in live testimony or video.
- Plaintiff can present the property value gap in a way that makes the leaseback seem like a fig leaf rather than consideration.
- The court allows broad evidence of later medical decline, dependence, and communications.
- The defense tone sounds dismissive of Brynda or too focused on Nikki/Natalie.
- The jury does not understand why the leaseback had real economic value.

That risk decreases if:

- Brynda's deposition memory problems come in clearly.
- The jury sees the transaction documents and acknowledgments in a clean, repeated way.
- The defense explains the leaseback as the central consideration, not an afterthought.
- Wes presents as patient, consistent, and practical rather than opportunistic.
- The judge keeps the trial tightly focused on undue influence at the time of signing.

## Settlement Implication

The defense should treat mediation as being in a strong position, but not as a no-risk position. Judge Collins's ruling removed the most dangerous statutory and fee-shifting claims, which should reduce settlement value. The remaining trial risk is reputational, emotional, and equitable: an elderly-homeowner jury narrative can still create exposure even when the legal record favors the defense.

The economically rational settlement posture is to price the remaining risk as a nuisance-to-moderate undue-influence risk, not as a full Chapter 75 or fraud case. Any settlement should buy finality: dismissal with prejudice, termination/release of any lease or occupancy interest, comprehensive mutual releases, and no future property-related claims by Brynda or Natalie to the extent counsel can obtain them.

## Expected Outcome

Expected result if tried: defense-favorable outcome.

Most likely: defense verdict or defense win after plaintiff's evidence because plaintiff cannot prove that Wes overbore Brynda's will when she signed.

Meaningful adverse risk: a plaintiff verdict or settlement-pressure result if the jury reacts strongly to the elderly-owner/foreclosure/low-price story and treats the transaction as unfair enough to infer undue influence.

Practical strategy: prepare as if the defense should win, mediate as if a jury could still punish the optics, and keep the trial story disciplined around consent, signed acknowledgments, leaseback value, foreclosure cure, and plaintiff's burden of proof.
