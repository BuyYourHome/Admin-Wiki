# Defense Strategy Update - Notary Signing Witness

Source: `C:\Codex\Wiki Files\Project Rooms\Brynda Suit\sources\email\2026-07-16-181902-brynda-suit-notary-aware-cheerful.md`

This update is based on Jenny's routed 2026-07-16 email. It is an internal strategy note, not legal advice, and should be reconciled with Keith Johnson's trial plan.

## New Fact Added

Jenny reports that Wes had a notary present when Brynda signed the Purchase and Sale Agreement. After the lawsuit was filed, the notary spoke with the attorney and with Wes/Jenny. The notary reportedly remembered Brynda as cheerful and completely aware, and said she would not have agreed to notarize if Brynda had been compromised in any way.

## Strategy Impact

This strengthens the defense strategy on the remaining undue influence claim because it adds a neutral or semi-neutral signing witness who can speak to Brynda's presentation at the moment of execution.

The best use of this fact is not as a broad character point. It should be used narrowly:

- Brynda appeared cheerful at signing.
- Brynda appeared completely aware at signing.
- A notary was present for the signing process.
- The notary says she would not have notarized if Brynda appeared compromised.
- This corroborates the defense theme that Brynda knowingly signed the transaction documents.

This directly supports the core defense theme from the jury-trial analysis: the question is not whether Brynda later became vulnerable or whether the transaction now looks emotionally difficult; the trial question is whether Wes or Buy Your Home overcame Brynda's free will when she signed.

## How It Changes The Trial Presentation

The notary should become part of the "signing moment" proof stack:

1. Signed Purchase and Sale Agreement.
2. Seller acknowledgments.
3. Leaseback documents.
4. Deed/signature record.
5. Any signing video.
6. Wes's testimony about reviewing the documents.
7. Notary testimony that Brynda appeared cheerful, aware, and not compromised.

That stack is stronger than relying only on Wes's testimony and the documents. It also gives the jury someone other than a defendant to hear from on Brynda's condition at signing.

## Effect On Likely Outcome

This fact modestly improves the defense trial position if it can be supported by admissible testimony or a written statement. It does not eliminate jury risk, but it makes the plaintiff's undue-influence burden harder because it pushes against the idea that Brynda was visibly compromised or unaware at the transaction.

Prior practical trial range was roughly 65-75% defense-favorable. With a credible notary witness available, the defense-favorable range could move toward the upper end of that band, and potentially slightly above it, assuming the notary testimony is clean, specific, and consistent with the documents/video.

I would not materially change the legal-expense recovery estimate from this fact alone. It helps win the remaining claim; it does not by itself create a fee-shifting basis.

## Mediation Use

This fact should be used in mediation as a concise pressure point:

"The signing was not just Wes and Brynda. A notary was present, recalls Brynda as cheerful and completely aware, and says she would not have notarized if Brynda appeared compromised."

That is useful because plaintiff's remaining claim depends on proving undue influence at signing. A notary witness undercuts that story in a way that is easy for a mediator or jury to understand.
