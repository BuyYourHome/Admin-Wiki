## Purpose

Identify parts of the Simplified OA that are specifically relevant to ownership by retirement or tax-favored accounts, and flag provisions that may need to change before the language is reused for another entity such as BYH or BYH 401K LLC.

This is a drafting analysis only. Legal, tax, ERISA, prohibited-transaction, UBTI/UDFI, and plan-compliance questions need attorney or CPA review before final use.

## High-Level Finding

The Simplified OA is heavily tailored to an entity owned by IRA/HSA/ESA-style account Members and by named account holders. It should not be reused for another entity by only changing the entity name. The retirement-account provisions affect membership identity, voting, management authority, buy-sell rights, capital contributions, borrowing, tax reporting, annual valuations, signatures, and Exhibit A.

For another entity, especially one connected to a 401(k), the drafter should first decide:

- Which retirement or tax-favored account type owns the interest.
- Whether the legal Member is a custodian, trustee, plan, IRA, HSA, ESA, 401(k), or another entity.
- Who has voting authority.
- Who is a disqualified person.
- Who may act as Manager without causing prohibited-transaction issues.
- Whether the entity will borrow money, generate UBTI/UDFI, or need annual valuation reporting.

## Provisions To Review Before Reuse

### 1. Opening Replacement Of Prior Retirement Account Members

The opening paragraphs replace Quest Trust Company FBO accounts with Heritage IRA FBO accounts for Wes's HSA, Wes's Roth IRA, and Jeanette Hollinger's Roth IRA.

Why it matters:

These are entity-specific member substitutions. They should be removed or rewritten for any entity that does not have these exact prior and replacement account Members.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

If BYH or BYH 401K LLC is owned by a 401(k), trust, plan, or other retirement vehicle, the member identity and replacement language must match that structure. Do not carry over HSA/Roth IRA substitutions unless those exact accounts are Members.

### 2. Definition And Role Of Account Holder

The agreement uses "Account Holder" for the beneficial owner of an IRA or HSA, or the Responsible Individual for a Coverdell ESA.

Why it matters:

Many later provisions depend on this definition, including death/disability buy-sell rights, voting, manager eligibility, compensation, and prohibited-transaction controls.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

A 401(k) structure may require different terminology, such as participant, trustee, plan administrator, custodian, fiduciary, or plan sponsor. The current Account Holder definition may not fit a 401(k) member.

### 3. Buy-Sell Rights After Death Or Disability

The buy-sell provisions give the Company a right to purchase Units if an individual Member, or the Account Holder of a non-individual Member, dies or becomes permanently disabled.

Why it matters:

This treats the human beneficial owner as a triggering person even where the legal Member may be an account, custodian, or entity.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

If the Member is a 401(k) trust or plan, the triggering events may need to be tied to the plan, participant, trustee, beneficiary, or another control point. Attorney review is needed before reusing this trigger.

### 4. Voting By Retirement Account Holders And Fiduciaries

The voting section says Units held in retirement or savings accounts, including IRAs, HSAs, and ESAs, may be voted by the account holder or ESA Responsible Individual until the Company receives notice of death, incapacity, a competing claim, or fiduciary control.

Why it matters:

This is a practical voting rule for account-owned Units, but it assumes the human account holder can direct voting unless a fiduciary asserts control.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

For a 401(k), voting authority may belong to a trustee, plan fiduciary, participant-directed account, plan document process, or other authorized signer. The rule should be rewritten to match the actual plan documents and custodian/trustee authority.

### 5. Entity Member Voting And Signatures

The agreement says Units held by an entity must be voted by the person the entity designates in writing, and later says the Company may rely on the authority of the person signing for an entity Member.

Why it matters:

This is relevant if the Member is a custodian, trust company, plan, LLC, trust, or other entity.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

For a 401(k)-owned entity, signer authority should be tied to the plan/trust documents and trustee or custodian authority. This language may need more precision.

### 6. Manager Eligibility And Account Holder Restrictions

The agreement states that a Manager generally should not be an Account Holder of a Member or another disqualified person under IRC Section 4975, unless Members approve otherwise. It also states that named account holders Wesley D. Browning and Jeanette W. Hollinger have no signature authority, operational responsibility, authority to bind the entity, or unfettered access/control over assets.

Why it matters:

This is one of the central prohibited-transaction safeguards. It separates retirement account owners from direct operational control.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

The named individuals and their restrictions are entity-specific. For another entity, the drafter must identify the correct account holders, participants, trustees, fiduciaries, and disqualified persons. If the entity is 401(k)-owned, the control restrictions may need to be rewritten for 401(k) rules and plan fiduciary roles.

### 7. Manager Compensation

The agreement allows Manager compensation with 75% Member approval, but prohibits compensation if the Manager is an Account Holder or disqualified person under IRC Section 4975.

Why it matters:

Compensating a disqualified person can create prohibited-transaction risk.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

Before reuse, confirm whether the proposed manager is a participant, trustee, fiduciary, family member, service provider, or other disqualified person. The compensation rule may need different thresholds or a stricter prohibition.

### 8. Major Decisions And Nonrecourse Borrowing

The agreement includes borrowing above $10,000 on a nonrecourse basis as a Major Decision and later prohibits Members described in the retirement-account provisions, or other disqualified persons, from guaranteeing Company debt.

Why it matters:

Debt involving retirement-account-owned entities can create UDFI/UBTI issues, and guarantees by disqualified persons can create prohibited-transaction issues.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

For another entity, especially real-estate entities, borrowing rules should be tailored to expected financing. If any retirement account owns the entity, personal guarantees and recourse debt need attorney/CPA review.

### 9. Prohibited Transactions

Article 9 restricts transactions that would be prohibited under IRC Section 4975 for IRA, ESA, or HSA Members. It also references statutory restrictions under IRC Sections 408, 408A, 530, and 223.

Why it matters:

This is core retirement-account compliance language.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

If the relevant owner is a 401(k), this list may be incomplete or misdirected. A 401(k) may require references to qualified-plan rules, plan fiduciary rules, ERISA considerations, plan documents, or other Internal Revenue Code provisions. Do not assume IRA/HSA/ESA language is enough.

### 10. UBTI And UDFI

The agreement states that IRA, ESA, and HSA Members are subject to tax on Unrelated Business Taxable Income and Unrelated Debt-Financed Income, that pass-through income flows to Members, and that the Manager must provide information for Form 990-T and may make distributions to cover tax liabilities.

Why it matters:

This affects tax reporting, cash planning, and member expectations.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

If a 401(k) or other plan owns the entity, UBTI/UDFI rules may still matter, but reporting mechanics and responsible parties may differ. Confirm who files Form 990-T and who receives tax-reporting information.

### 11. Annual Valuations

The agreement requires the Manager to provide fair market value of Member interests by January 15 each year, based on reliable data or a professional appraiser if needed, for IRS reporting and custodian/administrator requirements.

Why it matters:

Retirement account custodians and administrators often require annual valuations.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

The valuation date, deadline, standard, and recipient may need to match the specific custodian, trustee, plan administrator, or 401(k) reporting process.

### 12. Additional Capital, Member Loans, And Services

The agreement says additional capital contributions require IRS confirmation that they will not violate prohibited-transaction rules, must be proportional, and Members cannot loan money or provide services to the Company.

Why it matters:

Contributions, loans, and services by retirement account owners or disqualified persons can create prohibited-transaction problems.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

For another entity, determine whether capital calls are allowed, whether all contributions must be proportional, and whether any owner or related person will provide services. This likely needs attorney and CPA review.

### 13. Pass-Through Tax Classification

The agreement states that the Company will be treated as a pass-through entity, either disregarded if one Member or partnership if multiple Members, and that Manager(s) must not change that classification.

Why it matters:

Tax classification affects reporting to retirement account owners and UBTI/UDFI handling.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

If BYH or BYH 401K LLC has a different ownership structure, single-member status, partnership status, or tax election, this language may need to change.

### 14. RMD Provision

The agreement says an IRA Member's Account Holder subject to required minimum distributions must rely on other IRAs or ensure enough cash is available in the IRA to satisfy RMDs, and that the Company cannot guarantee distributions will match RMD timing. The text includes an internal drafting comment: "I would rather prohibit accounts that require RMD."

Why it matters:

This is a live drafting issue, not final polished language.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

The RMD language should be revisited before any derivative use. For a 401(k), RMD rules and responsible parties may differ. Decide whether to prohibit RMD-subject accounts, require separate liquidity, or use a different rule.

### 15. Certification, Signature Blocks, And Exhibit A

The certification and Exhibit A list specific Heritage IRA FBO account names, account numbers, addresses, emails, and unit allocations.

Why it matters:

These are not generic provisions. They define who is signing and who owns the Units.

Possible BYH/BYH 401K issue:

All signature blocks and Exhibit A must be replaced for any other entity. For a 401(k)-owned entity, determine the exact legal Member name, trustee/custodian signer, plan name, participant role, and ownership units.

## Likely Reuse Categories

### Keep But Tailor

- Prohibited transaction restrictions.
- Disqualified-person management restrictions.
- No personal guarantees by disqualified persons.
- UBTI/UDFI reporting provisions.
- Annual valuation requirements.
- Manager fiduciary duties.
- No services or loans by retirement-account Members or disqualified persons.

### Replace For Each Entity

- Entity name.
- Member/account names.
- Account numbers.
- Account holder names.
- Prior-account replacement recitals.
- Signature blocks.
- Exhibit A.
- Manager identity and authority.
- Tax classification if ownership changes.

### Needs Attorney/CPA Review Before Reuse

- Whether IRA/HSA/ESA language also works for a 401(k).
- Who may vote interests owned by a plan, trust, custodian, or account.
- Whether any proposed Manager is a disqualified person.
- Whether compensation to a Manager or service provider is allowed.
- Whether capital contributions, loans, guarantees, or services are prohibited.
- How UBTI/UDFI and Form 990-T reporting will be handled.
- Whether RMD-subject accounts should be prohibited or handled by liquidity rules.

## BYH/BYH 401K Drafting Warning

If the Simplified OA is used for BYH, BYH 401K LLC, or another entity, do not simply copy the Sell Your Home retirement-account provisions. First identify the actual owner type and authority structure. If the owner is connected to a 401(k), the IRA/HSA/ESA-specific language may be underinclusive, misnamed, or assigned to the wrong person.

Use this analysis as a checklist before preparing any derivative OA.
