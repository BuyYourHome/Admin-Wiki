# Planning Cost Framework

This cost analysis separates the project into three major cost categories:

1. Design and entitlement
2. Development and pre-construction
3. Build and delivery

The project is assumed to include:

* approximately **5,000-6,000 SF** of residential building area,
* 8 private residential suites,
* shared common areas,
* post-frame construction,
* slab-on-grade construction,
* engineered onsite wastewater,
* private utility infrastructure,
* sponsor-owned equipment,
* sponsor-controlled crew capacity,
* and long-term rental operation rather than speculative resale.

This file keeps two cost lenses in view:

1. **Sponsor-controlled working case**: reflects post-frame construction, sponsor-owned equipment, and sponsor-controlled crew capacity.
2. **Outside-market benchmark**: reflects a more conservative third-party development and construction pricing scenario.

External estimating references support keeping both lenses visible. RSMeans describes square-foot estimating as localized by building type and market, and notes that cost data should account for productivity, crew composition, overhead, profit, and location factors. Post-frame cost guides show materially lower shell costs than conventional commercial/residential construction, but also note that slab/foundation, site preparation, and custom finishes increase the total cost. North Carolina septic references show conventional systems can be modest, while advanced/engineered systems can rise materially depending on soil, county, and system type.

## Sponsor-Controlled Cost Justification

The sponsor-controlled case can be justified only if the project captures meaningful cost advantages from post-frame construction and self-performing parts of the work. The sponsor has confirmed the following cost-saving assumptions:

* post-frame construction lowers the structural shell cost compared with a fully third-party conventional build,
* sponsor-owned equipment reduces equipment rental, mobilization, and subcontractor markups,
* sponsor-controlled crew capacity reduces general contractor overhead and profit on self-performed work,
* the building program remains disciplined and does not drift into custom apartment-level finishes,
* major subcontracted trades are competitively bid,
* suite count and bathroom/kitchenette count do not expand,
* finish package stays durable and efficient rather than luxury,
* and the project carries a disciplined contingency.

The sponsor did not confirm the assumption that wastewater will avoid a high-end engineered solution, and confirmed the wastewater and finish-drift risks as relevant. The working case has therefore been adjusted upward from **700,000-1,300,000** to **750,000-1,500,000**.

The current target range implies approximately:

| Metric | Low Case | High Case |
|---|---:|---:|
| Total project cost | 750,000 | 1,500,000 |
| Cost per SF at 5,000 SF | 150/SF | 300/SF |
| Cost per SF at 6,000 SF | 125/SF | 250/SF |

That range is plausible as a sponsor-controlled target, but only because it assumes a different delivery method than the outside-market benchmark.

## Conditions Required To Support 750,000-1,500,000

The range is supportable only if the following remain true:

* the post-frame approach is accepted through design and permitting,
* the sponsor can self-perform or directly manage a meaningful portion of construction,
* equipment ownership materially reduces sitework and construction logistics cost,
* major subcontracted trades are competitively bid,
* the suite count and bathroom/kitchenette count do not expand,
* the finish package stays durable and efficient rather than luxury,
* and the project carries a disciplined contingency.

The wastewater assumption remains unresolved and is therefore carried as a higher risk-adjusted allowance.

## Risks That Could Break The Target Range

The project could move above 1,500,000 if:

* Wake County or wastewater engineering requires an advanced/high-cost system,
* post-frame design requires expensive fire, code, or occupancy upgrades,
* mechanical/electrical/plumbing scope is more intensive than expected,
* the project requires more extensive parking, drainage, or access improvements,
* labor availability limits the sponsor crew advantage,
* or the building finish level moves closer to conventional apartment construction.
